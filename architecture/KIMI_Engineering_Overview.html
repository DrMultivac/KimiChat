Pain Phenotype Classification

Nociceptive (Mechanical)

Localized pain proportional to tissue injury. Mechanical factors reproduce symptoms. Normal imaging, no neurological signs.

Rx Bias: Manual therapy, progressive exercise, postural correction

Neuropathic (Nerve)

Radiating pain with burning quality. Positive nerve tension tests, dermatomal distribution, paresthesias present.

Rx Bias: Neurodynamic exercises, desensitization, nerve education

Nociplastic (Central)

Disproportionate to injury, widespread distribution, sympathetic features. Sleep disruption, mood disorders, hyperalgesia.

Rx Bias: Graded activity, CBT, pain neuroscience education, pacing

Why phenotyping matters: Pain phenotype determines the entire treatment trajectory. A patient with nociceptive knee OA gets exercise-heavy treatment. A patient with nociplastic features (central sensitization, widespread pain, sleep disruption) gets CBT-heavy treatment with graded activity. The phenotyper runs at intake AND at every reassessment, because phenotypes can shift as treatment progresses — a nociceptive presentation can become nociplastic if pain catastrophizing develops.

4-Phase Rehabilitation Protocol

Foundation

Weeks 1–4

Pain reduction, gentle mobility, isometric strengthening, pain neuroscience education. PROMIS PI ≥60, PF ≤35.

Building

Weeks 5–8

Core activation, isotonic strengthening, CBT modules introduced. PROMIS PI 55-59, PF 36-40.

Strengthening

Weeks 9–16

Closed-chain stability, functional strengthening, graded exposure. PROMIS PF 41-45.

Maintenance

Weeks 17–52

Loading complexity, sport/occupation-specific progressions, self-management transition. PROMIS PF ≥46.

Score-based, not time-based: Progression isn't automatic — it's driven by PROMIS scores. A patient who hasn't improved by MCID after 3 consecutive weekly assessments triggers automatic clinician escalation. Regression is equally data-driven: if scores decline by ≥MCID, the patient drops back a phase and gets clinical review. This ensures no patient is advanced prematurely or left stuck without intervention.

Patient Safety

Safety Architecture

KIMI is not a diagnostic tool — it is a coaching platform with non-bypassable safety rails and systematic escalation to human clinicians.

4-Tier Escalation Protocol

Tier 1 — Automated

Real-time

LLM phrase detection identifies red flag language. Auto-generates evidence summary. Routes to Clinical Decision Engine without alarming patient.

Tier 2 — Physician Review

4–12 hours

Physician or APP reviews flagged case. Applies clinical judgment. Decision to monitor, escalate, or resolve. Full documentation.

Tier 3 — Urgent

Same day

Direct patient communication. Urgent specialist/ER referral. KIMI care plan pause. Enhanced monitoring. Family notification if needed.

Tier 4 — Emergency

Immediate / 911

ED routing. EMS notification. Emergency contact auto-call. KIMI care plan suspension. Post-emergency coordination.

100% Sensitivity Target

Zero false negatives for serious pathology. The scanner is deliberately tuned to over-identify — false positives (unnecessary escalations) are always preferable to false negatives (missed pathology). The Mayo Clinic-validated risk algorithm achieved 100% sensitivity in clinical validation.

Non-Bypassable Screening

Red flag scanning runs at intake, periodic reassessment, and whenever new symptoms are reported. It cannot be skipped, deferred, or overridden by any system component. This is architecturally enforced, not just policy.

Never Minimizes or Delays

KIMI never minimizes concerning symptoms or delays escalation based on uncertainty. The design principle: "When in doubt, escalate." Clinical decisions remain with licensed providers — KIMI's role is to surface and route, not to diagnose.

Mental Health Crisis Protocol

Dedicated pathway for suicidal ideation, severe psychological distress, and psychiatric emergencies. Immediate Tier 4 escalation with 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline resource provision and emergency contact notification.

Why this matters for engineering: The Red Flag Scanner processes every single patient input — not just clinical check-ins, but also free-text messages, exercise feedback, and even scheduling requests. If a patient writes "my legs feel weird when I walk" in a message about rescheduling an appointment, the scanner catches it (possible myelopathy). This means the scanner must be performant enough to run inline without adding latency to the conversation, yet thorough enough to catch subtle linguistic patterns.

Data Pipeline

How Patient Data Flows Through KIMI

From initial input through clinical action and EHR documentation — every interaction passes through safety, intelligence, and compliance layers.

1

Patient Input Capture

Web/mobile interface collects pain reports, exercise feedback, PROM responses, and free-text messages from daily check-ins and ad-hoc interactions.

2

Validation & Standardization

Data validated against schema, normalized to FHIR-compatible formats. Completeness checked before downstream processing. Invalid data flagged for correction.

3

Real-Time Red Flag Processing

Every input immediately processed by the 8-region Red Flag Scanner. This is non-bypassable — concerning findings trigger instant escalation pathway before any other processing occurs.

4

Clinical Decision Engine

Pain phenotyping, PROM scoring, phase progression assessment, exercise selection, education sequencing, and care plan updates via rule-based + ML hybrid algorithms.

5

Knowledge Base Retrieval

BERT-based semantic search against 28 clinical content files (~34,500 lines) for relevant guidelines, exercises, contraindications, and education content. All retrieved content is evidence-tagged.

6

Conversation Engine Response

LLM synthesizes clinical context into empathetic, plain-language patient responses (6th-8th grade reading level) using motivational interviewing framework (OARS). Max 500 tokens.

7

Documentation & EHR Sync

All interactions logged for CMS audit trail. Clinical data exported via FHIR bridge (8 resource types) to Epic/Cerner/Athena. Real-time for critical data, nightly batch for routine. Population analytics computed.

Critical First Encounter

11-Step Intake Protocol

The single most important interaction in the 12-month care period — establishing the clinical foundation within 25-35 minutes.

6 Mandatory Intake Functions

Every enrollment must accomplish all six in a single conversational session:

1. Anatomical site classification → 8 ACCESS body regions
2. Pain phenotype characterization → nociceptive / neuropathic / nociplastic
3. Red flag screening → immediate escalation vs. proceed with program
4. ACCESS-mandated PROM baseline → 60-day CMS submission deadline
5. Psychosocial risk stratification → STarT Back, PHQ-2, GAD-2
6. Initial care plan construction → Phase 1 exercise prescription

Design Constraints

Operating within unique conversational AI limitations:

No physical examination — reliance on patient self-report and validated instruments

Session duration — 25-35 min optimal, 45 min max (patient fatigue risk)

Health literacy — 6th-8th grade reading level, plain language, teach-back verification

FHIR compliance — all data elements must map to standard FHIR resources

Same PROM throughout — baseline instrument locked for all 12 months
