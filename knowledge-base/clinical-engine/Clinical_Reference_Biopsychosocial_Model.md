# Biopsychosocial Model — Therapeutic Modules for Chronic MSK Pain

## 1. Conceptual Framework

### Engel's Biopsychosocial Model Applied to Chronic Pain

The biopsychosocial model, originally proposed by George Engel (1977), reconceptualizes illness as the product of biological, psychological, and social factors in dynamic interaction. For chronic musculoskeletal (MSK) pain, this framework moves beyond a reductionist biomedical lens focused solely on tissue pathology to integrate the patient's cognitive appraisals, emotional state, behaviors, and social context. This integration is critical because tissue pathology alone has poor predictive validity for pain severity, disability, and recovery in chronic MSK conditions (Sloan et al. 2022, PMID 35505570).

### Biological Factors

**Tissue Pathology and Structural Abnormalities:**
- While structural imaging findings (e.g., degenerative disc disease, osteoarthritis) are common, their correlation with pain intensity and disability is weak, particularly in chronic conditions (Brinjikji et al. 2015).
- Subclinical structural pathology is ubiquitous in asymptomatic populations, undermining a simplistic biomedical model.

**Peripheral and Central Sensitization:**
- **Peripheral sensitization:** Increased responsiveness of nociceptors due to inflammatory mediators and tissue injury.
- **Central sensitization:** Amplified processing of pain signals in the dorsal horn and brain due to prolonged nociceptive input, neuroinflammation, or altered endogenous pain modulation.
- Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI) cutoff ≥40 identifies patients with widespread pain and hyperalgesia, many of whom show phenotypic overlap with fibromyalgia and other central sensitivity syndromes.

**Deconditioning and Physical Dysfunction:**
- Chronic pain-related avoidance of movement leads to progressive deconditioning, reduced aerobic capacity, muscle atrophy, and further functional decline.
- PROMIS Physical Function scores decline progressively in untreated chronic pain, creating a vicious cycle of pain → fear-avoidance → deconditioning → pain.

### Psychological Factors

**Cognitive Appraisals and Catastrophizing:**
- **Pain catastrophizing** (expectation that pain will worsen, rumination about suffering, helplessness) is one of the strongest psychological predictors of pain intensity and disability (Sullivan et al. 2001).
- Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS) cutoff ≥30 identifies clinically significant catastrophizing and predicts poor treatment response.
- Catastrophizing mediates the relationship between pain intensity and disability, making it a key intervention target.

**Fear-Avoidance and Kinesiophobia:**
- **Fear-avoidance** is the anxiety-driven avoidance of movement or activity due to pain-related fear (Vlaeyen & Linton 2012).
- Kinesiophobia (fear of movement/reinjury) maintains disability through avoidance learning and deconditioning.
- Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK) cutoff ≥37 identifies high fear-avoidance; Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ) physical activity subscale ≥15 and work subscale ≥34 are clinical thresholds.
- High fear-avoidance predicts persistent disability and treatment non-response.

**Depression and Anxiety:**
- Comorbid depression (PHQ-9 ≥10, moderate symptoms) is present in approximately 30–50% of chronic pain populations and bidirectionally worsens pain and disability.
- Anxiety (GAD-7 ≥10) amplifies pain perception and reduces exercise tolerance.
- Both conditions reduce pain self-efficacy and engagement in adaptive coping.

**Pain-Related Beliefs and Self-Efficacy:**
- **Pain Self-Efficacy Questionnaire (PSEQ):** Lower scores indicate reduced confidence in performing activities despite pain; low self-efficacy predicts disability and treatment non-response.
- Maladaptive beliefs about the meaning of pain (e.g., "pain means tissue damage is ongoing"), permanence ("my pain will never improve"), and controllability (e.g., "I cannot control my pain") perpetuate distress and disability.

**Central Sensitization as a Psychological-Neurobiological Construct:**
- Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI) cutoff ≥40 identifies cognitive-somatic symptoms consistent with central sensitization (widespread pain, fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, mood disturbance).
- Cognitive restructuring targeting health anxiety and catastrophic beliefs about widespread pain is a core intervention.

### Social Factors

**Social Support and Social Isolation:**
- Strong social support is protective against pain-related disability; social isolation worsens outcomes.
- Family responses to pain (solicitousness vs. punishing behaviors) shape pain behaviors and avoidance.

**Work Status and Socioeconomic Factors:**
- Work-related disability strongly predicts chronicity; prolonged work absence creates economic stress and identity loss.
- Lower socioeconomic status is associated with worse pain outcomes, reduced access to multimodal care, and higher disability rates.
- Vocational rehabilitation is a key element of social reintegration.

**Health Literacy:**
- Low health literacy is associated with reduced understanding of pain neurophysiology, higher catastrophizing, and lower engagement in self-management (Oosterhaven et al. 2023, PMID 37868618).
- Tailoring education to literacy level is essential for effective pain neuroscience education (PNE).

**Cultural Beliefs About Pain:**
- Cultural background influences pain expression, beliefs about causation, and acceptability of different treatments.
- Person-centered, culturally informed communication is essential for building trust and engagement.

### Evidence for Biopsychosocial Approach Superiority

**Cochrane and Systematic Review Evidence:**
- Cochrane systematic reviews consistently demonstrate that multimodal biopsychosocial interventions (combining pain education, cognitive-behavioral therapy, and exercise) produce larger and more sustained improvements in pain and disability than biomedical-only approaches (e.g., surgery alone, injections alone) (Welch et al. 2020).
- For chronic low back pain, combined psychological and physical interventions outperform either modality alone (Pincus et al. 2013).
- For fibromyalgia, cognitive-behavioral therapy combined with aerobic exercise produces effect sizes (d=0.7–1.2) substantially larger than medication alone.

## 2. Validated Assessment Tools

### Biological Domain

#### PROMIS Physical Function (PF-6b and CAT versions)
- **Description:** Item Response Theory (IRT)-calibrated measure of self-reported physical functioning across domains (mobility, self-care, upper and lower extremity).
- **Scoring:** T-score metric (mean=50, SD=10 in U.S. general population).
  - T-score 40–60: Within normal limits.
  - T-score <40: Below average function (clinically significant impairment).
  - T-score ≤30: Severe functional limitation.
- **Interpretation:** Higher T-scores indicate better function. Minimally important clinical difference (MCID) ≈ 3–5 T-score points.
- **Use in Coaching:** Baseline and periodic assessment (8–12 weeks) to track functional recovery and adjust activity prescription.

#### PROMIS Pain Interference (PI-6a and CAT versions)
- **Description:** Self-reported impact of pain on daily activities, social participation, and emotional well-being.
- **Scoring:** T-score metric (mean=50, SD=10 in U.S. general population with chronic pain).
  - T-score ≤40: Minimal pain interference.
  - T-score 40–60: Mild to moderate interference (clinically significant).
  - T-score >60: Severe interference.
- **Interpretation:** Higher T-scores indicate greater pain impact. MCID ≈ 3–5 T-score points.
- **Use in Coaching:** Track response to interventions; high baseline scores may indicate need for intensive psychosocial support.

#### Numeric Rating Scale (NRS 0–10) for Pain Intensity
- **Description:** Single-item, 0–10 scale self-report of current or average pain intensity.
- **Scoring:**
  - 0 = No pain.
  - 1–3 = Mild pain.
  - 4–6 = Moderate pain.
  - 7–10 = Severe pain.
- **Interpretation:** MCID in chronic pain ≈ 1–2 points (15–20% reduction from baseline). Useful for tracking daily fluctuations.
- **Use in Coaching:** Daily self-monitoring; coach should emphasize that pain intensity alone does not determine disability (dissociating pain from activity/disability is a key PNE goal).

#### Region-Specific Patient-Reported Outcome Measures (PROMs)

**Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) — Low Back Pain**
- 10-item measure of back pain-related functional disability.
- Scoring: 0–100 scale; ≥30% indicates clinically significant disability.
- MCID ≈ 10–15 points.

**Neck Disability Index (NDI) — Neck Pain**
- 10-item counterpart to ODI for cervical spine.
- Scoring: 0–100 scale; ≥22% indicates clinically significant disability.
- MCID ≈ 5–10 points.

**Quick Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand (QuickDASH) — Upper Extremity**
- 11-item measure of upper extremity function.
- Scoring: 0–100 scale; lower scores = better function.
- MCID ≈ 15–20 points.

**Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score for Joint Replacement (KOOS JR) — Knee OA**
- 7-item short form assessing knee pain, function, and quality of life.
- Scoring: 0–100 scale; higher scores = better outcome.
- MCID ≈ 10–15 points.

**Hip Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score for Joint Replacement (HOOS JR) — Hip OA**
- 7-item short form analogous to KOOS JR for hip.
- Scoring: 0–100 scale; higher scores = better outcome.
- MCID ≈ 10–15 points.

### Psychological Domain

#### Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS)
- **Description:** 13-item self-report measure of catastrophic thinking about pain (rumination, magnification, helplessness).
- **Scoring:**
  - 0–30: Non-catastrophizers.
  - ≥30: Clinically significant catastrophizing (strong predictor of poor prognosis).
  - ≥52: Severe catastrophizing.
- **Interpretation:** PCS is bidirectionally related to pain intensity, disability, and depression. Strong evidence for targeting catastrophizing through cognitive-behavioral intervention.
- **Use in Coaching:** Baseline screening; if PCS ≥30, prioritize cognitive restructuring and behavioral exposure modules.

#### Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK)
- **Description:** 17-item measure of fear of movement and reinjury.
- **Scoring:**
  - <37: Low fear-avoidance.
  - 37–40: Moderate fear-avoidance.
  - >40: High fear-avoidance (strong predictor of disability chronicity).
- **Interpretation:** TSK captures the anxiety-driven avoidance loop; high scores predict poor response to standard biomedical interventions.
- **Use in Coaching:** If TSK ≥37, graded exposure (systematic desensitization to feared movements) is indicated; coach should frame exercises as "learning" experiments rather than symptom provocation.

#### Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ)
- **Description:** 16-item measure assessing fear-avoidance beliefs about physical activity and work.
- **Scoring:**
  - Physical Activity subscale: ≥15 indicates high fear of physical activity.
  - Work subscale: ≥34 indicates high fear-avoidance regarding work.
- **Interpretation:** Work-related fear-avoidance strongly predicts occupational disability.
- **Use in Coaching:** Stratify patients for vocational rehabilitation; if work subscale ≥34, integrate work-specific exposure and return-to-work planning.

#### Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9)
- **Description:** 9-item screening and severity measure for depressive disorder.
- **Scoring:**
  - 0–4: Minimal symptoms.
  - 5–9: Mild depression.
  - ≥10: Moderate depression (clinical threshold).
  - ≥15: Moderately severe depression.
  - ≥20: Severe depression.
- **Interpretation:** Comorbid depression amplifies pain and disability; PHQ-9 ≥10 warrants assessment for treatment (antidepressant, psychotherapy, or both).
- **Use in Coaching:** If PHQ-9 ≥10, refer for concurrent mental health evaluation; coach integrates behavioral activation (scheduling pleasant/meaningful activities) and mood monitoring.

#### Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7)
- **Description:** 7-item screening and severity measure for generalized anxiety disorder.
- **Scoring:**
  - 0–4: Minimal anxiety.
  - 5–9: Mild anxiety.
  - ≥10: Moderate anxiety (clinical threshold).
  - ≥15: Severe anxiety.
- **Interpretation:** Anxiety amplifies pain perception and avoidance behaviors.
- **Use in Coaching:** If GAD-7 ≥10, refer for concurrent mental health support; coach integrates relaxation training (diaphragmatic breathing, progressive muscle relaxation) and behavioral exposure.

#### Pain Self-Efficacy Questionnaire (PSEQ)
- **Description:** 10-item measure of patient confidence in performing activities despite pain.
- **Scoring:** 0–60 scale.
  - 0–20: Low self-efficacy (risk factor for disability).
  - 20–40: Moderate self-efficacy.
  - ≥40: High self-efficacy (protective).
- **Interpretation:** Low self-efficacy strongly predicts disability. Self-efficacy is malleable through graded success experiences (active learning) and motivational interviewing.
- **Use in Coaching:** Track PSEQ as outcome; coach uses confidence rulers ("How confident are you that you can walk 10 minutes?") to set achievable goals and build self-efficacy incrementally.

#### Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI)
- **Description:** 25-item measure of somatic and cognitive symptoms consistent with central sensitization (widespread pain, fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, mood disturbance, autonomic symptoms).
- **Scoring:** 0–100 scale.
  - <40: Low likelihood of central sensitization.
  - ≥40: Probable central sensitization (Suokas et al. 2014, PMID 24530604).
- **Interpretation:** CSI ≥40 identifies patients with phenotypic overlap with fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue, and multiple somatic complaints. These patients require modified exercise dosing and health anxiety management.
- **Use in Coaching:** If CSI ≥40, prioritize pacing strategies (avoiding boom-bust cycles), pain neuroscience education, and gentle gradation; exercise prescription must be conservative (low intensity, frequent, consistent).

---

### Social Domain

#### Single-Item Health Literacy Screener
- **Description:** Single question: "How confident are you filling out medical forms by yourself?" (Chew et al. 2008).
- **Scoring:**
  - Extremely/Very confident: Adequate health literacy.
  - Somewhat confident/Not very confident: Limited health literacy (risk factor for poor engagement).
- **Interpretation:** Limited health literacy is associated with reduced understanding of pain mechanisms, higher catastrophizing, and lower engagement in self-management.
- **Use in Coaching:** If limited health literacy, simplify educational language, use analogies (e.g., "pain like smoke alarm sensitivity"), and prioritize conversational PNE delivery.

#### Social Support Assessment
- **Description:** Brief structured interview or self-report checklist assessing:
  - Availability of family/friend support.
  - Quality of support (emotional vs. solicitous/punishing).
  - Access to community resources.
- **Interpretation:** Strong support predicts better adherence; family involvement in coaching (when appropriate) enhances outcomes.
- **Use in Coaching:** If social support is limited, develop peer support strategies or community resource connections; if family response is maladaptive (solicitousness or blame), consider partner involvement in education.

#### Work Status and Functional Limitations
- **Description:** Structured assessment of:
  - Current employment status (employed, on leave, unemployed, retired).
  - Job demands (physical, cognitive, emotional).
  - Barriers to return-to-work (fear-avoidance, lack of accommodations, economic disincentive).
- **Use in Coaching:** Stratify patients for vocational rehabilitation; integrate work-specific exposure and return-to-work goal setting.

#### AHRQ SHARE Approach for Shared Decision-Making
- **Description:** Framework for collaborative goal-setting and preference elicitation:
  - **Seek:** Ask about the patient's preferences, values, concerns.
  - **Help:** Provide evidence-based information about options.
  - **Ask:** Check understanding and invite questions.
  - **Reach:** Collaborate on a mutually agreed-upon plan.
- **Use in Coaching:** Integrate AHRQ SHARE into all goal-setting conversations; patient-centered goal selection improves adherence and satisfaction.

## 3. Therapeutic Module: Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE)

### Definition and Rationale

Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) is a structured, evidence-based educational intervention that reconceptualizes pain as an output of the central nervous system (CNS) rather than a direct measure of tissue damage. PNE aims to normalize pain experiences, reduce threat appraisal, and enhance self-efficacy for activity and recovery. By explaining the mechanisms of pain processing, neuroplasticity, and the role of psychological factors, PNE addresses the catastrophic, fear-driven beliefs that maintain chronic pain and disability.

**Key Educational Elements:**
1. **Pain Neurophysiology:** Explanation of nociception (detection of harmful stimuli) vs. pain (a biopsychosocial output); the role of the dorsal horn, ascending and descending pathways, and cortical processing.
2. **Central Sensitization:** How prolonged pain input, combined with psychological stress and avoidance, sensitizes the nervous system, amplifying pain signals.
3. **Neuroplasticity:** The nervous system's capacity to rewire and normalize pain processing through repeated safe experiences (desensitization) and cognitive change.
4. **Threat Appraisal:** How beliefs about pain severity, permanence, and controllability shape pain perception and avoidance; the role of catastrophizing and fear-avoidance in maintaining pain.
5. **Pain Behavior Loop:** How avoidance and inactivity perpetuate deconditioning and pain, while graded activity and engagement interrupt this cycle.

### Targeted Outcomes

- Reduced pain catastrophizing (PCS <30).
- Reduced kinesiophobia (TSK <37).
- Reduced fear-avoidance (FABQ physical activity <15).
- Improved pain self-efficacy (PSEQ >40).
- Increased engagement in physical activity and functional goals.

### Optimal Dosing (Evidence-Based)

Recent meta-analyses (Núñez-Cortés et al. 2023, PMID 37516218; Salazar-Méndez et al. 2023) synthesized data from 88+ randomized controlled trials to establish PNE dosing thresholds for achieving Minimally Important Clinical Difference (MCID):

**Núñez-Cortés 2023 Dosing Thresholds (PMID 37516218):**
- **Pain MCID:** 200 min total PNE (example: 50 min/week × 4 weeks, or 10 min/day × 20 days).
- **Disability MCID:** 150 min total PNE.

**Salazar-Méndez 2023 Dosing Thresholds:**
- **Kinesiophobia MCID:** 100 min total PNE.
- **Anxiety MCID:** 200 min total PNE.
- **Catastrophizing MCID:** 400 min total PNE.

**Interpretation:**
- Patients with high catastrophizing (PCS ≥30) or high anxiety (GAD-7 ≥10) require ≥400 min PNE to achieve MCID.
- Standard-risk patients can achieve pain/disability MCID with 150–200 min.
- PNE is most effective when combined with physical activity and behavioral interventions (see Module 6: Motivational Interviewing).

### Key Components of Effective PNE

#### 1. Explaining Central Sensitization and Pain Biology
- Use accessible metaphors: pain as a "volume knob" in the nervous system; the "protection mechanism" becomes overactive due to threat appraisal.
- Normalize widespread or non-anatomical pain: "Your nervous system is protecting too much; we can retrain it."
- Avoid the "pain means damage" message; introduce the "pain means the nervous system perceives threat" message.

#### 2. Neuroplasticity and Learning
- Explain that repetition of safe experiences (movement, activity) can "reprogram" the nervous system.
- Link PNE to exercise adherence: "The more you move, the more the nervous system learns it's safe."

#### 3. Threat Appraisal and Cognitions
- Identify catastrophic thoughts ("This pain will ruin my life," "My spine is deteriorating").
- Reframe: "Your nervous system is interpreting pain as more dangerous than it is. Let's prove to your nervous system that movement is safe."
- Normalize pain variability and environmental influences (stress, sleep, activity).

#### 4. Pain Behavior Loop
- Illustrate the avoidance-deconditioning-pain cycle: avoidance → reduced activity → muscle weakness → pain amplification → further avoidance.
- Empower: "Breaking the cycle requires gradually increasing activity despite pain."

### Delivery Modalities

#### Conversational AI-Delivered PNE (Most Effective)
- **Evidence (Watson et al. 2019, PMID 30831273):** Mixed-methods review of 43 qualitative studies showed that effective PNE delivery allows patients to "tell their own story" and have concerns addressed in real-time.
- **AI Advantage:** Conversational AI can:
  - Respond dynamically to patient questions and concerns.
  - Tailor explanations to patient's conceptual level.
  - Provide reassurance and emotional support.
  - Reinforce key messages across multiple interactions.

#### Structured Group Education
- 2–4 sessions, 60–90 min each; typically delivered by physiotherapists or health psychologists.
- Allows peer support and normalization ("I'm not the only one").
- Less flexible for individualized concerns.

#### Digital/Self-Paced Learning
- Animated videos, interactive modules.
- More scalable but lacks conversational responsiveness.

#### One-on-One In-Person Education
- Gold standard for engagement and personalization; requires more clinical resources.

### Health Literacy Considerations

**Evidence (Oosterhaven et al. 2023, PMID 37868618):** Systematic review found that patients in low-literacy groups were significantly less likely to receive PNE as intended, and when they did, comprehension was lower. Key recommendations:

1. **Assess health literacy** using single-item screener (Section 2).
2. **Simplify language:**
   - Avoid jargon ("sensitization," "nociception"); use "alarm," "volume knob," "protection."
   - Short sentences, active voice.
   - Use analogies and visual metaphors.
3. **Check understanding:** Ask patients to explain concepts in their own words.
4. **Repetition:** Core messages should be reinforced across multiple sessions.
5. **Conversational approach:** Allow questions and address concerns in real-time (more effective than written materials alone).

### Success Factors for PNE

**Evidence (Ciolan et al. 2024, PMID 39225055, meta-synthesis of 32 qualitative studies):**

**Efficient Communication:**
- Concise explanations with visual aids (images, videos).
- Avoid overwhelming with too much neuroanatomical detail (only include detail relevant to patient's beliefs).
- Use the "teach-back" method: have patient summarize key concepts.

**Emotional Support:**
- Validate patient's experience: "Your pain is real; let me explain why."
- Express confidence in recovery: "We have strong evidence that people improve with this approach."
- Normalize pain fluctuations and setbacks: "Pain days will happen; they don't mean your nervous system has reset."

**Empowerment Promotion:**
- Frame patient as agent of change: "You are going to teach your nervous system through movement and activity."
- Emphasize that PNE + activity is the evidence-based approach, not passive treatments.
- Celebrate small wins and progress on functional goals.

### AI Agent Behavior for PNE Delivery

1. **Opening:** Assess baseline pain beliefs and concerns ("What do you think is causing your pain?"). Acknowledge and validate.
2. **Core Message:** Introduce the nervous system model, emphasizing it's protective but overactive.
3. **Personalization:** Link explanation to patient's specific experience (e.g., "Your widespread pain is your nervous system being extra protective").
4. **Dosing Strategy:** Distribute PNE across 8–12 coaching sessions (e.g., 15–20 min per session = ~150 min total). For high-catastrophizing patients, extend to 16+ sessions (200–400 min).
5. **Reinforcement:** Repeat key concepts, ask for understanding checks, relate to upcoming activities and functional goals.
6. **Behavioral Link:** "The more active you become, the faster your nervous system learns it's safe. Exercise is the primary treatment."

## 4. Therapeutic Module: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Pain

### Overview

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Pain (CBT-P) is a structured, collaborative intervention addressing the thoughts, behaviors, and emotions that perpetuate chronic pain. CBT-P is based on the cognitive model: pain → appraisal (thoughts) → emotion (distress) → behavior (avoidance) → disability. By intervening at each stage, CBT-P disrupts the maladaptive pain cycle and restores function.

### Core Components

#### 1. Cognitive Restructuring (Thought Challenging)

**Process:**
1. Identify automatic thoughts triggered by pain: "My back is permanently damaged," "I'll never return to work," "Pain means I shouldn't move."
2. Evaluate evidence: Is the thought fact or assumption? What's the evidence for/against?
3. Generate alternative thoughts: "My imaging is normal; pain is from nervous system sensitivity," "I've recovered from pain before," "Movement gradually retrains my nervous system."
4. Practice: Anchor alternative thoughts to specific situations (e.g., before exercise, during activity flares).

**Targets:**
- Pain catastrophizing (PCS ≥30).
- Permanence beliefs ("My pain is forever").
- Control beliefs ("I can't influence my pain").
- Health anxiety ("My pain means serious disease").

**Example Thought Challenging:**
- **Automatic thought:** "Sharp pain in my knee means I'm damaging my cartilage."
- **Evidence:** Recent MRI was normal; orthopedist said no structural damage; pain has been episodic for years with no progression.
- **Alternative:** "Sharp pain is a pain signal from my nervous system, not proof of damage. I can move despite this signal."

#### 2. Behavioral Activation (Scheduling Valued Activities)

**Process:**
1. Identify pain-related avoidance: activities the patient has stopped (gardening, socializing, hobbies, work).
2. Assess values: Which activities are most meaningful or essential?
3. Schedule: Gradually reintroduce valued activities on a fixed schedule (not contingent on pain level).
4. Track: Monitor mood and disability changes as activity increases (separate pain from disability).

**Rationale:** Activity withdrawal maintains deconditioning, mood disturbance, and disability. Resuming valued activity, regardless of pain level, is a powerful lever for disability reduction.

**Example Schedule:**
- Week 1–2: 20 min gardening 2×/week (even if painful).
- Week 3–4: 30 min gardening 3×/week + one social outing (dinner with friend).
- Week 5+: Increase duration/frequency as confidence builds.

**Expectation Setting:** "Initially, this activity may be uncomfortable, but discomfort does not mean harm. Staying inactive guarantees your disability worsens."

#### 3. Graded Exposure (Systematic Desensitization to Feared Activities)

**Process:**
1. Identify feared movements/activities: stairs, bending, walking, typing, sports.
2. Create hierarchy: Rank feared activities by difficulty (1–10 scale).
3. Systematic exposure: Start with lower-difficulty items; repeat until anxiety habituates; progress to next item.
4. Cognitive reframing: "I'm learning my nervous system is safe with this movement."

**Evidence:** Graded exposure reduces kinesiophobia faster than education alone (Leeuw et al. 2007, PMID 17194887).

**Example Hierarchy (Low Back Pain):**
1. Lie down and roll to side (SUDS 20).
2. Stand from sitting using armrests (SUDS 30).
3. Walk 5 min on flat surface (SUDS 35).
4. Climb one flight of stairs (SUDS 45).
5. Bend to touch toes (SUDS 60).
6. Pick up a 10-lb box (SUDS 70).
7. Shovel light snow (SUDS 80).

**Progress:** Advance only when SUDS drops by ≥50% (habituation).

#### 4. Activity Pacing (Balancing Activity and Rest)

**Problem:** Patients often cycle between extreme inactivity (pain exacerbation, deconditioning) and overactivity (pain flare). Pacing breaks this boom-bust cycle.

**Process:**
1. Establish baseline tolerance: "On a normal day, what activity level is sustainable without major pain increase?"
2. Plan consistent activity: Rather than pain-contingent rests, schedule fixed rest intervals (e.g., 10 min activity, 2 min rest break).
3. Avoid boom-bust: "If you feel good on day 1, don't overdo it; pace is more important than intensity."
4. Gradually increase: Increase duration or intensity by 10% every 1–2 weeks, if tolerated.

**Application to Exercise:** Aerobic activity 3–5 days/week at moderate intensity (60–70% max heart rate) is more sustainable than sporadic high-intensity exercise followed by flares.

#### 5. Relaxation Training

**Techniques:**

**Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR):**
- Systematically tense and relax muscle groups (forehead, shoulders, arms, abdomen, legs) for 5–10 min daily.
- Reduces muscle tension, lowers sympathetic arousal, and enhances parasympathetic tone.

**Diaphragmatic Breathing:**
- 4-count inhale (through nose), 6-count exhale (through mouth); 5–10 min daily.
- Reduces hyperventilation, lowers cortisol, and interrupts pain-anxiety-avoidance loop.

**Guided Imagery:**
- Visualize a calm, safe place (beach, forest) for 10–15 min; engages parasympathetic system.
- Evidence: Reduces pain intensity and anxiety in acute and chronic pain (Torge et al. 2022).

**Integration:** Relaxation training is most effective when paired with activity (use breathing during exposure) and self-monitoring (track physiological changes).

#### 6. Sleep Hygiene and CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)

**Pain-Related Sleep Disturbance:**
- Chronic pain commonly disrupts sleep; poor sleep amplifies pain perception and catastrophizing.
- Insomnia severity predicts pain outcomes; addressing sleep improves pain management.

**CBT-I Principles for Pain:**
1. **Sleep restriction:** Limit time in bed to match actual sleep duration (e.g., if sleeping 5 hr, stay in bed 5.5 hr until consolidation improves).
2. **Stimulus control:** Bed is for sleep only (not worry, rumination, screens).
3. **Cognitive restructuring:** Challenge insomnia-related catastrophes ("I'll never sleep," "Insomnia will worsen my pain").
4. **Relaxation for sleep onset:** Use guided body scan or breathing 15 min before bed.
5. **Pain management for sleep:** Adjust pain medication timing or use relaxation techniques pre-sleep.

#### 7. Goal Setting (SMART Goals Tied to Function)

**SMART Framework:**
- **Specific:** "Walk 30 min continuously" (not "exercise more").
- **Measurable:** Frequency (3×/week), duration (30 min), or distance (2 miles).
- **Achievable:** Realistic given current capacity; start low and progress.
- **Relevant:** Aligned with patient values (return to work, gardening, sports).
- **Time-bound:** Achievable within 8–12 weeks.

**Example Goals:**
- "Climb stairs to my bedroom without stopping for rest 5 days/week by week 8."
- "Work full 8-hour days with no home modifications by week 12."
- "Return to golf, playing 9 holes, by week 16."

**Monitoring:** Track goal progress weekly; adjust as needed (progress may not be linear).

## 5. Therapeutic Module: Self-Management Skills

### Overview

Self-management empowers patients to monitor their condition, prevent flares, and maintain gains between coaching sessions. Key skills include self-monitoring, flare management, ergonomics, physical activity maintenance, and social engagement.

### Components

#### 1. Self-Monitoring (Pain Diaries, Activity Logs, Mood Tracking)

**Pain Diary:**
- Daily pain intensity (NRS 0–10), timing, triggers (activity, stress, sleep, weather).
- Goal: Identify patterns and modifiable triggers.
- Frequency: 2–4 weeks minimum; can transition to weekly after patterns emerge.

**Activity Log:**
- Duration and type of activities completed.
- Relationship to pain (does activity worsen, improve, or not affect pain?).
- Goal: Track progress toward functional goals and identify avoidance patterns.

**Mood Tracking:**
- Daily mood (PHQ-9 item 1: "How often have you felt down?") or mood rating (0–10 scale).
- Goal: Identify mood-pain links; optimize antidepressant/psychotherapy timing.

**Interpretation:** Consistent activity engagement despite pain variability is a sign of progress and nervous system habituation (primary coaching goal).

#### 2. Flare Management Plan

**Flare Definition:** Temporary increase in pain intensity, typically 24 hr to 1–2 weeks, triggered by activity, stress, or unknown factors.

**Pre-Established Flare Protocol:**
1. **Identify trigger** (if obvious): Overactivity, stress, sleep disruption, infection, or unknown.
2. **Maintain activity at modified level:** Continue gentle movement (walking, stretching); avoid complete bed rest.
3. **Relaxation:** Increase breathing/relaxation practice; 10–15 min, 2–3×/day during flare.
4. **Pain medication optimization:** Use medication regularly (not PRN only) for 2–3 days if pain >7/10.
5. **Sleep support:** Ensure adequate sleep; use relaxation techniques pre-sleep.
6. **Pacing:** Reduce activity intensity by 25–50%, but continue.
7. **Normalization:** "Flares are temporary and do not mean damage is happening; they are nervous system overreactions."

**When to Seek Clinician Help:**
- Flare lasts >2 weeks.
- New neurological symptoms (numbness, weakness, bowel/bladder changes).
- Flare follows trauma or unusual event.

#### 3. Ergonomic and Postural Education

**Principles:**
- **Neutral posture:** Spine aligned (minimal forward head, minimal lumbar hyperlordosis).
- **Movement variety:** Avoid static postures; change position every 20–30 min.
- **Load management:** Avoid heavy lifting; use proper body mechanics (squat, don't bend).

**Specific Ergonomics:**
- **Desk work:** Monitor at eye level, feet flat, elbows 90°, chair with lumbar support.
- **Smartphone use:** Avoid "text neck"; hold device at eye level.
- **Lifting:** Squat, keep load close, avoid twisting.
- **Sleeping:** Neutral spine; pillow height supports cervical spine.

**Outcome:** Reduces mechanical stress and pain flares; improves postural awareness.

#### 4. Weight Management and Nutrition (Anti-Inflammatory Diet)

**For Osteoarthritis (OA):**
- Excess weight increases joint loading and inflammatory cytokines.
- Weight loss of 5–10% improves pain and disability in knee OA (Christensen et al. 2007).

**Anti-Inflammatory Diet Principles:**
- **Emphasize:** Fatty fish (omega-3), berries, leafy greens, nuts, olive oil.
- **Minimize:** Refined carbohydrates, processed foods, sugar, trans fats (all promote systemic inflammation).
- **Moderate:** Dairy (some evidence for OA; individual tolerance varies).

**Evidence:** While diet alone is not sufficient for chronic pain management, combined with physical activity and CBT, anti-inflammatory patterns support recovery (Manson et al. 2022).

#### 5. Physical Activity Maintenance (Habit Formation and Environmental Restructuring)

**Habit Formation (Lally et al. 2009):**
- New habits typically require 66 days (range 18–254 days) of consistent repetition.
- Environmental cues (placing workout clothes on bed, scheduling same time daily) reduce reliance on motivation.

**Strategies for Long-Term Adherence:**
- **Anchor to existing routine:** Exercise immediately after coffee or before work.
- **Environmental design:** Lay out workout clothes night before; position dumbbells in living room.
- **Social commitment:** Schedule exercise with friend or join class; social accountability increases adherence.
- **Intrinsic motivation:** Connect activity to personal values ("Walking allows me to spend time in nature").
- **Tracking:** Visual progress (calendar with checkmarks) and app-based tracking sustain motivation.

**Exercise Prescription for Chronic MSK Pain:**
- **Frequency:** 3–5 days/week (at least 3 for aerobic benefit).
- **Type:** Mix of aerobic (walking, cycling), strengthening (resistance bands, weights), and flexibility (stretching, yoga).
- **Intensity:** Moderate (60–70% max heart rate for aerobic); can be painful but not harmful.
- **Duration:** 20–45 min aerobic; 2–3 sets of 8–12 reps for strength.
- **Progression:** Increase frequency, duration, or intensity by 10% every 1–2 weeks.

#### 6. Social Engagement and Support Networks

**Strategies:**
- **Peer support groups:** In-person or online communities for chronic pain (provide normalization and shared strategies).
- **Family involvement:** Partner/family education about pain, encouragement of activity, and avoidance of solicitous (overprotective) responses.
- **Community resources:** Tai chi classes, water aerobics, yoga studios (low-cost, high-engagement activities).
- **Work reintegration:** Gradual return-to-work with employer accommodations; vocational rehabilitation if needed.

**Outcome:** Social connection improves pain outcomes; isolation worsens prognosis.

## 6. Therapeutic Module: Motivational Interviewing for Exercise Adherence

### Overview

Exercise is a cornerstone of chronic pain management, but adherence is poor (30–50% dropout within 6 months). Motivational Interviewing (MI) is a client-centered, evidence-based approach to resolving ambivalence about behavior change and building intrinsic motivation for sustained activity.

### OARS Framework

#### O: Open Questions
- Invite exploration rather than yes/no answers.
- **Example:** "What concerns do you have about starting exercise?" (not "Are you worried about exercise?").
- **Purpose:** Gather information about barriers, values, and readiness.

#### A: Affirmations
- Recognize and reinforce strengths, effort, and progress.
- **Example:** "You've worked through pain for years; that takes resilience. I believe you can learn to move confidently."
- **Purpose:** Build self-efficacy and reduce defensiveness.

#### R: Reflections
- Mirror or paraphrase what the patient says, showing empathy and understanding.
- **Example:** Patient: "I'm afraid exercise will make my pain worse." Coach: "You're worried that moving might trigger a pain flare. That's a valid concern many people have."
- **Purpose:** Deepen understanding and validate ambivalence (not all-or-nothing).

#### S: Summaries
- Periodically synthesize what the patient has said, highlighting readiness language.
- **Example:** "So far, you've mentioned that you want to garden again, you're worried about pain during exercise, and you've had some success with walking. Is that right?"
- **Purpose:** Consolidate motivation and identify discrepancies between values and behavior.

### Readiness Rulers (Importance and Confidence)

**Two key dimensions:**

**Importance Ruler:** "On a scale of 0–10, how important is it for you to become more active?"
- If ≥7: Patient sees value; explore reasons for importance and link to deeper values.
- If <7: Explore values ("What matters most to you in life?") and connect activity to those values.

**Confidence Ruler:** "On a scale of 0–10, how confident are you that you can stick with an exercise plan?"
- If ≥7: Build on confidence; identify specific strategies and supports.
- If <7: Identify barriers ("What would make you more confident?") and problem-solve collaboratively.

**Example Dialogue:**
- Coach: "Importance is 8, which is high. What would it take to move it to a 9?"
- Patient: "If I knew exactly what to do and that it was safe."
- Coach: "We can work on that. Let's start with an exercise plan tailored to your capacity."

### Rolling with Resistance

**Principle:** Rather than arguing against ambivalence or barriers, acknowledge and explore them collaboratively.

**Non-Collaborative (Avoidant):** "Exercise is essential; studies show it works. You have to do it."
- **Result:** Patient withdraws or becomes defensive.

**Collaborative (MI):** "You're concerned that exercise might flare your pain. That's a real concern. Let's talk about how to start gradually so you feel safe."
- **Result:** Patient feels heard; ambivalence becomes a focus for problem-solving.

**Strategies:**
- **Reframe barriers as reasonable:** "It makes sense you're cautious after a flare. Let's ease back in."
- **Offer choice:** "Which type of activity appeals to you—walking, swimming, or cycling? Your preference matters."
- **Normalize ambivalence:** "Most people have mixed feelings about starting exercise. Both sides of you are understandable."

### Developing Discrepancy (Values-Behavior Mismatch)

**Process:**
1. Identify core values: "What's most important to you in life?" (e.g., being active with family, independence, health).
2. Assess current behavior: "How do your current activity levels align with those values?"
3. Highlight discrepancy: "You value independence, but pain-related avoidance is increasing your dependence on others. How does that sit with you?"
4. Invite exploration: "What would alignment between your values and behavior look like?"

**Purpose:** Internal motivation (intrinsic) is more powerful than external pressure (extrinsic) for sustained behavior change.

### Evidence for MI + Exercise Adherence

**Key Studies:**

**REEF Trial (Ang et al. 2010, PMID 20828634):** Fibromyalgia, MI + exercise coaching vs. exercise alone.
- **Outcome:** MI + exercise showed higher adherence (75% vs. 45%) and greater pain improvement at 24 weeks.

**Morales Tejera et al. 2025:** Pain Neuroscience Education + Motivational Interviewing + Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (PNE-MI-CBT protocol).
- **Outcome:** Combined protocol superior to PNE alone for pain, disability, and exercise adherence.

### AI Agent Behavior for MI-Informed Exercise Coaching

1. **Opening:** Use open questions to explore patient's current activity, barriers, and past exercise experiences.
2. **Importance:** Assess and amplify importance of activity for functional goals; connect to patient values.
3. **Confidence:** Identify barriers to confidence; problem-solve collaboratively.
4. **Goal Setting:** Develop a specific, achievable activity plan aligned with patient's importance and confidence levels.
5. **Monitoring:** Track adherence non-judgmentally; if lapses occur, explore reasons (barriers, competing priorities) and adjust plan collaboratively.
6. **Reinforcement:** Celebrate small wins and progress; link activity to functional improvements ("You've been walking 3×/week; notice how your stairs feel easier?").

## 7. Therapeutic Module: Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) Elements

### Overview

While Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) focuses on changing thoughts and reducing pain, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) offers an alternative or complementary approach: reducing suffering by accepting pain while committing to valued actions despite it. For patients with high pain catastrophizing or limited pain reduction potential, ACT may be more feasible and empowering.

### Core Principles

#### 1. Psychological Flexibility

**Definition:** The ability to be present with pain (without fighting or denying it) while acting in alignment with personal values.

**Opposite (Psychological Rigidity):** Struggling against pain, avoidance behaviors, pain-contingent living ("I'll return to work when pain is gone").

**Key Shift:** From "How do I eliminate pain?" to "How do I live a meaningful life while managing pain?"

#### 2. Values-Based Action

**Process:**
1. Identify core values: health, family, work, creativity, spirituality, adventure, etc.
2. Clarify valued directions: "What actions would embody this value?" (e.g., value = "family connection"; action = "attend my son's soccer game").
3. Commit to action despite pain: "I can attend the game and experience pain at the same time."
4. Monitor alignment: Track whether daily activities reflect values; adjust if drifting.

**Example:**
- **Value:** Independence.
- **Barrier:** "Pain prevents me from driving."
- **ACT response:** "I can drive with pain. Pain does not make the drive impossible; I can do both."
- **Commitment:** Drive to work despite pain; pain intensity does not determine whether I drive.

#### 3. Cognitive Defusion (Detaching from Pain Thoughts)

**Problem:** Patients fuse with catastrophic thoughts ("My pain is destroying my life" becomes fact).

**Defusion Techniques:**

**Naming the Process:** Notice thoughts without believing them.
- Instead of: "I'm permanently damaged" (belief).
- Try: "I'm having the thought that I'm permanently damaged" (observation).

**Externalizing:**
- "My mind is predicting catastrophe. It's not a fact; it's a guess."
- "My fear-alarm is going off. The alarm doesn't mean danger is real."

**Metaphors:**
- **Passengers on a bus:** Pain thoughts are like unwelcome passengers on a bus heading toward valued action. Don't argue with them; let them ride, but don't let them drive.
- **Clouds in the sky:** Thoughts pass like clouds. You don't have to grab them or push them away; observe and let them pass.

#### 4. Mindfulness and Acceptance

**Mindfulness:** Present-moment awareness of pain, breath, and bodily sensations without judgment.
- **Practice:** Body scan (10 min), noting pain as a physical sensation without evaluating or fighting it.
- **Goal:** Reduce secondary suffering (suffering about pain) while primary pain may persist.

**Acceptance:** Making peace with pain as a chronic condition rather than continually fighting it.
- **Not**: "I accept that my pain will never improve" (resignation).
- **Instead**: "I accept that pain is here right now. I can still move, work, and engage in life."

### Evidence for ACT in Chronic Pain

**EAET Trial (Yarns et al. 2022):** Experiential Acceptance and Engagement Therapy (EAET, adapted ACT) vs. CBT in older adults with chronic pain.
- **Outcome:** EAET and CBT showed comparable improvements in pain and disability at 12 months. EAET may be less cognitively demanding for older adults, making it accessible for those with cognitive impairment.

**Meta-Analysis (Wetherell et al. 2011, PMID 21130098):** ACT for chronic pain.
- **Outcome:** ACT produces effect sizes comparable to CBT (d=0.4–0.7) for pain, disability, and anxiety. Effect sizes increase over time (suggesting longer-term maintenance).

### Integration with Coaching

**When to Prioritize ACT Elements:**
- High pain catastrophizing + low pain reduction on standard interventions → shift focus to acceptance and values.
- Long-term pain with unrealistic cure expectations → reframe toward living well despite pain.
- Advanced age, cognitive limitations → EAET may be more accessible than standard CBT.

**Sequencing:**
1. **Early:** Pain neuroscience education + CBT for initial motivation and cognitive change.
2. **Middle:** Behavioral activation and graded exposure (values-aligned).
3. **Late:** ACT elements if pain remains high; reinforce psychological flexibility and values-aligned action (not pain-contingent).

## 8. Integration Framework: Module Sequencing and Risk Stratification

### 12-Month Care Pathway

The KIMI coaching program spans 12 months (48–52 weeks), with modules introduced and reinforced sequentially based on patient risk profile and response.

#### Stratification System: STarT Back Tool (Randall et al. 2009)

The STarT Back Screening Tool risk-stratifies patients into low, medium, and high risk groups based on pain intensity, disability, and psychological factors (anxiety, depression, catastrophizing).

**Risk Categories:**
- **Low Risk:** Simple pain with minimal disability; good prognosis with education + exercise.
- **Medium Risk:** Moderate disability with some psychological factors; requires targeted interventions.
- **High Risk:** High disability and substantial psychological barriers (catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, depression); requires intensive multimodal intervention.

### 12-Month Pathway by Risk Group

#### LOW-RISK PATHWAY (Weeks 1–52)

**Aim:** Maintain function, prevent chronicity, minimize healthcare burden.

**Weeks 1–4 (Foundation):**
- PNE (50 min total): 2×/week, 15 min coaching calls.
  - Topics: Basic pain neurophysiology, normalization of pain, role of nervous system.
- Exercise prescription: Walking or swimming, 3×/week, 20 min (moderate intensity).
- Self-monitoring: Pain diary, activity log (daily).

**Weeks 5–12 (Reinforcement):**
- PNE (50 min cumulative): Reduce to 1×/week; focus on reinforcement and Q&A.
- Behavioral activation: Resume valued activities (gardening, socializing).
- Exercise progression: Increase duration to 30 min, 3–4×/week.

**Weeks 13–52 (Maintenance):**
- Coaching: 1×/month check-in (15 min) to track adherence and troubleshoot barriers.
- Self-management: Established routine; coach emphasizes consistency over pain intensity.
- Goal focus: Return-to-work, return-to-leisure, or functional milestone.

**Success Metrics:**
- Pain intensity stable or improving (NRS ≤5).
- PROMIS Physical Function T-score improves ≥3 points at 12 weeks.
- Adherence to exercise ≥3 days/week maintained.
- No catastrophizing escalation (PCS remains <30).

---

#### MEDIUM-RISK PATHWAY (Weeks 1–52)

**Aim:** Address psychological barriers, restore function, prevent high-risk trajectory.

**Weeks 1–8 (Intensive Foundation):**
- PNE (150 min total): 3×/week, 20–30 min coaching calls.
  - Topics: Detailed nervous system anatomy, central sensitization, threat appraisal, pain beliefs.
  - Assess health literacy; adjust language accordingly.
  - Check understanding at each session (teach-back method).
- CBT core modules: Cognitive restructuring (identify catastrophic thoughts, challenge with evidence).
- Exercise prescription: Walking, strength training (resistance bands), 3×/week, 20–30 min.
- Self-monitoring: Pain diary, activity log, mood tracking (daily); pain catastrophizing scale (weekly).

**Weeks 9–20 (Skill Building):**
- PNE (100 min cumulative, with reinforcement): Reduce to 2×/week; deepen understanding of neuroplasticity and behavior-pain links.
- CBT escalation: Behavioral activation (schedule valued activities weekly), graded exposure to feared movements.
- Motivational Interviewing: Assess importance and confidence for exercise; problem-solve barriers.
- Exercise progression: Increase intensity (RPE 5–6/10) or duration (40 min); add variety (swimming, cycling, yoga).

**Weeks 21–52 (Consolidation & Maintenance):**
- Coaching: 2×/month (15 min) to sustain CBT skills and exercise adherence.
- ACT elements (if pain not improving): Shift toward valued action and psychological flexibility.
- Flare management plan: Establish protocol for pain exacerbations.
- Goal focus: Return-to-work timeline, vocational rehabilitation if needed.

**Success Metrics:**
- Pain catastrophizing (PCS) improves to <20 by week 16.
- Fear-avoidance (TSK) improves to <37 by week 12.
- Pain Self-Efficacy Questionnaire (PSEQ) improves ≥10 points by week 16.
- PROMIS Pain Interference T-score improves ≥5 points by week 12.
- Exercise adherence ≥3 days/week sustained.
- No depression escalation (PHQ-9 remains <10); refer if PHQ-9 ≥10.

---

#### HIGH-RISK PATHWAY (Weeks 1–52)

**Aim:** Intensive multimodal intervention addressing psychological distress, kinesiophobia, and disability.

**Weeks 1–12 (Intensive Multimodal):**
- PNE (200–300 min total, extended dosing): 3–4×/week, 30–45 min coaching calls.
  - Extended neuroscience education tailored to patient's beliefs and learning style.
  - Multiple delivery formats: conversational, visual aids, metaphors, repeated reinforcement.
  - Explicit goal: Reduce catastrophizing and fear-avoidance as prerequisite to exercise.
- CBT intensive: Cognitive restructuring (2–3×/week), behavioral activation, relaxation training.
  - Identify core catastrophic beliefs ("My spine is collapsing," "I'm permanently disabled") and work systematically with evidence.
  - Graded exposure to feared movements (start with low-anxiety items on hierarchy).
- Motivational Interviewing: Assess stage of change; build importance and confidence incrementally.
- Exercise prescription: Conservative, graded start (walking 10 min daily at comfortable pace; no intensity targets initially).
- Psychosocial assessment: Screen for depression (PHQ-9), anxiety (GAD-7); refer for concurrent mental health care if thresholds met.
- Social support: Engage family/partner in education; address solicitous or punishing responses.

**Weeks 13–26 (Escalation & Skill Consolidation):**
- PNE (continued, 100 min cumulative): 2×/week to reinforce and deepen understanding.
- CBT progression: Continue cognitive restructuring; advance graded exposure hierarchy; introduce ACT elements (values-based action).
- Relaxation training: Daily practice (15 min); integrate into exercise (breathing during exertion).
- Exercise escalation: Gradually increase duration (20–30 min), frequency (4–5 days/week), and intensity as confidence builds.
- Flare management plan: Establish protocol with patient; practice response to minor pain flares.
- Work/role focus: If employed, begin work-specific exposure and return-to-work planning (graded).

**Weeks 27–52 (Maintenance & Long-Term Sustainability):**
- Coaching: 2×/month initially; taper to 1×/month by week 40 as skills solidify.
- CBT maintenance: Monthly review of thought patterns and behavioral progress; reinforce skills.
- ACT reinforcement: Emphasize values-aligned action over pain control; psychological flexibility as long-term goal.
- Exercise sustainability: Establish habit (66+ days of repetition); environmental cues and social support to maintain adherence.
- Relapse prevention: Discuss high-risk situations (pain flares, stress, illness) and coping strategies.
- Goal achievement tracking: Celebrate functional milestones (return-to-work, return-to-leisure).

**Success Metrics (Extended Timeline):**
- Pain catastrophizing (PCS) improves to <25 by week 16; <15 by week 26 (challenging, may require 400 min PNE).
- Fear-avoidance (TSK) improves to <37 by week 16; <30 by week 26.
- Pain Self-Efficacy Questionnaire (PSEQ) improves ≥15 points by week 26.
- PROMIS Pain Interference T-score improves ≥5 points by week 12; ≥8 points by week 26.
- Depression (PHQ-9) and anxiety (GAD-7) improve with concurrent mental health care or self-improvement due to functional gains.
- Exercise adherence ≥4 days/week sustained by week 26.
- Return-to-work status improved or timeline achieved by week 52.

### Module Matching to Risk Profile

| Module | Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|--------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Pain Neuroscience Education** | 50 min (weeks 1–4) | 150 min (weeks 1–20) | 200–400 min (weeks 1–26+) |
| **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy** | Minimal (thought-checking in context of activity) | Core modules (cognitive restructuring, behavioral activation, exposure) | Intensive (all modules: restructuring, activation, exposure, relaxation, sleep, goal-setting) |

## Summary: Clinical Implementation for AI Agent

This biopsychosocial framework provides KIMI with:

1. **Assessment-Driven Stratification:** Use validated instruments (PROMIS, PCS, TSK, PHQ-9, GAD-7) to identify risk profiles and guide module selection.

2. **Evidence-Based Module Dosing:** PNE, CBT, and motivational interviewing are dosed based on patient risk and research-supported MCID thresholds.

3. **Sequential Integration:** Modules are introduced in order (PNE → CBT core → behavioral activation → MI & exercise escalation → ACT elements as needed) aligned with 12-month care pathway.

4. **Personalization:** Low-risk patients receive brief education + exercise; high-risk patients receive intensive multimodal intervention with mental health referral.

5. **RAG-Ready Corpus:** This document is formatted for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), allowing KIMI to search and apply specific evidence-based protocols in real-time during coaching sessions.

**Core AI Coaching Behavior:**
- Lead with empathy and assessment (OARS framework).
- Tailor education to health literacy.
- Use conversational PNE with teach-back method.
- Integrate cognitive restructuring, behavioral activation, and graded exposure.
- Monitor adherence and psychological markers; escalate or adjust modules based on response.
- Celebrate functional gains; normalize pain variability.
- Emphasize that patient is the agent of change; coach is a guide.
