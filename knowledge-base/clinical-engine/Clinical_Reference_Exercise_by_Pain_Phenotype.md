# Exercise Prescription by Pain Phenotype — Clinical Reference

**Document Purpose**: This clinical reference synthesizes evidence-based exercise prescription organized by pain mechanism/phenotype for chronic musculoskeletal conditions. It serves as the knowledge corpus for Kimi's MSK care coaching decisions and is designed for integration into AI-driven clinical decision support systems.

**Last Updated**: April 2026
**Intended Audience**: AI MSK care coach (Kimi), clinical decision support systems, MSK practitioners

---

## 1. Pain Phenotype Classification Framework

### 1.1 Three Primary Pain Mechanisms

Modern pain neurobiology recognizes three primary pain mechanisms that may present independently or in combination:

1. **Nociceptive Pain**: Driven by peripheral tissue damage, inflammation, or ongoing nociceptor activation from identifiable pathology (osteoarthritis, muscle strain, ligamentous injury)
2. **Neuropathic Pain**: Resulting from peripheral or central nervous system damage or dysfunction (radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia)
3. **Nociplastic Pain** (Central Sensitization): Characterized by amplified central pain processing with reduced inhibitory mechanisms, independent of ongoing tissue damage

**Clinical Significance**: Pain phenotypes may coexist (mixed presentations). Accurate phenotyping drives treatment selection. A patient with lumbar radiculopathy may have both neuropathic pain (from nerve compromise) and nociplastic components (if chronic course with sensitization). Treatment must address the dominant mechanism.

### 1.2 Clinical Screening Criteria for Pain Phenotypes

#### Nociceptive Pain Indicators
- Clear temporal relationship between tissue injury/pathology and symptom onset
- Pain localized to site of anatomical pathology
- Pain reduces with rest, modified activity, or tissue-healing interventions
- Clinical exam findings consistent with tissue pathology (positive special tests, ROM limitation, swelling)
- Imaging/imaging findings correlate with symptoms
- Pain worsens with provocative loading; improves with unloading

#### Neuropathic Pain Indicators
- Pain following known nerve injury or compression (dermatome/peripheral nerve distribution)
- Presence of neurological signs: altered sensation, weakness, reflex changes
- Descriptors: burning, electric, radiating, tingling, numbness
- IASP Grading System (2021): Probable neuropathic pain requires pain distribution anatomically plausible for nerve involvement + abnormal somatosensory testing OR imaging evidence of nerve lesion
- Common neuropathic conditions: radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy, post-surgical nerve damage, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)

#### Nociplastic/Central Sensitization Indicators
- Widespread pain (affecting ≥4 body regions) or non-anatomical pain distribution
- Disproportionate pain intensity relative to objective pathology findings
- Prominent non-pain symptoms: fatigue, sleep disturbance, mood changes, cognitive fog, functional limitation
- Heightened pain sensitivity to stimuli (allodynia, hyperalgesia)
- Poor correlation between imaging findings and symptom severity
- Symptom worsening with stress, poor sleep, deconditioning
- Medical history suggestive of central sensitization: fibromyalgia diagnosis, chronic widespread pain, persistent post-traumatic pain

### 1.3 Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI)

**Screening Tool**: The Central Sensitization Inventory is a validated 25-item self-report questionnaire assessing symptoms commonly associated with central sensitization.

**Cutoff Scores**:
- **CSI ≥40**: Indicates likely central sensitization / nociplastic pain phenotype (sensitivity 80.5%, specificity 75.1% for fibromyalgia; applicable across chronic pain populations)
- **CSI 25-39**: Moderate symptoms suggestive of nociplastic component
- **CSI <25**: Lower likelihood of primary nociplastic pain

**Clinical Use**: CSI ≥40 warrants nociplastic-focused exercise prescription (time-contingent, aerobic emphasis, graded exposure) alongside pain neuroscience education.

### 1.4 Risk Stratification: STarT Back and Keele STarT MSK Tools

#### STarT Back Tool (Low Back Pain)
9-item questionnaire identifying low back pain patients at risk for poor prognosis.

**Risk Categories**:
- **Low Risk** (STarT Back <4): Good prognosis, minimal intervention needed
- **Medium Risk** (STarT Back 4-8): Moderate prognosis, structured exercise essential
- **High Risk** (STarT Back >8): Poor prognosis without psychologically-informed care; requires combined physical and psychological intervention, fear-avoidance assessment

#### Keele STarT MSK Tool (Multi-Site MSK Pain)
Identifies patients at risk of poor prognosis across shoulder, knee, and hip pain (separate from LBP).

**Components**: Pain severity, pain sites, depressive symptoms, pain catastrophizing, fear-avoidance beliefs, and self-efficacy.

**Risk Stratification**: Similar low/medium/high-risk categories. High-risk patients require psychological support integration.

**Clinical Application**: STarT Back and Keele STarT MSK guide exercise dosing intensity and psychological intervention priority. High-risk patients benefit from lower starting loads, fear-avoidance exposure, and psychologically-informed rehabilitation.

### 1.5 Pain Phenotype Assessment Algorithm

**Sequential Assessment Approach**:

1. **Gather History**: Injury/pathology mechanism, pain timeline, anatomical distribution, aggravating/easing factors
2. **Screen for Neuropathic Indicators**: PainDETECT questionnaire or IASP clinical grading criteria
3. **Assess Spread and Chronicity**: If widespread or non-anatomical, calculate CSI score
4. **Risk Stratification**: Administer STarT Back (if LBP) or Keele STarT MSK (if other regions)
5. **Phenotype Assignment**:
   - If clear neuropathic signs + neuropathic pain distribution → **Neuropathic dominant**
   - If localized pathology + pain-pathology correlation → **Nociceptive dominant**
   - If CSI ≥40 and/or widespread pain + non-correlating pathology → **Nociplastic dominant**
   - If mixed indicators → **Mixed phenotype** (specify percentage distribution if assessable)

**Reassessment**: Pain phenotype may shift during treatment. Re-assess at 4-6 weeks, especially if initial phenotype-matched treatment shows limited efficacy.

### 1.6 Fibromyalgia and Mixed-Pain Phenotyping

Fibromyalgia (FM) exemplifies complex mixed-pain phenotyping.

**Evidence**: Asquini et al. (2024) demonstrated that fibromyalgia frequently presents with:
- **Nociplastic component** (central sensitization with widespread pain, CSI typically >40)
- **Nociceptive component** (local tissue pathology; approximately 40% of FM patients have concurrent osteoarthritis or local inflammatory conditions)
- **Neuropathic component** (small fiber neuropathy in subset of FM patients; peripheral sensitization)

**Clinical Implication** (PMID 41337761): Treatment of fibromyalgia must address all three pain mechanisms simultaneously. Exercise prescription should emphasize aerobic activity (addresses nociplastic) combined with progressive functional loading (addresses nociceptive) and neural mobilization if neuropathic features present.

## 2. Exercise Prescription by Pain Phenotype

### 2.1 Nociceptive/Peripheral Pain

#### 2.1.1 Mechanism
Tissue damage, inflammation, or ongoing nociceptor activation from identifiable pathology (degenerative joint disease, muscle strain, ligamentous sprain, soft-tissue inflammation).

#### 2.1.2 Exercise Philosophy
**Goal**: Progressive tissue loading and adaptation, restoration of joint mechanics, strength restoration, functional capacity building.

**Principles**:
- **Load Management**: Match loading dose to tissue healing capacity and symptom response
- **Tissue-Specific Rehabilitation**: Tailor exercises to anatomical pathology
- **Progressive Overload**: Systematic increase in load, volume, or complexity
- **Pain-Contingent Progression**: Pain response guides load advancement (mild exacerbation acceptable if resolves within 2 hours)

#### 2.1.3 Exercise Dosing Parameters

**Intensity**: 40-60% of 1-repetition maximum (1RM) for strengthening; moderate intensity for aerobic work (60-70% heart rate maximum)

**Frequency**: 2-3 sessions per week for strengthening; 150 minutes/week moderate-intensity aerobic activity or 75 minutes/week vigorous-intensity aerobic activity

**Duration**: Progressive—begin with 2-3 sets of 8-12 repetitions; progress to 3-4 sets of 12-15 repetitions or add load while maintaining 8-12 rep range

**Progression Criteria**:
- Completion of prescribed volume with minimal pain exacerbation
- Ability to maintain form/movement quality at current load
- Subjective report of improved function/reduced pain with loading
- Target: increase load 5-10% when able to perform all prescribed reps/sets with ease

#### 2.1.4 Body-Region Specific Protocols

##### Lower Back Pain (Nociceptive Dominant)

**Anatomical Target**: Restoration of lumbar stability, general fitness, and functional capacity; treat identified pathology (disc herniation, facet-mediated pain, muscular strain).

**Core Stabilization (Motor Control)**:
- Transverse abdominis activation (drawing-in maneuver, dead bug progressions)
- Multifidus activation (prone hip extension, bird dog)
- Quadratus lumborum strengthening (side plank, lateral bending with resistance)
- Perform 3 sets of 10-15 repetitions, daily or 5x/week initially; progress to functional integration

**Progressive Loading**:
- Controlled lumbar flexion/extension based on directional preference
- Prone press-ups (if extension-preference) or flexion-based exercises (if flexion-preference) per McKenzie assessment
- Progress to compound movements: bodyweight rows, step-ups, controlled deadlifts (ensuring core bracing)
- Load: Begin with bodyweight, progress to 40-50% estimated 1RM, advance 5-10% weekly if tolerated

**Aerobic Conditioning**:
- Walking: 20-30 minutes at moderate intensity, 3-5x/week
- Cycling or aquatic exercise: 20-30 minutes at moderate intensity, 2-3x/week
- Frequency and intensity support cardiovascular health, deconditioning reversal, pain reduction

**Flexibility/Mobility**:
- Hamstring stretching: 3 sets of 30-second holds, 5x/week
- Hip flexor stretching: 3 sets of 30-second holds, daily
- Lumbar rotation mobility: progressive, pain-free range

**Evidence**: AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline (2021) strongly recommends structured exercise for chronic low back pain. VA/DoD LBP Clinical Practice Guideline (2022) supports motor control training with progressive strengthening as first-line non-surgical management. McKenzie directional preference exercises show high efficacy for acute/subacute disc-related back pain.

##### Knee Osteoarthritis (Nociceptive Dominant)

**Anatomical Target**: Quadriceps strength (primary driver of knee OA outcomes), hip abductor/extensor strength, proprioceptive control, functional mobility.

**Quadriceps Strengthening** (Critical):
- Short-arc quads (isometric holds in 30° flexion): 3 sets of 10-15 reps, daily
- Straight leg raises: 3 sets of 15-20 reps, daily
- Mini squats (0-45° flexion): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week; progress depth as tolerated
- Full squats (if ROM permits): 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps at 50-70% 1RM, 2-3x/week
- Leg press: 3-4 sets of 10-15 reps at 50-70% 1RM, 2x/week
- Progression: Increase load 5-10% weekly; may alternate high-volume (light load, 15-20 reps) and moderate-volume (moderate load, 8-12 reps) sessions

**Hip Strengthening**:
- Hip abduction (sidelying, standing, resisted): 3 sets of 15-20 reps, 3x/week
- Hip extension (prone, standing): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Single-leg stance (proprioceptive): 3 sets of 30-60 seconds, 3x/week; progress to tandem stance, eyes closed

**Weight-Bearing Functional Exercise**:
- Sit-to-stand (chair transfer): 3 sets of 10-15 reps, daily
- Step-ups: 3 sets of 12-15 reps (each leg), 2-3x/week; progress step height
- Tandem walking (heel-to-toe), figure-8 walking: functional balance/proprioception, 2-3x/week

**Aerobic Exercise**:
- Walking: 30-45 minutes at moderate intensity (pain <5/10), 5-7x/week
- Swimming/aquatic therapy: 20-30 minutes, 2-3x/week (reduced joint load)
- Cycling (stationary preferred): 20-30 minutes at moderate intensity, 2-3x/week
- Frequency/duration drives pain reduction, cardiovascular health, weight management

**Evidence**: AAOS Clinical Practice Guideline (2021) strongly recommends exercise (especially quadriceps strengthening) for knee OA, with potential MCID for pain (PROMIS PI ≥3.5 points) and function (PROMIS PF ≥3 points). Level 1 evidence supports combined strengthening + aerobic exercise as superior to either modality alone.

##### Hip Osteoarthritis (Nociceptive Dominant)

**Anatomical Target**: Hip abductor (gluteus medius/minimus) strength (critical for hip joint stability), hip extensor strength, functional mobility.

**Hip Abductor Strengthening** (Priority):
- Sidelying hip abduction: 3 sets of 15-20 reps, daily
- Standing hip abduction (resisted): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Clamshells (sidelying, hip external rotation + abduction): 3 sets of 15-20 reps, 3x/week
- Lateral band walks: 3 sets of 15-20 steps each direction, 2-3x/week
- Single-leg stance on affected side: 3 sets of 30-60 seconds, 3x/week

**Hip Extension Strengthening**:
- Prone hip extension: 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Bridging (bilateral/single-leg progressions): 3 sets of 10-15 reps, 3x/week
- Step-ups: 3 sets of 12-15 reps per leg, 2x/week

**Functional Mobility**:
- Hip flexion ROM in standing/supine: pain-free ROM, hold 30 seconds, 3-5x/week
- Hip rotation (internal/external): gentle mobility, daily

**Tai Chi or Martial Arts Forms**:
- 20-30 minute sessions, 2-3x/week
- Improves balance, proprioception, strength endurance; particularly effective for older adults with hip OA

**Evidence**: Systematic reviews support hip abductor strengthening, functional exercise, and tai chi as effective interventions for hip OA pain and function. Tai chi additionally reduces fall risk in geriatric populations.

##### Shoulder Pain (Nociceptive Dominant)

**Anatomical Targets**:
- **Rotator Cuff Integrity** (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, subscapularis, teres minor): Progressive strengthening from isometric to dynamic loading
- **Scapular Stabilization** (serratus anterior, trapezius): Restore scapular positioning and kinetics
- **Glenohumeral Mobility**: Restore pain-free ROM while maintaining stability

**Rotator Cuff Progressive Loading**:
- **Isometric Phase** (Weeks 1-2): Side-lying external rotation hold at neutral, 3 sets of 15-second holds, daily
- **Isotonic Phase** (Weeks 3-4): Prone shoulder external rotation with light resistance (1-2 lbs), 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- **Progressive Resistance** (Weeks 5+): Side-lying external rotation, prone external rotation, prone T-Y-I progressions; increase resistance 5-10% as tolerated; 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, 2-3x/week

**Scapular Stabilization**:
- Prone Y-T-W-I raises: 3 sets of 10-12 reps, 3x/week (target lower trapezius, serratus anterior)
- Prone horizontal abduction: 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Serratus anterior punch (supine floor press with protraction): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Quadruped scapular control: 3 sets of 30-second holds, daily

**Glenohumeral Mobility**:
- Cross-body shoulder stretch: 3 sets of 30-second holds, 5x/week
- Sleeper stretch (internal rotation): 3 sets of 30-second holds, 5x/week
- Pendulum circles: 2-3 sets of 10-15 circles each direction, daily (in early phases)

**Functional Integration**:
- Progress to compound movements: prone rows, quadruped shoulder taps, pushup progressions
- Sport/occupation-specific training: overhead reaching, throwing, lifting mechanics

**Evidence**: Structured progressive loading of rotator cuff with integrated scapular stabilization reduces pain and improves function. Combining isolated rotator cuff training with scapular control and functional integration shows superior outcomes to rotator cuff training alone.

##### Cervical Spine Pain (Nociceptive Dominant)

**Anatomical Targets**:
- **Deep Cervical Flexor Activation** (longus colli, longus capitis): Restore anterior neck stabilization
- **Cervical/Thoracic Extensor Strengthening**: Restore posterior cervical stability
- **Cervicothoracic Mobility**: Restore functional ROM and postural control

**Deep Cervical Flexor Training** (Priority):
- Gentle isometric neck flexion: 5 sets of 10-second holds with gentle chin tuck, performed in supine or sitting; daily or 5-6x/week
- Progressive to 20-30 second holds over 2-4 weeks
- Ensure no upper trapezius/sternocleidomastoid dominance; gentle, low-load activation

**Cervical Strengthening**:
- Seated neck flexion (against mild hand resistance): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Seated neck extension (against mild resistance): 3 sets of 12-15 reps, 3x/week
- Seated neck lateral flexion: 3 sets of 12-15 reps each side, 3x/week
- Quadruped cat-camel mobilization: dynamic mobility, 3 sets of 10-15 reps, 3x/week

**Thoracic/Cervicothoracic Mobility**:
- Thoracic rotation (quadruped, half-kneeling, standing): 3 sets of 10-15 reps each direction, 3x/week
- Upper thoracic extension (foam roller supine, hands behind head): 3 sets of 10-15 reps, 3x/week
- Chest opening stretch (doorway, corner, prone arms overhead): 3 sets of 30-second holds, daily

**Posture Integration**:
- Ergonomic assessment and correction (desk/workstation setup)
- Frequent position changes (not static postures)
- Regular neck ROM reminders during computer work

**Evidence**: Deep cervical flexor training with integrated thoracic mobility reduces neck pain and improves cervical proprioception and stability. Multifaceted approach (mobilization + strengthening + postural correction) superior to single-modality intervention.

### 2.2 Neuropathic Pain

#### 2.2.1 Mechanism
Peripheral or central nervous system damage or dysfunction causing pain in anatomically-plausible distribution of affected nerve(s). Examples: radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia, post-surgical neuropathy, complex regional pain syndrome.

#### 2.2.2 Exercise Philosophy
**Goal**: Promote neural recovery and neuroprotection, restore function, manage pain through neural mobilization and graded loading.

**Principles**:
- **Neural Mobilization**: Gentle neural gliding/flossing exercises to improve nerve mobility and reduce mechanical tension
- **Avoid Provocation**: Initially avoid sustained end-range stretching or positions that reproduce neurological symptoms
- **Gradual Exposure**: Pain-contingent initially; transition to time-contingent as tolerance improves
- **Aerobic Foundation**: Systemic exercise supports neuroplasticity and pain modulation
- **Monitor Neurological Status**: Watch for worsening sensory loss, progressive weakness, or new neurological signs

#### 2.2.3 Exercise Dosing Parameters

**Intensity**: Low-to-moderate; pain during or immediately after exercise should be ≤3/10. If pain increases significantly or neurological symptoms worsen, reduce intensity.

**Frequency**: Frequent short bouts (2-3 times daily initially) often more tolerable than single longer session

**Duration**: Start 5-10 minutes, progress gradually to 15-30 minutes as tolerated

**Progression Criteria**:
- Pain tolerability during/after exercise
- Absence of increased neurological deficit
- Functional improvement (increased ROM, reduced symptoms during activities of daily living)
- Gradual addition of load or increased ROM ranges

#### 2.2.4 Key Interventions

**Neural Mobilization/Gliding Exercises**:

Exercises designed to promote relative nerve motion within its sheath and improve flexibility.

- **Radial Nerve Mobilization** (for lateral elbow pain/radial nerve distribution):
  - Supine, affected arm in shoulder abduction/external rotation, gentle elbow/wrist extension
  - Rhythmic gentle movement (not static stretch), 2-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions, 2-3x/day
  - Avoid positions that reproduce sharp/burning pain

- **Median Nerve Mobilization** (for carpal tunnel symptoms, wrist/hand pain):
  - Supine, affected arm shoulder abduction/external rotation, elbow extension, wrist/finger extension
  - Gentle oscillatory movement, 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps, 2-3x/day
  - Avoid sustained end-range positions

- **Sciatic Nerve Mobilization** (for sciatica, leg pain in sciatic distribution):
  - Supine, bend non-affected knee, affected knee gently extends/flexes (straight leg raise oscillations)
  - Avoid pushing into pain; gentle motion within comfortable range
  - 2-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions, 2-3x/day

- **Ulnar Nerve Mobilization** (for elbow/forearm/hand symptoms):
  - Supine, shoulder abduction/external rotation, elbow flexion/extension, wrist extension
  - Rhythmic motion, 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps, 2-3x/day

**Walking Programs**:
- Start: 10-15 minutes at slow pace, pain ≤3/10
- Progress: +5 minutes weekly, maintaining tolerable pain levels
- Target: 30-45 minutes at moderate intensity, 4-5x/week
- Walking promotes systemic circulation, neuroplasticity, and pain modulation

**Aquatic Therapy**:
- Reduced gravitational load improves tolerance
- Warm water reduces pain, improves tissue extensibility
- 15-20 minute sessions, 2-3x/week initially; progress to 30 minutes
- Combine with walking, gentle ROM exercises in water

**Progressive Functional Loading**:
- After initial pain/neural sensitivity reduces, progressively introduce loaded activities
- Example (radiculopathy): Begin with pain-free ROM in mobilization exercises → progress to light functional arm activity (lifting light object from shelf) → progress to moderate loading (strengthening)
- Load should not reproduce acute neurological symptoms (sharp pain, radiating symptoms); mild ache acceptable

#### 2.2.5 Cautions and Monitoring

- **Avoid sustained end-range stretching** of affected nerves; use gentle rhythmic motion instead
- **Monitor for neurological progression**: Increasing numbness, weakness, or loss of reflexes may indicate disease progression requiring medical reassessment
- **Pain centralization monitoring**: If symptoms remain in anatomical nerve distribution, neuropathic approach appropriate; if pain becomes widespread, consider nociplastic component
- **Activity pacing**: Frequent short sessions often better tolerated than single prolonged session
- **Adjunctive medication management**: Exercise does not replace pharmacological management of neuropathic pain (gabapentinoids, SNRIs); coordinate timing/dosing with exercise sessions

#### 2.2.6 Evidence

Systematic reviews support neural mobilization combined with aerobic exercise and activity progression for neuropathic pain conditions. Most evidence available for radiculopathy and peripheral neuropathy; less robust evidence for post-herpetic neuralgia and other neuropathic conditions, but extrapolation reasonable given neurophysiology.

### 2.3 Nociplastic/Central Sensitization Pain

#### 2.3.1 Mechanism
Amplified central pain processing with reduced endogenous pain inhibition (reduced conditioned pain modulation). Pain persists despite resolution of peripheral pathology or in absence of identifiable peripheral lesion. Common in fibromyalgia, chronic widespread pain, post-traumatic pain syndromes, and chronic regional pain conditions after sensitization develops.

#### 2.3.2 Exercise Philosophy
**Goal**: Desensitize central nervous system through graded exposure, rebuild exercise tolerance, restore normal pain modulatory mechanisms.

**Principles**:
- **Time-Contingent NOT Pain-Contingent**: Exercise progression based on time/volume completed, NOT pain response. Pain response should NOT trigger exercise cessation or load reduction.
- **Start Below Pain Threshold**: Initial exercises performed at intensity unlikely to provoke increased pain, reducing fear-avoidance and building self-efficacy
- **Quota-Based Progression**: Increase volume (time or repetitions) by set schedule (e.g., +5 minutes/week) regardless of pain levels
- **Aerobic Emphasis**: Aerobic exercise shows strongest evidence for central sensitization; walking, cycling, aquatic exercise preferred
- **Integration with Pain Neuroscience Education**: Combine exercise with PNE to address catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, and central sensitization beliefs
- **Gradual Exposure**: Introduce previously avoided activities progressively as tolerance builds

#### 2.3.3 Exercise Dosing Parameters

**Intensity**:
- **Initial (Weeks 1-4)**: 40-50% age-predicted maximal heart rate (approximate formula: 220 - age × 0.40-0.50), or perceived exertion 2-3/10
- **Intermediate (Weeks 5-8)**: 50-60% age-predicted maximal heart rate, or perceived exertion 4-5/10
- **Maintenance (Week 8+)**: 60-70% age-predicted maximal heart rate, or perceived exertion 5-6/10
- **Critical**: Do NOT reduce load/intensity due to pain increase; maintain prescribed quota

**Frequency**:
- **Initial**: Daily or 6x/week (frequent, short bouts build tolerance and demonstrate safety)
- **Intermediate**: 4-5x/week
- **Maintenance**: 5-7x/week (aerobic component); 2-3x/week for strengthening

**Duration**:
- **Initial**: 5-10 minutes continuous aerobic activity
- **Week 4**: 10-15 minutes
- **Week 8**: 15-20 minutes
- **Maintenance**: 20-30 minutes aerobic activity
- **Progression Schedule**: Fixed increases (e.g., +2-3 minutes/week) regardless of pain responses

**Progression Criteria**:
- **Time-based**: Increase duration per schedule (e.g., +5 minutes/month)
- **Volume-based**: Increase total weekly minutes (e.g., add one additional session/week)
- **Intensity-based** (secondary): Once duration established, gradually increase intensity per HR zones
- **Activity diversification**: Introduce new exercise modalities to prevent boredom, build functional confidence

#### 2.3.4 Key Interventions

**Aerobic Exercise (Priority)**:

Walking, cycling, swimming, aquatic exercise, elliptical training.

- **Walking Protocol (Most Accessible)**:
  - Week 1-2: 10 minutes at easy pace, daily
  - Week 3-4: 12-15 minutes at easy pace, 6x/week
  - Week 5-6: 15-20 minutes at moderate pace, 5x/week
  - Week 7-8: 20 minutes at moderate pace, 5x/week
  - Week 8+: 25-30 minutes at moderate pace, 5-7x/week
  - **Key**: Pain increase expected (often "symptom flare"); do NOT reduce—continue prescribed duration; pain typically reduces within 2-4 hours

- **Stationary Cycling Protocol**:
  - Week 1-2: 5-10 minutes at low resistance, daily
  - Week 3-4: 10-15 minutes at low-moderate resistance, 5-6x/week
  - Week 5-6: 15-20 minutes at moderate resistance, 4-5x/week
  - Week 7-8: 20 minutes at moderate resistance, 5x/week
  - Week 8+: 25-30 minutes at moderate-vigorous resistance, 5-7x/week

- **Aquatic Exercise Protocol** (preferred if weight-bearing problematic):
  - Week 1-2: 10-15 minutes water walking or shallow water jogging, daily
  - Week 3-4: 15-20 minutes with varied water aerobic activities, 5-6x/week
  - Week 5-6: 20-25 minutes at moderate intensity, 4-5x/week
  - Week 7-8+: 25-30 minutes at moderate-vigorous intensity, 5-7x/week

**Yoga and Tai Chi**:
- 20-30 minute sessions, 2-3x/week
- Combines gentle strengthening, flexibility, aerobic components with mindfulness
- Benefits: Pain reduction, improved sleep, reduced anxiety, functional capacity building
- Styles: Hatha yoga, chair yoga, tai chi for arthritis all evidence-supported

**Graded Motor Imagery (Advanced)**:
- For localized widespread pain or suspected neuropathic component
- Left/Right Recognition Training: Patient views images of affected body part in various positions, determines if left or right limb
- Imagined Movements: Patient mentally rehearses movements of affected limb without actual movement
- Mirror Therapy (if applicable): Mirror reflects unaffected limb creating visual illusion of affected limb moving pain-free
- Progression: Begin with 10-15 minutes daily, progress to functional movements

**Strengthening Exercise** (Secondary to aerobic in nociplastic phenotype):
- Light resistance training (30-50% estimated 1RM)
- 2-3x/week, 2-3 sets of 10-15 repetitions
- Functional movements: sit-to-stand, step-ups, arm raises, controlled squats
- Progression: Increase resistance per time-based schedule (e.g., +5-10% every 2 weeks) not pain response

**Flexibility/Stretching**:
- Gentle dynamic stretching: 3-5 minutes daily
- Static stretching: 3-5 sets of 20-30 second holds, 3-4x/week
- Yoga stretching incorporated into yoga practice
- **Caution**: Avoid aggressive stretching or end-range holding in early phases; pain increase should not trigger increased stretching intensity

#### 2.3.5 Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) Integration

**Critical Component**: Exercise efficacy in nociplastic pain substantially enhanced by concurrent pain neuroscience education addressing:
- Central sensitization mechanisms (how CNS amplifies pain signals)
- Neuroplasticity (pain system can "relearn")
- Threat-reduction: Exercise as safe exposure, not tissue danger signal
- Sleep, stress, and mood relationships with pain and pain sensitivity
- Role of self-efficacy, activity engagement, and psychological flexibility

**Dosing**: 200 minutes total PNE (across multiple sessions, counseling, education modules) associated with clinically meaningful pain reduction (MCID per Núñez-Cortés 2023, PMID 37519842).

**Format**: Individual counseling (30-60 minute sessions), group education, digital modules, patient handouts, combination approaches.

**Specific Content Topics**:
- Pain neurobiology: nociception vs. pain, central sensitization, neuroplasticity
- Threat-reduction and safety signaling through movement
- Cognitive-behavioral strategies: catastrophizing reduction, acceptance, behavioral activation
- Sleep hygiene and pain interaction
- Stress/anxiety management

**Evidence**: Systematic reviews demonstrate PNE alone produces small-to-moderate pain reduction; combined with exercise produces larger effects (pain MCID ~20-30% improvement).

#### 2.3.6 Fear-Avoidance Integration

Many nociplastic pain patients develop fear-avoidance beliefs ("activity will cause harm") limiting exercise engagement.

**Screening**: Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ) with subscales for work/activity avoidance.

**Graded Exposure Protocol for High Fear-Avoidance**:
- Identify feared activities (functional capacity evaluation or patient report)
- Rank feared activities by avoidance intensity (0-100 scale)
- Introduce feared activities progressively in hierarchy
- Example: Patient fears "overhead reaching causes nerve damage"
  - Week 1-2: Imagine reaching, visual exposure (watch others reach), verbal reassurance
  - Week 3-4: Reach to shoulder height, pain-free ROM
  - Week 5-6: Reach to overhead height, low resistance
  - Week 7-8: Functional overhead activity (light lifting, sports-specific reaching)

**Behavioral Activation**: Systematically increase activity engagement through quota-based progression regardless of pain, demonstrating that feared consequences do not occur.

**Evidence**: Systematic reviews support graded exposure combined with exercise for fear-avoidance reduction and improved outcomes in chronic pain (PMID 41356326, 38366560).

#### 2.3.7 Monitoring and Adjustment

**Expected Response**: Symptom flare/pain increase common in first 2-4 weeks as system habituates to increased stimulus. This is **desensitization in progress**, not treatment failure.

**Red Flag Adjustments** (if applied):
- Progressive neurological deficit → refer for medical reassessment
- Severe mood deterioration → add psychological support
- Non-compliance/dropout → assess barriers, consider supervised program, group classes

**Reassessment Points** (every 4-6 weeks):
- CSI score trend
- Pain severity/functional capacity (PROMIS scales)
- Sleep quality, mood, anxiety
- Activity engagement and self-efficacy
- Adherence barriers

#### 2.3.8 Evidence Base

Strong evidence for aerobic exercise in nociplastic pain conditions (fibromyalgia, chronic widespread pain). Motor control exercise enhances conditioned pain modulation (Chen 2024, PMID 34564256). Graded exposure effective for fear-avoidance reduction. Combined exercise + PNE > either modality alone.

### 2.4 Mixed Pain Presentations

#### 2.4.1 Approach to Mixed Phenotypes

Approximately 40-50% of chronic MSK pain patients present with **mixed pain phenotypes**—combination of nociceptive, neuropathic, and nociplastic components.

**Assessment Approach**:
1. Identify all pain components present (per Section 1.2-1.4 screening)
2. Estimate relative contribution of each phenotype (e.g., 50% nociceptive, 30% neuropathic, 20% nociplastic)
3. Prioritize treatment by dominant phenotype + address secondary components

#### 2.4.2 Treatment Matching Examples

**Example 1: Lumbar Radiculopathy with Central Sensitization (Mixed)**
- Dominant: Neuropathic (radicular pain in L5 distribution, positive Lasègue test)
- Secondary: Nociplastic (CSI 45, widespread pain beyond L5 distribution, significant sleep disruption)
- **Treatment Blueprint**:
  - **Priority**: Neural mobilization, sciatic/nerve-root gliding exercises (Section 2.2.4)
  - **Secondary**: Aerobic exercise per nociplastic protocol (walking 20-30 min, 5x/week)
  - **Adjunct**: Progressive functional loading (gradually restore hip/knee strengthening)
  - **Integrated**: Pain neuroscience education addressing both nerve pain and sensitization

**Example 2: Knee OA with Nociplastic Component (Mixed)**
- Dominant: Nociceptive (localized knee pain, positive joint line tenderness, OA imaging)
- Secondary: Nociplastic (CSI 42, pain spreading to other joints, low pain threshold to touch)
- **Treatment Blueprint**:
  - **Priority**: Progressive quadriceps/hip strengthening, knee-specific loading (Section 2.1.4)
  - **Secondary**: Aerobic exercise emphasizing duration/consistency (walking 30-45 min daily)
  - **Adjunct**: Yoga/tai chi (addresses both nociplastic and functional components)
  - **Integrated**: Education on joint protection AND central sensitization mechanisms

**Example 3: Fibromyalgia (Triadic Mixed)**
- Dominant: Nociplastic (widespread pain, CSI >40, sleep disruption, FM diagnosis)
- Secondary: Nociceptive (40% have concurrent local pathology—joint pain, muscle trigger points)
- Tertiary: Neuropathic (subset with small fiber neuropathy symptoms, paresthesias)
- **Treatment Blueprint**:
  - **Priority**: Aerobic exercise per nociplastic protocol (walking, cycling, aquatic)
  - **Secondary**: Light-moderate progressive strengthening for identified local pathology
  - **Tertiary**: Neural mobilization if neuropathic symptoms present
  - **Integrated**: Pain neuroscience education, sleep management, psychological support

#### 2.4.3 Reassessment and Treatment Adjustment

Pain phenotype may **shift during treatment**:
- Successful nociceptive-focused treatment may unmask underlying nociplastic component
- Effective treatment of nociplastic component may reveal previously masked neuropathic pain

**Reassess at 4-6 week intervals**:
- Repeat pain phenotype screening (IASP criteria, PainDETECT, CSI)
- Measure outcomes (PROMIS Pain Intensity, PROMIS Physical Function, pain severity)
- Adjust treatment if phenotype distribution has shifted

## 4. Four-Phase Rehabilitation Protocol Integration (Limber Platform)

The Limber platform phases align with standard rehabilitation progression from acute pain management through return-to-sport/activity. Exercise prescription adapts to each phase while respecting pain phenotype.

### 4.1 Phase 1: Acute/Pain Dominant (Weeks 1-4)

**Goals**: Pain modulation, protection/stabilization, restore basic ROM, initiate gentle mobility, establish exercise tolerance

**Pain Phenotype Adaptations**:

**Nociceptive Acute Pain**:
- Focus: Protect inflamed/injured tissue, reduce pain triggers
- ROM: Active-assisted ROM within pain-free ranges; progress to active ROM
- Isometric stabilization: Gentle low-load muscle activation without joint movement
- Position changes: Frequent positional variation, avoid prolonged static postures
- Modalities: Ice/heat as appropriate, soft tissue mobilization (therapist-assisted or self)
- Example—Acute Low Back Strain: Prone press-ups (if extension preference) or flexion-based positioning (if flexion preference), minimal load; 2-3 sets of 10-15 reps daily

**Neuropathic Acute Pain**:
- Focus: Neural protection, gentle neural mobilization, pain modulation
- ROM: Gentle oscillatory neural gliding (avoid end-range tension)
- Avoid: Sustained end-range stretching, positions that reproduce neurological symptoms
- Aerobic: Gentle walking (5-10 min, pain ≤2/10), 2-3x/day
- Example—Acute Radiculopathy: Gentle sciatic nerve mobilization (oscillations), pain-free ROM walking, positioning to reduce nerve tension

**Nociplastic Acute Flare**:
- Focus: Pain neuroscience education, gentle movement exploration, self-efficacy building
- ROM: Gentle ROM exploration, non-painful movement
- Aerobic: Initiate very gentle aerobic activity below pain threshold (5 min walking at conversational pace, daily)
- Education: Introduce central sensitization concepts, normalize symptom flare expectations
- Psychological: Assess for catastrophizing, fear-avoidance; early intervention

**Phase 1 Progression Criteria** (All phenotypes):
- Pain reduction ≥1 point on 0-10 pain scale, OR
- Improved ROM ≥10° without pain increase, OR
- Increased exercise tolerance (able to complete prescribed volume without exacerbation)
- Sleep improvement if disrupted
- Psychological indicators: reduced fear-avoidance, improved self-efficacy

---

### 4.2 Phase 2: Sub-Acute/Early Loading (Weeks 5-8)

**Goals**: Progressive loading tolerance, muscle activation/strengthening initiation, functional ROM restoration, deconditioning reversal

**Pain Phenotype Adaptations**:

**Nociceptive Progressive Loading**:
- Progressive resistance training: 2-3x/week, 40-50% 1RM, 2-3 sets of 10-12 reps
- ROM/mobility: Full pain-free ROM active exercise
- Tissue-specific progressions: Begin sport/function-specific movements at low intensity
- Aerobic: Walking 20-25 min at moderate intensity, 3-5x/week
- Example—Low Back Strain Progression: Controlled squats (bodyweight, 0-45° flexion), prone rows (light resistance), walking 20-25 min daily

**Neuropathic Early Loading**:
- Neural mobilization: Continued with increased ROM/frequency of oscillations
- Functional loading: Light functional activity in pain-free ranges
- Aerobic: Walking 15-20 min, 4x/week at moderate pace
- Progression: Gentle strengthening avoiding neurological provocation
- Example—Radiculopathy Progression: Increased walking duration, gentle hip/knee strengthening, continued sciatic mobilization with expanded ROM

**Nociplastic Gradual Progression**:
- Aerobic progression per time-based schedule: walking 12-18 min, 5-6x/week
- Light strengthening: 30-40% typical loads, 2x/week, building confidence
- PNE continuation: Reinforcing central sensitization concepts, normalizing symptom responses
- Fear-avoidance exposure: Gradual introduction of previously avoided activities
- Psychological: Behavioral activation, addressing catastrophizing
- Example—Fibromyalgia Progression: Walking progresses to 12-18 min, light resistance added (legs, arms), yoga 1-2x/week

**Phase 2 Progression Criteria**:
- PROMIS Physical Function improvement ≥2 points AND PROMIS Pain Intensity improvement ≥2 points, OR
- Functional capacity improvement (able to perform activities previously avoided/painful), OR
- Objective measures: ROM ±5° of target, strength grade improvement (MMT scale), pain-free exercise tolerance improvement

---

### 4.3 Phase 3: Functional/Strengthening (Weeks 9-16)

**Goals**: Sport/activity-specific strengthening, endurance building, functional movement restoration, injury prevention capacity

**Pain Phenotype Adaptations**:

**Nociceptive Functional Strengthening**:
- Progressive resistance: 50-70% 1RM, 3-4 sets of 8-12 reps, 2-3x/week
- Functional movements: Sport/occupation-specific activities at moderate intensity
- Aerobic: 30-45 min at moderate intensity, 4-5x/week
- Balance/proprioception: Integrated into functional movements
- Sport-specific training: Begin skill rehearsal at sub-maximal intensity
- Example—Knee OA Phase 3: Full squat progressions to 70° flexion, single-leg stance 60 sec, step-ups with light resistance, walking/cycling 30-45 min daily

**Neuropathic Functional Integration**:
- Strengthening: Progressive loading of muscles in nerve distribution (60-70% typical loads)
- Functional activity: Sport/occupation-specific movements with gradual load increases
- Aerobic: 30-45 min at moderate-vigorous intensity, 4-5x/week
- Neural mobility: Maintained, but secondary to functional integration
- Example—Radiculopathy Phase 3: Hip/knee strengthening at higher loads, return to sport-specific movements with progressive intensity, continued aerobic activity

**Nociplastic Functional Capacity Building**:
- Aerobic maintenance: 25-30 min at 60-70% HRmax, 5-7x/week (or per sustainable schedule)
- Sport/occupation-specific activities: Progressive intensity within non-pain-contingent framework
- Strength/endurance: 50-70% typical loads, 3x/week, functional movements
- Psychological integration: Self-efficacy reinforcement, activity engagement for well-being
- Example—Fibromyalgia Phase 3: Walking/cycling maintained, sport/hobby reintroduction, resistance training 3x/week, yoga/tai chi 2x/week

**Phase 3 Progression Criteria**:
- PROMIS Physical Function improvement ≥3 points (cumulative from baseline) AND PROMIS Pain Intensity improvement ≥3 points, OR
- Functional capacity: Able to perform target sport/activity at 75-80% intensity without functional limitation
- Strength measures: 70-80% of non-affected limb (when applicable)
- Psychological: Self-efficacy improvement, activity engagement, reduced fear-avoidance

---

### 4.4 Phase 4: Return to Play/Maintenance (Week 16+)

**Goals**: Full functional capacity restoration, sport/activity return to pre-injury/pre-condition level, injury prevention, long-term maintenance

**Pain Phenotype Adaptations**:

**Nociceptive Return to Play**:
- Strength/power training: 70-100% 1RM, 3-4 sets of 6-10 reps, 2-3x/week
- Sport-specific training: Full-intensity sport/occupation-specific movements, 3-4x/week
- Aerobic maintenance: 45+ min at moderate-vigorous intensity, 5-7x/week (or per training demands)
- Injury prevention: Ongoing strengthening, proprioceptive maintenance
- Example—ACL reconstruction return-to-sport: Full squat depth, single-leg hop tests >90% limb symmetry index, full-speed cutting drills, sport participation

**Neuropathic Maintained Functional Capacity**:
- Strength maintenance: Ongoing 2-3x/week strengthening at 60-80% loads
- Functional activity: Full sport/occupation at normal intensity
- Aerobic: Sustained 30-45 min at moderate-vigorous intensity, 4-5x/week
- Neurological monitoring: Ongoing assessment for symptom progression
- Example—Neuropathy Maintenance: Continued strengthening, full-intensity occupational/sport activity, aerobic activity, periodic reassessment

**Nociplastic Long-Term Maintenance**:
- Aerobic activity: 30-45 min at 60-70% HRmax, ≥5x/week (lifestyle integration, not just exercise)
- Strength/functional activity: 2-3x/week, integrated into lifestyle (active hobbies, occupational demands)
- Psychological: Continued activity engagement, stress/sleep management, self-management strategies
- Re-engagement: Full activity participation for quality-of-life and well-being
- Example—Fibromyalgia Long-term: Maintained daily aerobic activity, hobby-based strength engagement, psychological self-management, periodic symptom reassessment

**Phase 4 Progression Criteria/Maintenance Benchmarks**:
- Pain scores: Stable/minimal (pain level acceptable for life demands)
- Function: Full sport/occupation participation without functional limitation
- Psychological: High self-efficacy, activity engagement, well-being
- Long-term: Sustained improvement over 12+ months, relapse prevention plan in place

## 5. Fear-Avoidance and Exercise Progression

### 5.1 Fear-Avoidance Beliefs Questionnaire (FABQ)

**Purpose**: Quantifies patient beliefs that activity/work causes harm, driving avoidance behaviors that perpetuate disability.

**Administration**: 16-item questionnaire scored on 0-6 scale per item

**Scoring**:
- **FABQ Total Score**: 0-96 (higher = greater fear-avoidance)
  - 0-36: Low fear-avoidance
  - 37-59: Moderate fear-avoidance
  - 60+: High fear-avoidance
- **FABQ Work Subscale** (7 items): 0-42
- **FABQ Activity Subscale** (4 items): 0-24

**Clinical Cutoff**: FABQ ≥37 indicates clinically significant fear-avoidance requiring intervention; ≥60 indicates severe fear-avoidance with prognostic implications (slower recovery, higher chronicity risk without intervention)

### 5.2 Graded Exposure Protocol for High Fear-Avoidance Patients

**Rationale**: Exposure to feared activities with demonstrated safety (without harmful consequences) reduces fear-avoidance beliefs and avoidance behavior.

**Assessment Phase** (Session 1-2):
1. **Identify Feared Activities**: Ask patient "What activities do you believe cause harm or worsening of your condition?"
2. **Hierarchy Development**: Rank feared activities by avoidance intensity (0-100 scale)
   - **Example Hierarchy for Low Back Pain**:
     - Level 10: Picking up 20-lb object from floor
     - Level 8: Bending forward to touch toes
     - Level 7: Walking for 30+ minutes
     - Level 6: Doing laundry (bending, lifting basket)
     - Level 5: Light housecleaning (sweeping)
     - Level 4: Sitting upright for 1 hour
     - Level 2: Walking slowly around house
3. **Threat Assessment**: Ask "If you performed this activity, what would happen?" → Identify catastrophic beliefs (e.g., "My disc will slip and cause permanent paralysis")

**Exposure Introduction** (Session 3+):
1. **Start at Low Hierarchy Level** (avoidance intensity 20-40)
2. **Combination Exposure Strategies**:
   - **Imaginal Exposure**: Patient imagines performing feared activity without actual movement
   - **Visual Exposure**: Watch therapist or others perform feared activity without harm
   - **Verbal Reassurance**: Provide education (e.g., "Bending does not cause disc herniation; your disc is anatomically stable")
   - **Actual Exposure**: Perform feared activity under supervision, demonstrating safety

3. **Exposure Progression** (Example—Feared Picking Up Object):
   - Week 1-2: Imagine picking up 5-lb object (imaginal), watch therapist pick up object (visual), therapist education on load tolerance
   - Week 3: Physically pick up 2-lb object with therapist supervision, observe no pain worsening
   - Week 4: Pick up 5-lb object, observe no worsening
   - Week 5: Pick up 10-lb object, demonstrate progressive load tolerance
   - Week 6-8: Functional progression (grocery shopping with 15-lb bags, yard work with tools)

4. **Exposure Session Protocol**:
   - Explain rationale: "We're testing your beliefs about this activity with evidence"
   - Predict outcome: Ask patient "Rate expected pain/harm on 0-10 if we do this activity" (pre-prediction)
   - Perform activity: Therapist coaches through feared activity at tolerable intensity
   - Monitor: Observe for catastrophizing during activity; provide reassurance and corrective evidence
   - Review: Compare predicted outcome to actual outcome (usually significantly better than predicted)
   - Repeat: Multiple exposures to same activity (5-10 repetitions over multiple sessions) to consolidate learning

5. **Behavioral Activation**:
   - Assign between-session homework: "Perform [feared activity] once daily at prescribed intensity"
   - Document adherence and outcomes
   - Gradually increase real-world exposure frequency/intensity

### 5.3 Graded Exposure Integrated with Exercise Prescription

**For High Fear-Avoidance Patients**:
1. **Combine exposure with exercise prescription**: The exercise itself serves as exposure
   - Example: Patient fears "squatting causes knee damage"
   - Exercise prescription: Bodyweight squats, starting shallow (20° flexion) progressing to full ROM
   - Exposure framework: Predict pain/harm → perform squats → compare actual to predicted → repeat for learning consolidation

2. **Quota-based progression** (especially relevant for nociplastic pain):
   - Set exercises on time-contingent basis, not pain-contingent
   - Example: "Perform 10 minutes walking regardless of pain response" (time-contingent exposure)
   - Pain response does NOT trigger exercise cessation, reinforcing that pain ≠ danger

3. **Therapist coaching during exercise**:
   - Normalize pain responses: "Pain during activity is a signal of sensitization, not tissue damage"
   - Redirect catastrophizing: "Your prediction was 8/10 pain; you experienced 4/10; what does that tell you about your beliefs?"
   - Reinforce safety: "You've now performed this 10 times; no harm has occurred; your nervous system can learn it is safe"

### 5.4 Behavioral Activation Strategies

Beyond exposure to feared activities, broader behavioral activation addresses avoidance-driven inactivity:

1. **Activity Scheduling**: Patient schedules valued activities (not pain-contingent) into daily calendar
   - Valued activities: hobbies, social engagement, occupation, family activities
   - Frequency: At least one valued activity daily
   - Intensity: Graded progression (start below typical capacity, increase systematically)

2. **Values-Oriented Goal Setting**:
   - Ask "What activities/roles matter most to you?" (e.g., "spend time with grandchildren," "return to work," "garden")
   - Connect exercises/exposure activities to values (e.g., "This hip strengthening will allow you to play with grandchildren without pain")
   - Motivation comes from intrinsic values, not just pain reduction

3. **Social Engagement**:
   - Group-based exercise programs reduce isolation, provide peer support
   - Social accountability improves adherence

### 5.5 Evidence for Fear-Avoidance Intervention with Exercise

**Systematic Reviews**:
- High fear-avoidance beliefs predict worse pain and functional outcomes in chronic MSK pain
- Graded exposure combined with exercise produces superior pain reduction/function improvement compared to exercise alone (PMID 41356326, 38366560)
- Fear-avoidance reduction is mechanism by which psychological interventions improve exercise outcomes

## 6. Special Populations

### 6.1 Geriatric Patients (≥65 Years)

**Physiological Considerations**:
- Reduced muscle mass (sarcopenia), bone density loss, postural instability
- Slower recovery from exercise-induced soreness
- Comorbidities (hypertension, diabetes, cardiovascular disease) may limit intensity
- Medication interactions (pain medication, blood pressure, anticoagulants)
- Cognitive changes (memory, processing speed)
- Social isolation risk

**Exercise Modifications**:

**Fall Risk Screening** (PRIOR to exercise):
- Perform Timed Up and Go (TUG) test: Normal <12 sec; high fall risk >12 sec
- Berg Balance Scale: <45 points indicates high fall risk
- Assess home environment safety

**Lower Starting Doses**:
- Strength: 30-50% 1RM (versus 40-60% standard), increased reps (15-20 reps)
- Aerobic: Start 10-15 minutes (versus 20+ minutes standard), lower intensity (40-50% HRmax)
- Progression: +2-3 minutes weekly (slower than standard progression)

**Balance Integration** (Critical):
- Single-leg stance progressions: 3 sets of 20-30 sec, 3-4x/week
- Tandem stance (heel-to-toe): 3 sets of 20-30 sec, 3-4x/week
- Functional balance: Step-ups, walking on varied surfaces, turn-and-reach activities
- Consider tai chi or balance-specific classes (evidence-supported fall prevention)

**Strength Focus** (Hip and Lower Extremity):
- Hip abductors (gluteus medius): Essential for hip stability, fall prevention
- Ankle/foot proprioception: Prevent inversion sprains
- Quadriceps: Fall recovery (ability to rise from fall)

**Flexibility Maintenance**:
- Gentle ROM maintained daily
- Avoid aggressive stretching (fracture risk if significant osteoporosis)

**Cardiovascular Monitoring**:
- Blood pressure, heart rate response to exercise
- Monitor for exertional chest discomfort, shortness of breath, dizziness
- Coordinate with primary care if significant hypertension/cardiac history

**Cognitive Support**:
- Simplified exercise instructions (written with pictures/diagrams)
- Consistent exercise schedule (habit formation)
- Consider supervised group classes for motivation/social engagement

### 6.2 Comorbid Obesity (BMI ≥30)

**Exercise Challenges**:
- Weight-bearing activities (walking, jogging) increase joint loading (knee, hip, low back)
- Deconditioning often severe
- Metabolic syndrome common (hypertension, glucose intolerance)

**Exercise Modifications**:

**Non-Weight-Bearing/Reduced Load Alternatives** (Priority):
- Aquatic exercise: Most effective for obese individuals with joint pain (water buoyancy reduces loading ~50-90% depending on water depth)
  - 15-20 minute sessions, 3-4x/week initially; progress to 30-45 min
  - Combined with land-based strengthening
- Recumbent/stationary cycling: Seated, reduced joint loading, low-impact
  - 15-20 min, 3-4x/week; progress to 30-45 min
- Seated/supported exercises: Chair-based strengthening, modified squats/step-ups

**Progressive Weight-Bearing Introduction**:
- Begin non-weight-bearing (aquatic, cycling)
- Introduce brief weight-bearing intervals (e.g., "walk 2 minutes, cycle 3 minutes" alternating)
- Gradually increase weight-bearing proportion as fitness improves
- Target: 50% of aerobic activity weight-bearing within 8 weeks

**Strength Training**:
- Lower extremity: Critical (hip, thigh, calf strengthening for load tolerance)
- Core/trunk stabilization: Support for spinal loads
- Upper extremity: General fitness
- Intensity: 30-50% 1RM (conservative), higher reps (15-20), 2x/week
- Progression: Moderate (increase load 5% every 3-4 weeks, not weekly)

**Nutritional Coordination**:
- Exercise alone insufficient for weight loss; coordinate with nutrition counseling
- Caloric deficit needed for weight loss; exercise supports metabolic health, fitness
- Consider referral to registered dietitian

**Metabolic Management**:
- Monitor blood pressure, blood glucose (if diabetic) during exercise
- Coordinate with primary care if significant comorbidities

### 6.3 Comorbid Depression/Anxiety

**Exercise as Anxiolytic/Mood Intervention**:
- Aerobic exercise shows efficacy for depression/anxiety reduction (moderate effect sizes)
- Mechanism: Neuroplasticity (BDNF), endorphins, sleep improvement, social engagement, self-efficacy
- Integration critical for optimal outcomes

**Modifications**:

**Group-Based Exercise** (When Feasible):
- Socially engaging (reduces isolation)
- Peer support, accountability
- Structured group classes (yoga, tai chi, walking groups)

**Timing/Frequency**:
- Daily or near-daily aerobic activity (mood regulation benefit)
- Morning exercise may support mood/energy for day
- Consistency more important than intensity for mood benefit

**Intensity/Type**:
- Moderate-intensity aerobic (walking, cycling, swimming) most evidence-supported
- Yoga/tai chi additionally support anxiety reduction, mindfulness

**Psychological Integration**:
- Coordinate with mental health provider (therapist, psychiatrist)
- Exercise not replacement for psychotherapy/medication, but powerful adjunct
- Address catastrophizing, rumination during exercise (mindfulness component)

**Motivation Support**:
- Depression reduces motivation; external structure helps (group class, scheduled sessions, accountability partner)
- Values-oriented goals (connect exercise to meaningful activities)
- Early success building (start with achievable goals, experience positive outcomes)

### 6.4 Fibromyalgia/Widespread Pain

(Detailed in Section 2.3; highlighted here for special population considerations)

**Unique Aspects**:

**Very Gradual Progression**:
- Even slow progressions (standard 5% load increase weekly) may trigger flares
- Conservative approach: +5-10% load every 2-3 weeks
- Or progression by time/frequency: Add one additional 5-minute session weekly, rather than increasing individual session duration

**Pain Flare Management**:
- Flares expected in initial 4-6 weeks; normalize this
- Flare protocol: Maintain quota (time-contingent), not increase. Pain response should not trigger pause/reduction.
- Continue aerobic activity even during flare (maintains desensitization trajectory)
- Non-exercise pain management: Sleep optimization, stress reduction, medical management coordination

**Sleep Prioritization**:
- Sleep disturbance near-universal in FM; exercise can improve sleep, but requires time
- Poor sleep → exercise tolerance reduced; coordinate sleep interventions
- Consider sleep study if severe sleep apnea suspected

**Medication Coordination**:
- Many patients on duloxetine, pregabalin, gabapentin (CNS-active medications)
- Timing: Exercise ideally 2-3 hours after medication (peak efficacy) for maximal tolerance
- Monitor for dizziness/orthostasis with medications; adjust exercise as needed

**Comorbidity Prevalence**:
- High rates of depression, anxiety, irritable bowel syndrome
- Address comorbidities concurrently (integrated care model)
