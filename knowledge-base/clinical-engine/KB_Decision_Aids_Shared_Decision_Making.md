# Decision Aids and Shared Decision-Making Reference

**For KIMI MSK Care Coach Knowledge Base**

This document provides comprehensive guidance on shared decision-making (SDM) frameworks, treatment decision aids, and how KIMI facilitates evidence-based, patient-centered decisions about hip and knee osteoarthritis management.

---

## 1. AHRQ SHARE Framework for Shared Decision-Making

The SHARE framework (developed by the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality) is a five-step structured approach to shared decision-making. KIMI integrates this framework into every treatment-related conversation.

### Step 1: **S**eek Patient Participation

**Purpose:** Actively invite the patient into the decision-making process and establish that their voice matters.

**KIMI Script Examples:**
- "I want to understand what's most important to you about managing your hip pain. What would you like to be able to do that you can't do right now?"
- "There are several ways we can approach managing your knee osteoarthritis, and I'd like to help you explore what might work best for your life. What are your thoughts?"
- "Before we talk about treatment options, tell me: what would success look like for you? What activities or roles matter most?"

**Key Principles:**
- Present as a collaborative partner, not an authority delivering orders
- Frame patients as the expert in their own experience
- Acknowledge that the patient has legitimate preferences and values

---

### Step 2: **H**elp Patients Explore Options

**Purpose:** Present available treatment options with clear, evidence-based information about benefits and harms.

**KIMI's Role:**
KIMI presents treatment options in a stepped framework, from most conservative to more intensive:
- **Level 0:** Symptom awareness, activity modification, weight management
- **Level 1:** Exercise, physical therapy, weight loss, acupuncture, acetaminophen
- **Level 2:** Topical NSAIDs, ice/heat, joint injections (corticosteroid, viscosupplementation)
- **Level 3:** Oral NSAIDs (with GI/cardiac risk discussion)
- **Level 4:** Opioid medications (only with awareness of addiction risks)
- **Level 5:** Joint replacement surgery

**KIMI Presentation for Each Option:**

#### Exercise Therapy (Level 1)
**Benefits:**
- First-line evidence-based treatment per ACR, AAOS, APTA, and OARSI guidelines
- Improves pain, function, and quality of life
- Addresses strength, flexibility, and balance
- No systemic side effects
- Supports long-term self-management
- Reduces progression of OA
- Improves sleep quality and mood

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Requires consistent effort (weeks to months for benefit)
- Initial soreness when starting (typical, temporary)
- Requires patient self-efficacy and adherence
- May be challenging if pain is severe at baseline

**Evidence Presentation to Patients:**
"Research shows that people who do targeted exercises for their knee or hip pain often improve as much as those who have surgery—but without the risks of anesthesia or recovery time. It takes time and consistency, usually 6-12 weeks to notice real improvement. Many people also find that as they move more confidently, their pain actually goes down."

---

#### Physical Therapy (Level 1)
**Benefits:**
- Individualized assessment and program design
- Expert instruction in proper technique
- Progression based on tolerance and goals
- Addresses functional limitations
- May prevent or delay surgery

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Requires multiple visits (time/logistics commitment)
- Out-of-pocket costs may apply
- Requires travel to clinical setting (unless telehealth available)

---

#### Topical NSAIDs (Level 2)
**Benefits:**
- ACR-recommended first-line medication for OA
- Lower systemic absorption than oral NSAIDs
- Fewer gastrointestinal and cardiovascular risks
- Available over-the-counter
- Minimal whole-body side effects
- Works within about 1 week

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Modest benefit (10% above placebo)
- Local skin reactions possible
- Doesn't address underlying condition
- Total daily dose limits (max 32g/day)
- Cannot be used with oral NSAIDs simultaneously

**Evidence Presentation:**
"Topical diclofenac (Voltaren) is a good option if you want pain relief without pills. It works about as well as oral pain relievers but with much lower risk. It's not a cure—it helps manage pain while you're working on other things like exercise."

---

#### Corticosteroid Joint Injections (Level 2)
**Benefits:**
- Reduces inflammation and pain quickly
- Allows participation in exercise/PT
- May extend the time before surgery is needed
- Allows temporary reprieve to focus on exercise

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Temporary benefit (typically 4-12 weeks)
- Limits per year (typically 3-4 maximum)
- Small infection risk
- Does not prevent OA progression
- Repeated injections may accelerate cartilage breakdown (debated in literature)

**Evidence Presentation:**
"A steroid injection can quiet down inflammation and give you relief for several weeks to a couple of months. The main benefit is using that window of better comfort to get stronger with exercise. The injection itself doesn't slow down or reverse osteoarthritis—think of it as creating a better opportunity for the other treatments to work."

---

#### Viscosupplementation (Hyaluronic Acid Injections) (Level 2)
**Benefits:**
- May improve lubrication of joint
- Used when other conservative measures haven't fully worked
- Minimal side effects
- 1-5 injections over several weeks

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Effectiveness unclear (mixed research evidence)
- Most beneficial if arthritis is mild to moderate
- Does not reverse cartilage damage
- Requires multiple injections
- Does not prevent OA progression
- Cost may not be covered by insurance

**Evidence Presentation:**
"Hyaluronic acid injections are a treatment where we add a substance to your joint that acts like lubrication. Some people find relief, but research shows it helps some people more than others. It works best if your arthritis is mild to moderate. It's not a cure, but for some people it can bridge to feeling better while you're working on exercise and other strategies."

---

#### Oral NSAIDs (Level 3)
**Benefits:**
- Effective pain relief
- Reduces inflammation
- Allows improved function for exercise/activity

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Gastrointestinal risks (ulcers, bleeding)
- Cardiovascular risks (especially with prolonged use or in high-risk patients)
- Kidney risks
- Drug interactions
- Not recommended as first-line for OA

**KIMI's Statement:**
"Oral pain relievers can help manage pain, but they come with more side effects than topical options or exercise. KIMI doesn't recommend specific medications—that's a conversation between you and your doctor who knows your full medical history. If you're considering oral NSAIDs, ask your doctor about your personal risk for stomach or heart issues."

---

#### Joint Replacement Surgery (Level 5)
**When to Consider:**
- Persistent pain despite 6+ months of conservative care
- Significant functional limitation affecting quality of life
- Desire to pursue surgery after informed decision-making
- Appropriate surgical candidacy (medical fitness)

**Benefits:**
- High success rate (85-90% report significant improvement)
- Allows return to many activities
- Durable (most joints last 15-20+ years)
- Definitive treatment for end-stage OA

**Harms/Challenges:**
- Surgical risks (infection, blood clots, anesthesia complications)
- Recovery period (weeks to months)
- Rehabilitation requirements
- Joint revision may be needed in future
- Some activity restrictions may persist

---

### Step 3: **A**ssess Values and Preferences

**Purpose:** Understand what matters most to the patient so recommendations align with their priorities.

**KIMI's Values Clarification Questions:**

"Help me understand what matters most to you. When you think about managing your pain, which of these is most important?"

**Exercise-Related Values:**
- "How do you feel about committing to a regular exercise routine? Is that appealing or daunting?"
- "Would you prefer to do exercises at home, at a clinic, or in a group setting?"
- "How important is it to you to avoid medications if possible?"

**Pain Management Values:**
- "What's your pain tolerance? Are you someone who wants to explore all non-medication options first, or would you prefer medication to allow you to be more active?"
- "How much side effect risk are you willing to accept for pain relief?"

**Function and Activity Values:**
- "What activities do you most want to get back to? What would feel like success for you?"
- "Are you willing to invest several months of effort for potential improvement, or do you need faster relief?"

**Medical Decision Values:**
- "How do you feel about surgery? Is it something you'd want to eventually consider if conservative care doesn't fully work, or would you prefer to avoid it?"
- "How involved do you want to be in decisions about your care? Do you want detailed information about all options, or do you prefer recommendations?"

**Broader Life Values:**
- "What's most important to you in your daily life right now? What do you want to protect or improve?"
- "How does your pain affect your mood, sleep, relationships, or ability to do things you enjoy?"
- "Are there cultural or personal beliefs about health and treatment that I should understand?"

**Processing Values Into Decisions:**

Once you understand patient values, explicitly connect them to treatment choices:

- If patient values avoiding medications: "Based on what you've told me about wanting to avoid pills if possible, let's focus first on exercise and topical options if you need them."
- If patient values quick results: "I hear you need relief relatively soon. Here's a realistic timeline for what you might expect from exercise (4-6 weeks), injections (1-2 weeks), and how those can work together."
- If patient is motivated by specific activities: "Your goal is to get back to gardening. Let's choose exercises that build the strength and flexibility you need for that, and I'll coach you on how to modify gardening to protect your knee while you're getting stronger."

---

### Step 4: **R**each a Decision Together

**Purpose:** Co-create a personalized care plan that reflects the patient's values and circumstances.

**KIMI's Decision Documentation:**

"Based on our conversation, here's what I understand about your situation and preferences:

- **Your main goal:** [patient's stated goal]
- **What matters most to you:** [values identified]
- **Your concerns about treatment:** [expressed concerns]
- **What you're willing to commit to:** [realistic commitments]

**Here's what we're going to try first:**
[Treatment plan with rationale tied to patient values]

**Here's what success will look like:**
[Specific, measurable, timeframe-based goals]

**Here's what we'll track to know if it's working:**
[Specific PROMs or functional measures]

**Here's how often we'll check in:**
[Coaching cadence]

**Here's when we'll reassess and potentially adjust:**
[Decision points for escalation or modification]

Is this plan something you feel good about? What questions do you have?"

**Shared Decision-Making Checkpoints:**

Before finalizing a decision, confirm patient understanding and agreement:

1. "On a scale of 0-10, how confident are you that this plan is right for you?"
2. "What do you understand about how this treatment works and what to expect?"
3. "What are you most concerned about with this plan?"
4. "Do you have any other options you'd like to discuss?"
5. "Are you willing to commit to this plan? If not, what would make it more workable?"

---

### Step 5: **E**valuate the Decision Over Time

**Purpose:** Monitor outcomes, gather feedback, and adjust as needed based on evidence and evolving preferences.

**KIMI's Evaluation Schedule:**

| Timeframe | KIMI Action |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1-2 | Daily check-ins on pain, exercise adherence, barriers |
| Weekly (Weeks 1-12) | PROM assessment; pain tracking; exercise progression |
| Every 4 weeks | Comprehensive review of progress toward goals, satisfaction with plan |
| Week 12 | Major decision point: Continue? Modify? Escalate? |
| Months 3-6 | Monthly comprehensive reviews |
| Months 6-12 | Quarterly reviews with focus on sustainability |

**Evaluation Conversations:**

"Let's look at how this plan is working. Here's what I'm seeing:

- **Your pain:** [show trend]
- **Your function:** [show improvement in specific activities]
- **Your adherence:** [show pattern of engagement]
- **Your satisfaction:** [reflect patient feedback]

**What's working well?** [Acknowledge and reinforce]

**What's not working?** [Explore barriers]

**Based on this, here's what I recommend:**
- Continue as is? Yes / No / With modifications
- Escalate to the next level? Why or why not?
- Change any aspect of the plan? What and why?"

**Triggers for Escalation Discussion:**

KIMI raises the question of moving to the next treatment level when:
- Pain is not improving after 6-8 weeks of consistent effort at current level
- Patient reports that pain is significantly limiting their ability to exercise
- Patient expresses readiness and motivation to try stronger interventions
- Current level has reached its limit of effectiveness or tolerance
- Patient goals cannot be achieved at current level

## 2. Hip and Knee Osteoarthritis Decision Aid

### Understanding Osteoarthritis

**What Patients Need to Know:**

Osteoarthritis (OA) is a condition where the cartilage that cushions joint surfaces gradually breaks down. This causes pain, stiffness, and swelling. Unlike some conditions, OA develops slowly and usually gets worse over time if untreated, but it can be well-managed.

**Key Facts About OA:**
- It affects over 30 million Americans
- It's more common with age, previous joint injury, or excess weight
- Symptoms vary widely—some people have minimal impact, others significant
- There is no cure, but symptoms can be controlled
- It doesn't have to lead to surgery (many people manage well without it)
- Exercise and lifestyle changes are the most important factors in managing OA

---

### Decision Aid: Conservative Care vs. Surgery

**When to Consider Each Option:**

#### Conservative Care Is Appropriate When:
- Pain is manageable or mild to moderate
- You haven't yet tried comprehensive exercise and PT
- You want to delay or avoid surgery
- You're willing to invest 3-6+ months in structured treatment
- You can commit to regular exercise and lifestyle changes
- You want to preserve your natural joint

**What Conservative Care Involves:**
1. Exercise (most important—PT-guided or self-directed)
2. Activity modification and weight management if appropriate
3. Sleep optimization
4. Stress and mood management
5. Pain management (topical, injections, or oral meds if needed)
6. Education about OA and self-management
7. Regular monitoring and progression of exercise

**Timeline for Conservative Care:**
- Initial relief: 2-4 weeks (if using injections or topical NSAIDs)
- Meaningful improvement from exercise: 6-8 weeks
- Optimal benefit: 12-16 weeks of consistent engagement
- Long-term management: ongoing (like managing other chronic conditions)

---

#### Surgery (Joint Replacement) Is Appropriate When:
- You've tried comprehensive conservative care for 6+ months without sufficient relief
- Pain is significantly limiting your daily life and activities you care about
- You're ready for the surgery and recovery process
- You have a clear preference for surgery after informed discussion
- You are medically fit for surgery

**What Joint Replacement Involves:**
1. Pre-operative assessment and optimization
2. Surgical procedure (usually 1-2 hour surgery)
3. Hospital stay (usually 1-2 days)
4. Intensive rehabilitation (3-6 months)
5. Gradual return to activities (6-12 months)
6. Long-term follow-up

**Timeline for Surgery:**
- Recovery period: 3-6 months for most daily activities
- Return to light exercise: 6-8 weeks
- Return to more vigorous activity: 3-6 months
- Full benefit: 6-12 months

---

### Treatment Options Comparison Table

| Factor | Exercise | Injections | Topical NSAID | Surgery |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Speed of Relief** | Weeks (4-8) | Days to weeks | Days to week | Weeks to months |
| **Duration of Benefit** | Ongoing if maintained | 4-12 weeks | Daily dosing | 15-20+ years |
| **Commitment Required** | High (ongoing) | Low (periodic) | Low (daily application) | Very high (recovery) |
| **Systemic Side Effects** | None | Minimal | Minimal | Moderate (temporary) |
| **Addresses Root Issue** | Partially (builds strength, reduces progression) | No (temporary anti-inflammatory) | No (symptom relief) | Yes (replaces joint) |
| **Reversibility** | Yes | Yes (wears off) | Yes | No (permanent) |
| **Cost** | Low to moderate | Moderate | Low | High |
| **Maintenance** | Ongoing exercise | Occasional injections | Daily | Unlikely need for same joint |

---

### Key Decision Questions for Patients

**Understanding Current State:**
1. "On a scale of 0-10, how much is your pain affecting your daily life right now?"
2. "What activities do you want to get back to that you can't do now?"
3. "Have you tried structured exercise or physical therapy before? What was that experience like?"

**Understanding Preferences:**
4. "How do you feel about trying intensive conservative care before considering surgery?"
5. "If surgery were successful, what would be worth the recovery time?"
6. "What scares you most about surgery? What appeals to you about it?"

**Understanding Realistic Expectations:**
7. "Are you willing to commit to exercise 3-5 times per week for at least 12 weeks?"
8. "If you improve 50% but not 100%, would that feel like success?"
9. "How much risk from medication side effects are you willing to accept?"

**Understanding Values:**
10. "What matters most to you: avoiding surgery, getting the fastest relief, or something else?"
11. "How does this pain affect other important things in your life—sleep, mood, relationships?"
12. "What does a good quality of life look like to you?"

## 3. Treatment Options Overview for Patients

### Level 0: Awareness and Modification (Baseline)

**Activity Modification:**
- Identify activities that aggravate pain; modify technique or frequency, don't eliminate
- Pacing strategies (do less, more often, rather than pushing through)
- Use assistive devices if helpful (cane, walker, reaching tools)
- Proper footwear and joint support as needed

**Weight Management:**
- For every 1 pound of weight loss, there's roughly 4 pounds less pressure on the knee
- Not required, but can significantly help symptoms

**Education:**
- Understanding OA as a manageable condition, not a catastrophe
- Recognizing that pain ≠ damage
- Understanding "flare-ups" are temporary and don't mean OA is worsening

---

### Level 1: Exercise, PT, and Lifestyle (First-Line)

**Exercise Therapy:**
- Most evidence-based treatment for hip and knee OA
- Types: strengthening, flexibility, balance, aerobic
- Dosing: 2-3 hours per week of combined exercise
- Progressive: starts gentle, advances based on tolerance

**Physical Therapy:**
- Assessment of movement, strength, flexibility
- Individualized program design
- Instruction in proper technique
- Progression and modification as patient improves

**Weight Loss (if appropriate):**
- Supported by dietary changes, not severe restriction
- Realistic goals: 5-10% body weight reduction meaningful
- Combined with exercise is most effective

**Sleep Hygiene:**
- Good sleep improves pain perception and healing
- Sleep quality often improves as pain improves

---

### Level 2: Topical and Injectable Treatments

**Topical NSAIDs (Diclofenac/Voltaren):**
- Available over-the-counter
- Applied to skin over affected joint
- Works within about 1 week
- Low systemic absorption
- Effective for pain relief with minimal side effects
- Modest benefit (10% better than placebo)

**Corticosteroid Injections:**
- Reduces inflammation quickly (days)
- Benefit typically lasts 4-12 weeks
- Creates window for improved exercise participation
- Limit: typically 3-4 per year per joint
- Does not slow OA progression

**Viscosupplementation (Hyaluronic Acid):**
- 1-5 injections over several weeks
- Acts as joint lubricant
- Most effective if arthritis is mild to moderate
- Benefit variable and unclear
- Does not reverse cartilage damage

---

### Level 3: Oral Medications

**Oral NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen):**
- More effective pain relief than topical
- Greater systemic risks (GI, cardiovascular, renal)
- Not first-line for OA
- Risk increases with age and existing conditions
- **KIMI does not recommend specific oral medications—that's a discussion with your doctor**

**Acetaminophen:**
- Used for pain relief
- Lower effectiveness than NSAIDs
- Safer profile but risk at high doses
- Does not address inflammation

---

### Level 4-5: Stronger Interventions

**Opioid Medications:**
- Carry high risk of dependence and side effects
- Not recommended for OA management
- Used only in specific circumstances under close medical supervision
- **KIMI does not recommend opioids and will not coach patients taking opioids without physician oversight**

**Joint Replacement Surgery:**
- Definitive treatment for severe, end-stage OA
- High success rate (85-90% achieve significant improvement)
- Requires surgery, anesthesia, and recovery
- Long-term durability (15-20+ years typical)

---

## 4. Predicting Hip and Knee Outcomes

### Factors That Influence Surgical Outcomes

Research on hip and knee replacement outcomes shows certain pre-operative factors predict who will have better or worse outcomes.

**Positive Predictors (Better Outcomes):**
- Younger age
- Better pre-operative function
- Mild to moderate pre-operative pain
- Good mental health (absence of depression/anxiety)
- High motivation and expectations aligned with realistic possibilities
- Good social support
- Ability to comply with rehabilitation

**Negative Predictors (Worse Outcomes):**
- Older age
- Severe pre-operative pain
- Very poor pre-operative function
- Depression or anxiety
- Poor social support
- Unrealistic expectations (expecting perfect outcome)
- Medical comorbidities affecting healing
- Obesity (though not absolute contraindication)

### Machine Learning Prediction Models

Recent research has developed machine learning models that can predict with 85-87% accuracy whether a patient will achieve meaningful improvement after hip or knee replacement.

**Pre-operative measures most predictive of good outcomes:**
1. Pre-operative pain levels (paradoxically, moderate pain does better than extreme)
2. Mental health status (depression/anxiety screening)
3. Pre-operative function level
4. Age
5. General health status

**KIMI's Use of Outcome Prediction:**
KIMI helps patients understand:
- "Based on patients similar to you, here's the typical outcome rate..."
- "These factors in your situation suggest good potential for improvement..."
- "This factor might mean recovery takes longer, but still typically improves..."

**KIMI Does NOT:**
- Promise specific outcomes
- Discourage surgery based on any single factor
- Use prediction models as clinical decision-making tools
- Share model-generated predictions as binding predictions

## 5. Kimi's Role in Shared Decision-Making

### What KIMI Can Do

**Information Provision:**
- Present evidence-based information about options
- Explain benefits and harms in plain language
- Connect treatment options to patient values

**Values Clarification:**
- Ask questions to understand what matters to patient
- Help patient articulate preferences
- Reflect back what you understand

**Decision Support:**
- Help patient organize thoughts about options
- Provide structured worksheets or tools
- Summarize pros/cons tied to patient values

**Coaching and Implementation:**
- Execute the care plan collaboratively
- Provide encouragement and problem-solving
- Track progress and adjust

**Escalation Support:**
- Recognize when patient needs to discuss surgery with surgeon
- Prepare patient for that conversation
- Provide structured information for surgeon visit

---

### What KIMI Cannot and Will Not Do

**KIMI Will NOT:**
- Recommend or advise against specific medications (including OTC)
- Recommend surgery or advise against it
- Tell patient which option is "best"
- Prescribe or suggest medication dosing
- Recommend discontinuing prescribed medications
- Diagnose conditions beyond MSK OA
- Provide specific dietary prescriptions
- Make treatment decisions for patient
- Minimize or dismiss patient's concerns

**When KIMI Needs to Escalate:**

KIMI recognizes decision-making requires physician input when:
- Patient wants to discuss medication options in detail
- Patient is considering surgery
- Patient reports new symptoms suggesting a different condition
- Patient's pain is severe and conservative care alone feels insufficient
- Patient has medical complexity (multiple comorbidities, taking many medications)
- Patient reports mental health concerns (depression, anxiety, catastrophic thinking)

**KIMI's Escalation Script:**
"Based on what you've told me, I think it would be really helpful to talk with [your doctor/surgeon/rheumatologist]. They know your full medical history and can discuss medication options / surgery / other treatments with you. Here's what I'd suggest bringing up in that conversation: [structured points]. I'll keep supporting you with exercise and other tools while you're exploring those options."

### Exercise 3: Risk-Benefit Trade-off

**Instructions to Patient:**
"For each treatment option, I'm going to ask you how much benefit you'd need to see to make it worth any risks or inconveniences."

**Exercise Example:**
- "If exercise took 45 minutes, 3 days per week for 12 weeks, and had a 70% chance of improving your pain by 50%, would that be worth it?"
- "If injections worked for 2 months but you had to repeat them every 3 months indefinitely, would that work for you?"
- "If surgery required 3 months of recovery time and 6 months of rehabilitation, but had an 85% chance of major pain improvement and lasting relief, would you consider it?"

---

### Exercise 4: The "Ideal Scenario" Conversation

**Instructions to Patient:**
"Imagine we've found the perfect treatment for you. Describe what that would look like."

**Prompt Questions:**
- How often would you need to do it?
- What would the improvement feel like?
- How long would it take to see results?
- What side effects or inconveniences would you tolerate?
- What would make you consider it a failure or not worth it?

**KIMI's Use:**
This reveals the patient's ideal outcome, which you can then match to realistic options:
"That sounds like you'd prefer [option], but here's what's realistic about [option]: [honest assessment]. Can we find a middle ground that gets you close to your ideal?"

## 7. Common Patient Questions and KIMI's Evidence-Based Responses

**"Will exercise cure my osteoarthritis?"**
Response: "Exercise can't cure OA because the cartilage damage doesn't reverse. But it can reduce pain, improve strength, and slow progression. Many people improve so much with exercise that they don't need surgery. Exercise is the most evidence-supported treatment we have."

**"Don't I need an injection/medication to start exercising?"**
Response: "Some people do best starting with an injection or medication to get pain relief, then using that window to exercise. Others jump into exercise without injections. There's no one-size-fits-all. Let's figure out what works for you."

**"If my pain gets worse, doesn't that mean the arthritis is progressing?"**
Response: "Pain and arthritis progression aren't directly connected. Sometimes pain increases because of inflammation, tight muscles, or normal fluctuations. If we see real loss of function over months, that's worth discussing with your doctor. But a bad day or week doesn't mean things are getting worse."

**"Should I get a different injection to prevent surgery?"**
Response: "Injections can be part of a good plan, especially to help you exercise. They don't prevent OA from progressing, but they might help you manage it better. If you're thinking about surgery, let's have a clear conversation with your surgeon about all options."

**"Can I move my joint too much and make it worse?"**
Response: "Movement in pain-controlled, progressive ranges is healing. Moving within tolerable pain levels actually helps. The old 'protect it completely' approach actually makes OA worse. Gentle, progressive movement is what we want."
