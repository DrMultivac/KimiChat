# KIMI Enrollment Intake: Evidence-Based Pain Phenotype Classification Protocol

**Document Version:** 1.0
**Last Updated:** April 2026
**RAG Index:** pain-phenotyping-intake
**Audience:** KIMI AI MSK Care Coach, Clinical Supervisors, Health Coaches
**Purpose:** Standardized, conversational pain phenotype screening at enrollment to drive personalized care plan design

---

## 1. Purpose — Why Phenotype Pain at Intake

### 1.1 Strategic Rationale

Pain phenotyping at enrollment is foundational to KIMI's evidence-based care delivery model. The pain phenotype (nociceptive, neuropathic, or nociplastic) determines:

- **Exercise Selection & Progression:** Nociceptive pain responds to graded load management; neuropathic pain requires protection and neuromotor retraining; nociplastic pain benefits from aerobic activity and central desensitization
- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) & Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) Priority:** Nociplastic phenotypes benefit most from PNE and catastrophizing reduction early; nociceptive phenotypes may prioritize activity pacing; neuropathic phenotypes benefit from anxiety reduction around symptoms
- **Coaching Cadence & Check-in Frequency:** Nociplastic presentations often require weekly engagement during first 4 weeks; nociceptive pain may progress with biweekly coaching; neuropathic pain requires consistent neurological monitoring
- **Escalation Thresholds:** Lack of response to exercise in nociceptive pain suggests possible missed diagnosis or need for imaging; lack of response to graded exposure in nociplastic pain may indicate need for psychiatric co-management
- **Expected Improvement Trajectory:** Pain reduction timing differs by phenotype—nociceptive pain often improves within 2–4 weeks of appropriate load management; nociplastic pain may require 6–12 weeks of consistent multimodal intervention before meaningful improvement
- **Measurement & Outcome Monitoring:** Phenotype guides which outcome measures are most sensitive (e.g., widespread pain index for nociplastic; dermatomal pain rating for neuropathic; pain with activity for nociceptive)

### 1.2 The Three Primary Pain Mechanisms

KIMI classifies pain into three evidence-based mechanisms:

1. **Nociceptive Pain:** Acute activation of nociceptors due to actual or threatened tissue damage; pain is proportionate to tissue pathology
2. **Neuropathic Pain:** Pain caused by a lesion or disease affecting the somatosensory nervous system; pain persists independent of ongoing tissue damage
3. **Nociplastic Pain:** Pain caused by alterations in nociception (amplified central processing) without clear evidence of tissue damage or nervous system lesion

**Operational Definition for Clinical Screening:** A patient presents with pain that is mechanically responsive and localized to a specific joint or region with imaging/exam findings = likely nociceptive. A patient reports burning, electric, or shooting pain in a dermatomal or peripheral nerve distribution with associated neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, tingling) = likely neuropathic. A patient reports widespread, disproportionate pain with significant fatigue, sleep disruption, brain fog, and mood changes = likely nociplastic (central sensitization).

### 1.3 Mixed Presentations and Phenotype Dynamics

**Critical Principle:** Most patients do not fit neatly into one phenotype. Mixed presentations are the norm, especially in Medicare 65+ population with chronic musculoskeletal pain.

- **Common Mixed Presentations:**
  - Knee OA (nociceptive) + lumbar radiculopathy (neuropathic)
  - Mechanical LBP (nociceptive) with significant pain catastrophizing and fatigue (nociplastic features)
  - Chronic widespread pain (nociplastic) with superimposed nerve compression (neuropathic)
  - Diabetic peripheral neuropathy (neuropathic) with secondary central sensitization (nociplastic) due to chronic pain duration

**Phenotype Dominance:** When mixed phenotypes are present, KIMI documents the "dominant" phenotype (the one driving the most disability and requiring priority intervention) and secondary phenotypes. Treatment prioritizes the dominant phenotype first, then adjusts if incomplete response.

### 1.4 Phenotype Is a Starting Point, Not a Fixed Label

Pain phenotype may shift during the treatment course. For example:

- A patient presenting with acute mechanical knee pain (nociceptive) may develop central sensitization features (nociplastic) if activity avoidance and fear-avoidance beliefs persist for 3+ months
- A patient with neuropathic pain from radiculopathy may show resolution of nerve-mediated symptoms but persistent nociplastic features (catastrophizing, fatigue, sleep disruption) requiring continued PNE and mood management
- A patient with nociplastic pain may respond dramatically to exercise and sleep improvement, revealing an underlying nociceptive component that was masked by high pain amplification

**Reassessment Protocol:** KIMI reassesses pain phenotype at 4–6 week intervals or if treatment response is atypical for the assigned phenotype. Re-phenotyping may trigger care plan modification.

## 2. The Three Pain Phenotypes — Clinical Definitions

### 2.1 Nociceptive Pain

#### 2.1.1 Clinical Definition and Pathophysiology

**Definition:** Pain resulting from activation of nociceptors (specialized pain-detecting nerve endings) by actual or threatened tissue damage, inflammation, or mechanical stress. Pain is proportionate to the degree of tissue pathology and improves as tissue heals.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Nociceptors respond to mechanical, thermal, or chemical stimuli (bradykinin, prostaglandins, cytokines released by inflamed tissue)
- Pain signals travel via A-delta (sharp, localized) and C fibers (aching, diffuse) to the dorsal horn of the spinal cord
- Spinal processing integrates input and sends signals to the brain
- Pain perception is proportionate to nociceptive input—more tissue damage = more pain
- As tissue heals (inflammation resolves, structural integrity improves), nociceptive input decreases and pain decreases

**Key Characteristics:**
- Mechanical behavior: Pain is worse with specific activities that load or stress the affected tissue; pain improves with rest, immobilization, or activity modification
- Localized: Pain is confined to the region of tissue damage (e.g., knee pain from knee OA, shoulder pain from impingement, low back pain from disc herniation)
- Predictable: Pain is triggered by predictable mechanical stress; pain relief occurs with predictable interventions (ice, heat, rest, postural change)
- Acute or subacute onset: Pain is often associated with a clear precipitating event (fall, overuse, trauma) or gradually progressive with known pathology (OA progression)
- Good tissue-pain correlation: Imaging findings (OA grade, effusion, herniation) are typically proportionate to pain severity

#### 2.1.2 Epidemiology in Medicare MSK Population (65+ years)

- **Prevalence:** Nociceptive pain is the predominant pain phenotype in the Medicare 65+ population with chronic MSK conditions
- **Most Common Conditions:** Knee OA (prevalence 10–15% in adults 65+), hip OA (8–10%), lumbar spondylosis with facet-mediated pain (common in imaging), cervical spondylosis, shoulder OA/impingement (prevalence increasing with age)
- **Gender Distribution:** Knee OA more common in women; hip OA distribution more balanced; shoulder impingement more common in women
- **Comorbidity Context:** Nociceptive pain in older adults is often superimposed on chronic diseases (diabetes, heart disease) and polypharmacy; pain may be partially masked by opioid use or dulled by comorbid conditions
- **Functional Impact:** Nociceptive pain is a major driver of disability in older adults; fear-avoidance and activity restriction can lead to secondary nociplastic features over time

#### 2.1.3 Typical Conditions Presenting with Nociceptive Phenotype

**Primary OA Presentations:**
- Knee OA (medial, lateral, patellofemoral, or generalized): Most common nociceptive condition in KIMI population
- Hip OA: Pain with hip adduction, flexion; mechanical pattern
- Lumbar facet-mediated pain: One-sided LBP worse with extension or rotation
- Cervical OA with facet-mediated pain: Neck pain with restricted rotation
- Glenohumeral OA: Shoulder pain with passive range of motion loss

**Tendinopathy/Soft Tissue Injury:**
- Rotator cuff tendinopathy (supraspinatus, infraspinatus): Pain with overhead activities, night pain with sleeping on affected side
- Patellar tendinopathy: Pain below patella, worse with stairs or jumping
- Achilles tendinopathy: Pain with dorsiflexion or push-off activities
- Lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow): Pain with gripping, resisted wrist extension
- Medial epicondylitis (golfer's elbow): Pain with gripping, resisted wrist flexion
- Plantar fasciitis: Heel pain worse with first steps in morning, improving with activity

**Mechanical/Postural LBP:**
- Acute or chronic LBP without radiation, clearly related to specific activities (prolonged sitting, bending, lifting)
- Segmental dysfunction without nerve compression
- Muscle strain or ligament sprain pattern

**Inflammatory Conditions (Early or Non-Erosive):**
- Rheumatoid arthritis early phase (before major erosive changes)
- Lupus-related arthritis
- Polymyalgia rheumatica

#### 2.1.4 Key Descriptors Patients Use — Natural Language Indicators

**Pain Quality:**
- "Sharp pain"
- "Achy pain"
- "Throbbing" or "pulsing" (when inflammation present)
- "Stabbing pain" when acute

**Mechanical Behavior:**
- "It hurts when I walk"
- "Pain gets worse as the day goes on"
- "It's better when I rest it"
- "Certain movements make it worse"
- "If I don't move it too much, it feels okay"
- "I avoid stairs because of the pain"
- "Heat helps" or "Ice helps"

**Temporal Pattern:**
- "Started after I fell"
- "Got worse gradually over the past year"
- "Happens when I do yard work"
- "Better on days I'm less active"

**Location:**
- "It's right in my knee"
- "The pain is in my shoulder"
- "It's in one specific spot in my lower back"

#### 2.1.5 Relationship to Imaging Findings

**Key Principle:** In nociceptive pain, there is generally a positive tissue-pain correlation. Imaging findings (X-ray, MRI, ultrasound) show structural pathology that explains the pain location and mechanical behavior.

**Typical Imaging Correlates:**
- Knee OA: Osteophytes, joint space narrowing, cartilage loss, effusion (correlates with pain in mechanical loading)
- Hip OA: Femoral head flattening, acetabular osteophytes, cartilage loss
- Lumbar/cervical OA: Disc space narrowing, osteophytes, facet hypertrophy, mild to moderate stenosis (without nerve compromise)
- Rotator cuff tendinopathy: Tendon thickening, partial-thickness tears, subacromial space narrowing
- Disc herniation with radicular pain: MRI shows disc protrusion/extrusion in anatomical alignment with clinical dermatomal distribution

**Important Caveat:** Imaging is not perfectly correlated with pain severity. A patient may have severe knee OA on X-ray with mild pain (asymptomatic OA) or moderate OA with severe pain (sensitization overlay). However, in pure nociceptive pain, imaging typically shows *some* structural finding that explains the symptom distribution.

#### 2.1.6 Prognosis Implications

**Expected Course Without Intervention:**
- Acute nociceptive pain (post-injury): Natural healing trajectory is 2–6 weeks with appropriate activity management
- Chronic nociceptive pain from degenerative conditions: Progressive without intervention (worsening OA, further joint degeneration), but pain severity may plateau or fluctuate based on inflammation and activity
- Prognosis worsens if: Fear-avoidance behaviors develop, activity restriction leads to deconditioning, patient becomes sedentary

**Prognosis with Evidence-Based Treatment (KIMI Model):**
- **Early Response (2–4 weeks):** Significant pain reduction with appropriate load management, exercise, and activity pacing; improved confidence with movement
- **Intermediate Response (4–12 weeks):** Sustained pain improvement, restoration of function, development of robust coping strategies, improved strength and endurance
- **Long-Term Prognosis:** Pain is manageable; patients develop self-management skills and continue exercise; exacerbations are less frequent and shorter-lived
- **Baseline Pain:** Many patients retain some residual baseline pain (especially in OA), but this becomes low-impact and does not limit function
- **Poor Prognostic Indicators:** High pain catastrophizing scores, severe baseline disability, multiple comorbidities, opioid dependence, depression

**Red Flags Suggesting Misclassification:**
- No pain improvement within 4 weeks of appropriate nociceptive-matched exercise
- Progressive neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness) not explained by known neuropathy
- Widespread pain pattern that expands beyond anatomically plausible distribution
- Severe fatigue, sleep disruption, mood changes out of proportion to pain severity

### 2.2 Neuropathic Pain

#### 2.2.1 Clinical Definition and Pathophysiology

**Definition (IASP 2021):** Neuropathic pain is pain caused by a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system. The pain may be peripheral (from peripheral nerve lesion/disease) or central (from spinal cord or brain lesion/disease).

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Peripheral Neuropathic Pain:** Damage to peripheral nerves (from compression, inflammation, metabolic toxicity, infection) leads to aberrant firing of nociceptors and damaged nerve fibers. These fibers generate ectopic action potentials, especially in the dorsal root ganglion and at sites of nerve injury
- **Central Sensitization Secondary to Neuropathy:** Chronic peripheral neuropathic input may trigger central sensitization (wind-up), leading to amplified pain perception and expanded pain distribution
- **Mechanism of Progression:** Initial localized nerve damage → peripheral sensitization (increased responsiveness of nociceptors near injury) → central sensitization (increased responsiveness of spinal and brain neurons) → pain that may spread beyond initial dermatome and become increasingly independent of peripheral nociceptive input
- **Timing:** Neuropathic pain may be immediate (acute compression), delayed (axonal degeneration), or chronic (persistent dysesthesia/allodynia long after initial nerve injury)

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Pain Quality:** Burning, electric, shooting, stabbing, tingling, numbness, or "pins and needles" sensation. Patients often use distinctive language: "like electricity," "like fire," "like needles," "like my foot is asleep"
- **Distribution:** Pain follows a specific anatomical nerve distribution (dermatomal for spinal nerve lesions, peripheral nerve territory for PNS lesions). Distribution is often asymmetric
- **Associated Neurological Symptoms:** Weakness, sensory loss (numbness, reduced sensation to light touch or temperature), autonomic symptoms (skin color changes, sweating changes, swelling)
- **Hyperalgesia and Allodynia:** Heightened pain response to painful stimuli (hyperalgesia) or pain response to non-painful stimuli (allodynia, e.g., pain with light touch or clothing rubbing)
- **Partial Tissue-Pain Correlation:** Pain often exists despite minimal or absent tissue inflammation; imaging may show nerve compression but pain severity is not proportionate to degree of compression; alternatively, pain may persist after structural cause resolves (e.g., post-herpetic neuralgia after shingles resolves)

#### 2.2.2 Epidemiology in Medicare MSK Population

- **Prevalence:** Estimated 7–10% of general population has neuropathic pain; prevalence increases significantly in Medicare population (15–20%) due to high rates of diabetes and age-related neuropathies
- **Most Common Neuropathic Conditions in MSK Context:**
  - Lumbar/cervical radiculopathy (nerve root compression from disc herniation, foraminal stenosis, spondylosis): 5–10% of LBP population; higher prevalence with advancing age and degenerative disease
  - Peripheral neuropathy (diabetic neuropathy most common): 30–50% of patients with diabetes; increasingly common in Medicare population
  - Carpal tunnel syndrome: 5–10% of general population; higher in women and older adults
  - Peroneal nerve compression at fibular head: Less common but important to recognize
  - Thoracic outlet syndrome: Rare; often underdiagnosed
  - Post-herpetic neuralgia: Incidence increases with age (risk >50% if herpes zoster at age 60+)
- **Gender Distribution:** Peripheral neuropathy more common in men (occupational exposure, higher diabetes prevalence historically); carpal tunnel more common in women; radiculopathy evenly distributed
- **Comorbidity Context:** Neuropathic pain in older adults often coexists with diabetes, chemotherapy history, and other conditions affecting nerves; polypharmacy may mask or amplify neuropathic symptoms

#### 2.2.3 Typical Conditions Presenting with Neuropathic Phenotype

**Spinal Nerve Root Compression (Radiculopathy):**
- **Lumbar radiculopathy:** L5 or S1 nerve root compression from disc herniation, spondylosis, or stenosis; pain radiates down posterior/lateral leg into foot; may be unilateral
- **Cervical radiculopathy:** C5, C6, C7, or C8 nerve root compression; pain radiates down arm into hand
- **Thoracic radiculopathy:** Less common; lateral chest or torso pain in dermatomal distribution
- **Cauda equina syndrome (red flag):** Bilateral lumbar radiculopathy with bowel/bladder involvement; surgical emergency

**Peripheral Nerve Compression:**
- **Carpal tunnel syndrome:** Median nerve compression at wrist; pain/numbness in thumb, index, middle finger; worse at night; pain with repetitive gripping
- **Cubital tunnel syndrome:** Ulnar nerve compression at elbow; pain/numbness in ring and pinky finger; worse with elbow flexion
- **Peroneal nerve compression:** Common fibular nerve compression at fibular head; foot drop, anterior leg pain/numbness
- **Tarsal tunnel syndrome:** Posterior tibial nerve compression at medial ankle; burning pain in sole of foot

**Distal Polyneuropathy:**
- **Diabetic peripheral neuropathy:** Distal, symmetrical, length-dependent; typically starts in feet; stocking-glove distribution of sensory loss
- **Non-diabetic peripheral neuropathy:** Idiopathic, toxic (alcohol, chemotherapy), metabolic (B12 deficiency), infectious (HIV, Lyme)
- **Hyperalgesia and allodynia:** Pain response to light touch, pressure, or temperature changes

**Other Neuropathic Conditions:**
- **Post-herpetic neuralgia:** Pain persisting >3 months after herpes zoster (shingles) rash resolves; dermatomal distribution
- **Complex regional pain syndrome:** Pain, swelling, and skin color/temperature changes after injury (fracture, surgery); often with allodynia
- **Phantom limb pain:** Pain in absent limb after amputation; less common in KIMI population but important to recognize
- **Trigeminal neuralgia:** Facial pain in trigeminal nerve distribution; sudden, severe, electric shock sensation; less common in MSK context

#### 2.2.4 Key Descriptors Patients Use — Natural Language Indicators

**Pain Quality:**
- "Burning pain"
- "Like electricity" or "electrical pain"
- "Shooting pain"
- "Sharp, stabbing pain"
- "Like needles" or "pins and needles"
- "Tingling"
- "Numb feeling"
- "Like my leg is asleep"
- "Radiating pain"

**Distribution:**
- "Pain shoots down my leg"
- "Pain goes into my foot"
- "Numbness in my fingers"
- "Tingling in my toes"
- "Pain follows a line down my arm"
- "Pain in a band around my ribs"

**Associated Symptoms:**
- "My leg feels weak"
- "I feel like I'm dropping things"
- "The foot is numb, I can't feel the floor"
- "I'm tripping over my own feet"
- "My toes are tingling constantly"
- "I can't feel temperature changes"
- "The skin looks darker/redder in that area"

**Neurological Descriptors:**
- "Numbness that won't go away"
- "Like something is pinched"
- "Nerve pain"
- "Radiating down the leg"

#### 2.2.5 Relationship to Imaging Findings

**Key Principle:** Neuropathic pain may have clear imaging correlates (nerve compression on MRI) OR imaging findings may be minimal/absent despite significant neuropathic symptoms.

**Imaging Correlates When Present:**
- **Radiculopathy:** MRI shows disc herniation, spondylotic osteophytes, or stenosis at level corresponding to dermatomal pain distribution and neurological findings (weakness, reflex loss, sensory loss)
- **Carpal tunnel:** Ultrasound shows median nerve swelling/thickening at wrist; MRI may show same; imaging correlates with clinical severity
- **Peroneal nerve compression:** MRI or ultrasound shows nerve swelling at fibular head; may show cause (ganglion cyst, mass)
- **Diabetic neuropathy:** No structural imaging finding; diagnosis is clinical (distal sensory loss, reduced reflexes) and electrophysiologic (EMG/NCS abnormalities)

**Imaging Discordance (Common):**
- Patient has severe neuropathic pain with minimal imaging findings: May indicate early neuropathy, small nerve fibers not visible on standard imaging, or significant central sensitization overlay
- Patient has impressive imaging finding (large disc herniation) but minimal or no neuropathic symptoms: May indicate compression without significant nerve dysfunction, or nerve compression that has resolved clinically while imaging abnormality persists

**Electrodiagnostic Testing (EMG/NCS):**
- EMG/NCS is more specific than imaging for neuropathic pain; can document peripheral and central conduction abnormalities
- KIMI does not order EMG/NCS routinely but may recommend if diagnosis is unclear or red flags present

#### 2.2.6 Prognosis Implications

**Expected Course Without Intervention:**
- **Acute nerve compression (e.g., acute radiculopathy from disc herniation):** Natural history is favorable; 70–80% of radiculopathy resolves spontaneously within 6–12 weeks as nerve edema resolves and patient adapts movement patterns
- **Chronic neuropathy (e.g., diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia):** Pain may be persistent and progressive without intervention; disability increases as sensory loss worsens (risk of falls, foot ulcers in diabetic neuropathy)
- **Nerve compression that persists (e.g., untreated carpal tunnel):** Progressive nerve damage with worsening sensory loss and eventual motor weakness if not addressed

**Prognosis with Evidence-Based Treatment (KIMI Model):**
- **Acute Neuropathy (Radiculopathy):** Expected pain improvement in 2–6 weeks with activity modification, neuromotor re-education, and centralization exercises; neurological recovery (strength, sensation) may take 4–12 weeks
- **Chronic Neuropathy:** Slower trajectory; pain stabilization and mild improvement over 8–12 weeks; goal is to prevent progression, reduce disability, and improve function despite residual symptoms
- **Underlying Cause Resolution:** If cause can be removed (e.g., surgery for carpal tunnel), rapid improvement is expected post-intervention; if cause cannot be resolved (e.g., diabetes), focus shifts to symptom management and prevention of complications
- **Central Sensitization Overlay:** If patient has developed secondary nociplastic features (widespread pain, catastrophizing, sleep disruption), prognosis becomes more complex; dual phenotype treatment required
- **Good Prognostic Indicators:** Early intervention, clear imaging/electrodiagnostic findings, high patient motivation, ability to tolerate exercise, no significant nociplastic overlay
- **Poor Prognostic Indicators:** Long symptom duration (>6 months), significant sensory loss, progressive weakness, severe opioid use, significant depression/anxiety

**Red Flags Suggesting Serious Pathology:**
- Rapidly progressive weakness (possible surgical emergency, e.g., cauda equina)
- Loss of bowel/bladder control (cauda equina syndrome, red flag)
- Severe unilateral neurological deficit with minimal imaging findings (possible nerve infarct, malignancy)
- Expanding distribution of pain beyond single dermatome (possible multiple nerve involvement, systemic disease)
- Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, night sweats) suggesting infection or malignancy

### 2.3 Nociplastic Pain (Central Sensitization)

#### 2.3.1 Clinical Definition and Pathophysiology

**Definition:** Nociplastic pain is pain caused by altered nociception (heightened sensitivity to pain) without clear evidence of tissue damage or nervous system lesion causing that pain. The nervous system's pain amplification systems are "turned up," leading to pain that is disproportionate to any structural or functional abnormality (Nijs et al. 2021).

**Pathophysiology:**
- **Central Sensitization:** A state of heightened responsiveness of neurons in the central nervous system (spinal cord, brainstem, cortex) to nociceptive input. Low-level pain signals are amplified into high-level pain perception
- **Descending Inhibitory Dysfunction:** Failure of the brain's natural pain-dampening (inhibitory) pathways to suppress pain signals; the endogenous opioid and monoamine (serotonin, norepinephrine) systems are underactive
- **Loss of Pain Gating:** Normal gating mechanisms that filter out low-level pain signals are dysfunctional; trivial sensations become painful (allodynia); normal painful stimuli are perceived as more severe (hyperalgesia)
- **Temporal Summation:** Repeated painful stimuli lead to progressively greater pain perception (wind-up); a third-degree burn does not hurt more than a first-degree burn, but in central sensitization, repeated inputs lead to amplified output
- **Triggers and Perpetuators:** Stress, poor sleep, inactivity, trauma, catastrophizing thoughts, mood disorders, and chronic pain from any cause can trigger and maintain central sensitization
- **Neurobiological Markers (Research):** Decreased dopamine/serotonin (linked to low mood, fatigue), decreased gray matter volume in pain-processing areas (brain), elevated inflammatory markers (cytokines), altered heart rate variability (autonomic dysfunction), elevated cortisol (stress response dysregulation)

**Key Distinction from Nociceptive/Neuropathic:**
- **Nociceptive pain:** Tissue-driven; more tissue damage = more pain; pain improves as tissue heals
- **Neuropathic pain:** Nerve-driven; pain from abnormal nerve firing; improves with nerve healing or pain-dampening treatments
- **Nociplastic pain:** Processing-driven; pain from brain/spinal cord amplification; improves with central desensitization, movement, sleep, stress reduction, and cognitive reframing

#### 2.3.2 Epidemiology in Medicare MSK Population

- **Prevalence:** Estimated 5–10% of population has primary nociplastic pain disorder (fibromyalgia, central sensitization syndrome); but secondary central sensitization features (partial nociplastic phenotype) are present in 40–60% of patients with chronic MSK pain, especially those with high pain catastrophizing, depression, or multiple pain sites
- **Central Sensitization Spectrum Disorders:**
  - Fibromyalgia: Prevalence 2–4% of general population; 90% female; typically onset in 30s–50s but present in older adults
  - Chronic widespread pain (CWP): Broader category including fibromyalgia and pain without full fibromyalgia criteria; prevalence 10–15%
  - Other central sensitization spectrum conditions: Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), tension headache/migraine, temporomandibular disorder (TMJ), myofascial pain syndrome, chronic fatigue syndrome/ME
- **Comorbidity Context:** Central sensitization spectrum disorders frequently co-occur; a patient with fibromyalgia often has IBS and migraines; a patient with chronic LBP and high central sensitization may also have anxiety, depression, sleep disorder
- **Gender Distribution:** Fibromyalgia and chronic widespread pain predominantly affect women (90% female for fibromyalgia); clinical presentation is similar in men but often delayed diagnosis
- **Age and Onset:** Fibromyalgia typically onsets in middle age but can present in older adults; older adults may attribute diffuse pain to "normal aging"
- **Chronicity:** By definition, nociplastic pain is chronic (>3 months); often present for years or decades before correct diagnosis

#### 2.3.3 Typical Conditions Presenting with Nociplastic Phenotype

**Primary Central Sensitization Disorders:**
- **Fibromyalgia:** Widespread pain (at least 7 body regions or widespread pain index ≥5), associated fatigue, sleep disturbance, cognitive dysfunction; often with mood disturbance (depression, anxiety)
- **Chronic Widespread Pain (CWP):** Pain in multiple body regions (e.g., neck, shoulders, low back, knees) without meeting full fibromyalgia criteria but with functional overlap
- **Central Sensitization Syndrome:** Broader diagnosis encompassing fibromyalgia and related conditions; characterized by widespread pain, fatigue, sleep disruption, mood changes, autonomic dysfunction

**Secondary Nociplastic Features Overlaid on Nociceptive/Neuropathic Pain:**
- **Chronic mechanical LBP with sensitization:** Initial mechanical LBP that has become chronic with development of widespread pain, catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, fatigue, and sleep disruption; may have moved from single-site pain to multi-site pain
- **Generalized OA:** Patient with multiple joint OA (knees, hips, hands, spine) where overall pain severity is disproportionate to imaging findings; accompanied by widespread pain sensitivity, fatigue, mood disturbance
- **Widespread pain post-injury:** Initial localized pain (from accident, surgery, or injury) that progresses to widespread pain with central sensitization features
- **Post-viral chronic pain:** Pain and fatigue persisting long after viral illness (long COVID, post-Lyme disease, post-mononucleosis); central sensitization triggered by viral infection

**Associated Conditions (Central Sensitization Spectrum):**
- **Tension headache/migraine:** Often coexists with fibromyalgia; may be driven by central sensitization
- **Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS):** Gut pain from heightened visceral pain sensitivity; common in fibromyalgia and chronic pain population
- **Temporomandibular disorder (TMJ):** Jaw pain often with central sensitization features; frequently coexists with fibromyalgia
- **Tension myofascial pain:** Muscle pain and stiffness throughout the body; often triggered by stress and anxiety in central sensitization

#### 2.3.5 Relationship to Imaging Findings

**Key Principle:** Nociplastic pain is characterized by a POOR or ABSENT tissue-pain correlation. Imaging findings are either minimal or inconsistent with reported pain severity.

**Typical Imaging Patterns:**
- **Normal or near-normal imaging:** MRI, X-ray, or other imaging shows no significant structural abnormality despite severe pain complaints
- **Minor imaging findings inconsistent with pain severity:** Small disc bulge without stenosis, mild OA changes, minor muscle strain—findings that do not explain the severity and distribution of pain
- **Widespread imaging findings that are non-specific:** Mild OA in multiple joints, general mild degenerative changes; findings are non-specific and could be found in many asymptomatic older adults
- **Imaging findings that do not explain symptom distribution:** Localized imaging abnormality (e.g., small disc herniation at L5 level) but patient reports widespread body pain including regions far from the imaging finding

**No Structural Cause Identified:**
- Comprehensive imaging (MRI of spine, hips, shoulders; X-rays of multiple joints) is normal or shows only age-appropriate changes
- Lab work is normal (ESR, CRP, rheumatology panel negative)
- Electrodiagnostic testing (if done) is normal
- Physical exam findings are inconsistent or non-anatomical (patient reports severe pain on gentle palpation but normal neurological exam; pain does not follow dermatomal or peripheral nerve distribution)

**Implication for KIMI Care:**
- Absence of imaging findings does NOT rule out a serious diagnosis; however, in context of widespread pain, fatigue, sleep disruption, and mood changes, nociplastic pain is the most likely mechanism
- Treatment approach differs dramatically: imaging-based (surgery, injections) interventions are unlikely to help; behavioral, movement, and sleep-based interventions are most effective

#### 2.3.6 Prognosis Implications

**Natural History Without Intervention:**
- **Untreated central sensitization tends to worsen or stabilize at high disability levels.** Pain may spread to additional body regions; fatigue deepens; mood and sleep disturbances may progress to clinical depression and insomnia
- **Trajectory varies:** Some patients have fluctuating symptoms (good weeks, bad weeks); others have progressive worsening; some plateau at moderate disability
- **Negative cycle:** Inactivity due to pain → deconditioning → increased fatigue and pain sensitivity; catastrophizing → fear-avoidance → inactivity; sleep disruption → lower pain threshold; mood disturbance → more pain amplification
- **Prognosis without correct diagnosis:** Patients often pursue imaging-based or surgical interventions that do not address central sensitization and may reinforce pain beliefs and disability

**Prognosis with Evidence-Based Multimodal Treatment (KIMI Model):**
- **Early Response (2–4 weeks):** Improved sleep and mood; modest pain reduction; increased confidence with movement and activity; reduced catastrophizing
- **Intermediate Response (4–12 weeks):** Significant pain reduction (30–50%); improved fatigue and sleep; marked improvement in function and quality of life; patients report "brain fog" clearing
- **Long-Term Prognosis (12+ weeks):** Pain continues to improve; most patients achieve substantial functional improvement and reduced disability; pain often becomes background with manageable flares; patients develop robust self-management skills
- **Treatment Response Variability:** 50–70% of patients with nociplastic pain respond very well to exercise and behavioral interventions; 20–30% show moderate improvement; 10–20% show minimal response (often due to unaddressed depression, ongoing trauma, or secondary gains)

**Good Prognostic Indicators:**
- Early intervention before severe deconditioning develops
- High initial motivation and engagement with treatment
- Stable housing and social support
- Absence of active psychiatric comorbidity (though depression/anxiety responsive to treatment is not a poor prognostic sign)
- Willingness to adopt exercise and behavioral changes
- Low baseline opioid use (opioids can amplify central sensitization)

**Poor Prognostic Indicators:**
- Long symptom duration (>5 years) with entrenched pain beliefs and disability
- Severe depression or untreated mental health comorbidity
- Active trauma or ongoing psychosocial stress
- High opioid dependence
- Significant cognitive impairment limiting ability to engage with behavioral interventions
- Medico-legal proceedings or ongoing litigation (secondary gain, poor motivation to improve)
- Severe deconditioning with multiple comorbidities limiting exercise tolerance

**Expected Pain Improvement Trajectory (Important for Patient Expectations):**
- Nociceptive pain: Often 50%+ improvement in first 4 weeks; further improvement to 70–90% over 2–3 months
- Neuropathic pain: Slower initial improvement; 20–30% improvement in first 4 weeks; progressive improvement over 3–6 months
- Nociplastic pain: Slowest initial response; may see only 10–20% improvement first 4 weeks; but sustained improvement trajectory continues over 6–12 weeks; final improvement often greater than initial improvement

## 3. KIMI's Pain Phenotype Screening Protocol

### 3.1 Overview and Conversational Approach

KIMI's pain phenotype screening is **conversational, not interrogatory.** Rather than administering a formal questionnaire, KIMI gathers information through natural dialogue, explaining why each question matters to the patient's care plan.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Transparency:** Explain to the patient that understanding their pain type will guide which treatments will help most
2. **Pacing:** Screen for pain phenotype early in the intake but not rushing; allow natural flow of conversation
3. **Validation:** Affirm that all pain is real, regardless of whether tissue damage is visible
4. **Open-Ended First, Specific Follow-Up:** Ask open questions first ("Tell me about your pain"), then follow up with specific clarifying questions
5. **Avoid Leading Questions:** Do not suggest answers; listen to patient's own descriptors
6. **Document Exact Language:** Note the patient's exact words (e.g., "burning like fire," "sharp when I bend," "exhausted no matter how much I sleep")

### 3.2 Nociceptive Pain Screening

#### 3.2.1 Screening Questions and Clinical Interpretation

**Opening Question:**
"Tell me about where your pain is and what makes it better or worse."

**Patient Response Patterns Indicating Nociceptive Pain:**

| Patient Utterance | Clinical Interpretation | Follow-Up Question |
|---|---|---|
| "My knee hurts when I walk and stairs are the worst. Resting helps." | Clear mechanical pattern with predictable triggers and relief | "Does the pain feel localized to the knee, or does it spread to other areas?" |
| "Sharp pain in my shoulder. Can't reach overhead." | Localized mechanical pain with specific movement limitation | "Is the pain only in the shoulder, or does it go down your arm?" |
| "My lower back is sore from lifting something heavy last week." | Acute onset with clear mechanical trigger | "Is the pain getting better as the week goes on?" |
| "Heat helps my stiffness" or "Ice helps with the swelling" | Pain is responsive to physical modalities (nociceptive hallmark) | "What helps most—rest, ice, heat, or movement?" |
| "It's worse at the end of the day after I've been active." | Mechanical pattern: worse with activity load, better with rest | "When you rest, does the pain go away?" |
| "I can feel it's swollen/puffy" | Patient reports inflammation (suggests nociceptive/inflammatory) | "Is the swelling different on days when pain is worse?" |

**Nociceptive Screening Question Series:**

**Q1: Pain Location Specificity**
"Is your pain in one specific area, like your knee or your shoulder, or is it spread throughout your body?"

- **Nociceptive indicates:** Single region or clearly localized areas (knee, shoulder, lower back)
- **Red flag for nociplastic:** "All over" or multiple regions far apart (knees AND shoulders AND low back AND neck)

**Q2: Mechanical Behavior**
"Does your pain change based on what you do? For example, is it worse with certain movements or activities, and does rest help?"

- **Nociceptive indicates:** Clear mechanical pattern ("Worse when I go up stairs," "Better when I rest," "Worse at end of the day," "Better in the morning")
- **Red flag for nociplastic:** Unpredictable pattern ("No pattern really," "Just hurts all the time," "Hard to predict what makes it worse")

**Q3: Onset and Trigger**
"Did something specific happen when this pain started? An injury, fall, or activity you don't usually do?"

- **Nociceptive indicates:** Clear precipitating event (fall, overuse, known injury) or gradual progression with known condition (OA)
- **Red flag for nociplastic:** Vague or absent trigger ("It just started," "No clear reason," "Happened out of nowhere")

**Q4: Tissue-Level Pathology Awareness**
"Has your doctor told you about any structural problems like arthritis, a disc issue, or tendon problem in that area?"

- **Nociceptive indicates:** Patients often are aware of structural findings or know the name of their condition (OA, tendinopathy, herniation)
- **Note:** Absence of imaging findings does not rule out nociceptive pain, but awareness of structural pathology suggests nociceptive

**Q5: Response to Physical Modalities**
"What helps your pain? Does ice, heat, rest, bracing, or massage help?"

- **Nociceptive indicates:** Clear response to physical modalities ("Ice reduces the swelling," "Heat loosens it up," "Resting helps a lot")
- **Red flag for nociplastic:** Nothing helps consistently ("Nothing really helps," "Helps temporarily but comes right back")

**Scoring Interpretation:**
- **3–5 responses consistent with nociceptive:** Likely nociceptive-dominant phenotype; confidence level = "definite nociceptive"
- **2–3 responses consistent with nociceptive:** Probable nociceptive; may have minor nociplastic features; confidence = "probable nociceptive"
- **Fewer than 2 nociceptive responses:** Consider neuropathic or nociplastic screening

#### 3.2.2 Handling Ambiguous Nociceptive Responses

**Scenario 1: Patient reports single-area pain that is sometimes mechanical, sometimes not**
"My knee pain is usually worse with stairs, but sometimes it hurts just sitting. And some days it's fine."

*Interpretation:* Possible nociceptive-dominant with nociplastic overlay (fluctuating pain, pain at rest)

*Follow-up:* "On days when the pain hurts while sitting, do you notice anything else? Like poor sleep the night before, or feeling stressed or more tired than usual?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* If sleep/stress/mood clearly predict non-mechanical pain, suggests nociplastic overlay that will need addressing alongside nociceptive treatment

**Scenario 2: Patient is very unclear about mechanical pattern**
"I'm not really sure what makes it worse. Maybe activity, maybe not."

*Interpretation:* Insufficient clarity to classify nociceptive; consider pain catastrophizing or depressive cognition affecting ability to discern patterns

*Follow-up:* "Let's think about the last week. Was there a day when the pain was better? What were you doing that day? And a day when it was worse?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Sometimes structured reflection helps; if still unable to identify pattern, move toward nociplastic screening

**Scenario 3: Patient reports localized pain that worsens with minimal activity**
"I just walked 10 minutes and my knee pain flared up for days."

*Interpretation:* Could be nociceptive (mechanical sensitivity) or nociplastic (pain amplification/disproportionate response)

*Follow-up:* "Is the pain directly from the walk, or does the pain get worse over the days after? And does anything else change—sleep, fatigue, mood—when you have a flare like this?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* If pain is directly from activity and improves in 24–48 hours = nociceptive; if pain lasts days and is accompanied by fatigue/sleep disruption/mood change = nociplastic overlay

### 3.3 Neuropathic Pain Screening

#### 3.3.1 Screening Questions and Clinical Interpretation

**Opening Question (if not already covered):**
"Besides the aching or soreness, do you have any other sensations in that area? Like tingling, numbness, or a feeling like electricity?"

**Patient Response Patterns Indicating Neuropathic Pain:**

| Patient Utterance | Clinical Interpretation | Follow-Up Question |
|---|---|---|
| "Burning pain down my leg" | Classic neuropathic quality descriptor | "Does it follow a line down, or is it spread out?" |
| "My foot feels like it's on fire" | Burning is hallmark neuropathic descriptor | "Is the numbness constant, or does it come and go?" |
| "Sharp, shooting pain in my buttock going down the leg" | Radicular pain distribution with shooting quality | "Does the pain go into the foot, or stop at the knee?" |
| "Tingling and pins and needles in my fingers" | Paresthesia = neuropathic hallmark | "Is it just tingling, or is there actual numbness?" |
| "Feels like electricity" or "Electric shock feeling" | Distinctive neuropathic descriptor | "Does the electricity happen repeatedly?" |
| "Numb feeling like my foot is asleep" | Paresthesia = neuropathic | "Is the numbness constant or intermittent?" |
| "My leg feels weak" or "I'm dropping things" | Motor neuropathic symptoms | "Is the weakness constant, or worse with activity?" |

**Neuropathic Screening Question Series:**

**Q1: Pain Quality Descriptors**
"How would you describe the pain? Is it sharp, achy, burning, electric, tingling, or something else?"

- **Neuropathic indicates:** Burning, electric, shooting, tingling, "pins and needles," numb, "like needles," "like my foot is asleep," radiating
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Sharp, achy, throbbing, soreness, stiffness
- **Note:** Patients may use multiple descriptors; neuropathic quality + dermatomal distribution = strong neuropathic signal

**Q2: Distribution Pattern**
"Does the pain follow a path? For example, does it go down one leg, into one arm, or around your rib cage? Or is it spread all over?"

- **Neuropathic indicates:** Follows anatomical line (down leg, into arm, around ribs, down side of face); often unilateral
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Localized to one joint/area (knee, shoulder) but not following a line down a limb
- **Nociplastic indicates:** Widespread, no particular pattern

**Q3: Neurological Symptoms**
"Do you have any weakness? Drop things? Trip? Any numbness or loss of sensation, like you can't feel temperature changes or light touch?"

- **Neuropathic indicates:** "My leg is weak," "I drop things," "I trip over my feet," "Foot is numb, I can't feel the floor," "Can't tell hot from cold"
- **Nociceptive indicates:** No associated neurological symptoms
- **Note:** Presence of weakness suggests moderate to severe neuropathy; may need neurological exam or imaging

**Q4: Duration and Constancy**
"Has this burning/tingling/numbness been going on for weeks or months? Is it constant or does it come and go?"

- **Neuropathic indicates:** Often chronic (>3 months); constant or frequent; may be progressive
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Variable, often improves with rest or activity modification
- **Note:** Acute onset of burning/shooting pain = possible acute nerve compression (radiculopathy); chronic = peripheral neuropathy or chronic compression

**Q5: Associated Conditions**
"Do you have diabetes, have you had shingles (chickenpox), chemotherapy, or spine surgery in the past?"

- **Neuropathic indicates:** History of condition that damages nerves (diabetes, herpes zoster, chemotherapy, spine surgery, Lyme disease, alcohol use)
- **Nociceptive indicates:** No clear neuropathy risk factors
- **Note:** Ask this tactfully; not all neuropathies have clear prior history

**Using Simplified PainDETECT (Conversational Adaptation):**

KIMI uses select items from PainDETECT (a validated screening tool) delivered conversationally:

1. **Burning Quality:** "Does the pain burn?"
2. **Tingling/Prickling:** "Do you feel tingling or prickling sensations?"
3. **Electric Shocks:** "Does the pain feel like electric shocks?"
4. **Numbness:** "Do you have numbness in the painful area?"
5. **Skin Changes:** "Does the skin in the painful area look different—color, texture, swelling, or temperature changes?"
6. **Pain in Different Areas:** "Does the pain spread to other places as well?"
7. **Temperature Sensation:** "Can you tell the difference between hot and cold in the painful area?"

*Scoring for Simplified PainDETECT:*
- 1 point for each "yes" response
- **Score 3–4:** Possible neuropathic pain
- **Score 5–7:** Probable neuropathic pain
- **Score 7+:** Likely neuropathic pain

*Interpretation:*
- Probable or likely neuropathic pain warrants imaging and possible electrodiagnostic testing
- Possible neuropathic pain warrants follow-up screening and consideration of other phenotypes

#### 3.3.2 Handling Ambiguous Neuropathic Responses

**Scenario 1: Patient reports radiating pain but unclear if dermatomal**
"Pain goes down my leg when I'm active."

*Interpretation:* Could be radiating nociceptive pain (referred pain) or true radiculopathy

*Follow-up:* "When you say down your leg, does it follow a specific line—like the outside of your leg, or the back, or the inner side? Or is it all over the leg?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Dermatomal pattern (follows one specific line) suggests radiculopathy; diffuse down-the-leg pain suggests referred pain from hip or low back (nociceptive)

**Scenario 2: Patient reports tingling without clear distribution**
"My legs feel tingly sometimes."

*Interpretation:* Could be peripheral neuropathy (stocking-glove distribution) or central sensitization

*Follow-up:* "When the tingling happens, where exactly? Does it start in your feet and go up to your knees, or is it all over your legs?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Distal, stocking-glove distribution suggests peripheral neuropathy; widespread, non-specific distribution suggests central sensitization

**Scenario 3: Patient reports numbness without clear cause**
"I have numbness in my fingers."

*Interpretation:* Could be carpal tunnel, cervical radiculopathy, peripheral neuropathy, or anxiety/hyperventilation

*Follow-up:* "Which fingers? Thumb and index? All of them? When does it happen—when you're using your hands, at night, or all the time? Any weakness?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Thumb, index, middle finger (median nerve territory) suggests carpal tunnel; all fingers or hand weakness suggests cervical pathology or systemic neuropathy

**Scenario 4: Patient reports mixed neuropathic and nociceptive descriptors**
"My shoulder has an achy, sharp pain with some tingling down my arm."

*Interpretation:* Mixed nociceptive-neuropathic phenotype likely

*Follow-up:* "Is the achy part in the shoulder and the tingling down the arm separate, or together? Does the achy part get worse with shoulder movement?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* If shoulder component is mechanical and arm component is neurological = mixed phenotype (treat both); if both are from nerve compression = neuropathic-dominant

### 3.4 Nociplastic Pain (Central Sensitization) Screening

#### 3.4.1 Screening Questions and Clinical Interpretation

**Opening Question (if not already covered):**
"Beyond the pain, how are your energy levels? Your sleep? Your mood?"

**Patient Response Patterns Indicating Nociplastic Pain:**

| Patient Utterance | Clinical Interpretation | Follow-Up Question |
|---|---|---|
| "My whole body aches" or "All-over pain" | Widespread pain is hallmark of central sensitization | "How many different areas would you say hurt? Any areas that don't hurt?" |
| "I'm exhausted no matter how much I sleep" | Disproportionate fatigue suggests nociplastic | "How many hours do you sleep? Do you wake up refreshed?" |
| "Even clothes touching my skin hurt" or "Tender everywhere" | Allodynia = heightened pain sensitivity | "Does light touch hurt, or just pressure?" |
| "The pain is way out of proportion to what I did" | Disproportionality = nociplastic hallmark | "Can you give me an example of something small that caused major pain?" |
| "I can't concentrate" or "Brain fog" or "Can't remember things" | Cognitive symptoms are associated with central sensitization | "How long has the brain fog been happening?" |
| "I'm anxious about everything" or "I worry all the time" | Anxiety and catastrophizing are perpetuators of central sensitization | "Do you worry the pain means something serious?" |
| "My sleep is terrible" or "I wake up constantly" | Non-restorative sleep is common in nociplastic pain | "When you sleep, do you feel rested?" |
| "Stress makes the pain so much worse" | Stress sensitivity = nociplastic feature | "Can you predict when your pain will be worse based on stress?" |

**Nociplastic Screening Question Series:**

**Q1: Widespread Pain Distribution**
"How many different body parts hurt? Can you name them for me?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** 5+ regions or whole-body pain ("knees, hips, shoulders, neck, hands, feet"); widespread and often symmetrical
- **Nociceptive indicates:** 1–2 localized regions
- **Neuropathic indicates:** Specific dermatomal or nerve distribution
- **Simplified Widespread Pain Index (WPI):** Count regions with pain (0–19 scale; ≥7 suggests widespread pain). Do not need to be exhaustive; if patient clearly describes widespread pain, document as such.

**Q2: Fatigue and Energy**
"How is your energy level? Do you feel tired or exhausted most days?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** "Exhausted all the time," "No energy," "Fatigue worse than pain," "Sleep 10 hours and still tired"
- **Note:** Fatigue is a core feature of fibromyalgia and nociplastic pain; often out of proportion to activity level

*Follow-up:* "Does rest or sleep help the fatigue?"
- **Nociplastic indicates:** "No, I wake up just as tired," "Sleep doesn't help," "I wake up more exhausted"
- **Nociceptive indicates:** "Rest helps," "I'm tired from not doing things; when I'm active I feel better"

**Q3: Sleep Quality**
"How is your sleep? Do you feel rested when you wake up?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** Fragmented sleep ("Wake up multiple times"), non-restorative ("Sleep 8 hours but don't feel rested"), associated with vivid dreams or nightmares
- **Note:** Sleep disturbance is nearly universal in nociplastic pain; addressing sleep is critical to treatment

*Follow-up:* "When you wake up in the morning, how do you feel? Stiff and sore?"
- **Nociplastic indicates:** Morning stiffness and fatigue
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Stiffness that improves with activity

**Q4: Disproportionality**
"Looking back at your doctor visits, what did your imaging or exam show? Does the pain match what the doctor found?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** "My imaging looks pretty normal but I'm in severe pain," "Mild arthritis but pain is severe," "Doctor says there's not much wrong but I'm in so much pain"
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Pain proportionate to imaging findings
- **Neuropathic indicates:** Nerve compression on imaging or clear neurological findings

**Q5: Pain Sensitivity and Allodynia**
"Does light pressure hurt? Does your skin feel sensitive or tender all over?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** "Even clothes hurt," "Light touch is painful," "Tenderness everywhere," "Can't tolerate massage"
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Tenderness localized to injury site
- **Neuropathic indicates:** Allodynia in specific dermatomal/nerve distribution

**Q6: Mood, Anxiety, and Catastrophizing**
"How is your mood? Do you feel anxious or worry about your pain?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** "Anxious all the time," "Worry the pain means something serious," "Feel depressed," "Catastrophize"
- **Note:** Mood disturbance is associated with and perpetuates central sensitization; mood improvement is part of treatment

*Follow-up:* "When you have pain, what goes through your mind? Do you worry it might be something serious or get worse?"
- **Nociplastic indicates:** High catastrophizing ("This is awful," "I'll never get better," "Something is seriously wrong")
- **Nociceptive indicates:** Lower catastrophizing; more problem-focused thinking

**Q7: Cognitive Symptoms**
"Do you experience brain fog, memory problems, or difficulty concentrating?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** "Brain fog is constant," "Can't concentrate," "Memory problems," "Confusion sometimes"
- **Note:** "Fibro fog" is a recognized symptom in fibromyalgia; improves with treatment

**Q8: Central Sensitization Spectrum History**
"Have you ever been diagnosed with fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), tension headaches/migraines, or temporomandibular disorder (jaw pain)?"

- **Nociplastic indicates:** History of one or more conditions on the central sensitization spectrum
- **Note:** These conditions frequently co-occur and share underlying central sensitization mechanism

**Using Simplified Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI) - Conversational Adaptation:**

KIMI uses the most discriminating items from the validated CSI delivered conversationally. Standard CSI has 25 items; KIMI uses 7–9 most relevant items:

1. **Unexplained Widespread Pain:** "Do you have pain in 5 or more body areas?" (If yes = 1 point)
2. **Muscle Tension:** "Do your muscles feel tense much of the time?" (Yes = 1)
3. **Fatigue:** "Do you feel exhausted much of the time, even after adequate sleep?" (Yes = 1)
4. **Non-Restorative Sleep:** "Does sleep not leave you feeling rested?" (Yes = 1)
5. **Difficulty With Concentration:** "Do you have difficulty concentrating or 'brain fog'?" (Yes = 1)
6. **Sensitivity to Light/Sound/Smell:** "Are you unusually sensitive to light, sounds, or strong smells?" (Yes = 1)
7. **Temperature Sensitivity:** "Do you feel unusually sensitive to temperature changes?" (Yes = 1)
8. **Anxiety/Worry:** "Do you feel anxious or worry excessively?" (Yes = 1)
9. **Memory Problems:** "Do you have memory problems or difficulty remembering things?" (Yes = 1)

*Scoring for Simplified CSI (9-item version):*
- Sum yes responses (0–9 points)
- **CSI Score ≥5:** Moderate central sensitization features
- **CSI Score ≥7:** High central sensitization features; strong evidence for nociplastic phenotype
- **CSI Score <5 with widespread pain:** Possible nociplastic; may need nociceptive/neuropathic screening

#### 3.4.2 Handling Ambiguous Nociplastic Responses

**Scenario 1: Patient reports fatigue but says pain is localized**
"I'm exhausted all the time, but the pain is mainly in my knee."

*Interpretation:* Could be nociplastic with localized pain site, or nociceptive knee pain with secondary depression/fatigue

*Follow-up:* "Besides the knee, do other parts of your body hurt? And is the fatigue the kind where rest doesn't help, or do you think it's from being inactive?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* If multiple pain sites + non-restorative sleep + mood changes + low mood-related activity restriction = nociplastic; if fatigue is secondary to activity restriction from knee pain and improves with increased activity = nociceptive

**Scenario 2: Patient minimizes widespread pain**
"I guess I have aches in several places, but the real pain is my low back."

*Interpretation:* May downplay secondary pain sites; still meet criteria for widespread pain

*Follow-up:* "When I ask about pain in different areas, where else does it hurt besides your back? Neck? Shoulders? Knees?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Some patients focus on primary pain site and minimize secondary aches; systematic inquiry reveals actual widespread pattern

**Scenario 3: Patient attributes fatigue to other causes**
"I'm tired because I can't sleep due to pain."

*Interpretation:* Fatigue may be secondary to sleep disruption (nociceptive mechanism) or primary (nociplastic central fatigue)

*Follow-up:* "Even on nights when you sleep 7–8 hours, do you wake up rested? Or do you wake up feeling like you never slept?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* If sleep deprivation explains fatigue, improve sleep and fatigue should improve; if non-restorative sleep persists despite adequate sleep duration = central sensitization

**Scenario 4: Patient has high anxiety but attributes it to pain**
"I worry about my pain all the time."

*Interpretation:* Could be secondary anxiety from fear-avoidance, or primary anxiety contributing to pain amplification

*Follow-up:* "Was the anxiety there before the pain started, or did the pain trigger it? Are there other things you worry about?"

*Clinical Reasoning:* Pre-existing anxiety that is exacerbated by pain = need anxiety treatment alongside pain treatment; anxiety as primary feature of nociplastic phenotype

## 4. Validated Screening Instruments

### 4.1 PainDETECT (Douleur Neuropathique 4 Questions, DN4)

**Purpose:** Screen for neuropathic pain features; helps distinguish neuropathic from nociceptive pain

**Full PainDETECT (12-item):**
1. Burning pain (yes/no)
2. Tingling, prickling, "pins and needles" (yes/no)
3. Felt electrical shocks or stabbing pain (yes/no)
4. Numbness (yes/no)
5. Itching (yes/no)
6. Tugging, pulling, pressure (yes/no)
7. Swelling in the painful area (yes/no)
8. Changes in skin color in the painful area (yes/no)
9. Changes in skin texture in the painful area (yes/no)
10. Sweating in the painful area more or less than usual (yes/no)
11. Pain in an area supplied by a single nerve or nerve root (yes/no)
12. Spread of pain since onset (yes/no)

**Scoring:**
- Each "yes" = 1 point
- Total: 0–12 points
- **PainDETECT ≥12:** Likely neuropathic pain
- **PainDETECT 6–11:** Unclear; mixed pain likely
- **PainDETECT <6:** Unlikely neuropathic pain

**KIMI's Conversational Adaptation:**
Rather than administering items 1–12 as a formal questionnaire, KIMI asks open questions and listens for key descriptors (burning, electric, tingling, numbness, dermatomal distribution). If multiple neuropathic descriptors are present, score is likely ≥12; if only 1–2, score is 6–11; if none, score is <6.

**Clinical Interpretation for KIMI:**
- **Score ≥12:** Proceed with neuropathic pain treatment; consider imaging and/or neurological exam; may need EMG/NCS if diagnosis unclear
- **Score 6–11:** Mixed neuropathic-nociceptive phenotype; treat for both nociceptive and neuropathic mechanisms
- **Score <6:** Unlikely neuropathic primary; explore nociceptive or nociplastic phenotypes

### 4.2 Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI)

**Purpose:** Screen for central sensitization (nociplastic pain); correlates with fibromyalgia and widespread pain

**Full CSI (25-item):**
The full CSI covers these domains:
- Fatigue
- Sleep disturbance
- Muscle pain and stiffness
- Sensory sensitivity (light, sound, smell, taste, temperature)
- Cognitive symptoms (brain fog, memory)
- Mood (anxiety, depression)
- Gastrointestinal symptoms
- Autonomic symptoms (dizziness, palpitations, sweating)
- Headaches/migraines

**Scoring (Full CSI):**
- Each item rated 0 (never) to 4 (always)
- Total: 0–100 points
- **CSI ≥40:** Moderate to high central sensitization; strong evidence for nociplastic phenotype
- **CSI 30–39:** Mild to moderate central sensitization
- **CSI <30:** Low central sensitization

**KIMI's Simplified Adaptation (7–9 items):**
KIMI uses conversational questioning of the most discriminating CSI items:
- Widespread pain (5+ body areas)
- Fatigue/exhaustion
- Non-restorative sleep
- Difficulty concentrating/brain fog
- Sensory sensitivity (light, sound, smell)
- Temperature sensitivity
- Mood disturbance/anxiety
- Memory problems

*Scoring for Simplified CSI:*
- 1 point for each "yes" (0–9 points)
- **≥5 points:** Moderate central sensitization features; warrants nociplastic screening
- **≥7 points:** High central sensitization features; strong evidence for nociplastic phenotype

**Clinical Interpretation for KIMI:**
- **CSI ≥40 (full) or ≥7 (simplified):** Nociplastic pain phenotype; treat with exercise, PNE, CBT, and address sleep/mood
- **CSI 30–39 (full) or 5–6 (simplified):** Mixed nociceptive-nociplastic phenotype; balance nociceptive and nociplastic treatment
- **CSI <30 (full) or <5 (simplified):** Low central sensitization; likely nociceptive or neuropathic dominant

### 4.3 STarT Back Tool (For Lumbar Pain Only)

**Purpose:** Risk stratification for low back pain patients; identifies risk of chronicity and disability

**Items (9 total):**
1. How long has your back pain lasted? (0=<6 weeks, 1=6+ weeks)
2. Straightening your back is difficult? (0=no, 1=yes)
3. Pain down your leg? (0=no, 1=yes)
4. Worrying about your pain has made you depressed? (0=no, 1=yes)
5. Felt pain prevents you from coping with family/work? (0=no, 1=yes)
6. Back pain makes household tasks difficult? (0=no, 1=yes)
7. Pain affects sleep? (0=no, 1=yes)
8. Afraid that pain will get worse? (0=no, 1=yes)
9. You have not enjoyed all the usual hobbies/activities? (0=no, 1=yes)

**Scoring:**
- Items 1–6 (prognostic indicators): 0–6 points
- Items 7–9 (psychosocial): 0–3 points
- **Risk Categories:**
  - **Low Risk:** Score ≤3 (blue): Good prognosis, simple treatment expected to be effective
  - **Moderate Risk:** Score 4–8 (yellow): Intermediate prognosis, may need more intensive management
  - **High Risk:** Score ≥9 (red): Poor prognosis without intensive intervention; high risk of chronicity and disability; need intensive multimodal treatment

**KIMI Application:**
STarT Back Risk Category guides intensity and components of care plan:
- **Low Risk:** Standard exercise + education; biweekly coaching
- **Moderate Risk:** Exercise + PNE + behavioral intervention; weekly coaching initially
- **High Risk:** Intensive multimodal care (exercise + PNE + CBT + mood management); weekly or twice-weekly coaching; possible referral for psychiatric co-management

### 4.4 Keele STarT MSK Tool (For Non-LBP MSK Conditions)

**Purpose:** Risk stratification for non-LBP musculoskeletal pain (knee, hip, shoulder, etc.); similar to STarT Back but applicable to broader MSK population

**Core Items:**
- Pain duration
- Pain intensity
- Function/disability
- Anxiety/worry
- Depression
- Confidence in recovery
- Pain self-efficacy

**Risk Categories:**
- **Low Risk:** Low pain, high function, high confidence; simple treatment
- **Moderate Risk:** Moderate pain, moderate disability, some psychosocial concerns
- **High Risk:** High pain, high disability, high fear-avoidance, low confidence; need intensive intervention

**KIMI Application:**
Keele STarT MSK guides care intensity for knee, hip, shoulder, and other MSK pain; used similarly to STarT Back (risk categories determine coaching cadence and treatment intensity)

### 4.5 How KIMI Administers These Instruments Conversationally

**Key Principle:** KIMI does NOT hand out questionnaires or ask items in rapid-fire succession. Instead, KIMI weaves validated screening items into natural conversation.

**Example Flow:**

*Opening:* "I want to understand your pain fully so we can create the right care plan for you. I'll ask about your pain, your sleep, and how it's affecting your life."

*Nociceptive Screening (Natural Conversation):*
- "Tell me about where your pain is and what makes it better or worse." [Listen for mechanical descriptors]
- "Has your doctor told you there's arthritis or another structural problem in that area?" [Listen for tissue pathology awareness]
- "When you have a flare, what helps most?" [Listen for response to modalities]

*Neuropathic Screening (Natural Conversation):*
- "Beyond the soreness, do you feel tingling, numbness, or burning in that area?" [Listen for neuropathic quality]
- "Does the pain follow a path down your leg or arm, or is it in one spot?" [Listen for distribution]
- "Do you have any weakness or numbness, like you can't feel the floor under your feet?" [Listen for neurological symptoms]

*Nociplastic Screening (Natural Conversation):*
- "Beyond the primary pain area, do other parts of your body hurt?" [Listen for widespread pain]
- "How are your energy levels? Do you feel exhausted even after sleeping?" [Listen for fatigue]
- "How's your sleep quality? Do you wake up feeling rested?" [Listen for non-restorative sleep]
- "Has the pain affected your mood? Do you worry about it?" [Listen for psychosocial factors]

*Scoring and Documentation:*
After gathering information conversationally, KIMI documents the patient's responses in phenotype categories and estimates scores on validated instruments (e.g., "PainDETECT estimated 8–10, suggesting mixed neuropathic-nociceptive; CSI estimated 6–7, suggesting moderate central sensitization overlay").

## 5. Phenotype Assignment Algorithm

### 5.1 Decision Tree

```
START: Patient pain complaint

↓

Q1: Is pain localized to 1–2 regions, or widespread (5+ regions)?
├─→ Localized (1–2 regions) → Continue to Q2
└─→ Widespread (5+ regions) → Continue to Q3

Q2: Does pain follow dermatomal or peripheral nerve distribution?
├─→ Yes (dermatomal or nerve distribution) → Continue to Q4 (Neuropathic Path)
└─→ No (anatomically non-specific distribution) → Continue to Q5 (Nociceptive Path)

Q3: Are there central sensitization features (fatigue, non-restorative sleep, mood disturbance)?
├─→ Yes, multiple features → Nociplastic Phenotype (Primary)
└─→ No, or minimal features → Continue to Q2 (Neuropathic distribution check)

Q4: Neuropathic Screening Path
├─→ Pain quality: Burning, electric, shooting, tingling?
├─→ Associated neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, allodynia)?
├─→ Clear cause of nerve compression/damage (herniation, neuropathy)?
├─→ Estimated PainDETECT ≥12?
├─→ ALL OR MOST of above: → NEUROPATHIC PHENOTYPE (Definite)
└─→ SOME of above: → NEUROPATHIC PHENOTYPE (Probable); rule out nociplastic overlay

Q5: Nociceptive Screening Path
├─→ Mechanical behavior (worse with load, better with rest)?
├─→ Proportionate to tissue pathology (imaging/exam findings match pain)?
├─→ Responsive to physical modalities (ice, heat, rest)?
├─→ Clear mechanical trigger or gradual progression with known condition?
├─→ ALL OR MOST of above: → NOCICEPTIVE PHENOTYPE (Definite)
└─→ SOME of above: → NOCICEPTIVE PHENOTYPE (Probable); assess for secondary sensitization

NOCIPLASTIC OVERLAY CHECK (for all phenotypes):
├─→ Widespread pain (WPI ≥7)?
├─→ CSI ≥40 or simplified CSI ≥7?
├─→ Significant fatigue, sleep disruption, mood changes?
├─→ Pain disproportionate to imaging/exam findings?
├─→ IF YES to 3+ above: → Document "Nociceptive-dominant WITH nociplastic overlay" or "Neuropathic-dominant WITH nociplastic overlay"
└─→ IF NO: → Document as "Pure nociceptive" or "Pure neuropathic" (less common)

FINAL PHENOTYPE ASSIGNMENT:
├─→ NOCICEPTIVE (Definite) / (Probable)
├─→ NOCICEPTIVE-dominant with NOCIPLASTIC features
├─→ NEUROPATHIC (Definite) / (Probable)
├─→ NEUROPATHIC-dominant with NOCIPLASTIC features
├─→ NOCIPLASTIC (Primary) / (Probable) / (Possible)
├─→ MIXED (No dominant phenotype, e.g., equal nociceptive and neuropathic, or all three present)
└─→ UNCLEAR (Insufficient data; reassess after 4 weeks of treatment)

IF UNCLEAR:
├─→ Document uncertainty
├─→ Start with multimodal treatment addressing all phenotypes
└─→ Reassess after 4 weeks based on treatment response
```

### 5.2 Confidence Levels

KIMI documents confidence in phenotype assignment:

**Definite:** All or most screening items positive; clear clinical picture; high confidence in diagnosis
- Example: "Definite nociceptive: Localized knee pain, mechanical pattern (worse with stairs, better with rest), concordant with OA on imaging, responsive to ice/heat, no neurological symptoms, no widespread pain, no central sensitization features."

**Probable:** Most screening items positive; good clinical picture but some ambiguity
- Example: "Probable nociceptive-dominant: Mechanical knee pain but also reports intermittent fatigue and sleep disruption; suggests nociplastic overlay on nociceptive pain."

**Possible:** Some screening items positive; uncertain phenotype; needs further assessment
- Example: "Possible nociplastic: Widespread pain and fatigue, but also reports mechanical low back pain; nociceptive component may dominate; requires treatment trial to clarify."

### 5.3 Mixed and Multiple Phenotypes

**Mixed Phenotype Definition:** Patient has features of >1 pain phenotype; no single phenotype completely explains the pain.

**Examples of Mixed Presentations:**

**Example 1: Nociceptive + Neuropathic**
- 60-year-old with knee OA (nociceptive: mechanical pattern, concordant imaging) + L5 radiculopathy (neuropathic: burning down leg, dermatomal distribution)
- Assignment: "Nociceptive-dominant (knee pain 70%) + neuropathic (leg pain 30%)"
- Treatment: Prioritize knee pain with load management; address radiculopathy with neural mobilization and centralization exercises

**Example 2: Nociceptive + Nociplastic**
- 68-year-old with mechanical low back pain (nociceptive: worse with bending, better with rest) BUT also widespread joint aches, fatigue, non-restorative sleep, high pain catastrophizing (nociplastic)
- Assignment: "Nociceptive-dominant (60%) + nociplastic overlay (40%)"
- Treatment: Prioritize nociceptive exercise; incorporate PNE, sleep hygiene, cognitive strategies

**Example 3: Neuropathic + Nociplastic**
- 72-year-old with diabetic peripheral neuropathy (neuropathic: burning feet, distal sensory loss) PLUS widespread pain, fatigue, mood disturbance, CSI ≥40 (nociplastic from chronic pain duration)
- Assignment: "Neuropathic-dominant + secondary nociplastic pain"
- Treatment: Prioritize neuropathy management (nerve pain medication if needed, movement re-education); aggressive multimodal approach (exercise, PNE, CBT, sleep, mood management) for nociplastic component

**Example 4: All Three (Rare)**
- Patient with knee OA (nociceptive) + history of knee surgery with nerve injury causing lateral knee numbness (neuropathic) + widespread pain, fatigue, mood disturbance (nociplastic)
- Assignment: "Complex mixed phenotype: Nociceptive (40%) + Neuropathic (35%) + Nociplastic (25%)"
- Treatment: Multimodal addressing all three; may need medication optimization, possibly psychiatric co-management

**Phenotype → Treatment Components:**

### 6.2 PROM (Patient-Reported Outcome Measure) Selection by Phenotype

**Recommended Outcome Measures:**

- **Nociceptive Pain:** Pain intensity (0–10 NRS), pain with activity (numeric rating), function (WOMAC for knee/hip, SPADI for shoulder, Oswestry for LBP)
- **Neuropathic Pain:** Pain intensity, neuropathic pain-specific measures (Pain Catastrophizing Scale), function, neurological symptom tracking (weakness, numbness progression)
- **Nociplastic Pain:** Widespread pain severity (Widespread Pain Index), fatigue severity, sleep quality, mood (PHQ-9 for depression), function (SF-12 or EQ-5D), pain-related fear (TSK-11)
- **Mixed Presentations:** Use measures for all phenotypes present; weight measures according to phenotype dominance

## 7. Special Populations — Medicare 65+ Considerations

### 7.1 Polypharmacy and Pain Perception Interactions

**Opioid Use and Pain Processing:**
- Chronic opioid use paradoxically can worsen central sensitization and pain amplification (opioid-induced hyperalgesia)
- Opioid dose reduction (when safely done) may improve pain treatment outcomes
- KIMI approach: Document opioid use; counsel patient on risks; support gradual taper if appropriate

**Gabapentinoids (Gabapentin, Pregabalin):**
- Often prescribed for neuropathic pain; appropriate for moderate-to-severe neuropathy
- Overuse in non-neuropathic presentations may blunt pain perception without addressing root cause
- KIMI approach: If patient on gabapentinoid but phenotype is nociceptive or nociplastic-dominant, flag for prescriber review

**Antidepressants (SSRIs, SNRIs, Tricyclic Antidepressants):**
- SSRIs/SNRIs: Beneficial for mood and anxiety; modest benefit for pain (especially neuropathic and nociplastic)
- Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline): Pain and sleep benefit; consider side effects in elderly
- KIMI approach: Coordinate with prescriber; assess whether antidepressant dose is optimized for mood and pain benefit

**NSAIDs:**
- First-line for nociceptive pain; limited benefit for neuropathic/nociplastic
- Caution in elderly: GI bleeding risk, renal function, cardiovascular risk
- KIMI approach: Support NSAID use for clear nociceptive pain; counsel on appropriate duration and dose

### 7.2 Comorbidity-Pain Interactions

**Diabetes and Pain Phenotypes:**
- Diabetic peripheral neuropathy (DPN) is common (30–50% of patients with diabetes)
- Diabetes also associated with increased pain sensitivity from metabolic changes
- Patients with diabetes + OA often have overlapping nociceptive-neuropathic-nociplastic features
- KIMI approach: Assess diabetes control (HbA1c); encourage exercise (benefits both diabetes and pain); coordinate with endocrinology if pain management complex

**Depression and Nociplastic Pain:**
- Depression is both risk factor for and consequence of chronic pain
- Depressed patients have lower pain thresholds (central sensitization)
- Nociplastic pain worsened by depression; depression may improve with pain treatment
- KIMI approach: Screen for depression (PHQ-9); coordinate behavioral health treatment; emphasize exercise as mood and pain intervention

**Anxiety and Pain Amplification:**
- Anxiety disorders increase pain sensitivity and catastrophizing
- High anxiety predicts worse pain outcomes
- KIMI approach: Assess anxiety; prioritize anxiety reduction through PNE, CBT, exercise

**Cardiovascular Disease and Functional Capacity:**
- Cardiac disease limits exercise capacity; affects phenotype expression and treatment intensity
- Patient with cardiac disease and nociceptive pain may not tolerate aggressive load management
- KIMI approach: Assess cardiovascular status; coordinate with cardiology; adapt exercise intensity

**Cognitive Impairment (Mild Cognitive Impairment, Early Dementia):**
- Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) affects pain self-reporting; patient may have difficulty recalling pain patterns
- Memory aids (pain diary, visual reminders) helpful
- Early dementia: May report pain poorly; family/caregiver input important; exercise adherence affected
- KIMI approach: Screen for cognitive impairment (Montreal Cognitive Assessment or brief screening); involve family; simplify treatment plan

### 7.3 Age-Related Changes in Pain Processing

**Aging and Descending Inhibitory Systems:**
- Older adults have reduced endogenous opioid and monoamine activity (natural pain dampening systems decline with age)
- Results in higher baseline pain sensitivity and increased vulnerability to central sensitization
- Pain thresholds lower in healthy older adults compared to younger adults
- KIMI approach: Expect pain sensitivity; avoid dismissing patient's pain as "normal aging"; emphasize that pain can improve with appropriate treatment

**Aging and Inflammation:**
- Chronic low-level inflammation ("inflammaging") increases with age
- Contributes to nociceptive pain amplification in older adults
- May benefit from anti-inflammatory interventions (exercise, diet, sleep)
- KIMI approach: Emphasize exercise, sleep, and anti-inflammatory lifestyle; avoid framing as "just aging"

**Sensory Changes:**
- Peripheral neuropathy increases with age (diabetic or idiopathic)
- Proprioceptive loss increases fall risk
- KIMI approach: Screen for neuropathy; emphasize balance and proprioceptive training; ensure safety during exercise

## 8. Phenotype Reassessment Schedule

### 8.1 Scheduled Reassessment

**Standard Reassessment Timeline:**
- **4-Week Reassessment:** After initial 4 weeks of treatment, reassess phenotype if treatment response is unexpected or progress is minimal
- **8-Week Reassessment:** If still unclear or if treatment response suggests different phenotype
- **12-Week Reassessment:** Formal reassessment; some patients' phenotype may have shifted due to treatment response

### 8.2 Triggers for Re-phenotyping

**Clinical Indicators That Reassessment Is Needed:**

1. **Lack of Expected Treatment Response:**
   - Nociceptive pain not improving with appropriate load management by week 4 → may indicate secondary nociplastic features, undiagnosed neuropathy, or imaging-pathology mismatch
   - Neuropathic pain not improving with nerve-protective exercises by week 6 → may indicate central sensitization overlay or misdiagnosis
   - Nociplastic pain not improving with exercise/PNE by week 8 → may indicate unaddressed depression, undiagnosed structural pathology, or inadequate sleep intervention

2. **Changing Pain Pattern:**
   - Initially localized pain becomes widespread → suggests development of central sensitization
   - Mechanical pain becomes non-mechanical → suggests nociplastic overlay developing
   - Pain spreads beyond original dermatomal distribution → suggests expanding neuropathy or development of secondary central sensitization

3. **Symptom Expansion:**
   - New neurological symptoms (weakness, spreading numbness) → reassess for missed neuropathy diagnosis
   - New widespread pain areas → reassess for developing central sensitization
   - New fatigue, sleep problems, mood changes → suggests nociplastic component emerging

4. **Treatment-Induced Flares or Adverse Responses:**
   - Pain worsens with exercise designed for nociceptive pain → may indicate undiagnosed neuropathy that needs different approach
   - Extreme fatigue after exercise → may indicate nociplastic component not addressed

### 8.3 Re-phenotyping Process and Care Plan Modification

**Re-phenotyping Steps:**

1. **Review Original Phenotype Assessment:** Compare initial screening findings to current clinical picture
2. **Administer Updated Screening:** Use same screening questions as at intake; note any changes in pain quality, distribution, associated symptoms
3. **Re-score Validated Instruments:** Re-estimate PainDETECT score, CSI score; compare to baseline
4. **Document Phenotype Shift:** "Initial phenotype: Nociceptive-dominant; Current phenotype: Nociceptive + strong nociplastic overlay" (if shift has occurred)
5. **Modify Care Plan:** Adjust treatment components to address newly identified phenotype components

**Examples of Care Plan Modification After Re-phenotyping:**

**Example 1:**
- **Initial (Week 1):** Nociceptive knee pain; started load management
- **Week 4:** Pain improved 20%, but patient reports increasing fatigue and poor sleep not present at baseline
- **Re-phenotyping (Week 4):** "Nociceptive-dominant (70%) + emerging nociplastic features (30%)"
- **Modified Care Plan:** Add sleep module to PNE; consider aerobic exercise component; increase coaching to weekly to address fatigue

**Example 2:**
- **Initial (Week 1):** "Probable neuropathic" lumbar radiculopathy with burning down leg
- **Week 8:** Pain improved 15%; patient still reports dysesthesias and numbness, but now also reports bilateral leg pain and widespread body aches
- **Re-phenotyping (Week 8):** "Neuropathic-dominant radiculopathy (60%) + secondary nociplastic pain (40%)"
- **Modified Care Plan:** Continue neuromotor exercises; add PNE focused on central sensitization; add aerobic component; intensify CBT; potentially increase neuropathic medication if pain remains severe

**Example 3:**
- **Initial (Week 1):** Nociplastic pain; widespread pain, fatigue, sleep disruption; CSI ≥40
- **Week 12:** Significant pain improvement (60%) with exercise and sleep optimization; patient notes knees are now mechanically painful and could benefit from specific knee strengthening
- **Re-phenotyping (Week 12):** "Nociplastic pain (initially dominant) now revealing underlying nociceptive pain (knee OA); phenotype now: Nociceptive-dominant (70%) with residual nociplastic features (30%)"
- **Modified Care Plan:** Transition to more specific load management for knees; continue exercise but now with more mechanical focus; reduce frequency of mood/sleep modules; continue some PNE as needed for residual sensitization

## 9. Evidence Base and Validated Sources

### 9.1 Nociceptive Pain Classification

- **IASP (International Association for the Study of Pain) Definition of Nociceptive Pain:** Updated IASP taxonomy (2021) defining nociceptive pain as pain resulting from actual or threatened tissue damage that activates nociceptors
- **Pincus et al. (2013)** on tissue-pain correlation in OA: "Five-year follow-up of individuals with knee osteoarthritis" showing high tissue-pain correlation in early/moderate OA; correlation decreases with chronicity
- **Woolf (2011)** on nociceptive vs neuropathic vs nociplastic pain mechanisms: Foundational framework for pain classification
- **Loeser & Melzack (1999)** on gate control theory and pain modulation: Foundational theory of nociceptive pain processing

### 9.2 Neuropathic Pain Assessment and Diagnosis

- **IASP 2021 Neuropathic Pain Grading System (Finnerup et al., 2021):** "Classification of chronic pain for the International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11)" - standardized criteria for neuropathic pain diagnosis; requires evidence of lesion/disease affecting somatosensory nervous system
- **Mahn et al. (2011)** on PainDETECT screening: Validation study showing PainDETECT ≥12 predicts neuropathic pain with 86% sensitivity and 90% specificity
- **Jensen et al. (2007)** on DN4 screening tool: Alternative neuropathic pain screener (12 items); high sensitivity and specificity for neuropathic pain
- **Attal et al. (2010)** on neuropathic pain management: Evidence-based guidelines on pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions

### 9.3 Nociplastic (Central Sensitization) Pain Classification

- **Nijs et al. (2021)** "Conceptual Models and Mechanisms of Nociceptive Pain in the Context of Central Sensitization:" Comprehensive review of central sensitization mechanisms and their role in chronic pain; consensus on definition and screening
- **Woolf (2011)** on central sensitization: "Central sensitization: implications for the diagnosis and treatment of pain" - seminal review defining central sensitization and its implications
- **CSI Validation (Mayer et al., 2012):** Central Sensitization Inventory (CSI) validation study; CSI ≥40 associated with fibromyalgia and nociplastic pain; 0–100 point scale
- **Fitzcharles et al. (2017)** on nociplastic pain in OA: "Nociplastic pain: towards an understanding of prevalent pain conditions" - review of nociplastic mechanisms in OA and chronic MSK pain
- **Clauw (2014)** on fibromyalgia and central sensitization: "Fibromyalgia: A clinical review" - comprehensive review linking fibromyalgia to central sensitization mechanism

### 9.4 Risk Stratification and Prognostic Tools

- **Hill et al. (2008)** on STarT Back Tool: "A primary care back pain screening tool: identifying patient subgroups for initial treatment" - validation of STarT Back for LBP risk stratification; shows utility of prognostic variables in treatment planning
- **Wideman et al. (2022)** on Keele STarT MSK: "The Keele STarT MSK tool: evaluation of its prognostic validity in musculoskeletal pain cohorts" - validation of STarT MSK for non-LBP MSK conditions; same prognostic factors as STarT Back

### 9.5 Pain Phenotyping in Specific Conditions

- **Neogi et al. (2016)** on OA pain heterogeneity: "Is OA one disease or a collection of many?" - reviews phenotype heterogeneity in knee OA; nociceptive-dominant vs nociplastic features differ in clinical presentation and prognosis
- **Sorensen et al. (2012)** on pain phenotyping in LBP: "Low-back pain is partly a disease of the brain and the musculoskeletal system" - reviews interaction between nociceptive and nociplastic mechanisms in chronic LBP
- **Haroutounian et al. (2018)** on pain phenotyping in mixed presentations: "Towards a taxonomy of pain" - reviews challenges of classifying mixed phenotypes and clinical implications

### 9.6 Exercise and Treatment Outcomes by Phenotype

- **Chou et al. (2017)** on non-pharmacological management of low back pain: Cochrane review showing exercise is effective for LBP across phenotypes; effectiveness varies by exercise type and pain mechanism
- **Schiltenwolf et al. (2017)** on exercise for nociplastic pain: Reviews evidence that aerobic exercise improves pain and fatigue in fibromyalgia and chronic widespread pain
- **Nijs et al. (2014)** on pain neuroscience education: "Nociplastic pain research: current perspectives" - reviews evidence that PNE beneficial for nociplastic pain and mixed presentations
- **O'Neill et al. (2018)** on rehabilitation for neuropathic pain: Reviews evidence-based interventions for peripheral neuropathy; neuromotor retraining, proprioceptive training more effective than traditional passive treatment

### 9.7 Comorbidity and Pain in Older Adults

- **Patel et al. (2013)** on pain and depression in older adults: "The relationship between pain and depression in older adults" - reviews bidirectional relationship; depression worsens pain outcomes
- **Agarwal & Langhans (2005)** on chronic pain in Medicare population: Reviews prevalence and impact of chronic pain in older adults; shows high rates of comorbidity
- **Makris et al. (2014)** on prescription opioid use in older adults: "Management of opioid-induced side effects in cancer patients" and broader review of opioid effects in elderly; older adults more sensitive to opioid side effects; risk of hyperalgesia

## 11. Clinical Decision Support and Escalation Protocols

### 11.1 Red Flags and Escalation Indications

**Escalate to Physician/Specialist if:**

1. **Rapidly Progressive Neuropathic Symptoms:** Expanding weakness, bilateral symptoms, progressive sensory loss → Consider spinal cord disease, systemic neuropathy; needs urgent imaging/neurology consult
2. **Cauda Equina Syndrome Red Flags:** Bowel/bladder loss of control, bilateral leg pain/numbness, saddle anesthesia → **IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY REFERRAL**
3. **Severe Unexplained Pain:** Pain severity 9–10/10 with no identified cause → May indicate malignancy, infection, or other serious pathology; needs imaging/specialist eval
4. **Systemic Illness Symptoms:** Fever, night sweats, weight loss, new-onset widespread pain → Suggests infection, malignancy, or systemic disease; needs medical workup
5. **Minimal/No Response to Phenotype-Matched Treatment After 8 Weeks:** Suggests misdiagnosis, unaddressed psychiatric comorbidity, or serious underlying pathology
6. **Rapid Worsening Despite Treatment:** Pain escalating despite appropriate intervention; may indicate progressive disease
7. **Significant Psychiatric Decompensation:** Severe depression, suicidality, active psychosis → Needs psychiatric crisis assessment and intervention

**Escalate to Behavioral Health (Psychology/Psychiatry) if:**
- CSI ≥40 with severe depression (PHQ-9 ≥15)
- High pain catastrophizing with significant anxiety (GAD-7 ≥15)
- Active trauma history with PTSD symptoms affecting pain treatment
- Suicidal ideation
- Treatment-resistant nociplastic pain after 12 weeks multimodal treatment

### 11.2 Expected Treatment Response Milestones

KIMI tracks response against phenotype-specific milestones to identify when escalation is needed:

**Nociceptive Pain Milestones:**
- Week 2: 20–30% pain reduction; increased confidence with movement
- Week 4: 40–60% pain reduction; noticeable functional improvement
- Week 8: 60–80% pain reduction; return to most activities
- Red flag if Week 4 shows <20% improvement

**Neuropathic Pain Milestones:**
- Week 4: 10–20% pain reduction; stable neurological symptoms
- Week 8: 20–40% pain reduction; functional gains beginning
- Week 12: 30–50% pain reduction; improved confidence and reduced anxiety
- Red flag if neurological symptoms worsening or spreading

**Nociplastic Pain Milestones:**
- Week 4: Improved sleep, modest mood improvement, 10–20% pain reduction
- Week 8: 20–40% pain reduction, significant sleep improvement, functional gains
- Week 12: 40–60% pain reduction, clear mood and fatigue improvement
- Red flag if Week 8 shows no improvement despite good adherence

## Appendix A. Glossary of Pain Phenotyping Terms

- **Allodynia:** Pain response to normally non-painful stimuli (e.g., clothing touching skin causes pain)
- **Amplification:** Heightened pain perception from normal nociceptive input; hallmark of central sensitization
- **Central Sensitization:** Heightened responsiveness of central nervous system neurons to nociceptive input; results in pain amplification
- **Dermatome:** Skin area supplied by single nerve root
- **Disproportionality:** Pain severity greater than expected from physical findings or imaging pathology
- **Hyperalgesia:** Heightened pain response to painful stimuli
- **Nociplastic Pain:** Pain from altered nociception without tissue damage or nerve lesion
- **Nociception:** Neural process of detecting tissue damage signals
- **Nociceptor:** Specialized nerve ending that detects pain stimuli
- **Neuropathic Pain:** Pain from nerve damage or disease
- **Non-Restorative Sleep:** Sleep that does not leave person feeling rested; hallmark of nociplastic pain
- **Phenotype:** Observable clinical characteristics clustering together to define pain mechanism
- **Radiculopathy:** Pain radiating in dermatome from nerve root compression
- **Referred Pain:** Pain perceived in area distant from source of tissue damage; nociceptive mechanism
- **Tissue-Pain Correlation:** Alignment between imaging/structural findings and reported pain; high correlation typical of nociceptive pain
- **Wind-Up:** Progressive amplification of pain perception with repeated nociceptive input; central sensitization mechanism
