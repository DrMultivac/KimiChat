# CBT and Pain Psychology Modules — KIMI MSK Care Coach Knowledge Base

## Overview

This document provides seven comprehensive cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and pain psychology modules for delivery by Kimi, the AI MSK Care Coach. Each module is designed for older adults (60+) with chronic musculoskeletal pain, written at a 6th-8th grade reading level, and includes conversational scripts, interactive exercises, reflection questions, and homework assignments.

**Clinical Foundation:** This curriculum integrates evidence-based CBT techniques (Socratic questioning, cognitive restructuring, behavioral activation), pain neuroscience education (PNE), and acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) elements. All modules are aligned with the biopsychosocial model and designed to reduce pain catastrophizing, kinesiophobia, and disability while building pain self-efficacy and valued living.

**Delivery Schedule:** Modules are sequenced over a 12-month care journey (see Section 8). Kimi introduces modules based on patient risk profile (STarT Back score), psychological screening results (PCS, TSK, PHQ-9, GAD-7), and clinical milestones.

## Module 1: Understanding the Fight-or-Flight Response

### 1.1 Module Overview

**Title:** How Your Nervous System Responds to Pain and Stress

**Learning Objective:** Understand how the nervous system's threat-detection system contributes to chronic pain; learn that ongoing pain keeps the nervous system "stuck" in a protective mode; recognize the stress-pain cycle.

**Evidence Base:** Vlaeyen & Linton (2012) fear-avoidance model; Van Oosterwijck et al. (2013) on autonomic dysfunction in chronic pain; Chou et al. (2017) on the neurobiology of chronic pain.

---

### 1.2 Educational Content

#### What Is Your Nervous System?

Your nervous system is like your body's alarm system. Its job is to keep you safe. It has two main settings:

1. **The Alert Setting (Fight-or-Flight):** When your nervous system senses danger — real or perceived — it sends out alarm signals. Your body responds by releasing stress hormones like adrenaline and cortisol. Your heart beats faster. Your muscles tense up. Your breathing quickens. Your attention narrows. This is great when you need to escape real danger.

2. **The Calm Setting (Rest-and-Digest):** When your nervous system decides you're safe, it switches to a calm mode. Your heart slows. Your muscles relax. Your digestion works better. You can think clearly and sleep well.

#### How Does This Relate to Pain?

In acute pain — like a sprain or cut — your nervous system's alarm system is very helpful. It tells you something is wrong and needs care. The alarm goes off, you protect the area, it heals, and the alarm turns off. This is normal.

But in chronic pain — pain lasting more than 3 months — something different happens. The alarm system can get "stuck" in the ON position. Even after the initial injury heals, the nervous system keeps sending pain signals. Why? Because the system has learned to see certain movements, activities, or situations as threats, even when they are actually safe.

#### The Car Alarm Analogy

Imagine a car alarm that is way too sensitive. Maybe it goes off when the wind blows. Maybe it goes off when someone taps the car. It's doing its job (protecting the car) but it's overdoing it. The alarm is so sensitive it goes off when there's no real threat.

Your chronic pain nervous system can work like that overly sensitive car alarm. It's trying to protect you, but it's sending alarm signals (pain) even when there's no real danger or tissue damage happening. The pain feels real — because it is real to your nervous system — but it doesn't mean something is being damaged.

#### The Stress-Pain Cycle

Here's something important: stress and pain feed each other.

- When you are stressed, anxious, or worried, your nervous system shifts toward the alert setting. This makes pain feel worse.
- When pain gets worse, you become more stressed and anxious. This shifts your nervous system further into alert mode.
- This creates a cycle: **Stress → More Pain → More Stress → Even More Pain.**

Common stress triggers that turn up pain include:
- Worry about what pain means ("Is it getting worse?")
- Fear of activity ("Will exercise damage me?")
- Poor sleep
- Work or family stress
- Social isolation
- Loss of activities you enjoy

#### Why This Matters for Your Recovery

The good news is that this cycle can be reversed. By teaching your nervous system that you are safe — through:
- Understanding your pain (PNE)
- Gradually returning to activities (graded exposure)
- Relaxation practices (turning down the alert setting)
- Correcting unhelpful thoughts (cognitive restructuring)

...you can shift your nervous system back toward the calm setting. When your nervous system is calmer, your pain decreases, your function improves, and your life expands.

## Module 2: Relaxed Breathing (Diaphragmatic Breathing)

### 2.1 Module Overview

**Title:** How to Calm Your Nervous System with Your Breath

**Learning Objective:** Learn and practice diaphragmatic breathing; understand how controlled breathing signals safety to the nervous system; practice using breathing during daily activities and pain flares.

**Evidence Base:** Porges (2011) polyvagal theory; Thayer & Lane (2000) on heart rate variability and vagal tone; systematic reviews of breathing in chronic pain (Busch et al., 2012).

---

### 2.2 Educational Content

#### Why Breathing Matters

Your breath is one of the few things your nervous system controls automatically, but that you can also control manually. This makes it a powerful tool for calming.

When your nervous system is in alert mode, your breathing changes:
- It becomes shallow (upper chest only)
- It gets faster
- You might hold your breath without noticing

When you intentionally slow and deepen your breathing, you send a message to your nervous system: "It's safe now. You can relax." Your body responds by:
- Slowing your heart rate
- Reducing stress hormones
- Relaxing your muscles
- Reducing pain

#### Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing

Most people breathe with their chest. This is fine for normal activity, but when you're stressed or in pain, shallow chest breathing can actually make anxiety worse.

Diaphragmatic breathing uses a muscle called the diaphragm (under your ribs). When you breathe from your belly, you:
- Take in more oxygen
- More strongly signal calm to your nervous system
- Reduce physical tension more effectively

**How to know if you're breathing from your belly:**
- Your belly expands when you inhale (not your chest)
- Your hand on your abdomen moves outward
- Your shoulders don't rise up

---

### 2.3 Kimi's Conversational Delivery Script and Guided Practice

> **Kimi Introduction:**
>
> "Now that you understand how your nervous system gets stuck in alert mode, I want to teach you a simple technique to help shift it back to calm. It's called diaphragmatic breathing, or belly breathing. It takes just a few minutes to learn, and you can use it anywhere — at home, before an exercise, when pain flares up, or when stress hits.
>
> The beautiful thing about this technique is that it works. Research shows that when you slow and deepen your breathing, your nervous system gets the message that everything is okay. Your heart rate slows. Your muscles relax. Your pain volume actually goes down.
>
> The technique is simple, but I want to teach you exactly how to do it so you get the most benefit. Ready to try?"
>
> [Once patient is ready:]
>
> "First, let's get you comfortable. Sit up straight but not rigid — think 'alert but relaxed.' If sitting is hard, lying on your back is fine.
>
> Now, put one hand on your chest and one hand on your belly, right below your belly button. This helps you feel what's happening.
>

>
> **Breathe in through your nose for a count of 4.** (In... 2... 3... 4)

>
> **Breathe out through your mouth for a count of 6.** (Out... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6)
>

>
> **Breath 1:**

>

> 'In... 2... 3... 4. Hold. Out... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6. Feel your belly rise and fall.'
>
> **Breath 3:**
> 'In... 2... 3... 4. Hold. Out... 2... 3... 4... 5... 6. Notice how you feel now.'
>
> [Pause]
>
> Great. What did you notice? Did your belly move? Did anything shift in your body?"
>
> [Listen to patient response. Validate.]
>
> "Perfect. That shift you might have noticed? That's your nervous system responding. The longer exhale is the magic. Even though nothing changed around you, your body received the signal: 'We're safe.'
>
> Now, here's what I want you to know: you don't have to do this perfectly. You don't have to count exactly. If it's 4-in and 5-out, that's fine. If you do it 4-6, that's perfect. The goal is just: **exhale a bit longer than you inhale.** Your body will still get the message to calm down.
>
> When to use this:
> - **Every morning:** 5 breaths to start your day calm
> - **Before exercise:** 3-5 breaths to reduce fear and prepare your body
> - **When pain increases:** 5-10 breaths to interrupt the stress-pain cycle
> - **When you're worried or anxious:** Anytime you notice those feelings, use your breathing
>
> This is something you can do forever. It costs nothing. It has no side effects. And it works. You just practiced it."

## Module 3: Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR)

### 3.1 Module Overview

**Title:** Deep Muscle Relaxation to Release Tension and Pain

**Learning Objective:** Learn and practice progressive muscle relaxation (PMR); understand the connection between muscle tension and pain; practice releasing tension systematically through your whole body.

**Evidence Base:** Jacobson (1938); updated evidence in Howarth et al. (2016) systematic review showing PMR efficacy in chronic pain.

---

### 3.2 Educational Content

#### How Muscle Tension Feeds Pain

Your muscles and pain work together. When your nervous system is in alert mode, your muscles tighten automatically. You might not even notice it. You clench your jaw. Your shoulders creep up toward your ears. Your back tightens. Your legs get stiff.

This tension:
- Reduces blood flow to the muscle, creating more pain
- Creates fatigue
- Limits your range of motion
- Feeds anxiety (the tight body sends "alert" signals back to the brain)

Progressive Muscle Relaxation (PMR) is a way to:
- Actively notice where you hold tension
- Deliberately release that tension
- Train your muscles to relax

Unlike stretching, PMR doesn't require flexibility. You don't have to be in pain. You simply tense each muscle group gently, then release it. This contrast (tight-then-loose) helps your nervous system understand the difference and deepens relaxation.

#### How PMR Works

1. **Tense** each muscle group for about 5 seconds (just enough to feel the tension, not to create pain)
2. **Release** completely and notice the relaxation for about 15 seconds
3. **Observe** the difference (tight vs. loose)
4. **Move to the next area**

---

### 3.3 Kimi's Conversational Delivery Script and Guided PMR

> **Kimi Introduction:**
>
> "You've learned how to calm your nervous system with breathing. Now I want to teach you how to release tension from your muscles. Most people with chronic pain carry tension they don't even realize is there — shoulders up, jaw clenched, belly tight. This tension feeds the pain.
>
> I'm going to teach you a technique called Progressive Muscle Relaxation, or PMR. It's one of the best ways to teach your whole body to relax.
>
> Here's how it works: You'll tense each muscle group for just a few seconds, then release it. As you release, you'll notice how different it feels — how loose and heavy the muscle becomes. Your nervous system learns from that contrast: 'Ah, this is what relaxation feels like.'
>
> The best part? You don't need flexibility. You don't need to be strong. You just need to be willing to tense and release.
>
> We're going to go through your whole body, from your hands all the way down to your feet. It takes about 20 minutes. Some people do it at night before bed — it's perfect for sleep. Others do it in the morning to start calm. Some do it in the afternoon if pain spikes.
>
> Ready to try? Let's start."

**Full Guided PMR Script (20-30 minutes):**

"Find a comfortable position — sitting or lying down is fine. Your goal is comfort, not perfection. If you need to shift during the exercise, that's totally fine.

Let me give you the ground rules:

1. When I ask you to tense a muscle, use gentle tension — like 50% of maximum. If it causes pain, don't do it. Just skip that muscle group. This is not a test. It's about relaxation.

2. When you tense, hold it for about 5 seconds. Notice the tension.

3. When I say release, let go completely. Notice the relaxation for about 15 seconds. This is the important part.

4. Take your time. Breathe normally. If your mind wanders, that's okay. Just bring it back to your body.

Ready? Let's begin.

---

**HANDS AND FOREARMS**

Let's start with your hands. Hold out your hands in front of you — or just imagine them if that's easier. Make tight fists. Really squeeze them. Feel the tension in your fingers, your palms, your forearms. Hold it... hold it... 5, 4, 3, 2, 1. Now release. Let your hands go completely loose. Let them be heavy. Feel the relaxation. Notice how different this feels from the tension. Just observe that for a moment. Good.

---

**BICEPS AND FOREARMS (upper arm)**

Now bend your elbows and tense your biceps. Imagine you're making muscles. Tighten those upper arms. Hold... hold... and release. Let them go. Feel how your arms become heavy and loose. Notice the warmth as blood flows back. Just feel that.

---

**SHOULDERS**

Now pull your shoulders up toward your ears. Shrug them. Feel the tension in your shoulders, your neck, your upper back. This is where so many people hold stress. Hold that tension... and release. Drop your shoulders. Let them fall. Feel how much space opens up in your neck. How much lighter that feels. Good.

---

**NECK AND JAW**

Clench your jaw. Press your teeth together gently. Feel the tension in your jaw, your cheeks, your temples. Many people hold tension here all day without noticing. Now release. Let your jaw hang loose. Let your tongue rest flat. Your teeth can be slightly apart. Notice the difference.

---

**FOREHEAD AND EYES**

Raise your eyebrows and wrinkle your forehead. Make a surprised look. Feel the tension across your forehead. Hold it... and release. Let your forehead smooth out. Feel it become completely relaxed. Smooth and soft.

---

**WHOLE FACE**

Scrunch your whole face — eyes, nose, mouth, all of it. Make a tight grimace. Hold that tension... and now release. Let your face become completely soft and relaxed. Your face has so many small muscles. When they all relax at once, it's a wonderful feeling.

---

**CHEST AND BREATHING**

Take a deep breath in. Fill your lungs. Feel the tension across your chest. Hold it... and exhale slowly. As you exhale, feel your chest relax completely. Each time you breathe, let yourself become a little more relaxed.

---

**ABDOMEN AND STOMACH**

Now tense your stomach muscles. Pull your belly in tight, or push it out — whichever you can do comfortably. Feel the tension. Hold it... and release. Let your belly become completely relaxed. Soft. No effort. Just resting.

---

**BACK AND SPINE**

Arch your back slightly or tense the muscles along your spine. Feel the tension in your back. Hold it... and release. Let your back settle. Notice if there are any areas where you still feel tension. You can release them too. Just let your whole back become heavy and relaxed.

---

**BUTTOCKS AND THIGHS**

Tense your buttocks and thigh muscles. Squeeze them. Feel how firm they become. Hold... and release. Let them go soft and heavy. These are big muscles, and when they relax, you feel a real sense of heaviness through your lower body.

---

**CALVES AND SHINS**

Point your toes away from you. Feel the tension in your calves. Hold it... and release. Now flex your toes back toward you. Feel the tension in your shins. Hold... and release. Both directions. Now just let both relax completely.

---

**FEET AND TOES**

Curl your toes. Make tight fists with your feet. Feel the tension in your arches, your toes, your whole feet. Hold... and release. Spread your toes. Wiggle them. Then let them settle. Completely relaxed. Heavy.

---

**FULL BODY RELAXATION**

Now take a moment. Scan your whole body from head to toe. Notice how different everything feels. Most people describe it as:

- Heavy
- Warm
- Tingly
- Relaxed
- Peaceful

You did this. You commanded your muscles to tense, and you commanded them to release. You have power over your physical state.

In a moment, I'm going to count from 1 to 5. When I reach 5, you'll come back alert and refreshed. But for now, just sit with this feeling of relaxation.

Whenever you're ready... 1. Starting to come back. 2. Becoming more alert. 3. Feeling refreshed. 4. Almost back. 5. Eyes open, feeling great.

How do you feel?"

---

## Module 4: Unhelpful Thinking Styles and Cognitive Restructuring

### 4.1 Module Overview

**Title:** How Your Thoughts About Pain Shape Your Experience

**Learning Objective:** Identify pain-related cognitive distortions (catastrophizing, fortune telling, all-or-nothing thinking, etc.); learn how unhelpful thoughts worsen pain and disability; practice cognitive restructuring using evidence-based thinking.

**Evidence Base:** Beck & Emery (1985) cognitive theory; Sullivan et al. (2001) on pain catastrophizing; Vlaeyen & Linton (2012) on fear-avoidance beliefs.

---

### 4.2 Educational Content

#### What Are Unhelpful Thinking Styles?

An unhelpful thinking style is a pattern of thinking that feels true but actually creates more distress and pain than necessary. It's like wearing glasses with the wrong prescription — everything looks wrong, but the problem isn't the world. It's the lens you're looking through.

With chronic pain, ten thinking styles are very common:

#### 1. **Catastrophizing**

*Definition:* Expecting the worst possible outcome; believing pain will lead to disaster.

*Examples:*
- "This pain will never get better."
- "My knee will give out and I'll fall."
- "I'm going to end up disabled."

*What it does:* Catastrophizing activates your alarm system. Your nervous system responds to the catastrophic thought by increasing pain. The thought feels like a prediction, but it's actually a fear.

*Evidence against it:* Most catastrophic predictions don't come true. People with pain do recover. You've had other worries in the past that didn't happen.

*More balanced thought:* "I'm having pain right now, and I don't know what will happen, but most pain-related catastrophes don't actually occur. I can handle difficult situations."

---

#### 2. **Fortune Telling**

*Definition:* Believing you can predict the future with certainty, usually negatively.

*Examples:*
- "I know I'll fail at this exercise program."
- "I'll never be able to garden again."
- "This will only get worse."

*What it does:* Fortune telling creates a sense of hopelessness. If you're certain failure is coming, why try? This becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

*More balanced thought:* "I don't know what will happen in the future. I can't predict it. But I can take one step at a time and see what's possible."

---

#### 3. **All-or-Nothing Thinking**

*Definition:* Seeing things in black-and-white; if something isn't perfect, it's a failure.

*Examples:*
- "If I can't do the full 30-minute exercise, there's no point doing anything."
- "I can't do physical therapy anymore because I missed two weeks."
- "If I have one bad day, all my progress is gone."

*What it does:* All-or-nothing thinking leads to avoidance and gives up. Many people avoid starting something because they can't do it perfectly.

*More balanced thought:* "Something is better than nothing. Progress isn't linear. Doing 10 minutes is still progress. Missing a week doesn't erase what I've learned."

---

#### 4. **Mind Reading**

*Definition:* Assuming you know what someone else is thinking, usually negatively.

*Examples:*
- "My doctor thinks I'm making this up."
- "My family believes I'm lazy."
- "People think I'm exaggerating."

*What it does:* Mind reading creates shame, social isolation, and avoidance of help-seeking. It's almost always inaccurate.

*Evidence gathering:* What actual evidence do you have? Has anyone said these things? Or are you guessing?

*More balanced thought:* "I don't know what others are thinking. I can ask them directly if I'm unsure."

---

#### 5. **Magnification (Catastrophizing) and Minimization**

*Definition:* Exaggerating the importance of bad things or downplaying good things.

*Examples:*
- Magnification: "Because I have this one flare-up, my entire program isn't working."
- Minimization: "I walked 20 minutes without severe pain, but that's not a big deal."

*What it does:* This distortion prevents you from seeing progress. You notice failures vividly but dismiss successes.

*More balanced thought:* "One flare-up is data, not destiny. Walking 20 minutes IS a big deal if I couldn't do it before."

---

#### 6. **Emotional Reasoning**

*Definition:* Believing something is true because it feels true.

*Examples:*
- "I feel awful, so I must be getting worse."
- "My pain feels like something serious, so it must be."

*What it does:* This thought is very common with chronic pain. Feelings are real, but they don't always reflect reality. Pain can feel intense while tissues are healing normally.

*The reality:* Pain intensity ≠ Tissue damage. Feel bad ≠ Getting worse.

*More balanced thought:* "I feel terrible right now, AND I know from my history that feelings change. This feeling doesn't mean I'm permanently worse."

---

#### 7. **Should Statements**

*Definition:* Using rigid rules ("should," "must," "ought to") that create shame and pressure.

*Examples:*
- "I should be better by now."
- "I shouldn't need help."
- "I must be able to do what I did before."

*What it does:* Should statements create guilt and frustration. They're often based on comparison to who you were before pain or to unrealistic standards.

*The reality:* Healing takes time. Needing help is human. You've changed, and that's information to adapt to, not something to fight against.

*More balanced thought:* "I'm healing at my own pace. Asking for help is strength. I'm doing the best I can with what I have right now."

---

#### 8. **Labeling**

*Definition:* Defining yourself by a single characteristic or experience.

*Examples:*
- "I'm just an old person falling apart."
- "I'm broken."
- "I'm disabled" (instead of "I have a disability" or "I'm experiencing limitations.")

*What it does:* Labeling creates identity around the pain. This limits possibilities and feeds hopelessness.

*More balanced thought:* "I'm a person with chronic pain, AND I'm also [capable, loved, creative, etc.]. I have limitations right now, AND I have strengths."

---

#### 9. **Overgeneralization**

*Definition:* Treating one negative event as a never-ending pattern.

*Examples:*
- "I had a bad day, so this treatment isn't working."
- "Because this exercise hurt, all exercise is bad for me."
- "I failed once, so I'll always fail."

*What it does:* One setback becomes proof of permanent failure. This stops you from trying again or adjusting your approach.

*More balanced thought:* "One bad day is one data point. What's the overall trend? What can I learn from this one day?"

---

#### 10. **Mental Filtering**

*Definition:* Focusing only on negative information and filtering out positive.

*Examples:*
- Noticing the bad days but ignoring the good days
- Remembering pain but forgetting the activities you did successfully
- Focusing on what you can't do and ignoring what you can

*What it does:* Mental filtering creates depression and hopelessness. Your brain keeps score in a way that feels accurate but is actually skewed.

*More balanced thought:* "Today I'm going to notice good things too — what I could do, what felt better, what I handled well."

---

## Module 5: Pacing for Pain and Fatigue

### 5.1 Module Overview

**Title:** Finding Your Rhythm: Pacing Your Activity for Recovery

**Learning Objective:** Understand the boom-bust cycle; establish an activity baseline; learn time-contingent pacing; practice gradual, sustainable increases in activity.

**Evidence Base:** Bailey et al. (2010) on activity pacing in chronic fatigue; Feng & Pedlar (2019) on self-pacing in fibromyalgia; activity pacing is a core element of CBT for chronic pain.

---

### 5.2 Educational Content

#### The Boom-Bust Cycle

Many people with chronic pain follow a "boom-bust" pattern:

- **Boom phase:** They feel a bit better, so they do a lot — housecleaning, gardening, visiting friends, exercises, all in one day or weekend. It feels great.
- **Bust phase:** Two days later, pain flares. They're exhausted. They stop everything.
- **Recovery phase:** They rest. Pain slowly decreases.
- **Repeat:** They feel better again, do too much, and the cycle repeats.

This cycle actually delays recovery and keeps pain high. Here's why:

1. The boom phase causes inflammation and nervous system activation.
2. The bust phase involves avoidance, which increases fear and deconditioning.
3. You never build a stable baseline of activity.
4. Your nervous system never gets the signal: "Activity is safe."

#### The Solution: Pacing

Pacing means:
- Finding an activity level you can do even on bad days (your baseline)
- Doing roughly the same amount every day (consistency)
- Gradually increasing that amount (very slowly)
- Avoiding the boom-bust cycle

Pacing is different from pushing through pain. You're not exercising hard or fast. You're exercising smart: steady, sustainable, predictable.

#### Finding Your Baseline

Your baseline is an activity level you can do consistently, even on a bad pain day, without causing a flare.

For example:
- If you can walk 5 minutes comfortably on good days, your baseline might be 3 minutes.
- If you can garden for 30 minutes on good days, your baseline might be 10 minutes.
- If you can do strength exercises for 20 minutes, your baseline might be 10 minutes with lighter resistance.

**How to find your baseline:**
1. Think about an activity you need or want to do (walking, exercise, gardening, housework)
2. Ask yourself: "What amount could I do even on a bad pain day?"
3. Start there. Do that amount every day for at least one week.
4. Notice: Are you having flares? No? Good. Stay at this level.
5. After one week with no flares, increase by about 10-20%.

## Module 7: Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE)

### 7.1 Module Overview

**Title:** Understanding How Pain Works — And Why You Can Change It

**Learning Objective:** Understand pain as a nervous system output (not a direct measure of tissue damage); learn about central sensitization and neuroplasticity; understand why graded exposure and movement are therapeutic.

**Evidence Base:** Lorimer Moseley's research on pain neuroscience; Salazar-Méndez et al. (2023) on PNE dosing for chronic pain; Ciolan et al. (2024) on effective PNE delivery.

---

### 7.2 Educational Content

#### Pain Is an Output, Not an Input

Here's the most important thing to understand about chronic pain:

**Pain is not a direct signal from your tissues. Pain is a decision made by your brain.**

Your body sends signals to your brain: "Hey, here's information about that knee." Your brain receives millions of signals every moment. Then your brain decides: Is this a threat? Do I need to send a pain signal?

In acute pain (like a fresh injury), this system is perfect. Tissue damage → pain signal → protection → healing.

But in chronic pain, the system can get miscalibrated. Your brain decides something is a threat even though the tissue is fine. Your brain sends a pain signal not because damage is happening, but because it learned to be protective.

This is crucial: **Pain does not equal tissue damage.**

Evidence:
- People with arthritis visible on X-ray often feel no pain
- People with no structural abnormalities on imaging have severe pain
- A small knee injury (visible on MRI) might hurt a lot
- A major knee injury (visible on MRI) might hurt a little
- Pain improves with PNE even though no tissue changed

Your brain sends pain based on:
- What it learned in the past
- What it expects
- How threatened it feels
- What you're paying attention to
- What you're thinking and feeling

#### Central Sensitization

Central sensitization means your nervous system has become "turned up" — it amplifies normal signals into pain.

Imagine a volume dial on a speaker. In a healthy nervous system, the dial is at 5. A normal signal comes in, gets amplified to a 5, and you feel normal sensation.

In central sensitization, the dial is at 9. The same normal signal gets amplified to a 9, and you feel pain.

What turns up the dial?
- Chronic stress
- Poor sleep
- Isolation
- Fear and catastrophizing
- Lack of movement
- Past trauma
- Sustained pain itself

What turns down the dial?
- Movement and exercise
- Good sleep
- Social connection
- Understanding pain (PNE)
- Relaxation and calm
- Correcting unhelpful thoughts
- Gradual exposure to feared activities

The good news: The dial can move. Your nervous system is plastic. It can change.

#### Neuroplasticity: Your Nervous System Can Change

Your brain is constantly rewiring itself based on experience. This is called neuroplasticity.

When you learn something new — like a song, a language, or a skill — your brain literally changes. Nerves form new connections. The brain area used for that skill grows.

The same is true for pain. When you:
- Do an activity repeatedly (even if it's initially feared)
- Build strength and confidence
- Correct your thoughts about what's dangerous
- Practice relaxation

...your brain rewires. Pain pathways weaken. Calm pathways strengthen. Over weeks and months, the system resets.

This is why graded exposure works. You're not "pushing through." You're teaching your brain: "This activity is safe. We don't need to send a pain signal anymore."

#### The Alarm System Metaphor

Think of your pain system as an alarm system in a house.

In acute injury, the alarm is perfect. There's a break-in (tissue damage). The alarm goes off. You call the police. The situation is handled. The alarm turns off.

In chronic pain, it's like an alarm that has a false trigger. The alarm goes off constantly, even when there's no real break-in. Maybe a shadow sets it off. Maybe the wind. The alarm keeps screaming.

What happens next?
- If you believe there's always an intruder, you stop leaving your house (fear-avoidance)
- If you keep running when the alarm goes off, your nervous system learns the alarm is important (reinforces it)
- If you ignore the alarm, research shows over time it stops going off

The goal is to:
1. Understand the alarm is overly sensitive (PNE)
2. Stop treating every alarm as a real threat (cognitive restructuring)
3. Gradually be in the situation the alarm fears (graded exposure)
4. Over time, the alarm resets

#### Graded Exposure and the "Volume Knob"

Another way to think about it: your nervous system has a volume knob for different activities. Some activities are turned way up (painful, feared). Some are turned down (safe, easy).

Graded exposure gradually turns down the volume on feared activities.

For example:
- Week 1: Think about walking. Volume: 6. Pain: 4.
- Week 2: Walk 2 minutes on flat ground. Volume: 5. Pain: 2.
- Week 3: Walk 5 minutes including slight incline. Volume: 4. Pain: 1.
- Week 4: Walk 10 minutes at normal pace. Volume: 2. Pain: 0.

You're not doing more because the activity is less harmful. You're doing more because your nervous system has learned it's safe.

> This happens all the time. In fact, studies show that what you see on an MRI or X-ray is a really poor predictor of pain. Why? Because pain is not determined by tissue damage. Pain is determined by what your brain thinks is happening.
>
> Here's another example: You're walking fine, and someone says something that scares you — 'That fall you took might have fractured something.' Suddenly your pain increases. No tissue changed. But your brain's threat level increased, and pain went up.
>
> Or: You're in pain, you do some relaxation breathing, and suddenly the pain decreases. Again, no tissue changed, but your threat level decreased.
>
> Your brain is constantly asking: 'Is this situation a threat? Do I need to send a pain signal to protect?'
>
> When it decides yes, you feel pain. When it decides no, pain decreases, even with the same physical condition.
>
> Now, here's where it gets really interesting. Your brain's threat detector can get too sensitive. Like a smoke alarm in a kitchen that goes off whenever you cook. It's trying to protect you, but it's way too sensitive.
>
> We call this central sensitization. The volume is turned up too high. Normal signals get amplified into pain.
>
> What turns up the volume?
> - Stress and worry
> - Poor sleep
> - Not moving (deconditioning)
> - Scary thoughts about pain
> - Isolation
> - Catastrophizing
>
> And here's the beautiful part: We can turn the volume down. What turns it down?
> - Movement and exercise (which teaches the brain the activity is safe)
> - Good sleep
> - Relaxation and calm
> - Social connection
> - Correcting scary thoughts (which we've been doing)
> - Understanding pain itself (what we're doing right now)
>
> Your brain is not broken. Your brain is trying to protect you. But it's being overly protective. The job of everything we've done — breathing, relaxation, changing thoughts, pacing, and movement — is to teach your brain: 'This is safe. You don't need to send so much pain.'
>
> This takes time. Your nervous system has been in protection mode for months or years. It takes weeks and months to retrain it. But it does change.
>
> The evidence is clear: when people do pain neuroscience education combined with movement and behavioral change, pain and disability improve significantly.
>
> This isn't hope. It's not 'positive thinking.' It's neuroscience. Your nervous system can change. And you have direct control over that change.
>
> Does this make sense?"

### 7.4 Interactive Exercise: Building Your Personal Pain Model

**Purpose:** Help the patient create their own understanding of their pain using PNE concepts.

**Kimi's Script:**

"Let's apply this to your pain. Think about something that increases your pain. It could be an activity, a thought, or a situation.

[Wait for response. Let's say they say 'Climbing stairs']

Okay, climbing stairs increases your pain. Using what you've learned about pain science, let's think about why.

The stairs themselves haven't changed. The staircase is the same as it's always been. But your brain sends a pain signal when you climb.

Why might that be? Let's look at all the factors:

**Physical factors:** The activity itself requires your muscles to work. Your joints move. That creates physical signals.

**Threat factors:** What does your brain think about climbing stairs?
- 'This will damage my knee' (threat thought)
- 'My balance might fail and I'll fall' (threat thought)
- 'This is going to hurt' (threat thought)

**History factors:** What happened the last time you climbed stairs?
- Did you have pain? (Yes, so the brain learned: stairs = pain)
- Did you have a bad outcome? (Maybe you fell, or had a flare-up, so: stairs = danger)

**Context factors:** What's happening around you?
- Are you stressed? (Yes, so your threat system is already on high alert)
- Are you tired? (Yes, so your nervous system is less able to regulate)
- Are you alone or with support? (Alone, so less reassurance)

Now, what if you changed some of these factors?

What if you climbed stairs with the thought: 'My legs are strong enough for this. Climbing builds strength.'

What if you climbed stairs with calm breathing, so your threat system wasn't activated?

What if you climbed stairs gradually, so your brain learned over time: 'This is safe'?

All of those change what happens in your brain. They don't change the stairs. They change the threat assessment. When the threat assessment goes down, pain goes down.

Does that make sense for your situation?"
