## FOUR-PHASE PROGRESSION FRAMEWORK

### Phase Definitions and PROMIS Thresholds

The spine exercise program uses PROMIS (Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System) to track progress and determine readiness for phase advancement. Patients are classified into four phases based on their current pain interference (PI) and physical function (PF) scores.

| Phase | Name | PROMIS PI Score | PROMIS PF Score | Primary Goals | Typical Duration |
|-------|------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Phase 1** | Acute Pain Management | ≥60 | ≤35 | Pain control, gentle mobility, neural calm | 2-4 weeks |
| **Phase 2** | Sub-Acute Stabilization | 55-59 | 36-40 | Motor control, core stability, postural awareness | 3-4 weeks |
| **Phase 3** | Functional Strengthening | 50-54 | 41-45 | Functional strength, endurance, activity tolerance | 4-6 weeks |
| **Phase 4** | Return-to-Activity | <50 | ≥46 | Sport/activity-specific training, confidence building | 4-8 weeks |

**PROMIS Minimal Clinically Important Difference (MCID)**: 3-5 points = meaningful improvement
**Progression Rule**: Advance when PROMIS PI decreases by ≥5 points AND patient reports functional improvement
**Regression Rule**: Return to previous phase if pain increases >2 points or function declines >3 points

## WARM-UP PROTOCOL (5-10 minutes)

Before starting spine exercises, every patient completes this structured warm-up:

1. **Gentle Marching in Place** (2 minutes)
   - Warm up the nervous system and increase circulation
   - Keep arms relaxed at sides
   - Focus on rhythmic breathing

2. **Arm Circles** (1 minute)
   - 30 seconds forward circles (small, gradually increasing)
   - 30 seconds backward circles
   - Prepares shoulder girdle and thoracic spine

3. **Cat-Cow Mobilization** (1-2 minutes)
   - 5-8 repetitions of spinal flexion-extension cycles
   - Activates deep core muscles
   - Warms up lumbar, thoracic, and cervical segments

4. **Pelvic Tilts** (1 minute)
   - 10 repetitions focusing on pelvic and lumbar motion
   - Prepares core stabilizers for exercise

5. **Cervical Range of Motion** (1 minute)
   - Gentle nodding (yes), head turns (no), ear to shoulder stretches
   - Warms up cervical spine without loading

**Safety Note**: Pain during warm-up >3/10 indicates you should modify intensity or consult provider before proceeding to main exercises.

---

## COOL-DOWN PROTOCOL (2-5 minutes)

After completing all exercises, cool-down is essential:

1. **Gentle Walking** (1 minute)
   - Slow, rhythmic walking to normalize heart rate
   - Allows neural system to settle

2. **Spinal Stretches** (3-4 minutes)
   - Child's pose (30 seconds) - spinal flexion stretch
   - Supine figure-4 stretch (30 seconds each side) - glute/IT band/lumbar stretch
   - Supine knee-to-chest stretch (30 seconds each side) - lumbar extension
   - Supine twist (30 seconds each side) - thoracic/lumbar rotation

3. **Breathing Exercise** (1 minute)
   - Slow, deep breathing (4-count in, 6-count out, 10 repetitions)
   - Activates parasympathetic nervous system

**Pain Monitoring**: If pain increases during cool-down, reduce intensity of stretches. Pain should return to baseline within 30 minutes.

## PAIN MONITORING DURING EXERCISE

Monitor pain response throughout exercise sessions using this protocol:

| Pain Level | NRS Rating | Response Classification | Recommended Action |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Safe/Green Zone** | 0-2/10 | Appropriate intensity | Continue exercise as prescribed |
| **Acceptable/Yellow Zone** | 3-4/10 | Moderate effort response | Monitor closely; reduce if trending upward |
| **High Risk/Red Zone** | ≥5/10 | Excessive pain response | STOP exercise immediately; rest and reassess |

**Post-Exercise Assessment** (within 2 hours):
- **Appropriate Response**: Pain returns to baseline or lower
- **Overdosing Sign**: Pain remains elevated or worsens next day (reduce volume by 20%)
- **Under-dosing Sign**: Pain completely absent with no fatigue (increase intensity slightly)

**When to Hold Exercise**:
- Acute flare-up (pain >6/10 at rest for >3 days)
- New neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness)
- Recent injection or medical procedure (<48 hours)
- Acute illness or infection

## LUMBAR SPINE EXERCISES (PHASE 1-4)

### L1: PELVIC TILTS

**Target Muscle(s)**: Transverse abdominis, internal obliques, rectus abdominis, erector spinae (eccentric control)

**Position**: Supine (lying on back) with knees bent, feet flat on floor

**Clinical Relevance**: Pelvic tilts establish the foundation for core stability. They teach the brain how to control the deep abdominal muscles that stabilize the lumbar spine. This is essential before advancing to more complex exercises. Patients with lumbar pain often have weak or poorly coordinated core muscles; pelvic tilts help restore this control pattern.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat, about hip-width apart
2. Place one hand under the small of your back to feel the curve
3. Tighten your lower abdominal muscles (the area below your belly button)
4. Tilt your pelvis so the small of your back flattens against your hand
5. Hold for 2 seconds while breathing normally
6. Relax and return to starting position
7. Repeat for prescribed repetitions

**Dosage**: 
- Phase 1: 10 reps, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 15 reps, 2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 20 reps, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: Progress to L3 Dead Bug

**Tempo**: 2 seconds tighten, 2 seconds hold, 2 seconds release

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Place hand on lower belly to help feel muscle contraction; reduce hold time to 1 second
- **Harder**: Add 1-second pause at end of tilt; increase hold to 3 seconds; progress to bridges (L4)

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel muscle fatigue in lower belly, not pain in low back. If back pain increases, reduce hold time to 1 second.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Holding breath during the exercise (ALWAYS breathe)
2. Squeezing the glute muscles instead of the abdominals (feel lower belly tighten)
3. Tilting too aggressively (small, controlled movement)
4. Arching back when releasing (controlled relaxation)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Think of tucking your tailbone slightly, like you're trying to prevent spilling water from a cup balanced on your low back. Your back should make gentle contact with your hand. This is the starting position for almost every core exercise you'll do."

**Video Search**: "Pelvic tilts lumbar spine" or "Core activation pelvic tilts"

### L2: DEAD BUG

**Target Muscle(s)**: Transverse abdominis, rectus abdominis, deep spinal stabilizers, diaphragm

**Position**: Supine with arms extended toward ceiling, hips and knees bent 90 degrees

**Clinical Relevance**: Dead bug teaches the core to stabilize the spine while moving opposite limbs. This mimics real-life activities like reaching and walking. It's a critical progression from pelvic tilts because it adds limb movement without spinal extension or flexion stress.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie on your back with arms pointing straight up toward the ceiling
2. Bend hips and knees to 90 degrees (shins parallel to floor)
3. Take a breath in to prepare your core
4. As you exhale, slowly lower your right arm behind your head while straightening your left leg
5. Keep your lower back in contact with the floor throughout
6. Return to starting position as you inhale
7. Repeat on the opposite side (left arm back, right leg straight)

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: Not typically prescribed (too advanced)
- Phase 2: 8 reps each side, 1-2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 12 reps each side, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 15 reps each side, 3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to lower, 1 second hold, 2 seconds to return

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Keep knees bent instead of straightening leg; reduce range of arm motion
- **Harder**: Straighten both legs; lower both arms and legs together; add 1-second hold at bottom

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel abdominal engagement. Lower back should never leave the floor. If it arches, reduce range of motion.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Arching the lower back away from floor (sign core isn't engaged)
2. Moving too quickly (controlled, coordinated movement)
3. Holding breath (continuous breathing throughout)
4. Both sides moving at same time initially (alternating pattern first)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Pretend your belly button is connected to your spine. As you move your arm and leg, keep that connection tight. Your lower back should stay glued to the floor like it's superglued there."

**Video Search**: "Dead bug exercise core stability" or "Supine dead bug lumbar"

### L3: BRIDGES

**Target Muscle(s)**: Gluteus maximus, gluteus medius, erector spinae, transverse abdominis

**Position**: Supine with knees bent, feet flat, hip-width apart

**Clinical Relevance**: Bridges strengthen the posterior chain (glutes and back extensors) which is essential for upright posture and preventing forward flexion-based lumbar pain. Weak glutes force the low back to compensate, perpetuating pain. Bridges also teach hip extension without lumbar hyperextension.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat on the floor
2. Position feet about hip-width apart, parallel to each other
3. Place arms at sides, palms down for stability
4. Press through your heels to lift your hips off the floor
5. Raise hips until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders
6. Squeeze your glute muscles at the top
7. Hold for 2 seconds
8. Lower hips back to floor with control
9. Rest briefly and repeat

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 8 reps, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 12 reps, 2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 15 reps, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: Progress to single-leg bridges or add resistance band

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to lift, 2 seconds hold, 2 seconds to lower

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce hold time to 1 second; place feet closer to glutes (shorter range)
- **Harder**: Single-leg bridge; add resistance band across thighs; hold for 3-5 seconds; march legs while holding bridge

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel glute muscles working. Should NOT feel low back strain. If lower back hurts, reduce height of lift or decrease hold time.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Pushing through toes instead of heels (shifts work to quads)
2. Hiking one hip higher than the other (uneven glute activation)
3. Hyperextending the low back (form straight line from knees to shoulders)
4. Squeezing glutes TOO hard (controlled, functional contraction)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Think about pushing your heels through the floor, like you're trying to move the floor away from you. Your glutes should do most of the work, not your lower back. At the top, imagine you're balancing a bowl of soup on your hips without spilling it."

**Video Search**: "Glute bridge exercise" or "Hip bridge lumbar stability"

### L4: BIRD DOG

**Target Muscle(s)**: Transverse abdominis, erector spinae, gluteus maximus, deep core stabilizers, quadratus lumborum

**Position**: Quadruped (hands and knees)

**Clinical Relevance**: Bird dog is one of the most important exercises for lumbar stability. It trains the core to stabilize the spine against perturbations (disturbances) during opposite limb movement. This replicates walking and reaching patterns. Bird dog is highly recommended in APTA clinical practice guidelines.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Start on hands and knees with wrists under shoulders, knees under hips
2. Engage your core by gently bracing your abdominal muscles
3. Extend your right arm forward and left leg backward simultaneously
4. Arm should be at ear level, leg at hip height (don't arch the back)
5. Your body should form a straight line from extended arm to extended leg
6. Hold for 2 seconds
7. Return arm and leg to starting position
8. Repeat on the opposite side

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 6 reps each side, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 10 reps each side, 2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 12 reps each side, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 15 reps each side with 3-second hold; add resistance band

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to extend, 2 seconds hold, 2 seconds to return

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Lift arm only (keep knee on ground); lift leg only (keep hand down); reduce hold time to 1 second
- **Harder**: Hold for 3-5 seconds; add resistance band around leg; add slight hip abduction/adduction movements while extended

**Pain Monitoring**: Core muscles should feel engaged. Spine should feel stable, not twisted or arched. If lower back pain develops, reduce range or return to easier version.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Arching the lower back excessively (keep spine neutral)
2. Rotating the torso (keep hips square to floor)
3. Dropping the extended limbs quickly (controlled lowering)
4. Not engaging core before moving (brace first, then move)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Imagine your spine is a tall stack of coins. As you extend your arm and leg, keep those coins perfectly stacked—no twisting, no arching. Your core should feel like a tight corset holding everything in place."

**Video Search**: "Bird dog exercise" or "Quadruped bird dog core stability"

### L5: CAT-COW MOBILIZATION

**Target Muscle(s)**: Erector spinae, rectus abdominis, intercostal muscles, thoracic paraspinals

**Position**: Quadruped (hands and knees)

**Clinical Relevance**: Cat-cow combines spinal flexion and extension in a controlled, rhythmic pattern. This mobilizes the entire spine, improves segmental motion, and teaches coordinated core engagement. Research supports dynamic mobility exercises for chronic low back pain.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Start on hands and knees (wrists under shoulders, knees under hips)
2. In the "cat" phase: Round your spine, tuck your tailbone, and lower your head
   - Draw belly button toward spine
   - Shoulders come forward toward hands
   - Feel a stretch along entire spine
3. In the "cow" phase: Arch your lower back, lift your tailbone, and gaze slightly upward
   - Chest comes forward between hands
   - Lower belly sags toward floor
   - Feel a gentle extension stretch
4. Flow smoothly between cat and cow with your breath

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 8-10 cycles, 1 set, daily (as warm-up)
- Phase 2: 10-12 cycles, 1-2 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 3: 12-15 cycles, 2 sets, 5x/week (as warm-up or mobility work)
- Phase 4: 15 cycles, 2 sets, 3x/week (as warm-up)

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to cat, 2 seconds to cow (continuous flowing motion)

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce range of motion; do flexion only (cat phase) or extension only (cow phase) initially
- **Harder**: Add arm reaches during cow phase; add spinal rotation in addition to flexion/extension

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel gentle spinal movement. Avoid end-range pain. If pain limits movement, reduce range and progress slowly.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Bouncing at end-range (smooth, controlled motion)
2. Moving too fast (coordinate with breathing)
3. Holding breath (continuous breathing throughout)
4. Over-extending in cow phase (gentle arch only)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Imagine your spine is a chain. In the cat position, every link of that chain is pulling apart. In the cow position, your spine gently arches like a wave. Move smoothly back and forth, like you're waving through your entire spine."

**Video Search**: "Cat-cow stretch" or "Cat-cow spinal mobility"

### L6: PARTIAL CURL-UPS

**Target Muscle(s)**: Rectus abdominis, external obliques, transverse abdominis

**Position**: Supine with knees bent, feet flat

**Clinical Relevance**: Partial curl-ups build abdominal endurance needed for functional activities. Unlike full sit-ups, partial curl-ups avoid excessive lumbar flexion stress. They're recommended in APTA guidelines for patients without centralization symptoms.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie on your back with knees bent and feet flat
2. Place hands behind your head with elbows out to the sides
3. Engage your core by drawing belly button toward spine
4. Curl your upper back (not your entire spine) off the floor
5. Lift just the shoulders and upper chest—NOT the low back
6. Go up only 3-4 inches (partial range)
7. Hold for 1 second
8. Lower with control back to starting position
9. The curve in your lower back should NOT change

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: Not typically prescribed
- Phase 2: 10 reps, 1 set, alternate days
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2 sets, 4x/week
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to curl, 1 second hold, 2 seconds to lower

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Rest head on small pillow; place hands across chest; reduce range of motion
- **Harder**: Hands behind head with elbows pointing forward; add holds at top (3-5 seconds); perform oblique curl-ups (curl toward opposite knee)

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel abdominal muscles working. If low back pain develops, reduce range of motion significantly or return to dead bug (L2).

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Pulling on the head/neck (hands just for head support)
2. Curling the entire spine (upper back only)
3. Lifting low back off floor (it stays on floor)
4. Moving too quickly (controlled movement)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"You're not doing a full sit-up. You're just peeling your shoulders off the floor a few inches. Think of rolling a ball with your upper back—small movement, controlled pace. Your low back is a passenger, not the driver."

**Video Search**: "Partial curl-ups" or "Upper abdominal crunch"

### L7: PRONE PRESS-UPS (McKENZIE)

**Target Muscle(s)**: Spinal extensors, gluteus maximus, posterior core

**Position**: Prone (lying on stomach)

**Clinical Relevance**: Prone press-ups are a McKenzie Method exercise indicated for patients with centralization symptoms (pain moving from leg toward back/spine). They extend the lumbar spine, which can reduce nerve irritation in some patients. Critical: Use ONLY if directional preference assessment shows centralization with extension.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie face-down on the floor with legs extended
2. Place palms flat under your shoulders (like bottom of push-up position)
3. Keep hips on the floor and press the upper body up with your arms
4. Let your lower back relax and sag slightly
5. Push up as much as comfortable—progress from forearms to hands as tolerated
6. Hold for 1-2 seconds
7. Lower back down with control
8. Rest briefly before next repetition

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 10 reps, 1 set, 3-5x/week (if appropriate)
- Phase 2: 12 reps, 1-2 sets, 3-5x/week
- Phase 3: 15 reps, 2 sets, 5x/week; may hold top position for 2-3 seconds
- Phase 4: Progress to push-ups or other extension-based exercises

**Tempo**: 1-2 seconds to press up, 1 second hold, 1-2 seconds to lower

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Support on forearms only (not full arm extension); reduce range of motion
- **Harder**: Push up fully to extended arms (like a push-up); add hold at top for 2-3 seconds; perform in standing position against wall

**Pain Monitoring**: THIS IS CRITICAL. Prone press-ups are ONLY appropriate if pain centralizes (moves from leg/side toward center of spine). If pain peripheralizes (spreads down leg), STOP immediately and do NOT continue. Pain should decrease or centralize with this exercise.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Using press-ups when NOT indicated (must assess directional preference first)
2. Pressing up too aggressively (gradual progression)
3. Continuing when pain peripheralizes (use centralization assessment)
4. Moving too quickly (controlled tempo)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"McKenzie press-ups are special—they're only right for some people with certain types of back pain. They work by gently extending your spine. If you feel pain moving away from your leg and more toward your spine, that's a GOOD sign. But if you feel pain spreading down your leg, STOP and contact your care team."

**Video Search**: "McKenzie prone press-ups" or "Extension exercises low back pain"

### L8: PLANK HOLD (PROGRESSIVE)

**Target Muscle(s)**: Transverse abdominis, rectus abdominis, erector spinae, shoulder stabilizers

**Position**: Forearm plank or full plank

**Clinical Relevance**: Plank holds develop core endurance needed for sustained postures and activities. Unlike dynamic exercises, planks train the core to stabilize against gravity for extended periods. Essential for functional capacity building.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie face-down with forearms on the floor
2. Elbows positioned directly under shoulders
3. Engage core by bracing abdominal muscles
4. Push up through forearms to lift hips off floor
5. Body should form straight line from head to heels
6. Don't let hips sag (hips should be at same height as shoulders)
7. Hold this position while breathing normally

**Dosage**:
- Phase 2: 10-15 second hold, 2-3 reps, alternate days
- Phase 3: 20-30 second hold, 2-3 reps, 4x/week; progress to 45-60 seconds
- Phase 4: 45-60 second holds, 3 reps, 3x/week; advance to full plank on hands

**Tempo**: Hold static position with controlled breathing (don't hold breath)

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Wall plank (hands on wall); inclined plank (hands on bench); plank on knees
- **Harder**: Full plank on hands; side plank; plank with alternating arm lifts; plank with hip dips

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel core muscles working throughout. Pain should NOT develop in lower back. If back pain occurs, reduce hold time or drop to knees.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Hips sagging (maintains alignment throughout)
2. Holding breath (continuous steady breathing)
3. Pushing too hard too soon (gradual progression of hold time)
4. Looking up excessively (neutral spine position)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"A plank is like being a strong table. Your entire body is one solid surface—from your head to your heels. Nothing sags, nothing sways. Keep your core tight, breathe steadily, and hold that perfect alignment. You're building the endurance your back needs for everyday life."

**Video Search**: "Forearm plank exercise" or "Plank hold core strengthening"

## THORACIC SPINE EXERCISES (PHASE 1-4)

### T1: WALL ANGELS

**Target Muscle(s)**: Rhomboids, lower trapezius, serratus anterior, rotator cuff

**Position**: Supine with back against wall

**Clinical Relevance**: Wall angels improve thoracic mobility and scapular control. Poor thoracic mobility forces the lumbar spine to compensate, contributing to low back pain. Wall angels restore normal scapular movement patterns.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Lie on your back with your back against a wall (or on floor)
2. Bend elbows to 90 degrees, with upper arms at shoulder height
3. Press elbows, wrists, and back of head against the wall/floor
4. Slowly slide arms upward toward your head (like making a "Y" shape)
5. Keep contact with the wall throughout movement
6. Return arms down to starting position with control
7. Repeat smoothly

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 8-10 reps, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 12 reps, 2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 15 reps, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to slide up, 2 seconds to slide down

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce range of motion (don't slide all way up); place on carpeted floor for less friction
- **Harder**: Increase range of motion; hold 1-2 seconds at top; perform against wall in standing position

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel thoracic and shoulder muscles working. Should NOT cause shoulder or neck pain. If shoulder pain develops, reduce range of motion.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Losing contact with wall (maintain contact throughout)
2. Shrugging shoulders (shoulders stay relaxed)
3. Moving too fast (controlled, smooth motion)
4. Over-extending arms (stop before feeling pinching)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Imagine your arms are making an angel in the snow against the wall. Slide them up and down slowly, keeping your hands and elbows touching the wall the whole time. This opens up your chest and thoracic spine."

**Video Search**: "Wall angels exercise" or "Scapular wall slides"

---

### T2: THORACIC EXTENSION

**Target Muscle(s)**: Thoracic erector spinae, latissimus dorsi, posterior deltoid

**Position**: Quadruped or prone

**Clinical Relevance**: Thoracic extension mobility is essential for upright posture. Most people spend too much time in flexion (hunched forward). Thoracic extension exercises counteract this and reduce stress on the lumbar spine.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Start on hands and knees (quadruped position)
2. Place one hand behind your head with elbow pointing out
3. Drop your elbow toward your opposite knee (rotation and flexion)
4. Then extend your elbow upward, opening your chest toward the ceiling
5. Your thoracic spine gently arches as you extend
6. Return to starting position with control
7. Complete repetitions on one side, then switch sides

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 8 reps each side, 1 set, alternate days
- Phase 2: 10 reps each side, 2 sets, alternate days
- Phase 3: 12 reps each side, 2-3 sets, 4x/week
- Phase 4: 15 reps each side, 3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to close (flexion/rotation), 2 seconds to open (extension)

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce range of rotation/flexion phase
- **Harder**: Increase range of motion; add 1-2 second hold in extended position; perform in half-kneeling position

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel thoracic muscles working. Avoid pinching sensation in mid-back. If pain develops, reduce range of motion.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Not achieving adequate extension (open chest fully)
2. Moving too quickly (controlled motion)
3. Excessive lumbar motion (thoracic spine is the mover)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Think of your thoracic spine opening like a book. In the closed position, you're folding the pages together. In the open position, you're spreading the book wide open. Your mid-back should gently arch as you open."

**Video Search**: "Thoracic extension exercise" or "Quadruped thoracic rotation extension"

---

### T3: QUADRUPED SPINAL ROTATION

**Target Muscle(s)**: Oblique abdominals, transverse abdominis, thoracic rotators, serratus anterior

**Position**: Quadruped with one hand behind head

**Clinical Relevance**: Spinal rotation mobility is critical for functional activities like looking over your shoulder, swinging a golf club, or picking up objects. Thoracic restrictions force lumbar spine to compensate.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Start on hands and knees (quadruped position)
2. Place right hand behind your head with elbow pointing out
3. Rotate your right elbow toward your left arm (body flexes and rotates)
4. Then rotate your right elbow toward the ceiling, opening your chest
5. Your eyes follow your elbow throughout the motion
6. Lower back to starting position with control
7. Complete reps on one side, then switch sides

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 8 reps each side, 1 set, alternate days
- Phase 2: 10 reps each side, 2 sets, alternate days
- Phase 3: 12 reps each side, 2-3 sets, 4x/week
- Phase 4: 15 reps each side, 3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to rotate closed, 2 seconds to rotate open

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce range of rotation; keep lower back very still
- **Harder**: Increase rotational range; add 1-2 second hold at end-range; perform standing

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel oblique and thoracic muscles working. Avoid twisting sensation in low back. If low back pain develops, reduce rotation range.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Rotating lumbar spine instead of thoracic (keep low back stable)
2. Moving too quickly (smooth, controlled rotation)
3. Not achieving full rotation range (open chest toward ceiling)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Your lower back is staying still while your upper back and ribs rotate. Think of wringing out a wet towel—the twist comes from your mid-back, not your low back. As you open up, your elbow reaches toward the ceiling and your eyes follow."

**Video Search**: "Quadruped spinal rotation" or "Thoracic rotation exercise"

## CERVICAL SPINE EXERCISES (PHASE 1-4)

### C1: CHIN TUCKS

**Target Muscle(s)**: Deep cervical flexors, lower trapezius, anterior neck stabilizers

**Position**: Supine or sitting upright

**Clinical Relevance**: Chin tucks strengthen the deep cervical flexors, which are often weak in patients with neck pain and forward head posture. This is one of the most important exercises for cervical spine stability. The deep cervical flexors are difficult to access with other exercises.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:
1. Sit upright or lie on your back (both positions work)
2. Look straight ahead with neutral spine
3. Gently draw your chin straight backward (like making a "double chin")
4. Do NOT look down or up
5. Hold this position for 5 seconds while breathing normally
6. Return to neutral position
7. Relax and repeat

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 10 reps, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 12 reps, 2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 15 reps, 2-3 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3x/week; add 10-second holds

**Tempo**: 2 seconds to tuck, 5 seconds hold, 2 seconds to release

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Hold for 2-3 seconds only; perform with biofeedback from therapist or mirror
- **Harder**: Hold for 10 seconds; add gentle resistance from hand; combine with shoulder blade squeezes

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel muscles activate in front of neck. Should NOT cause neck pain. If pain develops, reduce hold time or consult provider.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Looking down instead of keeping neutral gaze (straight ahead)
2. Tilting head backward (chin draws straight back)
3. Over-correcting into exaggerated posture (gentle, subtle movement)
4. Holding breath (continuous normal breathing)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"A chin tuck is the opposite of forward head posture. Imagine there's a string pulling your chin straight backward—not up, not down, but straight back like a drawer sliding into a cabinet. You should feel muscles tighten in the front of your neck. It's a small movement but very powerful."

**Video Search**: "Chin tucks exercise" or "Deep cervical flexor training"

### C2: CERVICAL RANGE OF MOTION

**Target Muscle(s)**: Cervical erector spinae, sternocleidomastoid, scalenes, upper trapezius

**Position**: Sitting upright with good posture

**Clinical Relevance**: Cervical ROM exercises maintain normal movement patterns and prevent stiffness. They're appropriate for most neck pain conditions and serve as an excellent warm-up.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

**Flexion-Extension**:
1. Sit upright with shoulders relaxed
2. Slowly nod your head forward (like saying "yes")
3. Return to neutral, then gently extend head backward
4. Move slowly and only to point of mild stretch

**Rotation**:
1. From neutral position, slowly turn head to look over right shoulder
2. Return to center, then turn to look over left shoulder
3. Move slowly and controlled

**Lateral Flexion**:
1. From neutral, slowly bring right ear toward right shoulder
2. Return to center, then bring left ear toward left shoulder
3. Don't force; stop at comfortable stretch

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 5-8 reps each direction, 1 set, daily
- Phase 2: 8-10 reps each direction, 1-2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 10-12 reps each direction, 2 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 12-15 reps each direction, 2-3 sets, 3x/week

**Tempo**: 2-3 seconds in each direction

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce range of motion; move only in one direction (flexion-extension first)
- **Harder**: Hold end-range for 2-3 seconds; add gentle hand resistance; perform slow, continuous circles

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel gentle stretching. Avoid sharp pain or radicular symptoms (shooting pain down arm). If arm symptoms develop, STOP and contact provider.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Bouncing at end-range (smooth, controlled movement)
2. Moving too quickly (slow, deliberate motion)
3. Forcing into painful ranges (gentle stretching only)
4. Isolating movement to head only (move from neck base)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Your neck is made up of seven small bones (vertebrae) that need to move in all directions. These gentle movements remind each bone how to move properly and prevent stiffness. Imagine your movements are very slow and smooth, like treacle flowing."

**Video Search**: "Cervical range of motion exercises" or "Neck flexion extension rotation"

### C3: CERVICAL ISOMETRIC STRENGTHENING

**Target Muscle(s)**: Cervical erector spinae, deep cervical flexors, lateral neck stabilizers

**Position**: Sitting upright

**Clinical Relevance**: Isometric exercises build strength without moving the joints. This is ideal for acute neck pain when movement might be limited. Isometric work activates muscle without pain provocation.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

**Forward Resistance**:
1. Sit upright with shoulders relaxed
2. Place one hand on your forehead
3. Gently push your forehead into your hand (resist with hand)
4. Maintain this position for 5 seconds—NO MOVEMENT
5. The effort comes from your neck muscles, not your hand pushing
6. Relax and rest 5 seconds
7. Repeat 5-8 times

**Backward Resistance**:
1. Place hand on back of head
2. Gently push head backward into hand (hand resists)
3. Hold for 5 seconds with no movement
4. Relax and rest 5 seconds
5. Repeat 5-8 times

**Lateral Resistance**:
1. Place hand on right side of head (temple area)
2. Gently push head toward right against hand resistance
3. Hold for 5 seconds (no movement)
4. Relax and rest 5 seconds
5. Repeat 5-8 times, then switch to left side

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: 5-8 reps, 1 set, daily (all directions)
- Phase 2: 8-10 reps, 1-2 sets, daily
- Phase 3: 10-12 reps, 2 sets, 5x/week
- Phase 4: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3x/week; increase hold to 10 seconds

**Tempo**: 5-second sustained contraction against resistance

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce hold time to 2-3 seconds; use less force
- **Harder**: Increase hold to 10 seconds; increase resistance; perform all directions in sequence without rest

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel muscle contraction in neck. Should NOT cause pain. If neck pain develops, reduce force or hold time.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Allowing movement (isometric means NO movement)
2. Using excessive force (gentle, controlled effort)
3. Holding breath (continuous breathing throughout)
4. Not maintaining neutral spine (upright posture throughout)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"Isometric means you're creating muscle tension WITHOUT movement. Your neck muscles are working hard against your hand, but your head doesn't actually move. It's like pushing a wall—the wall doesn't move, but you feel the effort. This strengthens your neck without stressing the joints."

**Video Search**: "Cervical isometric strengthening" or "Neck isometric exercises"

### C4: SCALENE AND UPPER TRAPEZIUS STRETCHES

**Target Muscle(s)**: Scalene muscles (anterior, medial, posterior), upper trapezius, sternocleidomastoid

**Position**: Sitting or standing

**Clinical Relevance**: Tight scalenes and upper trapezius contribute to neck pain and can cause referred pain down the arm (mimicking nerve compression). Stretching these muscles reduces muscular tension and improves cervical mobility.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

**Scalene Stretch**:
1. Sit upright with shoulders relaxed
2. Tilt head gently to the right (right ear toward right shoulder)
3. Add gentle hand pressure on the left side of head, increasing the stretch
4. Hold for 20-30 seconds and breathe
5. Relax and return to neutral
6. Repeat on the left side

**Upper Trapezius Stretch**:
1. Sit upright
2. Gently pull head toward right shoulder with right hand on head
3. Add rotation (turn nose downward slightly toward right armpit)
4. Hold for 20-30 seconds
5. Return to neutral, repeat on left side

**Dosage**:
- Phase 1: Hold 15-20 seconds, 2 reps each side, daily
- Phase 2: Hold 20-30 seconds, 2 reps each side, daily
- Phase 3: Hold 30 seconds, 3 reps each side, 5x/week
- Phase 4: Hold 30 seconds, 3 reps each side, 3x/week

**Tempo**: Slow, sustained stretching (no bouncing)

**Modifications**:
- **Easier**: Reduce stretch duration; reduce stretch intensity (gentler pull)
- **Harder**: Increase duration to 45-60 seconds; combine stretches (rotation + lateral flexion); add gentle hand resistance

**Pain Monitoring**: Should feel comfortable stretch. Avoid sharp pain or radiating symptoms. If arm numbness/tingling develops, STOP and contact provider.

**Common Mistakes**:
1. Bouncing at end-range (smooth, sustained stretch)
2. Using excessive force (gentle pulling only)
3. Turning neck too much (primarily lateral flexion)
4. Holding breath (continuous relaxed breathing)

**Kimi Coaching Tips**:
"The scalenes are small muscles on the sides of your neck that get tight from stress and poor posture. This stretch gently lengthens them. You should feel a comfortable pulling sensation along the side and back of your neck, not pain. Breathe and relax into the stretch."

**Video Search**: "Scalene stretch" or "Upper trapezius stretch neck"

## McKENZIE DIRECTIONAL PREFERENCE ASSESSMENT

The McKenzie Method is a mechanical diagnosis and therapy approach that classifies spinal pain based on how it responds to directional movements (flexion vs. extension vs. side-gliding).

### Assessment Protocol

**Before prescribing prone press-ups or extension-based exercises**, perform this quick assessment:

**Step 1: Flexion Bias Assessment** (5 minutes)
- Patient stands and performs repeated forward bending
- Observe: Does pain centralize (move from leg toward spine) or peripheralize (move away from spine)?
- Document starting pain location and change pattern

**Step 2: Extension Bias Assessment** (5 minutes)
- Patient lies prone and performs repeated press-ups (starting gently)
- Observe: Does pain centralize or peripheralize?
- Document response

**Step 3: Lateral Shift Assessment** (2-3 minutes)
- Patient performs side-gliding movements in standing
- Observe: Which direction reduces pain?
- Document preferred direction

### Response Classifications

| Response | Meaning | Exercise Approach |
|----------|---------|------------------|
| **Centralization** | Pain moves from distal to proximal (e.g., leg → back) | Continue that direction; strong prognostic sign |
| **Peripheralization** | Pain moves from proximal to distal (e.g., back → leg) | STOP that direction; try opposite |
| **No Change** | Pain stays same regardless of direction | Less mechanical; consider other approaches |
| **Rapid Centralization** | Pain reduces significantly with direction | Excellent prognosis; use that direction |

### Clinical Decision-Making

**If Extension Centralizes**: Use extension-based exercises (prone press-ups, standing extension, lying back extensions). Avoid flexion exercises.

**If Flexion Helps**: Use flexion-based exercises (knee-to-chest, curl-ups) and avoid extension.

**If Lateral Shift Evident**: Perform initial lateral correction, then reassess directional preference.

**Important**: Centralization is a powerful prognostic indicator. Patients showing rapid centralization with extension typically respond well and quickly to extension-based therapy.

## GRADED WALKING PROGRAM (12-WEEK PROGRESSION)

Walking is one of the most evidence-based aerobic exercises for chronic spinal pain. This 12-week progression gradually increases duration and intensity.

### Walking Program Overview

Walking activates core muscles, improves aerobic capacity, and can reduce pain through various mechanisms (endorphin release, neural modulation, improved function). The program progresses duration first, then intensity.

### Weeks 1-2: FOUNDATION PHASE

**Goal**: Establish walking habit; build baseline endurance

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Pace | Distance | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------|------|----------|-------|
| 1 | 3x/week | 10 min | Comfortable conversation pace | 0.3-0.5 miles | Easy, flat terrain |
| 2 | 3x/week | 12 min | Comfortable conversation pace | 0.4-0.6 miles | Introduce slight variations |

**Coaching**: "Start slowly and comfortably. Walking should feel enjoyable, not exhausting. You should be able to have a conversation while walking. This builds your aerobic base."

### Weeks 3-4: DURATION BUILDING PHASE

**Goal**: Increase walking duration to 20 minutes

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Pace | Distance | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------|------|----------|-------|
| 3 | 4x/week | 15 min | Comfortable conversation pace | 0.6-0.8 miles | Mix terrain |
| 4 | 4x/week | 18 min | Comfortable conversation pace | 0.8-1.0 miles | Introduce gentle hills |

**Coaching**: "Your endurance is building. If walking still feels easy at the end, you can add a few extra minutes. Listen to your body—if pain is increasing, stay at 15 minutes longer."

### Weeks 5-7: INTERMEDIATE PHASE

**Goal**: Reach 30-minute walking duration

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Pace | Distance | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------|------|----------|-------|
| 5 | 4x/week | 20 min | Moderate pace (slightly faster) | 1.0-1.25 miles | Include varied terrain |
| 6 | 4-5x/week | 25 min | Moderate pace | 1.25-1.5 miles | Challenge yourself slightly |
| 7 | 5x/week | 30 min | Moderate pace | 1.5+ miles | Maintenance distance |

**Coaching**: "You're walking longer now. Try picking a slightly faster pace—you should feel like you're working moderately hard. You could still talk, but you'd need to take breath between sentences."

### Weeks 8-10: INTENSITY BUILDING PHASE

**Goal**: Increase intensity while maintaining duration

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Pace | Distance | Intensity | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------|------|----------|-----------|-------|
| 8 | 5x/week | 30 min | Moderate-brisk | 1.5+ miles | Include hills | 1-2 hills per walk |
| 9 | 5x/week | 30-35 min | Brisk | 1.75+ miles | Varied terrain | More challenging hills |
| 10 | 5x/week | 35 min | Brisk | 2+ miles | Tempo intervals | Add 2-3 min faster intervals |

**Coaching**: "You can walk longer and faster now. Try including some hills—they strengthen your legs and core. If you can, add 2-3 minutes of faster walking in the middle of your walk."

### Weeks 11-12: ADVANCED PHASE

**Goal**: Maintain fitness; vary walking for continued engagement

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Pace | Distance | Format | Notes |
|------|-----------|----------|------|----------|--------|-------|
| 11 | 5-6x/week | 35-40 min | Variable | 2+ miles | Mix easy/moderate | 2-3 moderate walks, 2-3 easier walks, 1-2 faster walks |
| 12 | 5-6x/week | 35-40 min | Variable | 2+ miles | Varied routes | Different routes; change pace variation |

**Coaching**: "You've built a strong walking foundation. Now maintain it by varying your walks—some easy, some faster, different routes. This keeps it interesting and continues building your fitness."

### Walking Pain Monitoring

**Safe Response**: Mild leg fatigue during walk, return to baseline within 1-2 hours

**Acceptable Response**: Mild pain that centralizes (moves toward spine), improving over the walk

**Red Flag Responses** (STOP and reassess):
- Severe leg pain that worsens throughout walk
- Pain that peripheralizes (moves down leg) during walk
- Numbness or tingling in leg
- Feeling of instability or leg giving way

### Walking Modifications

**If Pain Increases**: Reduce duration back to previous week; reduce intensity; walk more frequently for shorter durations (e.g., 3 x 15-min walks instead of 1 x 35-min walk)

**If No Progress**: Stay at current level 2 additional weeks; ensure adequate recovery between walks; consider adding other exercises

**If Walking Worsens Leg Pain**: May need McKenzie assessment; consider aquatic walking as alternative

## EMERGENCY STOP RULES

**IMMEDIATELY STOP ALL EXERCISE AND CONTACT YOUR PROVIDER IF**:

1. **Acute Neurological Symptoms**:
   - Numbness/tingling in genital area, anus, or both legs
   - Loss of bladder or bowel control
   - Progressive leg weakness
   - Both legs affected simultaneously

2. **Significant Pain Increase**:
   - Pain suddenly increases to 8/10 or higher
   - Pain that wakes you from sleep
   - Pain with fever (possible infection)

3. **New Symptoms**:
   - New arm numbness/weakness (not present at baseline)
   - Dizziness or vertigo with exercise
   - Visual changes

4. **Signs of Serious Pathology**:
   - Unexplained weight loss
   - Night sweats
   - Pain in multiple joints simultaneously

**MODIFICATION RULES**:

If pain increases to 5-6/10 during exercise, reduce volume by 20% (fewer reps, shorter duration) and regress to previous phase exercises. If pain doesn't improve within 3-5 sessions at reduced intensity, contact provider.

## RETURN-TO-ACTIVITY CLEARANCE CRITERIA

Before returning to sports, recreational activities, or heavy work, patients should meet these criteria:

### Functional Capacity Testing (Minimum Standards)

**Strength**:
- [ ] Single-leg stance: 30+ seconds each leg
- [ ] 30-second sit-to-stand: ≥15 repetitions
- [ ] Prone plank hold: 60+ seconds
- [ ] Single-leg mini squats: 15 repetitions each leg

**Endurance**:
- [ ] Walking: 45+ minutes at brisk pace
- [ ] Stairs: Can climb 3+ flights without symptoms

**Flexibility**:
- [ ] Forward bend: Can reach toward toes (within 6 inches)
- [ ] Cervical ROM: Full pain-free range in all directions

**Balance**:
- [ ] Single-leg stance: 30 seconds eyes closed
- [ ] Tandem stance (heel-to-toe): 30 seconds
- [ ] Single-leg mini squats: 15 reps each side

### Psychosocial Readiness

- [ ] Confidence in managing symptoms (Pain Self-Efficacy Questionnaire ≥50)
- [ ] Anxiety level acceptable (not catastrophizing about pain)
- [ ] Sleep quality good (6+ hours nightly)
- [ ] Mood positive (not depressed)
- [ ] Motivation to return to activity (not fear-avoidance)

### Clinical Thresholds

- [ ] PROMIS PF ≥46 (good functional capacity)
- [ ] PROMIS PI <50 (acceptable pain interference)
- [ ] No neurological progression
- [ ] Pain consistently ≤3/10 with daily activities
- [ ] No episodes of sharp pain or giving way

**If all criteria met**: Clear for return to activity (gradual progression)

**If any criteria NOT met**: Continue Phase 4; reassess in 2-4 weeks

## GLOSSARY

**Centralization**: Symptom movement from distal (far from spine) to proximal (toward spine); positive prognostic sign indicating mechanical origin of pain

**Core Stabilizers**: Deep abdominal muscles (transverse abdominis, multifidus) that maintain spinal stability; distinct from superficial "six-pack" muscles

**Directional Preference**: McKenzie concept that spinal pain responds preferentially to movement in one direction (flexion vs. extension)

**Eccentric Contraction**: Muscle lengthens while producing force; occurs when muscle resists a stronger opposing force

**Erector Spinae**: Paraspinal muscles along the spine that extend the spine and control flexion

**MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)**: Smallest change in score that represents meaningful improvement to patients; typically 3-5 points for PROMIS

**Multifidus**: Deep spinal stabilizer muscle; tends to atrophy with chronic low back pain and requires specific activation

**Peripheralization**: Symptom movement away from spine toward limb; negative prognostic sign indicating worsening classification

**PROMIS**: Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System; standardized assessment of pain interference and physical function

**Quadruped**: Hands and knees position (4-point contact with ground)

**Rhomboids**: Upper back muscles between shoulder blades; responsible for scapular retraction

**Serratus Anterior**: Muscle along ribs; responsible for scapular protraction; important for shoulder stability

**Transverse Abdominis**: Deepest abdominal muscle; acts like a corset to stabilize the spine; first muscle to engage with proper core activation

**Trapezius**: Large muscle between shoulders and spine; upper, middle, and lower divisions; important for postural stability
