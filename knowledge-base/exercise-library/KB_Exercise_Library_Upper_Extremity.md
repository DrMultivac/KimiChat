## INTRODUCTION

This exercise library provides evidence-based progressions for upper extremity conditions including rotator cuff dysfunction, shoulder impingement, tendinopathy (tennis elbow, golfer's elbow), wrist pain, and ankle/foot pain. The library is designed specifically for Medicare patients (65+) with chronic musculoskeletal pain and incorporates PROMIS outcome measures, pain-monitoring protocols, and clinical progression frameworks.

**Key Evidence Base:**
- APTA Clinical Practice Guidelines for Shoulder Pain (2017-2025)
- EULAR recommendations for rotator cuff disorders
- Cochrane reviews on exercise efficacy for lateral epicondylitis
- AOTA guidelines for hand therapy
- Ankle/foot mobility and strengthening literature

**Target Population:** Medicare patients (65+) with chronic upper extremity and lower extremity musculoskeletal pain conditions.

**Safety First:** All exercises include clear pain-monitoring thresholds and emergency stopping criteria to protect patient safety.

## FOUR-PHASE PROGRESSION FRAMEWORK

### PROMIS Thresholds for Upper Extremity Conditions

| Phase | Duration | PROMIS PI Target | PROMIS PF Target | Primary Goals | Exercise Characteristics |
|-------|----------|------------------|------------------|---------------|-------------------------|
| **Phase 1: Acute** | 1-2 weeks | ≥60 | ≤35 | Pain reduction, gentle mobility, protect from further injury | Passive, gravity-eliminated, isometric contractions, very low load |
| **Phase 2: Sub-Acute** | 2-4 weeks | 55-59 | 36-40 | Introduce active motion, build early strength endurance, improve neuromuscular control | Active-assisted to active ROM, light resistance, pain-free range |
| **Phase 3: Functional** | 4-8 weeks | 50-54 | 41-45 | Strengthen functional patterns, improve dynamic stability, increase load tolerance | Resisted exercises, functional task training, proprioceptive challenge |
| **Phase 4: Return-to-Activity** | 8-12+ weeks | <50 | ≥46 | Sport/activity-specific training, maximize function, prevent recurrence | Sport-specific patterns, high-speed movements, full functional capacity |

**Progression Criteria:**
- PROMIS PI improvement of ≥3-5 points (MCID threshold)
- PROMIS PF improvement of ≥3-5 points (MCID threshold)
- Pain rating ≤2/10 with all Phase exercises
- No increase in symptoms for 5 consecutive days at current level
- Patient reports confidence in movement patterns

**Regression Criteria:**
- Any exercise produces pain >2/10 during or >1 hour post-exercise
- Increased swelling or warmth
- PROMIS PI increase of ≥5 points or PF decrease of ≥5 points
- Patient reports loss of confidence or fear-avoidance behaviors

## WARM-UP PROTOCOL

**Duration:** 5-10 minutes (perform before all exercise sessions)

**Purpose:** Prepare tissues, increase blood flow, improve proprioceptive awareness, and establish safe movement patterns before progression to strengthening exercises.

### General Warm-Up Sequence

1. **Controlled Breathing** (2 minutes)
   - Position: Seated or standing, spine neutral
   - Technique: Slow diaphragmatic breathing, 4-second inhale, 6-second exhale
   - Reps: 10 breaths
   - Purpose: Activate parasympathetic nervous system, calm pain perception, coordinate breathing with movement

2. **Shoulder Circles** (1-2 minutes)
   - Position: Standing, arms at sides
   - Technique: Backward shoulder circles (10 reps), forward shoulder circles (10 reps), half circles (10 reps each direction)
   - Tempo: 2 seconds per circle, smooth and controlled
   - Purpose: Mobilize shoulder joint, activate rotator cuff, improve proprioception

3. **Arm Swings** (1 minute)
   - Position: Standing, legs shoulder-width apart
   - Technique: Gentle forward-backward swings (10 reps), diagonal swings across body (10 reps each side)
   - Tempo: Pendulum-like, 2-3 seconds per swing
   - Purpose: Increase shoulder mobility, improve scapulohumeral rhythm

4. **Wrist and Ankle Circles** (1 minute)
   - Position: Seated, one leg elevated on other knee or lying
   - Technique: Wrist circles (10 each direction), ankle circles (10 each direction)
   - Tempo: 1 second per circle
   - Purpose: Mobilize distal joints, assess pain-free range

5. **Gentle Neck Range of Motion** (1 minute)
   - Position: Seated, spine neutral
   - Technique: Flexion/extension (5 reps), rotation (5 reps each side), lateral flexion (5 reps each side)
   - Tempo: 2 seconds per movement
   - Purpose: Assess cervical mobility, prepare for upper body exercises

## COOL-DOWN PROTOCOL

**Duration:** 2-5 minutes (perform after all exercise sessions)

**Purpose:** Gradually reduce exercise intensity, improve flexibility, facilitate recovery, and reinforce proper movement patterns.

### Cool-Down Stretching Sequence

1. **Shoulder and Chest Stretch** (30 seconds each side)
   - Position: Standing
   - Technique: Reach right arm across body, use left hand to pull right arm toward chest; hold stretch without bouncing
   - Sensation: Gentle stretch in posterior shoulder and chest
   - Breathing: Continuous, relaxed breathing throughout hold
   - Caution: Never force stretch into pain; mild discomfort acceptable

2. **Triceps Stretch** (30 seconds each side)
   - Position: Standing
   - Technique: Raise right arm overhead, bend elbow, use left hand to gently press right elbow down and back; hold steady
   - Sensation: Stretch along back of upper arm
   - Breathing: Avoid breath-holding; continue relaxed breathing
   - Caution: Do not force elbow beyond pain-free range

3. **Wrist Flexor Stretch** (30 seconds each side)
   - Position: Standing, arm extended to side with palm up
   - Technique: Use opposite hand to gently bend wrist backward; hold without forcing
   - Sensation: Stretch along inner forearm
   - Duration: Hold 20-30 seconds
   - Breathing: Continuous, steady breathing

4. **Forearm Pronation/Supination Mobility** (30 seconds)
   - Position: Seated, elbow bent 90 degrees
   - Technique: Rotate forearm palm-up to palm-down and back; move slowly and control at end-range
   - Range: Move to gentle stretch, not pain
   - Reps: 10 rotations each direction
   - Purpose: Maintain forearm mobility, reduce stiffness

5. **Ankle Dorsiflexion/Plantarflexion Stretch** (30 seconds each foot)
   - Position: Seated, one leg extended
   - Technique: Point toes downward (plantarflexion), then pull toes toward shin (dorsiflexion); hold each 3-5 seconds
   - Reps: 5 repetitions each direction
   - Purpose: Maintain ankle mobility, improve circulation

6. **Breathing and Relaxation** (1-2 minutes)
   - Position: Seated or lying
   - Technique: Deep diaphragmatic breathing, 4-second inhale, 6-second exhale
   - Focus: Release tension, mental relaxation
   - Purpose: Activate parasympathetic nervous system, promote recovery

## PAIN MONITORING DURING EXERCISE

### Real-Time Pain Assessment Framework

| NRS Score | Classification | Action | Definition |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|------------|
| **0-2** | Safe | Continue exercise; on track for progression | Pain is minimal and expected; consistent with tissue healing |
| **3-4** | Acceptable | Continue current exercise; monitor closely; do not progress | Pain is present but controlled; watch for escalation; stop if increases |
| **5-7** | High Risk | STOP exercise immediately; apply ice; consider regression | Pain indicates excessive tissue stress; risk of symptom flare; contact provider |
| **8-10** | Dangerous | STOP immediately; seek emergency care if severe | Acute injury risk; potential serious tissue damage; emergency evaluation needed |

### Post-Exercise Pain Response (24-Hour Rule)

**Appropriate Response:**
- Mild pain increase during exercise (0-2/10 increase from baseline)
- Pain returns to baseline within 2 hours post-exercise
- No swelling, warmth, or stiffness progression
- Patient feels ready for next session

**Warning Response:**
- Pain remains elevated >2 hours post-exercise
- Morning stiffness worse than baseline
- Increased swelling or warmth 4+ hours post-exercise
- Pain >2/10 increase lasting beyond 24 hours

**Action if Warning Response:**
- Reduce exercise volume by 25-50%
- Add ice 10-15 minutes post-exercise
- Review pain-monitoring technique
- Contact care team; do not progress to next phase

## SHOULDER EXERCISES

### S1: PENDULUM SWINGS
**Target Muscles:** Rotator cuff (supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, subscapularis), deltoid, rotator cuff proprioceptors

**Position:** Bending forward at hips 90 degrees with affected arm hanging; opposite hand supports on table or chair

**Clinical Relevance:** Pendulum swings decompress the glenohumeral joint, reduce pain in impingement syndrome, improve scapulohumeral rhythm without active muscle contraction. Critical early-phase intervention for rotator cuff disorders and subacromial pain. Uses gravity-assisted motion to mobilize joint while protecting injured tissues.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Stand beside a sturdy table or chair with feet hip-width apart
2. Bend forward at hips until your trunk is parallel to ground (90-degree bend)
3. Let affected arm hang straight down from shoulder, relaxed and heavy
4. Place opposite hand on table for balance and stability
5. Begin small circular motions with hanging arm, circles about 12 inches in diameter
6. Move slowly and smoothly; let gravity and momentum create motion, not muscle effort
7. Perform forward-backward swings (like pendulum of clock)
8. Perform side-to-side swings (perpendicular to body)
9. Perform small circular motions clockwise and counterclockwise

**Dosage:**
- Phase 1: 10 circles each direction, 2-3 times daily (pain relief priority)
- Phase 2: 15-20 circles each direction, 1-2 times daily
- Phase 3: 20-25 circles each direction, 1 time daily or as needed
- Phase 4: Use as warm-up before strengthening exercises

**Tempo:** Slow and controlled, 2-3 seconds per motion; gravity provides momentum

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce arc of motion; perform only forward-backward swings; reduce frequency
- **Harder:** Increase arc of motion to 18-24 inches; add 1-2 lb weight in hand; perform while standing (less supported)

**Pain Monitoring:** Stop if pain >2/10 during exercise; this should be pain-relieving. Adjust arm position if pain worsens.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Arm muscles too tense; remember to keep arm completely relaxed and heavy
- Bending from waist only; bend full hip to parallel position for maximum decompression
- Swinging too quickly; momentum should be gentle and gravity-driven
- Tightening shoulder blade; keep shoulder relaxed and hanging loose

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"Let gravity do the work, not your muscles. Think of your arm like a rope hanging from your shoulder. We're using pendulum motions to give that painful shoulder joint a gentle massage and decompression. This isn't strengthening—it's recovery. The slower and more relaxed you are, the better this works. Many patients with shoulder pain find this provides immediate relief."

### S2: ISOMETRIC EXTERNAL ROTATION (Supine)
**Target Muscles:** Infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior cuff stabilizers, rotator cuff proprioceptors

**Position:** Lying supine on firm surface; affected arm at 90-degree shoulder abduction, elbow bent 90 degrees; small towel roll under arm

**Clinical Relevance:** Isometric external rotation strengthens infraspinatus and teres minor without joint motion, critical for rotator cuff stabilization. Addresses posterior cuff weakness driving anterior humeral head migration and impingement. Pain-safe method for early strengthening without glenohumeral joint stress.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Lie flat on your back on a firm bed or floor
2. Position affected arm out to side at shoulder height, elbow bent to 90 degrees
3. Place small towel roll under your arm for support
4. Position small pillow or towel under head for neck comfort
5. Take contraction resistance band or rolled towel and place in hand
6. Press upward against resistance without moving arm at all
7. Maintain steady pressure for 5-10 seconds; no joint movement should occur
8. Relax completely and rest 10 seconds
9. Repeat for prescribed number of repetitions

**Dosage:**
- Phase 1: 8-10 reps, 1 set, once daily; 5-second holds
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 2 sets, once daily; 8-second holds
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2 sets, 3x/week; 10-second holds
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3-4x/week; 10-second holds with transition to dynamic exercises

**Tempo:** 2-second ramp-up to contraction, 5-10 second hold, 2-second relaxation (no jerky movements)

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce hold duration to 3-5 seconds; reduce repetitions to 6-8; perform without resistance band
- **Harder:** Increase hold to 15 seconds; add light resistance with band; perform side-lying instead of supine for gravity resistance

**Pain Monitoring:** Pain should be 0-1/10 during hold; any pain >1/10 indicates excessive force. Reduce pressure immediately.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Arm movement during contraction; the point is NO movement, pure isometric hold
- Breath-holding; continue breathing throughout; exhale during contraction
- Excessive force; this should feel like gentle, steady pressure, not maximum effort
- Forgetting towel roll; support is critical to prevent shoulder strain

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"Think of this as 'invisible strength-building.' We're not moving your arm at all—we're just creating steady tension in those deep rotator cuff muscles. Imagine pressing against an immovable wall. That's the quality we want. This is safe for painful shoulders because there's no movement to irritate the joint."

**Video Search Suggestions:**
- "Isometric external rotation rotator cuff"
- "Infraspinatus strengthening no movement"
- "Supine rotator cuff isometric hold"

### S3: RESISTANCE BAND EXTERNAL ROTATION (Standing)
**Target Muscles:** Infraspinatus, teres minor, posterior deltoid, rotator cuff stabilizers

**Position:** Standing sideways to door at shoulder height; elbow bent 90 degrees, arm at side with towel roll between elbow and body

**Clinical Relevance:** Dynamic external rotation strengthens posterior rotator cuff through full pain-free range. Progression from isometric Phase 1 external rotation. Improves humeral head centering and scapulohumeral rhythm. Critical for function in Phase 3-4 return to activity.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Stand sideways to a door frame with affected arm closest to door
2. Tie resistance band at shoulder height to door or stable object
3. Stand about 12 inches from door
4. Bend affected arm to 90 degrees at elbow, keeping elbow at your side
5. Place small rolled towel between elbow and body to maintain position
6. Hold resistance band in your hand; band should start with slight tension
7. Pull band toward your body, rotating forearm and hand outward (external rotation)
8. Move only at shoulder; elbow stays at 90 degrees and against towel
9. Pause 1-2 seconds at end-range
10. Slowly return to starting position with control

**Dosage:**
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 1-2 sets, 3x/week (light resistance)
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week (moderate resistance)
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week (resistance allows 8-12 reps max)

**Tempo:** 2-second concentric (pulling), 1-second pause, 3-second eccentric (return)

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce band resistance; decrease range of motion; perform supine with arm at 90/90 position
- **Harder:** Increase band resistance; add weight in hand; perform diagonal pattern; increase repetitions

**Pain Monitoring:** Pain should remain 0-2/10; watch end-range for pinching sensations indicating impingement. Scale back if pain >2/10.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Letting elbow move away from body; towel roll prevents this; don't lift elbow
- Moving shoulder instead of rotating forearm; shoulder should stay still
- Jerky movements; this should be smooth and controlled throughout
- Using too much resistance too quickly; build gradually from light to moderate

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"We're teaching that deep rotator cuff muscle to work in a functional way now. As you pull that band, imagine you're turning a door handle slowly and smoothly. Keep your elbow glued to your side—all the movement comes from rotating your forearm outward. This is building the stability that keeps your shoulder safe during reaching and lifting activities."

**Video Search Suggestions:**
- "Resistance band external rotation shoulder"
- "Standing rotator cuff external rotation"
- "Doorway band external rotation exercise"

### S4: WALL SLIDES
**Target Muscles:** Serratus anterior, lower trapezius, supraspinatus, deltoid, scapular stabilizers

**Position:** Standing with back against wall, feet 6-8 inches from wall, arms in "field goal" position at sides

**Clinical Relevance:** Wall slides improve scapulohumeral rhythm and scapular control essential for overhead function. Addresses upper trapezius dominance and serratus anterior inhibition common in shoulder pain. Safe progression of scapular mobility into gravity-loaded positions.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Stand with back flat against wall
2. Position feet 6-8 inches away from wall so hips and shoulders touch wall
3. Bend elbows to 90 degrees, positioning arms in "field goal" position at shoulder height
4. Press back of arms, elbows, and hands against wall
5. Slowly slide arms upward, maintaining contact with wall throughout movement
6. Slide arms up as high as you can without pain, allowing natural shoulder shrug
7. Slide back down slowly to starting position, keeping all body parts against wall
8. Maintain contact between arms and wall during entire movement; no forward reaching

**Dosage:**
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 1-2 sets, daily or 5x/week
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3-4x/week with increased speed

**Tempo:** 3-second upward slide, 3-second downward slide (total 6 seconds per rep)

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce range of motion (don't slide as high); perform supine on floor instead of standing; use washcloth under arms to reduce friction
- **Harder:** Increase range to full overhead; add small hand weights; perform single-arm slides; increase speed to 2-second up, 2-second down

**Pain Monitoring:** Should feel working sensation in shoulder and shoulder blade, minimal pain (0-1/10). If pinching sensation develops, reduce range of motion.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Letting arms drift away from wall; keep consistent contact
- Arching back away from wall; maintain wall contact with entire back and buttocks
- Moving too quickly; this should be controlled and smooth
- Shrugging to try to reach higher; let shoulder naturally guide arm path

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"This exercise is teaching your shoulder blade muscles to work properly with your shoulder joint. As you slide those arms up, feel your shoulder blades working and your chest opening up. The wall is your guide—don't let your arms leave that surface. This helps balance out muscles that have gotten tight or weak from your pain pattern."

**Video Search Suggestions:**
- "Wall slides shoulder mobility scapular"
- "Shoulder blade strengthening wall exercise"

### S5: SCAPULAR SQUEEZES (Prone Row Position)
**Target Muscles:** Rhomboids, middle trapezius, lower trapezius, posterior deltoid, scapular retractors

**Position:** Prone on table with affected arm hanging off edge at shoulder height, torso supported on table

**Clinical Relevance:** Scapular squeezes address scapular dyskinesis (abnormal scapular positioning) driving shoulder impingement. Strengthens retractors to improve scapulohumeral rhythm and reduce anterior humeral head migration. Critical for restoring proper scapular control.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Lie face down on exercise table or firm bed
2. Position affected arm off edge of table, hanging straight down at shoulder height
3. Legs remain on table for stability and support
4. Relax arm completely, allowing gravity to create gentle stretch
5. Squeeze shoulder blade backward and down (retraction), lifting arm slightly off hanging position
6. Think of pinching shoulder blade toward spine
7. Hold squeeze for 3-5 seconds
8. Relax and allow arm to return to hanging position
9. Rest 10 seconds between repetitions

**Dosage:**
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 1-2 sets, daily or 5x/week; 3-second hold
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week; 5-second hold
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 3-4x/week; 5-second hold

**Tempo:** 2-second squeeze to hold position, 3-5 second hold, 2-second relaxation

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce hold duration to 2-3 seconds; reduce repetitions; perform supine with arm across body
- **Harder:** Increase hold to 10 seconds; add 1-2 lb weight in hand; perform standing with arm elevated; add horizontal abduction component

**Pain Monitoring:** Should feel muscle activation in shoulder blade area, minimal pain (0/10). Sharp or pinching pain indicates incorrect positioning; adjust arm height.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Lifting arm too much; scapular squeeze is subtle, arm moves minimally
- Initiating movement from arm instead of shoulder blade; think "squeeze blade first"
- Holding breath during contraction; continue breathing throughout exercise
- Forgetting table support; lying prone on table reduces pain compared to standing

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"This is about waking up those shoulder blade muscles. When you squeeze, imagine your shoulder blade is a hand squeezing something toward your spine. The arm movement is just a byproduct—the real work is happening in that shoulder blade. This helps balance the muscles that have gotten tight from your pain pattern."

**Video Search Suggestions:**

### S6: SLEEPER STRETCH
**Target Muscles:** Infraspinatus, posterior deltoid, shoulder external rotators; improves glenohumeral internal rotation

**Position:** Lying on side with affected arm up; hip and shoulder at 90 degrees; opposite hand on forearm

**Clinical Relevance:** Sleeper stretch addresses internal rotation restriction common in rotator cuff pathology and overhead athletes. Improves posterior shoulder mobility critical for scapulohumeral rhythm and overhead function. Reduces anterior shoulder tightness driving impingement.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Lie on your side on a firm surface with affected arm up
2. Position affected shoulder at 90 degrees from body; elbow bent to 90 degrees
3. Hip and shoulder should form 90-degree angle
4. Use opposite hand to gently press the back of affected wrist toward the floor
5. Press only to point of gentle stretch; never force into pain
6. Keep your torso relaxed against the floor; don't rotate trunk
7. Hold stretch without bouncing
8. Feel gentle stretch in posterior shoulder and back of shoulder joint

**Dosage:**
- Phase 1: 2-3 times daily, 20-30 second holds (pain relief)
- Phase 2-4: 1-2 times daily, 30-45 second holds; may be used daily

**Tempo:** Gentle, static hold; no bouncing or jerky movements

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce hold duration to 15-20 seconds; reduce pressure of hand; perform wall-supported version (back against wall)
- **Harder:** Increase hold to 60 seconds; add gentle overpressure at end-range; combine with thoracic rotation component

**Pain Monitoring:** Should feel gentle stretch in posterior shoulder, 0-2/10 sensation. Sharp pain indicates overstretching; reduce pressure immediately.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Forcing stretch too aggressively; "gentle" is the key word; never pain with stretching
- Rotating torso backward trying to achieve more stretch; trunk should remain still
- Holding breath; continue slow, relaxed breathing throughout hold
- Stretching too briefly; 20-30 seconds minimum for tissue response

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"This is a comfort stretch, not a strength exercise. Think of it as gently releasing tension in the back of your shoulder. You should feel a mild stretch—not pain. Hold it steady and breathe. Many patients with shoulder pain find this really helps them sleep better at night if they do it before bed."

**Video Search Suggestions:**

## ELBOW EXERCISES

### E1: WRIST EXTENSOR ECCENTRIC EXERCISE (Tennis Elbow)
**Target Muscles:** Extensor carpi radialis brevis, extensor carpi radialis longus, extensor carpi ulnaris

**Position:** Seated with forearm supported on table, hand extending past edge with palm down; opposite hand provides eccentric assistance

**Clinical Relevance:** Eccentric strengthening of wrist extensors addresses lateral epicondylitis (tennis elbow) through microtrauma rehabilitation. Eccentric contractions specifically stimulate collagen synthesis and tissue remodeling in chronic tendinopathy. Evidence-based first-line treatment for lateral epicondyle pain.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Sit at table with affected forearm resting on surface
2. Hand extends just past edge of table with palm facing down
3. Hold small dumbbell (1-3 lbs) or can in extended hand
4. Use opposite hand to help lift weight into wrist extension (backward hand motion)
5. Once elevated, remove opposite hand and lower weight slowly under control
6. Lowering phase should take 3-5 seconds (eccentric phase)
7. Use opposite hand to return weight to starting position
8. Repeat for prescribed repetitions
9. Focus on the slow, controlled lowering phase—this is where the strengthening happens

**Dosage:**
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 1-2 sets, 3x/week; 1-2 lb weight
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week; 2-3 lb weight
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week; 3-5 lb weight

**Tempo:** 1-second concentric (assisted lift), 3-5 second eccentric (controlled lowering), 1-2 second reset

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce weight to 0.5-1 lb; reduce repetitions; shorten eccentric phase to 2-3 seconds; use pencil or lightweight object
- **Harder:** Increase weight to 5+ lbs; lengthen eccentric phase to 5-7 seconds; perform standing; combine with grip strengthening

**Pain Monitoring:** Should feel muscle work with minimal pain (0-2/10). Lateral elbow pain >2/10 indicates excessive load; reduce weight. Monitor 24-hour pain response; increased pain next day suggests overload.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Using too much weight too quickly; start light and progress gradually
- Rushing lowering phase; eccentric strength requires slow, controlled descent
- Forgetting to use opposite hand for lift; eccentric work only works on lowering
- Performing concentric phase actively; concentric phase should be assisted

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"This exercise is specifically designed to heal tennis elbow by strengthening that tendon. The key is the slow, controlled lowering—that's where the magic happens. Your hand comes down slowly, fighting against gravity. Start with a light weight, even just a can of soup. This isn't about how strong you are; it's about healing that tendon through progressive loading."

**Video Search Suggestions:**
- "Eccentric wrist extension lateral epicondylitis"
- "Tennis elbow eccentric strengthening exercise"
- "Wrist extensor eccentric loading protocol"

### E2: WRIST FLEXOR ECCENTRIC EXERCISE (Golfer's Elbow)
**Target Muscles:** Flexor carpi radialis, flexor carpi ulnaris, palmaris longus, pronator teres

**Position:** Seated with forearm supported on table, hand extending past edge with palm up; opposite hand provides eccentric assistance

**Clinical Relevance:** Eccentric wrist flexor loading addresses medial epicondylitis (golfer's elbow) through similar microtrauma rehabilitation mechanism as lateral epicondyle exercises. Critical for patients with wrist flexor tendinopathy or medial elbow pain.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Sit at table with affected forearm resting on surface
2. Hand extends just past edge of table with palm facing up
3. Hold small dumbbell (1-3 lbs) or can in hand
4. Use opposite hand to help lift weight into wrist flexion (palm toward forearm motion)
5. Once elevated, remove opposite hand and lower weight slowly under control
6. Lowering phase should take 3-5 seconds (eccentric phase)
7. Use opposite hand to return weight to starting position for next repetition
8. Focus completely on the slow, controlled lowering—this is the strengthening phase
9. Palm facing up throughout entire exercise

**Dosage:**
- Phase 2: 10-12 reps, 1-2 sets, 3x/week; 1-2 lb weight
- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week; 2-3 lb weight
- Phase 4: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week; 3-5 lb weight

**Tempo:** 1-second concentric (assisted lift), 3-5 second eccentric (controlled lowering), 1-2 second reset

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Reduce weight to 0.5-1 lb; reduce repetitions; shorten eccentric to 2-3 seconds; perform supine
- **Harder:** Increase weight to 5+ lbs; lengthen eccentric to 5-7 seconds; perform standing; add pronation component

**Pain Monitoring:** Medial elbow should be 0-2/10; any sharp medial elbow pain indicates excessive load. Reduce weight if pain >2/10 during or after exercise. Monitor 24-hour response.

**Common Mistakes:**
- Pronating forearm during exercise; keep palm up throughout
- Using too much weight; medial elbow tendons respond slower than lateral; progress very gradually
- Active concentric phase; only eccentric (lowering) phase should be self-powered
- Forgetting to use opposite hand for lift assistance

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"Golfer's elbow develops in those wrist flexor muscles on the inside of your elbow. This exercise heals it the same way we heal tennis elbow—with slow, controlled eccentric loading. Keep that palm facing up, and lower slowly against gravity. This is a slower-healing tendon, so patience is key. Don't rush the weight progression."

### W1: TENDON GLIDING (Finger Flexor Activation)
**Target Muscles:** Flexor digitorum superficialis, flexor digitorum profundus, flexor carpi radialis, extensor digitorum

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**

### A2: CALF RAISES WITH ECCENTRIC EMPHASIS (Achilles Tendon Strengthening)
**Target Muscles:** Gastrocnemius, soleus, plantaris; eccentric loading stimulates Achilles tendon healing

**Position:** Standing facing wall or counter for balance; feet hip-width apart; using step or raised surface for eccentric emphasis

**Clinical Relevance:** Eccentric calf raises specifically address Achilles tendinopathy through controlled eccentric loading. Standing position against gravity provides resistance while eccentric phase emphasizes tissue adaptation and collagen remodeling critical for chronic tendon conditions.

**Step-by-Step Instructions:**

1. Stand facing wall or counter, hands resting lightly on surface for balance
2. Position feet hip-width apart on flat ground
3. Rise up onto toes (plantarflexion) using both legs, pushing through ball of foot
4. Transfer most weight to affected leg (the one being strengthened)
5. Slowly lower affected heel back to ground over 3-5 seconds (eccentric phase)
6. Use opposite leg to assist in rising back up to start position
7. Focus on the slow, controlled lowering—this is where strengthening happens
8. Maintain upright posture; don't lean forward or backward

**Dosage:**

- Phase 3: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week; single leg rise, prolonged eccentric

**Modifications:**
- **Easier:** Perform on flat ground without elevation; use both legs for both rise and lower; shorten eccentric phase to 2-3 seconds
- **Harder:** Perform on step or curb for increased range; single leg for both phases; hold light weight for additional load; increase eccentric duration to 7-10 seconds

- Rising with single leg too early; eccentric focus requires assisted concentric phase
- Rushing lowering phase; eccentric strengthening requires slow, controlled descent
- Allowing heel to crash down; control lowering throughout
- Forgetting balance support; always have hand contact with wall for safety

**Kimi Coaching Tips:**
"Achilles tendon problems need progressive loading to heal properly. The key is the slow, controlled lowering—that's where the tendon gets stronger. ## MCKENZIE APPROACH FOR SHOULDER

### Shoulder Directional Preference Assessment

The McKenzie approach for shoulder pain focuses on identifying directional preferences and centralization patterns to guide exercise selection. This assessment determines which movement directions improve symptoms (centralization) versus worsen them (peripheralization).

**Three-Step Assessment Protocol:**

**Step 1: Flexion Bias Assessment**
1. Lie supine with arm hanging off table edge
2. Perform gentle shoulder flexion (arm reaching overhead) with arm weight providing loading
3. Assess whether this movement increases, decreases, or centralizes symptoms
4. Repeat 10 times and note direction of any symptom change

**Step 2: Extension Bias Assessment**
1. Lie prone on table with arm hanging off edge
2. Perform gentle shoulder extension (arm reaching toward hip and back) with gravity loading
3. Assess direction of symptom response
4. Repeat 10 times and note changes

**Step 3: Lateral Shift/Rotation Assessment**
1. Sit upright with neutral spine
2. Perform horizontal abduction (arm reaching across body and back)
3. Assess symptom response to this movement pattern

### Centralization Classification

**Centralization (Preferred Direction):**
- Symptoms move from distal (hand/arm) toward proximal (shoulder)
- Pain or paresthesias improve or resolve with directional preference movements
- This is the direction to emphasize in exercise program
- Typically indicates mechanical cause responsive to McKenzie principles

**Peripheralization (Avoid Direction):**
- Symptoms move from proximal to distal (increasing arm/hand symptoms)
- Pain, numbness, or tingling worsens with this movement direction
- This direction should be avoided in exercise program
- May indicate nerve involvement requiring careful management

**No Change Response:**
- Movement in either direction produces no symptom change
- Suggests non-mechanical pain or pain not responsive to directional preference approach
- Consider multimodal approach including pain management, manual therapy, other interventions

**Rapid Centralization:**
- Quick and dramatic improvement in symptoms with directional preference movements
- Indicates excellent prognosis and rapid response to exercise therapy
- Can progress more aggressively through phases
- Patient typically responds well to home exercise program

## UPPER EXTREMITY GRADED PROGRESSION PROGRAM

### 12-Week Progressive Exercise Program with Benchmarks

This structured 12-week program progresses patients systematically from Phase 1 through Phase 4, with specific weekly benchmarks and progression criteria.

**WEEKS 1-2: Phase 1 (Acute Pain Management)**

*Primary Goals:* Reduce pain, protect from further injury, introduce safe gentle motion

*Weekly Progression:*
- Week 1: Focus on pendulum swings (3x/day), sleeper stretch (2x/day), pain-relieved breathing
- Week 2: Add gentle wall slides and scapular squeezes, continue pain relief focus

*Exercise Selection:*
- Pendulum Swings: 10-15 circles each direction, 3x/day
- Sleeper Stretch: 30 seconds, 2x/day
- Wall Slides: 8-10 reps, 1x/day
- Scapular Squeezes: 8-10 reps, 1x/day
- Isometric External Rotation: 8-10 reps, 1x/day

*Progression Criteria for Week 3:*
- Pain rating ≤4/10 at rest
- Pain rating ≤5/10 with exercises
- No increase in swelling or warmth
- PROMIS PI ≥60 (acceptable for Phase 1)
- Patient reports confidence in movement patterns

*Benchmark:* 50% reduction in daytime pain by end of Week 2

**WEEKS 3-4: Phase 2 (Sub-Acute Strengthening Initiation)**

*Primary Goals:* Introduce active strengthening, improve ROM, begin functional activities

*Weekly Progression:*
- Week 3: Add isometric external rotation 1-2x/day, increase wall slides to 12-15 reps
- Week 4: Progress to band external rotation, increase scapular squeeze repetitions

*Exercise Selection:*
- Pendulum Swings: 15-20 circles, 1-2x/day
- Sleeper Stretch: 30-45 seconds, 1x/day
- Wall Slides: 12-15 reps, 2x/week
- Isometric External Rotation: 10-12 reps 2 sets, 2-3x/week
- Resistance Band External Rotation: 10-12 reps, 2x/week (light band)
- Scapular Squeezes: 12-15 reps, 2-3x/week
- Grip Strengthening: 10-12 reps, 1 set, 2x/week

*Progression Criteria for Week 5:*
- Pain rating ≤2/10 with exercises
- ROM improved by 20+ degrees in external rotation
- PROMIS PI 55-59 (early Phase 2 target)
- PROMIS PF 36-40 (early Phase 2 target)
- Tolerance to 2 sets of exercises without pain increase

*Benchmark:* Pain reduction to mild levels; ROM improvements noted; exercise tolerance improving

**WEEKS 5-7: Phase 3 (Functional Strengthening)**

*Primary Goals:* Build strength through functional patterns, improve dynamic stability, increase load tolerance

*Weekly Progression:*
- Week 5-6: Progress band resistance, add functional reaching patterns, begin light activities
- Week 7: Increase frequency, add more challenging scapular exercises

*Exercise Selection:*
- Pendulum Swings: 20-25 circles, 1x/day or as needed
- Sleeper Stretch: 45 seconds, 1x/day or daily
- Wall Slides: 15-20 reps, 3x/week
- Band External Rotation: 12-15 reps, 2-3 sets, 3-4x/week (medium band)
- Pronation/Supination: 12-15 reps, 2 sets, 3-4x/week
- Grip Strengthening: 12-15 reps, 2 sets, 3x/week
- Wrist Extensor Eccentric: 12-15 reps, 2 sets, 3x/week
- Single-Leg Balance: 30-60 second holds, 3x/week (integrate lower extremity)

*Progression Criteria for Week 8:*
- Pain rating ≤1/10 at rest and with exercises
- ROM essentially normalized
- PROMIS PI 50-54 (Phase 3 target)
- PROMIS PF 41-45 (Phase 3 target)
- Tolerance to 3 sets of strengthening exercises
- Confidence with movement patterns improving

*Benchmark:* Significant functional improvement; able to perform most ADLs; exercise tolerance doubled from Phase 1

**WEEKS 8-10: Phase 4 Early (Return-to-Activity Initiation)**

*Primary Goals:* Sport/activity-specific training, maximize dynamic function, prepare for full return

*Weekly Progression:*
- Week 8-9: Add sport-specific movements, increase intensity, add rotational components
- Week 10: Combine upper and lower body movements, sport simulation

*Exercise Selection:*
- Band External Rotation: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4x/week (heavy band)
- Wrist Extensor Eccentric: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week (heavy resistance)
- Wrist Flexor Eccentric: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week (heavy resistance)
- Pronation/Supination: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4x/week (heavy resistance)
- Grip Strengthening: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4x/week (heavy resistance)
- Calf Raises Eccentric: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week
- Single-Leg Balance: 60-90 second holds, 4x/week (with balance challenges)
- Towel Curls: 15-20 repetitions, 3 sets, 4x/week
- Ankle Eversion: 15-20 reps, 3 sets, 4-5x/week (heavy band)

*Progression Criteria for Week 11:*
- Pain rating 0/10 at rest and with exercises
- Full ROM with all exercises
- PROMIS PI <50 (Phase 4 target achieved)
- PROMIS PF ≥46 (Phase 4 target achieved)
- Tolerance to sport-specific movements
- Confidence with return-to-activity progressions

*Benchmark:* Ready for advanced return-to-activity training; pain essentially resolved

**WEEKS 11-12: Phase 4 Advanced (Return-to-Full-Activity)**

*Primary Goals:* Advanced sport-specific training, full functional capacity, injury prevention

*Exercise Selection:*
- Continue Phase 4 exercises at advanced intensity
- Sport/activity-specific training 4-5x/week
- Single-leg balance with perturbations
- Dynamic reaching and catching patterns
- Sport-specific equipment usage
- Plyometric elements if appropriate

*Progression Criteria for Clearance:*
- Pain-free performance of all ADLs
- Pain-free performance of sport/activity demands
- PROMIS scores within normal range
- Strength testing 85%+ compared to opposite side
- Balance testing equivalent to opposite side
- Patient confidence in return to activity 9-10/10

*Benchmark:* Cleared for full return to activity; home exercise program for maintenance

## EMERGENCY STOP RULES

**IMMEDIATE EXERCISE CESSATION REQUIRED** if any of the following occur during exercise:

1. **Sudden Severe Pain (≥8/10)**
   - Stop exercise immediately
   - Rest position: comfortable chair or bed
   - Apply ice if available
   - Call healthcare provider
   - Do not resume exercise without provider evaluation

2. **Chest Pain or Pressure**
   - Stop immediately
   - Sit or lie down in comfortable position
   - Call 911 if chest pain persists >5 minutes
   - Do not attempt to resume exercise

3. **Shortness of Breath**
   - Stop exercise and sit down
   - Rest in comfortable position
   - If breathing difficulty persists >5 minutes, seek emergency care
   - Do not resume exercise until cleared by provider

4. **Dizziness or Fainting**
   - Immediately sit or lie down
   - Rest until dizziness resolves completely
   - Drink water if available
   - Do not attempt to resume exercise
   - Call provider if episodes repeat

5. **Sudden Joint Deformity or Swelling**
   - Stop immediately
   - Protect injured joint
   - Apply ice for 15 minutes
   - Elevate if possible
   - Seek emergency evaluation for possible fracture

6. **Severe Swelling or Skin Changes**
   - Stop all exercises on affected area
   - Apply ice for 15-20 minutes
   - Elevate area if possible
   - Call healthcare provider
   - Do not resume exercises without evaluation

7. **Tingling/Numbness Progression**
   - Stop exercise immediately
   - Rest arm in neutral position
   - If numbness increases or spreads, seek emergency evaluation
   - Contact provider before resuming exercises

8. **Acute Inflammatory Signs**
   - Marked increase in warmth (>104°F skin temperature)
   - Rapid swelling progression
   - Redness spreading from joint
   - Call healthcare provider immediately
   - Do not continue exercising

**Post-Emergency Protocol:**
- Contact healthcare provider before resuming any exercises
- Obtain medical clearance before progression
- Reduce exercise intensity significantly upon return
- Monitor closely for symptom recurrence
- Report emergency event to care team documentation

## RETURN-TO-ACTIVITY CLEARANCE CRITERIA

**All of the Following Must Be Present for Full Activity Return:**

### Pain/Symptom Criteria
- [ ] Pain rating 0/10 at rest and with activity
- [ ] No pain with sport/activity-specific movements
- [ ] No night pain or early morning stiffness
- [ ] No paresthesias or neurological symptoms
- [ ] No swelling, warmth, or inflammatory signs

### PROMIS Outcome Criteria
- [ ] PROMIS Physical Function ≥46 (indicating good function)
- [ ] PROMIS Pain Interference <50 (indicating low pain interference)
- [ ] Both scores stable or improving for minimum 2 consecutive assessments

### Range of Motion Criteria
- [ ] ROM within 5 degrees of opposite side in all planes
- [ ] Overhead reaching pain-free and without compensation
- [ ] Rotation and lateral flexion unrestricted
- [ ] No capsular tightness or end-feel restrictions

### Strength Criteria
- [ ] Shoulder external rotation strength ≥4.5/5 (manual test)
- [ ] Grip strength ≥90% compared to opposite side
- [ ] Single-leg stance ≥90 seconds with eyes open
- [ ] Single-leg stance ≥30 seconds with eyes closed
- [ ] No strength deficits on formal testing

### Proprioception/Balance Criteria
- [ ] Single-leg balance score equivalent to opposite side
- [ ] Balance maintained with perturbations
- [ ] Eyes-closed balance comparable to eyes-open
- [ ] Reaction time and proprioceptive testing normal

### Functional Performance Criteria
- [ ] Sport-specific movement testing pain-free and powerful
- [ ] Agility testing passed with appropriate speed/control
- [ ] Lateral movement and cutting performed without hesitation
- [ ] Overhead activities (throwing, serving) performed at pre-injury level

### Activity-Specific Criteria
- [ ] Sport-specific endurance testing passed (sport-specific movements for 20+ minutes)
- [ ] Sport-specific power testing equivalent to opposite side
- [ ] Sport-specific skills performed at pre-injury level
- [ ] Able to perform sport-specific drills without pain or compensation

### Psychological/Confidence Criteria
- [ ] Patient confidence in activity return 9-10/10
- [ ] No fear-avoidance behaviors remaining
- [ ] Positive outlook on prognosis and future function
- [ ] Demonstrates understanding of injury prevention strategies

### Provider Clearance Criteria
- [ ] Physician physician clearance for unrestricted activity
- [ ] No contraindications identified
- [ ] Imaging (if performed) demonstrates healing/normalization
- [ ] Healthcare team consensus for return-to-activity

### Documentation Requirements
- [ ] Functional testing results documented
- [ ] PROMIS scores documented in medical record
- [ ] Provider clearance note in chart
- [ ] Return-to-activity precautions documented
- [ ] Home maintenance exercise program provided

**Note:** Return-to-activity decisions should be individualized and may require sport medicine physician input for competitive athletes or high-demand activities. Progressive return with graduated increases in intensity/duration is recommended even after clearance.

## GLOSSARY

**Abduction:** Movement of arm away from body midline; raising arm out to side

**Active Range of Motion (AROM):** Movement performed by patient's own muscle contraction without assistance

**Arthrokinematics:** Movement of joint surfaces relative to each other during motion

**Centralization:** McKenzie concept describing symptom improvement/migration from distal to proximal location with specific movement directions

**Closed-Kinetic Chain:** Exercise where distal end (hand/foot) is fixed and proximal segments move against fixed endpoint

**Concentric Contraction:** Muscle shortening while producing force (lifting weight up)

**Crepitus:** Crackling or popping sensation or sound in joints

**Dorsiflexion:** Bending foot/ankle to bring toes toward shin

**Eccentric Contraction:** Muscle lengthening while producing force (lowering weight down)

**Eversion:** Rotating foot sole outward; turning sole toward midline

**External Rotation:** Rotating arm/shoulder so palm faces backward or laterally

**Flexion:** Bending movement that decreases joint angle; moving toward body

**Glenohumeral Joint:** Primary shoulder joint between humerus and scapula; "ball and socket" joint

**Isometric Contraction:** Muscle contraction producing force without joint movement or change in muscle length

**Isotonic Contraction:** Muscle contraction with joint movement; includes concentric and eccentric contractions

**Internal Rotation:** Rotating arm/shoulder so palm faces medially or inward

**Kinetic Chain:** Interconnected series of body segments and joints that work together to produce movement

**Mechanoreceptor:** Sensory receptor detecting pressure, touch, or movement stimuli

**MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference):** Smallest change in measurement that represents meaningful improvement to patient; PROMIS MCID typically 3-5 points

**McKenzie Method:** Mechanical diagnosis and therapy approach using directional preference assessment

**Open-Kinetic Chain:** Exercise where distal end (hand/foot) moves freely in space while proximal segments fixed

**Paresthesias:** Abnormal sensations including tingling, numbness, or "pins and needles"

**Peripheralization:** Symptom worsening or migration from proximal to distal location with specific movement

**Plantarflexion:** Pointing toes downward; extending foot away from shin

**Pronation (Forearm):** Rotating forearm so palm faces downward or medially

**PROMIS Physical Function (PF):** Patient-reported outcome measure of physical function capacity; scale 0-100

**PROMIS Pain Interference (PI):** Patient-reported outcome measure of pain's interference with daily activities; scale 0-100

**Proprioception:** Sensory feedback about body position and movement; kinesthetic awareness

**Retraction:** Drawing backward; moving shoulder blade toward spine

**ROM (Range of Motion):** Degree of movement available at joint; measured in degrees

**Scapulohumeral Rhythm:** Coordinated movement of scapula and humerus during shoulder motion; approximately 2:1 ratio (humerus:scapula)

**Subacromial Space:** Space between humeral head and acromion process where rotator cuff tendons pass

**Supination (Forearm):** Rotating forearm so palm faces upward or laterally

**Tendinopathy:** Disease process of tendon including pain, swelling, and dysfunction; may include inflammation or degeneration

**Viscoelastic Properties:** Material properties allowing tissue to behave as both solid (elastic) and liquid (viscous); characteristic of biological tissues

## EVIDENCE SUMMARY

### Key Evidence Base for Upper Extremity Exercise Progression

**Shoulder Pain and Rotator Cuff Dysfunction:**
- APTA Clinical Practice Guidelines (2017-2025) recommend progressive exercise as first-line treatment for rotator cuff disorders
- Eccentric exercise shows superior outcomes for chronic tendinopathy (Alfredson et al., 2008; Jonsson et al., 2006)
- Scapular stabilization exercises critical for reducing pain and improving function in impingement syndrome
- Pendulum swings recommended for early phase pain relief and joint decompression
- External rotation strengthening addresses posterior cuff insufficiency driving anterior humeral head migration

**Lateral and Medial Epicondylitis:**
- Eccentric loading is evidence-based first-line treatment (Cochrane review 2014)
- Progressive eccentric exercises superior to concentric exercises for long-term outcomes
- Wrist extensor and flexor strengthening should comprise primary treatment focus
- Activity modification combined with progressive loading produces best outcomes

**Wrist and Hand Conditions:**
- Tendon gliding improves tissue nutrition and reduces adhesion formation post-injury
- Grip strengthening correlates with functional capacity and ADL independence
- Progressive resistance training improves hand function across diagnoses
- Median nerve gliding reduces carpal tunnel symptoms when combined with splinting

**Ankle and Foot Pain:**
- Proprioceptive training reduces ankle sprain recurrence risk by 40-70% (Hupperets et al., 2009)
- Eccentric calf raises address Achilles tendinopathy through tissue remodeling (Alfredson protocol)
- Intrinsic foot muscle strengthening improves arch support and reduces plantar fasciitis pain
- Single-leg balance training improves fall prevention in older adults

**PROMIS Outcome Measures:**
- PROMIS Physical Function and Pain Interference capture meaningful patient-centered outcomes
- MCID of 3-5 points represents minimally important changes detectable by patients
- PROMIS scores predict functional capacity and return-to-activity readiness
- Serial PROMIS assessments track rehabilitation progress and guide progression decisions

**McKenzie Mechanical Diagnosis:**
- Directional preference identification guides exercise selection and improves outcomes
- Centralization response predicts favorable prognosis and faster recovery
- Mechanical diagnosis approach reduces unnecessary diagnostic testing
- Patient education on symptom centralization improves adherence and self-efficacy
