# KIMI MSK Care Coach — Exercise Library: Knee and Hip

**Document Purpose**: Comprehensive exercise library for chronic knee and hip pain conditions (primarily osteoarthritis and related conditions). Organized by four-phase rehabilitation progression with clinically-validated exercise prescriptions, modifications, and coaching cues for Medicare patients (65+).

**Last Updated**: April 2026
**Clinical Basis**: APTA CPG for Hip and Knee OA (2017, 2025), OARSI Guidelines (2019), ACR/AF Guidelines (2019)

---

## Four-Phase Progression Framework

### Phase Definitions and Progression Criteria

| Phase | PROMIS Pain (PI) | PROMIS Function (PF) | Clinical Focus | Minimum Sessions/Week |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 1: Acute** | ≥60 | ≤35 | Gentle mobility, pain education, isometric activation, protection of joint | 3 |
| **Phase 2: Sub-Acute** | 55–59 | 36–40 | Isotonic strengthening, progressive weight-bearing, motor control activation | 3–4 |
| **Phase 3: Functional** | — | 41–45 | Closed-chain loading, functional task training, balance/proprioception, global strengthening | 4 |
| **Phase 4: Return to Activity** | — | ≥46 | High-load strengthening, complex movement patterns, sports/ADL-specific training, self-management | 4–5 |

**Key Note**: PROMIS Function (PF) scores drive progression in knee and hip conditions. A 7-8 point improvement on PROMIS PF constitutes clinically meaningful improvement (MCID) and justifies phase advancement when demonstrated proficiency in current-phase exercises is confirmed.

### Phase Progression Rules

**Advancement Criteria:**
- Patient achieves PROMIS Function (PF) threshold for next phase
- Patient demonstrates movement quality and exercise proficiency at current phase (assessed through Kimi's exercise completion tracking and patient self-report)
- Pain remains manageable (NRS ≤4 during exercise, returns to baseline within 2 hours post-exercise)

**Regression Criteria:**
- PROMIS PF score declines ≥MCID (7-8 points) from most recent assessment
- New or worsening joint symptoms develop
- Patient develops compensatory pain patterns in adjacent joints
- Exercise compliance drops below 50%

**Plateau Management:**
- If PROMIS PF score remains stable (no improvement ≥MCID) for 3 consecutive weekly assessments within the same phase:
  1. Review exercise adherence and form
  2. Consider increasing exercise intensity or volume
  3. Escalate to clinician for care plan modification if plateau persists

## Warm-Up Protocol (5–10 minutes)

**Purpose**: Increase heart rate, synovial fluid circulation, and muscle temperature to prepare joints and muscles for exercise work while reducing injury risk.

**Standard Lower-Extremity Warm-Up Sequence**:

1. **Marching in Place** (2 minutes)
   - Step in place with gradually increasing step height
   - Exaggerate hip and knee flexion
   - Target heart rate: light exertion, conversational effort

2. **Seated Knee Extensions with Marching** (1 minute)
   - Sit in sturdy chair with back support
   - Lift one knee, straighten leg, lower
   - Alternate legs in rhythmic pattern
   - 20 total repetitions (10 per side)

3. **Arm Swings (Forward-Backward and Cross-Body)** (1 minute)
   - Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
   - Swing arms forward and back like pendulum (30 seconds)
   - Cross arms across chest in rhythmic pattern (30 seconds)
   - Coordinates with lower-body movement; promotes bilateral engagement

4. **Sit-to-Stand from Chair** (5–8 repetitions, 1 minute)
   - From sturdy chair, stand and sit repeatedly
   - Controlled pace, focus on knee and hip extension
   - Functional warm-up for exercises to follow

5. **Light Stationary Cycling or Walking** (2–3 minutes, if equipment available)
   - Stationary bike: low resistance, moderate cadence (50-60 RPM)
   - Or: marching in place, walking around room
   - Perceived exertion: "somewhat easy"

**Warm-Up Completion Checkpoint**: Perceived exertion should increase to "somewhat hard" by end of warm-up; light perspiration acceptable. Patient should report readiness to begin exercise work.

---

## Cool-Down Protocol (2–5 minutes)

**Purpose**: Gradually lower heart rate to resting levels, promote lymphatic circulation, and prepare body for post-exercise recovery.

**Standard Cool-Down Sequence**:

1. **Gentle Walking** (2 minutes)
   - Slow, deliberate pace around room or facility
   - Allow heart rate to gradually decrease
   - Breathing returns to normal rhythm

2. **Breathing Technique** (1 minute)
   - Seated or standing position
   - Slow, deep breathing: inhale for 4 counts, exhale for 4 counts
   - Repeat 8–10 cycles
   - Promotes parasympathetic activation

3. **Light Stretching** (2–3 minutes) — detailed in Cool-Down Stretches section below
   - Quadriceps stretches
   - Hamstring stretches
   - Hip flexor stretches
   - Held 20–30 seconds per side, no bouncing

**Cool-Down Completion Checkpoint**: Heart rate returns to near-resting levels; patient reports feeling calm and stable. Stretches should not cause pain (NRS 0–2).

## Pain Monitoring During and After Exercise

### Acute Pain Response During Exercise

| NRS Level | Classification | Clinical Interpretation | Kimi's Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **0–2** | **Safe** | Exercise intensity is appropriate; pain is minimal and expected with activity | Continue exercise as prescribed. Provide positive reinforcement. Monitor trend. |
| **3–4** | **Acceptable** | Pain is present but tolerable; monitoring required to ensure escalation does not occur | Continue with monitoring. Check if pain quality is familiar (vs. new/concerning). Educate patient on expected acute exertion-related discomfort. |
| **5+** | **High Risk** | Pain is concerning; exercise should be stopped or substantially modified | Stop exercise immediately. Assess pain character (sharp vs. achy, localized vs. radiating). If persistent or concerning, escalate to clinician. Reduce intensity/volume at next session. |

### Post-Exercise Pain Response (2-hour window)

| Post-Exercise Outcome | Classification | Clinical Interpretation | Kimi's Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pain returns to baseline within 2 hours** | **Appropriate dosing** | Exercise load was well-tolerated; tissue was appropriately challenged without exceeding capacity | Maintain or progress intensity at next session. Document adherence. |
| **Pain remains elevated above baseline next day** | **Overdosing** | Exercise volume or intensity exceeded tissue healing capacity | Reduce intensity/volume at next session by 20–30%. Educate on delayed-onset response. Contact clinician if pattern continues. |
| **Pain significantly worse next morning (NRS increase >2 points from baseline)** | **Adverse response** | Exercise may have triggered exacerbation or exceeded safe limits | Reduce intensity/volume by 50%. Consider rest day or modified exercise. Escalate to clinician. |
| **Swelling or warmth in joint next day** | **Inflammatory response** | Possible exercise-induced inflammation from overload or incorrect technique | Implement ice (15 min, 2–3 times daily). Reduce intensity. Review exercise form. Escalate if swelling persists >48 hours. |

## Cool-Down Stretches (Performed Post-Exercise)

Perform each stretch for 20–30 seconds per side. DO NOT bounce. Stretch should create light tension (NRS 0–2), not pain.

### Quadriceps Stretch (Standing or Seated)
- **Standing**: Hold chair; bend one knee; gently pull heel toward buttock
- **Seated**: Extend one leg behind body on chair; lean forward gently
- Feel stretch on front of thigh

### Hamstring Stretch (Seated)
- Sit on edge of chair
- Extend one leg straight with heel on ground
- Hinge forward at hip (keep back straight)
- Feel stretch on back of thigh

### Hip Flexor Stretch (Lunge Position, Modified)
- Half-kneeling position: one knee on cushion, one foot forward
- Gently press hips forward
- Feel stretch on front of hip (back leg side)
- Alternative: standing lunge with hand support on chair

### Calf Stretch (Standing)
- Stand facing chair/wall for balance
- Step one foot back, keep heel on ground
- Lean forward until stretch felt in calf
- Feel stretch on back of lower leg

# KNEE EXERCISES

## Phase 1: Acute (Gentle Mobility, Isometric Activation)

**Phase 1 Focus**: Reduce pain, restore gentle motion, activate stabilizing muscles, introduce pain education.

---

### Exercise K1: Quad Sets (Quadriceps Isometric Activation)

**Target Muscle**: Quadriceps (vastus medialis obliquus emphasis)
**Position**: Seated on floor with legs extended OR lying on back with pillow under knee
**Clinical Relevance**: Quadriceps weakness is a primary driver of knee OA progression. Isometric activation in Phase 1 begins rebuilding strength without joint shear.

**Step-by-Step Instructions** (6th-grade comprehension level):

1. Sit on floor or bed with your back supported and your legs straight in front of you
2. Place a rolled towel or small pillow under your knee (the one you're exercising)
3. Tighten your thigh muscle (the big muscle on top of your thigh) — try to straighten your knee completely
4. You should see the muscle above your knee flex and tighten
5. Hold the muscle tight for 5 full seconds (count slowly: 1-Mississippi, 2-Mississippi, etc.)
6. Relax the muscle completely
7. Rest for 2–3 seconds
8. Repeat

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10 repetitions, performed daily or 5–6 times per week
**Hold Duration**: 5 seconds per repetition
**Rest Between Sets**: 1–2 minutes

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Reduce hold duration to 3 seconds
- Perform only 2 sets × 8 reps
- Lie on back instead of sitting (gravity assists)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase hold to 7–10 seconds
- Add 2–4 lb ankle weight around lower thigh
- Perform 4 sets × 12 reps
- Add upper-body activity during hold (arm raises)

**Pain Monitoring**: Stop if NRS >4 during exercise. Expected response: mild fatigue or gentle muscle burn is normal and acceptable (NRS 0–2).

**Post-Exercise Rule**: Pain should return to baseline within 2 hours. If elevated next day, reduce reps by 25%.

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
- Holding breath (always breathe; exhale during contraction)
- Tightening other muscles (isolate thigh; keep buttocks and core relaxed)
- Incomplete muscle contraction (muscle should visibly tighten)
- Jerky movements (slow, smooth contraction and relaxation)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Quad sets are one of the most important exercises for knee pain. Your thigh muscle supports your knee joint. Doing these daily helps rebuild the strength that arthritis takes away. Even though it's simple, it's incredibly powerful. Start with just these three sets and listen to your body."

**Video Search**: "quad sets for knee osteoarthritis" or "isometric quadriceps exercises"

**Rationale**: Quadriceps strength correlates with reduced knee pain and improved functional outcomes. Isometric contractions avoid joint shear in acute phases.

### Exercise K2: Straight Leg Raises (Supine Quadriceps Strengthening)

**Target Muscle**: Quadriceps and hip flexors
**Position**: Lying on back on bed or cushioned floor with one leg bent, one straight
**Clinical Relevance**: Advances quad activation by combining isometric hold with hip flexion, increasing functional load.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back on a bed or firm surface with pillow under your head for comfort
2. Bend one knee and place that foot flat on the bed (this leg stays relaxed)
3. On the other side, tighten your thigh muscle and straighten that leg completely
4. Once your thigh muscle is tight, slowly lift your entire straight leg about 6–8 inches off the bed
5. Keep your thigh muscle tight (do not let your knee bend)
6. Hold this position for 3 full seconds
7. Slowly lower your leg back to the bed
8. Rest for 2 seconds
9. Repeat with the same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 8–10 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 3-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Do not hold at the top; just tap leg up and down gently
- Reduce lift height to 3–4 inches
- Perform 2 sets × 5 reps per leg
- Place hands under buttocks for support

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase lift height to 10–12 inches
- Hold at top for 5 seconds
- Perform sets sequentially without rest (10 reps one leg, 10 reps other leg, repeat)
- Add resistance band around thighs

**Pain Monitoring**: NRS should stay 0–3 during exercise. If pain >4, lower leg height or reduce reps.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Straight leg raises feel simple but they're building the muscles that support and protect your knee. You're doing the work without bending your knee, so there's less stress on the joint itself. That's smart exercise design."

**Video Search**: "straight leg raises for knee strengthening"

### Exercise K3: Heel Slides (Knee Flexion/Extension Mobility)

**Target**: Knee range of motion, quadriceps and hamstring activation
**Position**: Lying on back with knees bent
**Clinical Relevance**: Restores gentle knee flexion and extension range without resistive load; maintains synovial fluid circulation.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Bend both knees so your feet are flat on the bed, hip-width apart
3. Place a plastic bag or small towel under your heel (this helps your heel slide smoothly)
4. Slowly slide one heel away from you, straightening your knee as much as is comfortable
5. Do not push past comfort—stop if you feel pain
6. Hold for 1 second at maximum stretch
7. Slowly slide your heel back toward you, returning to the bent-knee position
8. Rest for 1 second
9. Repeat with the same leg, then switch legs

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed daily (1–2 times per day acceptable)
**Tempo**: Slow, 3-second straightening; 3-second return

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Slide heel only halfway (do not fully straighten knee)
- Reduce repetitions to 5 per leg
- Perform once daily instead of twice

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Place small weight (1–2 lbs) on thigh
- Perform standing (hold chair) with same sliding motion
- Add resistance band around thighs
- Perform standing heel slides with weight shift

**Pain Monitoring**: NRS 0–2 acceptable. Stretching sensation is normal. Stop if sharp pain or "catching" sensation.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your knee needs to move smoothly through its full range. These slides gently coax your knee to bend and straighten without pushing too hard. It's like oiling a hinge—the more you use it gently, the better it moves."

**Video Search**: "heel slides knee flexion"

### Exercise K4: Seated Knee Extension (Isotonic Quadriceps)

**Target**: Quadriceps strengthening, knee extension
**Position**: Seated on sturdy chair with back support
**Clinical Relevance**: Advances from isometric quads sets by adding movement against gravity; no external load required.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Sit in a sturdy chair with your back against the backrest
2. Your feet should touch the floor (or footstool if needed for height)
3. Starting position: both knees bent at 90 degrees
4. Tighten the thigh muscle of one leg
5. Slowly straighten that knee, lifting your foot forward until your leg is straight
6. Hold for 1–2 seconds with your leg fully extended
7. Slowly lower your foot back to the floor, bending your knee
8. Rest for 2 seconds
9. Repeat with the same leg, then switch legs

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 1-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Perform only partial straightening (do not fully extend knee)
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps per leg
- Hold for only 0.5 seconds
- Reduce frequency to 2–3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Add 2–5 lb ankle weight
- Hold knee straight for 3 seconds
- Perform 4 sets × 12 reps
- Add hip flexion (lift entire leg up, not just knee extension)
- Perform sets on one leg sequentially (10 reps left, 10 reps right, repeat)

**Pain Monitoring**: Pain should be 0–3 during exercise. If NRS reaches 4+, reduce range or reps.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "You're working against gravity here, which makes this exercise more challenging than quad sets. That's good—it means your muscles are working harder. By the end of Phase 1, these should feel easier. That's progress."

**Video Search**: "seated knee extensions arthritis"

### Exercise K5: Standing Mini Squats (Partial Weight-Bearing)

**Target**: Quadriceps, hip extensors, weight-bearing tolerance
**Position**: Standing with chair or counter for balance support
**Clinical Relevance**: Introduction to weight-bearing in Phase 1; mimics functional sit-to-stand pattern without full load.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand facing a sturdy chair or kitchen counter
2. Hold the chair/counter with both hands for balance (grip firmly)
3. Feet should be hip-width apart, toes pointing forward
4. Keeping your chest upright, slowly bend your knees as if starting to sit back
5. Lower yourself only 1–2 inches (this is a "mini" squat—do not go deep)
6. Your knees should NOT go past your toes
7. Keep your weight in your heels, not on your toes
8. Hold for 1 second at the bottom
9. Press through your heels to straighten your knees and return to standing
10. Rest for 2 seconds
11. Repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 8–10 repetitions, performed 3–4 times per week
**Tempo**: 2 seconds down, 1-second hold, 2 seconds up

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use both hands on chair/counter for more support
- Reduce depth to 0.5–1 inch (barely noticeable bend)
- Do 2 sets × 5 reps
- Perform less frequently (2 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Reduce hand support (use 1 hand, then fingertip contact)
- Increase depth to 3–4 inches
- Hold bottom position for 2–3 seconds
- Perform 3 sets × 12 reps
- Add slow squats without pausing at bottom

**Pain Monitoring**: NRS should stay ≤3. If pain >3, reduce depth immediately.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "These mini squats are teaching your knee how to handle weight-bearing. You're not going deep—just a gentle bend. This is how we rebuild confidence in your knee. As you get stronger, you'll be able to go deeper."

**Video Search**: "mini squats for knee osteoarthritis" or "partial squats knee arthritis"

### Exercise K6: Prone Knee Flexion (Hamstring and Glute Activation)

**Target**: Hamstring and glute activation; knee flexion range
**Position**: Lying face-down (prone) on bed or cushioned floor
**Clinical Relevance**: Activates posterior chain without weight-bearing; balances quad-dominant Phase 1.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie face-down on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Place a pillow under your hips for comfort
3. Your arms can be at your sides or folded under your chest
4. Tighten your buttock muscle and hamstring (back of your thigh) on one side
5. Slowly bend that knee, bringing your heel toward your buttock
6. Lift your heel about 6 inches off the bed
7. Hold for 2 seconds
8. Slowly lower your heel back down without touching the bed
9. Rest for 1 second
10. Repeat with the same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 2-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Touch heel to bed between reps (allow rest)
- Reduce lift height to 3–4 inches
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform less frequently (2–3 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Do not let heel touch bed (continuous tension)
- Add 1–2 lb ankle weight
- Perform 3 sets × 12 reps
- Hold top position for 3 seconds
- Perform pulses at top (small range-of-motion contractions)

**Pain Monitoring**: Pain should be 0–2. Avoid positions causing sharp or catching sensation in knee.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "We need to balance quad strengthening with hamstring and glute work. Your hamstring helps bend your knee, and your glute helps extend your hip. Strong hamstrings protect your knee. Don't overlook this exercise."

**Video Search**: "prone knee flexion hamstring strengthening"

## Phase 2: Sub-Acute (Progressive Isotonic and Closed-Chain Loading)

**Phase 2 Focus**: Increase resistance, add weight-bearing progression, introduce balance/proprioception, build functional capacity.

---

### Exercise K7: Step-Ups (Closed-Chain Lower Extremity Loading)

**Target**: Quadriceps, hip extensors, glute medius; weight-bearing functional pattern
**Position**: Standing at foot of low step (4–6 inches high initially)
**Clinical Relevance**: Mimics stair climbing; introduces progressively higher weight-bearing loads; challenges hip-knee coordination.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand facing a low step (4–6 inches high; can use stair, sturdy step stool, or aerobic step)
2. Hold onto a handrail or chair for balance
3. Step up with one leg: place entire foot on the step, press through your heel
4. Drive through your front leg to stand fully on the step
5. Your back leg will lift off the ground as you step up
6. Step down with the opposite leg back to the floor in a controlled manner
7. Rest for 1 second
8. Repeat for 10 steps, leading with the same leg, then switch legs and repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 8–10 steps per leg, performed 3–4 times per week
**Step Height**: 4–6 inches (Phase 2); progress to 8–10 inches by Phase 3
**Tempo**: 1 second up, 1 second down; controlled throughout

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lower step height (2–3 inches)
- Perform step-tap (step up, tap foot on step, step back down)
- Use both hands on handrail for support
- Do 2 sets × 6 steps per leg
- Reduce frequency to 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase step height to 10–12 inches
- Reduce or eliminate hand support (fingertip contact only)
- Lead with alternating legs (alternate continuously without stopping)
- Hold 1–2 lb weights in hands
- Add a step-up with knee drive (lift knee high when stepping up)

**Pain Monitoring**: NRS ≤3 acceptable. Pain should resolve within 2 hours post-exercise.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Not using full foot (stepping on toes only)
- Trailing leg doing the work (lead leg should drive the step up)
- Holding handrail too tightly (should provide balance, not support)
- Jerky movements (smooth, controlled tempo)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Step-ups are real-life function. Think about climbing stairs or stepping into a bathtub. You need your legs to be strong and coordinated for these everyday activities. This exercise prepares you for that."

**Video Search**: "step-ups for knee strengthening arthritis"

### Exercise K8: Hamstring Curls (Seated, Resistance Band)

**Target**: Hamstring strengthening; knee flexion with resistance
**Position**: Seated on chair with resistance band around ankle
**Clinical Relevance**: Adds resistance to hamstring activation; improves quad-hamstring balance.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Sit in a sturdy chair with your back against the backrest
2. Loop a resistance band around the ankle of one leg (the leg you'll exercise)
3. Hold the other end of the band with both hands (you'll create tension)
4. Starting position: knee bent at 90 degrees (foot flat on floor)
5. Tighten your hamstring (back of your thigh)
6. Slowly bend that knee further, pulling your heel toward your buttock against the band resistance
7. Bring your heel about 6–8 inches toward your buttock (create tension but not pain)
8. Hold for 1 second
9. Slowly straighten your knee, returning to starting position
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat with the same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed 3–4 times per week
**Resistance**: Light (band should provide noticeable but manageable resistance, NRS 0–2 during contraction)
**Tempo**: 2 seconds curl, 1-second hold, 2 seconds straighten

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lighter resistance band (less tension)
- Reduce range of motion (smaller curl)
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps per leg
- Perform less frequently (2 times per week)
- Do not hold at top; continuous movement allowed

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use heavier resistance band
- Hold top position for 3 seconds
- Perform 3–4 sets × 12 reps
- Add slow eccentric phase (3-second lowering)
- Perform leg curl machine (if available) instead of band

**Pain Monitoring**: Hamstring should feel fatigued but not painful. NRS ≤2 acceptable.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your hamstring has been taking a backseat while we built up your quad. Now we're balancing things out. A strong hamstring protects your knee just as much as a strong quad does. This band provides just enough resistance to challenge your muscle."

**Video Search**: "hamstring curls resistance band seated"

### Exercise K9: Calf Raises (Weight-Bearing Ankle/Calf Strengthening)

**Target**: Calf muscles (gastrocnemius and soleus); ankle stability; plantarflexion strength
**Position**: Standing with chair/counter for balance
**Clinical Relevance**: Strengthens distal lower extremity; improves walking mechanics and ankle stability; progresses weight-bearing tolerance.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand facing a chair or kitchen counter, holding it lightly with both hands
2. Feet should be hip-width apart, toes pointing forward
3. Distribute your weight evenly between both feet
4. Keeping your knees straight (but not locked), slowly press up onto the balls of your feet
5. Lift your heels about 1–2 inches off the ground
6. Hold for 1–2 seconds at the top
7. Slowly lower your heels back to the ground
8. Rest for 1 second
9. Repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 12–15 repetitions, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 1-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Perform seated calf raises (sit in chair, lift heels while keeping toes on ground, or vice versa)
- Reduce lift height to 0.5–1 inch
- Use both hands for more support
- Do 2 sets × 10 reps
- Perform 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform single-leg calf raises (lift one leg, balance on other)
- Reduce hand support (fingertip contact only)
- Add 2–5 lb weights in hands
- Perform 3 sets × 15 reps
- Perform calf raises on stairs (stand on edge of step for increased range)
- Add resistance band around ball of foot for additional load

**Pain Monitoring**: Calf should feel fatigued but not painful. NRS ≤2. Do not perform if sharp calf pain (risk of Achilles tendon issues).

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your calf muscles are constantly working to keep you balanced and stable. Strengthening them means better ankle control and stability as you walk. That translates directly to less knee stress."

**Video Search**: "calf raises standing" or "seated calf raises"

### Exercise K10: Short Arc Quads (Advanced Isometric)

**Target**: Quadriceps strengthening with partial range-of-motion; terminal knee extension emphasis
**Position**: Supine with rolled towel under knee
**Clinical Relevance**: Terminal knee extension (TKE) crucial for knee stability and walking; bridges gap between isometric and isotonic strengthening.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Place a rolled towel (about 3–4 inches thick) under your knee
3. Your knee should rest on the towel, bent at approximately 60 degrees
4. Your heel should be off the bed
5. Tighten your thigh muscle (quadriceps)
6. Slowly straighten your knee completely, pressing the back of your knee firmly into the rolled towel
7. This movement is small (only 3–5 inches of motion)
8. Hold the straightened position for 3 seconds
9. Relax and return to resting position
10. Rest for 2 seconds
11. Repeat

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 1 second straighten, 3-second hold, 1 second lower
**Towel Size**: Rolled towel approximately 3–4 inches in diameter

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Place higher rolled towel under knee (allows larger range)
- Reduce hold to 1 second
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform less frequently (2–3 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Place smaller towel under knee (reduces range, increases intensity)
- Increase hold to 5 seconds
- Add 2 lb ankle weight
- Perform 4 sets × 12 reps
- Squeeze glute during contraction for hip-knee coordination

**Pain Monitoring**: Pain should be 0–1. This exercise should feel comfortable despite intense muscle contraction.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "This is a very specific exercise. You're building strength in the last few degrees of knee straightening—that's the movement you need when you stand up or walk. It's subtle, but it's incredibly important."

**Video Search**: "short arc quads" or "terminal knee extension exercises"

## Phase 3: Functional (Closed-Chain Loading, Balance, and Multi-Joint Strengthening)

**Phase 3 Focus**: Dynamic balance, complex movement patterns, higher resistance, functional task training, proprioception development.

---

### Exercise K11: Wall Slides (Hip and Knee Flexion/Extension with Vertical Support)

**Target**: Hip extensors, quadriceps, controlled kinetic chain loading
**Position**: Standing with back against wall or door
**Clinical Relevance**: Controlled bilateral lower-extremity loading; assessment of movement quality and symmetry; prepares for squat progressions.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand with your back against a wall or door
2. Your heels should be about 6 inches from the wall
3. Feet hip-width apart, toes pointing forward
4. Press your back, shoulders, and head against the wall (maintain wall contact throughout)
5. Slowly bend your knees and hips simultaneously, sliding your body down the wall
6. Lower yourself until your knees are bent to approximately 60–80 degrees (thighs approximately parallel to ground, if tolerance allows)
7. Your back and shoulders stay in contact with the wall throughout
8. Hold for 2–3 seconds
9. Press through your heels to stand back up, sliding your body up the wall
10. Return to starting position
11. Rest for 2 seconds
12. Repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 8–12 repetitions, performed 3–4 times per week
**Depth**: Progress from 60 degrees (Phase 3) to 80–90 degrees (Phase 4)
**Tempo**: 2 seconds down, 2-second hold, 2 seconds up

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Reduce depth (only bend knees to 45–60 degrees)
- Reduce hold time to 1 second
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Place cushion or rolled towel behind back for comfort
- Perform 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase depth (approach parallel or slightly deeper)
- Hold bottom position for 5 seconds
- Perform 3 sets × 12 reps
- Add weight in hands or across chest
- Perform single-leg wall slides (if advanced)

**Pain Monitoring**: Quadriceps fatigue is expected. NRS should stay ≤3 during hold. If pain >3, reduce depth.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Knees moving past toes (adjust foot position forward)
- Back losing contact with wall (maintain continuous contact)
- Elbows moving away from wall (keep torso engaged)
- Asymmetrical depth (ensure both knees bend equally)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "This is controlled squat training. You're using the wall as a guide so you can focus on perfect form. Your body learns exactly how to move, and your muscles build strength gradually. Once you master this, you'll be ready for unsupported squats."

**Video Search**: "wall slides squat progression" or "wall squats for knee arthritis"

### Exercise K12: Lateral Band Walks (Hip Abduction and Glute Medius Activation)

**Target**: Hip abductors (glute medius); hip stability; frontal-plane strengthening
**Position**: Standing with resistance band around thighs, mini-squat position
**Clinical Relevance**: Lateral stability essential for single-leg support phases of gait; prevents knee valgus during dynamic activities.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Place a resistance band around your thighs, just above your knees
2. Stand with feet hip-width apart
3. Slightly bend your knees (mini-squat position, 15–20 degree knee bend)
4. Keep your chest upright and core engaged
5. Maintaining tension on the band, take a controlled step to the side with one leg
6. Your stepping leg moves sideways; your other leg stays relatively still
7. Feel the band tension on both legs (this indicates glute medius is working)
8. Take 10–12 steps in one direction
9. Rest for 30 seconds
10. Turn around and walk 10–12 steps in opposite direction
11. Rest and repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10–12 steps per direction, performed 3–4 times per week
**Band Resistance**: Light-to-moderate (should feel challenge without pain, NRS 0–2)
**Tempo**: Controlled, 1 second per step; maintain constant band tension

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lighter resistance band
- Reduce number of steps (6–8 per set)
- Take smaller steps
- Increase rest between sets to 60 seconds
- Perform 2 sets instead of 3

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use heavier resistance band
- Increase number of steps (15–20 per set)
- Perform with higher mini-squat position (deeper knee bend)
- Add holding positions: step, hold for 2 seconds, step
- Perform single-leg stance mini-walks (lift non-stance leg off ground)

**Pain Monitoring**: Hip and knee should feel strong but not painful. NRS ≤2. If lateral knee pain develops, reduce band tension.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your hip muscles are critical stabilizers. When your hips are weak, your knee has to compensate. That's often why you have pain. These band walks are about building hip strength so your knee doesn't have to work so hard."

**Video Search**: "lateral band walks hip abduction"

### Exercise K13: Single-Leg Stance (Balance and Proprioceptive Training)

**Target**: Single-leg balance; proprioception; ankle-knee-hip coordination; stabilizer muscle activation
**Position**: Standing unsupported or with light fingertip contact on chair/counter
**Clinical Relevance**: Mimics single-leg support phases of gait; essential for fall prevention; proprioceptive retraining crucial for OA recovery.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand facing a sturdy chair or counter
2. Feet together, arms at sides
3. Place fingertips lightly on the chair for emergencies (not for support)
4. Slowly shift all your weight onto one leg
5. Lift your opposite foot about 6 inches off the ground
6. Keep your lifted leg relaxed, knee slightly bent
7. Focus your eyes on a fixed point at eye level (this helps balance)
8. Hold this position as long as possible
9. If balance is lost, step down and rest 30 seconds
10. Switch legs and repeat

**Dosage**: 3–5 repetitions per leg, held for 20–30 seconds (progress to 45–60 seconds), performed 4–5 times per week
**Progression Sequence**:
- Week 1–2: Fingertip support, 20-second holds
- Week 2–3: Reduce hand support to near the chair, 30-second holds
- Week 3–4: No hand support, 45–60 second holds
- Phase 4: Add dynamic arm movements, head turns while standing

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Keep hand firmly on chair/counter for balance
- Reduce hold time to 10–15 seconds
- Perform near wall for emergencies
- Keep lifted foot touching ground lightly (toe-tap, not full weight shift)
- Perform 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform eyes closed (supervise carefully; have chair nearby)
- Add dynamic arm movements (arm circles, overhead reaches)
- Add head turns (look side-to-side while balancing)
- Stand on unstable surface (foam pad, balance disc)
- Perform on one leg while doing other tasks (reaching, throwing)
- Increase hold time to 60+ seconds

**Pain Monitoring**: Standing leg should feel strong and stable. NRS 0–1. If joint pain develops, reduce challenge level.

**Safety Considerations**:
- Always have chair or wall nearby for emergency support
- Perform in clear space away from obstacles
- Consider wearing sneakers with good grip
- Never force balance; rest if feeling unsteady

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Balance is something we lose if we don't use it. By standing on one leg regularly, you're teaching your body how to stay stable. This is how you prevent falls. It seems simple, but it's one of the most important things you can do."

**Video Search**: "single leg stance balance training"

### Exercise K14: Stationary Cycling (Low-Impact Aerobic Conditioning)

**Target**: Cardiovascular conditioning, knee range of motion, quadriceps endurance without impact stress
**Position**: Seated on stationary bike with proper seat height adjustment
**Clinical Relevance**: Non-weight-bearing aerobic conditioning; maintains knee ROM; reduces fear-avoidance related to exercise.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Adjust bike seat height: when pedal is at its lowest point, your knee should be bent at approximately 25–30 degrees (slight bend, not deep bend)
2. Your foot should be in the middle of the pedal (ball of foot on pedal)
3. Seat position fore-aft: when pedals are horizontal, your knee should be directly above the pedal axle (adjust by moving seat forward/backward)
4. Start pedaling at a comfortable, easy pace (RPM 50–60)
5. Perceived exertion should be "light" to "somewhat easy" for first 2–3 minutes (warm-up)
6. Gradually increase resistance and/or speed to reach "moderate" exertion (breathing slightly elevated, able to talk but not sing)
7. Maintain moderate intensity for prescribed duration
8. Gradually decrease resistance and speed for final 2–3 minutes (cool-down)
9. Stop pedaling when fully cooled

**Dosage**:
- Phase 2: 15–20 minutes, 3 times per week
- Phase 3: 20–30 minutes, 3–4 times per week
- Target: build to 150 minutes per week moderate-intensity aerobic activity

**Pedal Cadence**: 50–70 RPM (revolutions per minute) is typical; find comfortable rhythm
**Resistance**: Light enough to maintain steady pace; heavy enough to feel moderate exertion

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Reduce duration to 10–15 minutes
- Reduce resistance (easier pedaling)
- Reduce cadence (slower pedaling)
- Perform 2 times per week
- Use recumbent bike (seat with back support) for comfort

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase duration to 30–45 minutes
- Increase resistance (harder pedaling)
- Increase cadence to 70–90 RPM
- Add intervals: alternate 1 minute moderate intensity + 1 minute higher intensity
- Perform 4–5 times per week
- Use upright bike for increased core engagement

**Pain Monitoring**: Mild knee achiness acceptable. Sharp pain = stop immediately. Post-exercise pain should resolve within 2 hours.

**Bike Setup Troubleshooting**:
- Knee pain in front of knee (anterior knee pain): seat may be too high or too far back; move forward/lower
- Knee pain on inside of knee (medial knee pain): check alignment; consider foot position
- Knee pain on outside of knee (lateral knee pain): check seat height and foot position
- Lower back pain: increase seat height slightly; ensure upright posture

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Cycling is one of the best exercises for your knee. You get your heart pumping and you're moving your knee through its range of motion, but there's no impact—no pounding. Your joint stays happy while your muscles and heart get stronger."

**Video Search**: "stationary bike setup knee arthritis" or "stationary cycling arthritis"

### Exercise K15: Balance Board or Single-Leg Mini Squats (Advanced Proprioception)

**Target**: Single-leg strength, proprioceptive training, dynamic balance, ankle-knee coordination
**Position**: Standing on one leg, performing mini-squat movement
**Clinical Relevance**: Highest-level proprioceptive training; mimics dynamic single-leg balance during gait and ADLs.

**Step-by-Step Instructions** (Single-Leg Mini Squat):

1. Stand facing a chair or counter with fingertip contact available
2. Shift weight onto one leg (your working leg)
3. Lift your opposite leg slightly off ground
4. Keeping your working leg straight (initially), slowly bend that knee 1–2 inches (very shallow squat)
5. Hold for 1 second
6. Straighten your leg and return to standing
7. Perform 8–10 mini squats, then switch legs

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 8–10 repetitions per leg, performed 3–4 times per week
**Progression**: Start with fingertip support; progress to no support over 2–3 weeks

**Alternative: Balance Board Exercise**:

1. Place balance board (rocker or dome) on stable surface
2. Step onto board with both feet (board should rock/move)
3. Engage your core muscles and hip stabilizers to keep board level
4. Hold balanced position for 30–60 seconds
5. Step off and rest 30 seconds
6. Repeat 3–5 times

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use balance board before single-leg mini squats
- Keep both hands on chair/counter
- Reduce number of squats (5 per leg)
- Reduce depth (barely noticeable squat)
- Perform 2 sets instead of 3

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform with eyes closed
- Increase depth to 3–4 inches
- Add arm movements (reaching, balancing arms)
- Use balance disc or foam pad instead of firm surface
- Perform balance board while moving arms or throwing light ball
- Hold weights in hands while performing single-leg squat

**Pain Monitoring**: Working leg should feel strong and stable. NRS ≤2. If joint pain develops, reduce challenge.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "This is the hard stuff. You're training proprioception—your sense of where your body is in space. This is what prevents falls and what allows you to navigate uneven surfaces. It takes practice, but it's incredibly valuable."

**Video Search**: "single leg mini squat" or "balance board exercises"

### Exercise K16: Full Squats (Bilateral Closed-Chain Loading)

**Target**: Bilateral lower-extremity strength, knee extensors, hip extensors, functional movement pattern
**Position**: Standing, unsupported or with light support (progression)
**Clinical Relevance**: Return to full functional depth squatting; prepares for activities like carrying groceries, gardening, picking items up from floor.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand with feet hip-to-shoulder width apart
2. Toes pointing slightly outward (5–10 degree angle)
3. Hands either clasped in front of chest or on a chair for light support (progress to no support)
4. Keep chest upright, core engaged
5. Simultaneously bend hips and knees, lowering your body
6. Descend until thighs are approximately parallel to ground (or to tolerance if less depth is comfortable)
7. Knees should NOT go past toes; weight distributed through heels
8. Hold bottom position for 2 seconds
9. Press through heels to extend hips and knees, returning to standing
10. Rest for 2 seconds
11. Repeat

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions, performed 3–4 times per week
**Depth**: Full bilateral squat to parallel or slightly above (Phase 4 milestone)
**Tempo**: 2 seconds down, 2-second hold, 2 seconds up

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Reduce depth (3/4 or 1/2 squat)
- Keep both hands on chair/counter for support
- Perform 2 sets × 8 reps
- Use wall for back support
- Perform less frequently (2 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Add weights (dumbbells in hands, barbell on shoulders)
- Reduce hand support (fingertips, then no support)
- Perform pistol squat progression (single-leg squat)
- Add pause at bottom (3–5 second hold)
- Perform jump squats (explosive bilateral squat with lower-body power)
- Increase repetitions to 12–15

**Pain Monitoring**: Quadriceps and glutes should feel fatigued. NRS ≤2. If joint pain >2, reduce depth.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Knees caving inward (valgus collapse)—maintain alignment
- Excessive forward knee travel—shift hips back
- Excessive forward lean—engage core, upright torso
- Bouncing at bottom—controlled movement throughout

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Full squats are the foundation of lower-body strength and function. Once you can squat with good form and no pain, you've made it. You can get groceries, play with grandkids, garden, do whatever you want. This is the goal."

**Video Search**: "proper squat form arthritis" or "bilateral squat progression"

### Exercise K17: Lateral Lunges (Frontal-Plane Strength and Mobility)

**Target**: Hip abductors, hip extensors, quadriceps; frontal-plane functional movement
**Position**: Standing, progressing to unsupported
**Clinical Relevance**: Functional movement pattern for lateral activities (side-stepping, reaching to side, navigating obstacles); prevents Trendelenburg gait pattern.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand upright with feet hip-width apart
2. Keep hands on chair/counter for light balance support, or hands clasped in front of chest
3. Take a large step to one side (about 2–3 feet away from midline)
4. As you step, bend that knee and lower your body downward
5. Your stepping leg should be bent to approximately 60–90 degrees
6. Your non-stepping leg stays relatively straight (slight knee bend acceptable)
7. Your body weight shifts over your bent leg; torso stays upright
8. Hold for 2 seconds
9. Press through the heel of your bent leg to push back to standing
10. Return to center (feet hip-width apart)
11. Rest for 1 second
12. Repeat on opposite side
13. Perform 10 alternating lunges (5 per side)

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10 alternating lunges, performed 3–4 times per week
**Step Distance**: 2–3 feet to each side (adjust for comfort and range)
**Tempo**: 1.5 seconds down, 1-second hold, 1.5 seconds up

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use both hands on chair for support
- Take smaller step (1–1.5 feet from midline)
- Reduce depth (less knee bend)
- Do 2 sets × 8 lunges
- Perform 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform with no hand support
- Take larger step (3–4 feet)
- Perform dynamic step (continuous stepping without returning to center)
- Add weights (dumbbells, kettlebell)
- Hold pause at bottom (3 seconds)
- Add reaching movement (reach overhead, across body)

**Pain Monitoring**: Stepping leg should feel strong. NRS ≤2. Medial or lateral knee pain indicates form issue; check alignment.

**Form Cues**:
- "Sit back into hip"—weight shifts to back leg, bent-knee hip
- "Chest upright"—avoid forward lean
- "Heel planted"—maintain contact throughout
- "Opposite leg straight"—minimize knee bend in non-working leg

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Lateral lunges work your hip stabilizers in a side-to-side direction. Regular squats are forward-backward. Your body moves in all directions, so you need to train all directions. This is functional, real-life strength."

**Video Search**: "lateral lunges proper form" or "side lunge squat"

### Exercise K18: Step-Downs (Eccentric Quadriceps Loading, Descent Control)

**Target**: Eccentric quadriceps strengthening, knee control during descent, proprioceptive training
**Position**: Standing on low step (4–6 inches), lowering to ground
**Clinical Relevance**: Eccentric loading builds strength efficiently; mimics real-world movement (going downstairs, stepping down from curb); prevents Trendelenburg descent pattern.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand on a low step (4–6 inches high), facing away from the step
2. You can hold a handrail or chair for light balance support
3. Both feet should be on the step, hip-width apart
4. Slowly lower one foot to the ground behind you
5. As you lower, your weight shifts onto the foot being lowered
6. Your stepping leg is actively controlling this descent (this is the eccentric work)
7. Once that foot lightly touches the ground, immediately shift weight back onto the step leg
8. Return to standing on the step with both feet
9. Rest for 1 second
10. Repeat on opposite leg
11. Perform 10–12 alternating step-downs

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10–12 alternating step-downs, performed 3–4 times per week
**Step Height**: 4–6 inches (Phase 4); can progress to 8–10 inches
**Tempo**: 2–3 seconds down (slow, controlled eccentric); 1 second return

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lower step (2–3 inches)
- Hold handrail with both hands for support
- Perform fewer repetitions (6–8)
- Do 2 sets instead of 3
- Perform 2 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use higher step (8–10 inches)
- Reduce hand support (fingertip, then no support)
- Extend stepping leg forward (tap toe) instead of stepping back
- Hold pause at bottom (2–3 seconds) before returning to step
- Perform single-leg step-downs (step down, step up with same leg, alternate)
- Add weights (hold dumbbells)

**Pain Monitoring**: Descending leg should feel strong and controlled. NRS ≤2. Anterior or medial knee pain suggests control issue; reduce height.

**Eccentric Loading Education**: "Your muscles can produce more force when lengthening under tension (eccentric) than when shortening (concentric). This is why lowering is harder than lifting. We exploit this by controlling descent slowly—it builds strength very efficiently."

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Going down is actually harder for your muscles than going up. Your leg has to control the descent. This is exactly what happens when you walk down stairs or step down from a curb. Mastering step-downs means mastering real-world movement."

**Video Search**: "step-downs eccentric strengthening" or "step-down exercise"

### Exercise H1: Clamshells (Hip Abduction Isometric, Side-Lying)

**Target**: Hip abductors (glute medius), lateral hip stabilizers
**Position**: Side-lying position on mat or bed
**Clinical Relevance**: Glute medius weakness is primary driver of hip OA load distribution abnormalities. Isometric activation in Phase 1 begins rebuilding strength without weight-bearing stress.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your side on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Position yourself so your lower arm is under your head for support (or use a pillow)
3. Bend both hips and knees to approximately 45 degrees
4. Your hips and knees should be stacked (top hip directly over bottom hip)
5. Keep your feet together throughout this exercise
6. Keeping your feet connected, slowly lift your top knee toward the ceiling
7. Separate your knees as much as comfortable (create "clamshell" shape with knees)
8. Feel the contraction on the outside of your upper hip (glute medius)
9. Hold the top position for 2 seconds
10. Slowly lower your top knee back to rest position
11. Rest for 1 second
12. Repeat

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions per side, performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: 1.5 seconds up, 2-second hold, 1.5 seconds down
**Starting Hip Angle**: 45 degrees flexion (hips and knees bent); progress to 90 degrees as tolerated

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Increase hip and knee flexion to 90 degrees (easier lever arm)
- Reduce number of repetitions (8 reps)
- Hold for only 1 second
- Do 2 sets instead of 3
- Perform 3 times per week instead of daily

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Decrease hip and knee flexion to 30–45 degrees (harder lever arm)
- Hold top position for 3–4 seconds
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 reps
- Add resistance band around thighs
- Place light weight (1 lb) on upper thigh
- Perform pulses at top (small rapid movements)

**Pain Monitoring**: Outside of hip should feel tightening. NRS ≤2. If groin pain develops, reduce range or hold time.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Rotating trunk backward (stay side-lying, no trunk rotation)
- Separating feet (keep heels together)
- Lifting knee too high (controlled, small-to-moderate range)
- Holding breath (breathe throughout)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your hip abductor muscle—your glute medius—is like the stabilizer for your hip. When it's weak, your hip joint has to work too hard and becomes painful. These clamshells wake up that muscle and begin rebuilding its strength."

**Video Search**: "clamshells hip abduction arthritis"

### Exercise H2: Standing Hip Abduction (Weight-Bearing Hip Abduction)

**Target**: Hip abductors (glute medius), lateral hip stabilizers; weight-bearing tolerance
**Position**: Standing, holding chair/counter for balance
**Clinical Relevance**: Standing position mimics weight-bearing mechanics; advances from side-lying clamshells by incorporating gravity and weight-bearing load.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand sideways next to a sturdy chair or counter
2. Hold onto the back of the chair with your hand closest to it (the hand on your "support side")
3. Feet together, weight distributed evenly between both legs
4. Keep your trunk upright (no leaning forward or backward)
5. Keeping your leg straight (slight knee bend acceptable), slowly lift your outer leg to the side
6. Lift your leg approximately 8–10 inches to the side (create a "V" shape with your legs)
7. Your toes should point forward throughout
8. Hold the lifted position for 2 seconds
9. Slowly lower your leg back to starting position
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Complete all repetitions on one leg, then turn around and repeat on opposite side

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Lift Height**: 8–10 inches; progress to 12–15 inches as tolerated
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 2-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Hold chair with both hands for more stability
- Reduce lift height (5–6 inches)
- Reduce repetitions (8 reps)
- Do 2 sets instead of 3
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use only fingertip support on chair
- Increase lift height (15 inches)
- Perform on one hand only (other hand at side)
- Add resistance band around thighs
- Hold top position for 3–4 seconds
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 reps
- Perform double leg abduction (both feet leave ground—very advanced)

**Pain Monitoring**: Outer hip should feel strong. NRS ≤2. If lateral hip pain develops, reduce range.

**Body Alignment Checks**:
- Trunk remains upright (no side-bending toward lifted leg)
- Lifted leg moves directly to side (no forward/backward movement)
- Standing leg stays straight (slight knee bend acceptable)
- Hips stay level (no hiking one hip higher than other)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "This is the weight-bearing version of clamshells. You're standing on your legs while strengthening. Your hip stabilizer works as you balance and lift. This builds real, functional strength."

**Video Search**: "standing hip abduction arthritis" or "side leg lifts standing"

### Exercise H3: Bridges (Hip Extensor Activation, Supine)

**Target**: Gluteus maximus; hip extensors; core stabilizers
**Position**: Supine (lying on back) with knees bent
**Clinical Relevance**: Glute max activation essential for hip extension during gait; core co-activation improves trunk stability.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Bend your knees so your feet are flat on the surface, hip-width apart
3. Your arms should be at your sides, palms down (can use hands to push into surface for support)
4. Heels should be approximately 12 inches from your buttocks (when you look down, you should be able to see your toes)
5. Tighten your buttock muscles (glutes)
6. Push through your heels and raise your hips upward
7. Lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders
8. Hold this position for 3 seconds
9. Slowly lower your hips back to starting position
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions, performed 4–5 times per week
**Hold Duration**: 3 seconds (progress to 5+ seconds in Phase 2)
**Tempo**: 1.5 seconds up, 3-second hold, 1.5 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Do not fully extend hips (partial bridge)
- Reduce hold time to 1–2 seconds
- Use arms to assist (press hands into bed to help lift)
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase hold to 5 seconds
- Perform single-leg bridge (one foot lifted, opposite leg does work)
- Add pelvic thrust (small rapid movements at top)
- Place weight on hips (plates, sandbag)
- Lift one arm off ground at top (increase instability)
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 reps
- Add glute squeeze at top (additional contraction)

**Pain Monitoring**: Glutes should feel engaged. Hamstrings may feel mild fatigue. NRS ≤1. If lower back pain develops, reduce lift height or hold time.

**Common Mistakes**:
- Pushing off toes instead of heels (use heels; toes should lift slightly)
- Thrusting hips too high (gentle, controlled bridge)
- Letting hips sag to one side (maintain level hips)
- Straining neck or tensing shoulders (relax upper body)

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Your glute max is one of the biggest, strongest muscles in your body. When it's strong, it supports your hip and back beautifully. Bridges activate this muscle and build its strength. This is foundational work that pays dividends."

**Video Search**: "bridge exercise hip arthritis" or "glute bridges"

### Exercise H4: Seated Hip Flexion (Isometric, Seated)

**Target**: Hip flexors (iliopsoas); hip flexion strength
**Position**: Seated on chair with feet flat on floor
**Clinical Relevance**: Hip flexion strength needed for walking, climbing stairs, and sitting-to-standing transitions.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Sit in a sturdy chair with your back firmly against the backrest
2. Your feet should be flat on the floor
3. Keep your trunk upright
4. Tighten the muscles in your upper thigh and front of your hip
5. Slowly lift one foot off the floor about 1–2 inches
6. Your knee bends as your thigh lifts (hip flexion)
7. Keep your trunk stable (do not lean backward)
8. Hold this lifted position for 3 seconds
9. Slowly lower your foot back to the floor
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat with the same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Lift Height**: 1–2 inches (small controlled lift)
**Hold Duration**: 3 seconds

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Just tap foot off floor (no hold)
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform less frequently (3 times per week)
- Reduce hold to 1 second

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Lift higher (lift knee toward chest)
- Hold for 5 seconds
- Add resistance band around thigh
- Place weight on thigh
- Perform 4 sets × 12 reps
- Perform standing hip flexion (standing, lift knee while balancing)

**Pain Monitoring**: Front of hip should feel engaged. NRS ≤1. If groin pain develops, reduce range.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Hip flexion is crucial for every step you take. When you walk, you're constantly lifting your leg—that's hip flexion. Strengthening this movement improves your walking and climbing."

**Video Search**: "seated hip flexion strengthening"

### Exercise H5: Prone Hip Extension (Glute Activation, Prone)

**Target**: Gluteus maximus; hip extensors
**Position**: Lying face-down (prone) on bed or mat
**Clinical Relevance**: Hip extension needed for walking propulsion phase; glute activation crucial for posterior chain strength.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie face-down on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Place a pillow under your hips for comfort
3. Your arms can be at your sides or folded under your chest
4. Both legs should be extended straight behind you
5. Tighten your buttock muscle (glute) on one side
6. Slowly lift that leg about 6–8 inches off the bed, keeping your knee relatively straight
7. Your hip extends (leg moves backward)
8. Hold this lifted position for 2 seconds
9. Slowly lower your leg back down
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat with same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Lift Height**: 6–8 inches; progress to 10–12 inches
**Tempo**: 1.5 seconds up, 2-second hold, 1.5 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Reduce lift height (3–4 inches)
- Hold for 1 second
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Increase lift height (10–12 inches)
- Hold for 3 seconds
- Add ankle weight (1–2 lbs)
- Perform 3 sets × 12 reps
- Add hip abduction while extended (lift leg back and out to side)
- Squeeze glute maximally at top

**Pain Monitoring**: Glute should feel active. NRS ≤1. Avoid excessive lower-back arch (maintain neutral spine).

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Hip extension is the push-off phase of walking. Your glute provides the power. This exercise trains that movement pattern and builds strength for walking."

**Video Search**: "prone hip extension"

## Phase 2: Sub-Acute (Progressive Loading, Weight-Bearing Transitions)

**Phase 2 Focus**: Resistance band strengthening, progressive weight-bearing, functional pattern training.

### Exercise H6: Side-Lying Hip Abduction with Resistance Band

**Target**: Hip abductors (glute medius) with progressive resistance
**Position**: Side-lying with resistance band around thighs
**Clinical Relevance**: Adds resistive load to glute medius training; progresses from Phase 1 isometric work.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your side on a bed or cushioned floor
2. Bend both hips and knees to approximately 45 degrees
3. Place a resistance band around both thighs, just above the knees
4. Keep your feet together (in contact)
5. Slowly lift your top knee away from your bottom knee, creating tension on the band
6. Your hips should roll back slightly (but maintain side-lying position)
7. Lift your top knee about 8–10 inches (until good tension on band)
8. Hold for 2 seconds
9. Slowly lower your top knee back
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat on same side, then switch

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions per side, performed 4–5 times per week
**Band Resistance**: Light-to-moderate (should feel challenge)
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 2-second hold, 2 seconds down

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lighter resistance band
- Reduce lift height
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use heavier resistance band
- Perform series of pulses (small rapid movements at top)
- Hold for 3–4 seconds
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 reps
- Add extended-leg position (less knee flexion = harder lever)

**Pain Monitoring**: Lateral hip should feel strong. NRS ≤2. If lateral hip pain develops, reduce band tension.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Now you're adding resistance to your hip abductor work. The band provides just the right amount of challenge. Your muscle is getting stronger with each rep."

**Video Search**: "side lying hip abduction resistance band"

### Exercise H7: Bridges with Hip Marching (Dynamic Hip Extension)

**Target**: Gluteus maximus and stabilizers with dynamic element
**Position**: Bridge position with alternating leg lifts
**Clinical Relevance**: Combines stability (bridge hold) with dynamic movement (marching); mimics gait mechanics.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on floor (standard bridge starting position)
2. Push through your heels and raise your hips upward to full bridge position
3. Maintain this bridge position throughout entire exercise
4. Keeping your hips level and stable, slowly lift one foot off the floor about 2 inches
5. Hold that lifted position for 1 second
6. Lower that foot back to floor
7. Immediately lift the opposite foot
8. Hold for 1 second
9. Lower and rest for 1 second
10. Continue alternating feet (like marching while in bridge position)
11. Repeat for 10 total marches (5 per leg)

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10 total marches (5 per leg), performed 4–5 times per week
**Tempo**: Steady, continuous marching; 1 second per leg
**Core Engagement**: Maintain level hips; minimal trunk movement

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Hold bridge for longer duration before marching (hold 5 seconds, then march)
- Perform fewer marches (6–8 total)
- Do 2 sets instead of 3
- Perform less frequently (3 times per week)
- Lower bridge height slightly (partial bridge)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Hold bridge longer (pre-fatiguing glutes)
- Increase marching pace
- Perform more repetitions (15–20 marches)
- Add arm movements (while marching, lift opposite arm when lifting opposite leg)
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 marches
- Hold bridge on one leg (non-marching leg lifted, other leg marches)

**Pain Monitoring**: Glutes should feel strong throughout. NRS ≤1. If lower back pain develops, reduce bridge height.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Bridge marching trains your glutes to work dynamically. You're creating stability with one part of your glute while your hip is moving with the other. This is sophisticated movement training that mimics walking."

**Video Search**: "bridge marching exercise"

### Exercise H8: Sit-to-Stand Training (Functional Pattern, Progressive)

**Target**: Hip and knee extensors; functional sit-to-stand pattern; power development
**Position**: Seated in chair, standing
**Clinical Relevance**: Sit-to-stand is fundamental ADL; hip extension crucial for this movement; progression builds lower-extremity power.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Sit in a sturdy chair (with arms for support if needed; chair should be standard height, not too low)
2. Your feet should be flat on the floor, slightly wider than hip-width
3. Lean forward from your hips (nose should move toward your toes)
4. Push through your feet and stand up
5. As you stand, extend your hips and knees
6. Stand fully upright
7. Hold standing position for 2 seconds
8. Slowly lower yourself back to seated position
9. Sit for 2 seconds
10. Repeat

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 8–10 repetitions, performed 4–5 times per week
**Chair Height**: Standard height (18–20 inches); can use firmer chair in early Phase 2
**Tempo**: 2 seconds up, 2-second hold, 2–3 seconds down
**Progression Sequence**:
- Week 1–2: Use chair arms for assistance
- Week 2–3: Fingertip support on chair arms
- Week 3–4: No hand support, hands clasped in front

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use chair with arms; hold firmly during stand
- Use higher chair (add cushion to seat)
- Perform fewer repetitions (5–6)
- Do 2 sets instead of 3
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use standard or lower chair
- Remove hand support completely
- Hold standing position longer (5 seconds)
- Perform sit-stand-sit rapidly (repeated stands)
- Add weight (hold dumbbell on chest)
- Increase repetitions (12–15)

**Pain Monitoring**: Hip and knee should feel strong. NRS ≤2. If joint pain >2, use higher chair or reduce repetitions.

**Safety Considerations**:
- Use sturdy chair (not rolling chair)
- Clear space around chair
- Wear shoes with good grip
- Move slowly and deliberately
- Have spotter nearby if needed

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Sit-to-stand is something you do 50 times a day. If you're strong at this movement, you're strong at the basics of life. Making this easier and less painful is a major victory."

**Video Search**: "sit to stand exercise arthritis" or "sit to stand training"

## Phase 3–4: Functional and Return-to-Activity Hip Exercises

### Exercise H9: Standing Hip Extension (Weight-Bearing Hip Extension)

**Target**: Gluteus maximus; hip extension strength with weight-bearing; postural control
**Position**: Standing, holding chair/counter for balance
**Clinical Relevance**: Standing variation of prone hip extension; functional weight-bearing pattern for walking and climbing.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand upright facing a sturdy chair or counter
2. Hold the back of the chair with both hands
3. Feet hip-width apart
4. Keeping your trunk upright, slowly extend one leg backward
5. Your leg should move straight back (hip extension)
6. Move your leg about 8–10 inches backward
7. Do not arch your lower back excessively
8. Hold for 2 seconds
9. Slowly return your leg to starting position
10. Rest for 1 second
11. Repeat with same leg, then switch

**Dosage**: 3 sets × 10–12 repetitions per leg, performed 4–5 times per week
**Extension Range**: 8–10 inches (moderate range)
**Tempo**: 2 seconds back, 2-second hold, 2 seconds return

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use both hands on chair for support
- Reduce extension range (5 inches)
- Do 2 sets × 8 reps
- Perform 3 times per week

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use one-hand support, then no support
- Increase extension range (12–15 inches)
- Hold for 3 seconds
- Add resistance band around thighs
- Perform 4 sets × 12–15 reps
- Add weight (ankle weight, holding dumbbell)

**Pain Monitoring**: Glute should feel active. NRS ≤2. Avoid excessive lower-back arch.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "This is walking. When you push off to take a step, your hip is extending. Building strength in this movement makes walking stronger, faster, and more efficient."

**Video Search**: "standing hip extension arthritis"

---

### Exercise H10: Supine Hip Rotation (Internal/External Rotation ROM)

**Target**: Hip rotational ROM; graceful hip abductor/adductor activation
**Position**: Supine with one knee bent, crossing over body
**Clinical Relevance**: Hip rotation needed for changing direction while walking, getting in/out of cars; maintains rotational mobility.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Lie on your back on a bed or floor
2. Bend both knees, feet flat on floor
3. Slowly drop one knee toward the opposite side, crossing over your body
4. Your hip and knee rotate inward (internal rotation)
5. Allow gravity to gently stretch the hip
6. Hold for 20–30 seconds
7. Return to center (both feet flat)
8. Rest for 10 seconds
9. Repeat on opposite side (other knee drops toward opposite side)
10. Perform 2–3 repetitions per side

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets per session, performed daily (stretching is beneficial daily)
**Hold Duration**: 20–30 seconds per side
**Rotation Depth**: To tolerance (gentle stretch, NRS 0–2 acceptable)

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Place pillow under crossed knee for support
- Reduce hold time to 10–15 seconds
- Perform less frequently (3 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform hip rotation actively (rotate knee without gravity assistance)
- Add external rotation (opposite knee drops to opposite side)
- Combine with hip flexion (bring knee to chest before rotation)
- Increase hold to 45–60 seconds

**Pain Monitoring**: Stretch sensation is normal. NRS ≤2. If sharp pain develops, reduce range.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Hip rotation is mobility that keeps you flexible. If your hips can't rotate smoothly, you compensate with your spine or knees. These stretches maintain the mobility that keeps everything happy."

**Video Search**: "supine hip rotation stretch"

### Exercise H11: Step-Ups (Lateral, Hip-Focused)

**Target**: Hip abductors and extensors; frontal and sagittal plane strengthening
**Position**: Standing at side of low step
**Clinical Relevance**: Lateral step-ups emphasize hip abductors; lateral movement pattern strengthening.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Stand perpendicular to a low step (4–6 inches high), step to your side
2. Hold a chair or counter with the hand closest to the step for balance
3. Both feet together, parallel to the step
4. Step sideways onto the step with the leg closest to it
5. Drive through that leg to stand fully on the step
6. Your other leg may follow or may step down
7. Step back down with control
8. Return to starting position
9. Rest for 1 second
10. Repeat for 10 steps in one direction, then turn and repeat in opposite direction

**Dosage**: 2–3 sets × 10 steps per leg, performed 3–4 times per week
**Step Height**: 4–6 inches (Phase 3); progress to 8 inches (Phase 4)
**Tempo**: 1 second up, 1 second down; controlled

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Use lower step (2–3 inches)
- Use both hands for support
- Perform fewer steps (6–8)
- Do 2 sets instead of 3

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Use higher step (8–10 inches)
- Reduce hand support (fingertip, no support)
- Hold top position for 2 seconds
- Perform more steps (15–20)
- Add weight (hold dumbbell)

**Pain Monitoring**: Hip should feel strong. NRS ≤2. If lateral hip pain develops, check alignment.

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Lateral stepping is how you navigate the world sideways. You need hip strength for this. These lateral step-ups build that strength in the exact movement pattern you need."

**Video Search**: "lateral step-ups hip strengthening"

### Exercise H12: Aquatic Walking (Water-Based Aerobic Conditioning)

**Target**: Aerobic conditioning with reduced impact; hip ROM and endurance; walking gait training
**Position**: In swimming pool, waist-to-chest depth water
**Clinical Relevance**: Water buoyancy reduces joint loading while providing resistance; excellent for patients with significant pain or obesity.

**Step-by-Step Instructions**:

1. Enter pool at waist-to-chest depth (water supports body weight)
2. Stand upright in water
3. Begin walking forward at comfortable pace
4. Engage hip flexors to lift legs
5. Push off with hip extensors
6. Walk as if you're walking on land, just in water
7. Maintain upright posture
8. Continue for prescribed duration
9. Walk backward: same motion but reversed direction
10. Walk sideways: lateral stepping pattern in water

**Dosage**:
- Phase 2: 10–15 minutes forward/backward walking, 2–3 times per week
- Phase 3: 15–25 minutes with intervals (forward, backward, sideways), 3–4 times per week
- Target: build to 30 minutes, 3–4 times per week

**Water Depth**: Waist-to-chest depth (reduces weight-bearing load by 50–90% depending on depth)
**Pace**: Comfortable pace; perceived exertion light-to-moderate
**Tempo**: Natural walking rhythm; moderate cadence

**Modification (Easier)**:
- Perform in shallower water (less resistance)
- Use flotation belt (reduces load further)
- Reduce duration (5–10 minutes)
- Reduce frequency (1–2 times per week)

**Modification (Harder)**:
- Perform in deeper water (more resistance)
- Add resistance paddles or gloves (hand/arm resistance)
- Increase pace (faster walking)
- Add interval training (fast/slow alternations)
- Increase duration (30–45 minutes)
- Add water aerobics class (choreographed movements)

**Pain Monitoring**: Water exercise should feel easier on joints. NRS ≤1. If pain develops, move to shallower water.

**Additional Water Exercises** (Advanced):
- Water jogging (running without feet leaving bottom)
- High knee marching (exaggerated hip flexion)
- Side-stepping (lateral walking)
- Back-walking
- Resistance with water arm movements

**Kimi Coaching Tip**: "Water is magical for arthritis joints. Your body weighs much less in water, so there's no pounding on your joint. You can move freely and build fitness without pain. If you have access to a pool, take full advantage."

**Video Search**: "aquatic walking exercise arthritis" or "water exercise for hip pain"

## Cross-Phase Progressions and Return-to-Function Milestones

### Phase Transition Checklist

**Before advancing from Phase 2 to Phase 3**, patient should demonstrate:
- ✓ Pain-free (NRS ≤1) performance of Phase 2 exercises
- ✓ Hip strength adequate for sit-to-stand without upper-extremity support
- ✓ Ability to walk 20+ minutes without pain exacerbation
- ✓ PROMIS PF score ≥36 (Phase 2 threshold)
- ✓ Confidence and comfort in weight-bearing activities

**Before advancing from Phase 3 to Phase 4**, patient should demonstrate:
- ✓ Pain-free single-leg balance for 30+ seconds
- ✓ Full squats to parallel without compensatory movement
- ✓ Lateral stepping and walking without difficulties
- ✓ PROMIS PF score ≥41 (Phase 3 threshold)
- ✓ Ability to perform ADLs (stairs, grocery shopping, yard work) without exacerbation

### Return-to-Activity Clearance Criteria

Patient is cleared for return to specific activities when:
- NRS pain ≤1 during and ≤2 post-activity
- Demonstrates movement quality without compensations
- Demonstrates strength and endurance specific to activity
- Confidence in joint capacity (subjective report)
- PROMIS scores support functional demand

## Glossary and Common Terms

- **Adduction**: Movement of leg toward body midline (inward)
- **Abduction**: Movement of leg away from body midline (outward)
- **Extension**: Straightening movement of joint
- **Flexion**: Bending movement of joint
- **Isotonic**: Muscle contraction with movement against resistance
- **Isometric**: Muscle contraction without joint movement
- **MCID**: Minimal Clinically Important Difference (smallest meaningful change patient can detect)
- **NRS**: Numeric Rating Scale (0–10 pain scale)
- **OA**: Osteoarthritis
- **PROMIS**: Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System (standardized assessment)
- **ROM**: Range of Motion
- **RPM**: Revolutions Per Minute (cycling pedal cadence)

## Emergency Stop Rules

**STOP exercise immediately and escalate to clinician if:**
- Sharp, shooting pain during exercise (NRS >6)
- Pain with radiation (radiating down leg)
- Joint swelling >0.5 inches compared to opposite side
- Warmth or redness around joint
- Giving way or instability sensation
- Locking or catching sensation
- Pain significantly worse next morning despite reduced exertion
- New symptoms in adjacent joints (cascading pain patterns)
