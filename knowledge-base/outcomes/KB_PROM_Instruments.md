## 1. Overview and Administration Framework

### 1.1 CMS ACCESS Model PROM Compliance

The CMS ACCESS Model MSK Track requires administration of standardized, validated Patient-Reported Outcome Measures to track patient progress and measure the quality metric "Musculoskeletal Pain: Improvement in Pain and Function."

**Core Principle:** Kimi must administer PROMs in a **standardized, unbiased manner** — reading items verbatim, not leading or influencing responses, and allowing patients to answer freely based on their authentic experience.

### 1.2 Required Instruments by Body Region

| Body Region | Primary PROM(s) | Secondary PROM(s) | Universal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Low Back / Lumbar Spine** | ODI v2.1a | NRS, PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Yes |
| **Neck / Cervical Spine** | NDI | NRS, PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Yes |
| **Shoulder, Arm, Hand / Upper Extremity** | QuickDASH | NRS, PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Yes |
| **Knee / Tibiofemoral** | KOOS JR | NRS, PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Yes |
| **Hip / Femoroacetabular** | HOOS JR | NRS, PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Yes |
| **General MSK / Multi-region** | PROMIS PF 6b/CAT, PROMIS PI 6a/CAT | NRS | Yes |

**Legend:**
- **Primary PROM:** Region-specific instrument that captures functional disability for that body region
- **Secondary PROM:** Optional complementary instruments to assess cross-cutting domains
- **Universal:** Must be administered for all patients, regardless of region

### 1.3 Administration Best Practices (CMS Appendix C Compliance)

| Principle | Implementation |
|---|---|
| **Standardized Wording** | Read item text verbatim from this reference. Do NOT paraphrase, simplify, or reword items. Validated instruments depend on exact wording. |
| **Unbiased Administration** | Do NOT lead the patient toward any response. Use neutral tone. Do NOT suggest answers. |
| **Timing** | Administer PROMs BEFORE discussing treatment to avoid priming effects. |
| **Environment** | Private, quiet setting free of distractions. Allow patient adequate time to think and answer. |
| **Consistency** | Use same wording at each timepoint. Do NOT modify items between baseline and follow-up. |
| **Documentation** | Record date, time, administration method (conversational vs. form-based), and all raw item-level responses. |
| **Item Skipping** | Only allow skipping where the scoring algorithm explicitly permits. Otherwise, treat as missing item per algorithm (see Section 13). |
| **Missing Items** | If a patient cannot or refuses to answer an item, record as missing and follow the instrument-specific missing item protocol. |
| **Patient Privacy** | Ensure patient feels safe answering honestly. Frame PROMs as "feedback about YOUR experience" not as a test they can pass/fail. |

## 2. PROMIS Physical Function 6b & CAT

### 2.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System (PROMIS) Physical Function 6b
**Abbreviation:** PROMIS PF 6b (Fixed Form); PROMIS PF CAT (Computerized Adaptive Testing version)
**Validated Version:** PROMIS v1.0
**Copyright:** HealthMeasures, Northwestern University (publicly available for research and clinical use)
**LOINC Code:** 71963-7 (General PROMIS Physical Function domain)

**What It Measures:** Capability to perform self-care, mobility, and functional activities (e.g., climbing stairs, walking, ADLs)
**Body Regions:** General MSK (applicable to all body sites)
**Item Count:** 6 items (fixed form); 4-12 items (CAT, adaptive)
**Estimated Completion Time:** 1–2 minutes (fixed form); 2–3 minutes (CAT)
**Response Scale:** 5-point Likert (Without any difficulty → Unable to do)
**Score Range:** T-score metric (mean = 50, SD = 10 in US general population; range typically 20–80)

### 2.2 PROMIS Physical Function 6b Items

**Administration Format:** Conversational or form-based
**Instruction to Patient:** "The next questions ask about your ability to do everyday activities. Think about the last 7 days. For each activity, tell me if you are able to do it and, if so, how much difficulty you have. There are no right or wrong answers."

| Item # | Item Text | Response Options |
|---|---|---|
| PF 1 | Are you able to do chores such as vacuuming or yard work? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| PF 2 | Are you able to climb up five steps or stairs without stopping? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| PF 3 | Are you able to get out of bed on your own? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| PF 4 | Are you able to walk for at least 15 minutes? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| PF 5 | Are you able to reach up and get something from a high shelf or cabinet? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| PF 6 | Are you able to carry a heavy bag of groceries or a heavy object, like a suitcase? | 1 = Without any difficulty<br>2 = With a little difficulty<br>3 = With some difficulty<br>4 = With much difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |

### 2.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Raw Responses**
Record the numeric response (1–5) for each of the 6 items.

**Step 2: Check for Missing Items**
- If ≤1 item is missing, proceed to Step 3.
- If ≥2 items are missing, DO NOT score the instrument. Report as "insufficient data."

**Step 3: Convert to T-Score**
Use the PROMIS Conversion Table provided in HealthMeasures documentation. The 6-item fixed form has a lookup table based on raw sum scores.

**Alternative (Simplified): Raw Sum to T-Score Conversion**
Raw sum range: 6–30

The conversion from raw sum to T-score is non-linear. **Use the official HealthMeasures PROMIS PF conversion table** — this is critical for accurate scoring. If a conversion table is unavailable, calculate the raw sum as follows and note that T-score conversion requires calibration data.

**Raw Sum Calculation:**
```
Raw Sum = Sum of all 6 item responses (before imputation)
```

**T-Score Interpretation:**
The PROMIS scoring software or HealthMeasures online calculator converts raw sums to T-scores. T-scores have a standard mean of 50 (SD 10) in the US general population.

### 2.4 Score Interpretation

| T-Score Range | Interpretation | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **70+** | Very High Physical Function | Functioning well above average; minimal physical limitations |
| **60–69** | High Physical Function | Functioning above average; mild or no limitations in everyday activities |
| **50–59** | Average Physical Function | Functioning at population average; typical for age/condition |
| **40–49** | Below-Average Physical Function | Functioning below average; noticeable limitations in mobility or ADLs |
| **30–39** | Low Physical Function | Significant functional impairment; difficulty with many everyday activities |
| **<30** | Very Low Physical Function | Severe functional limitations; substantial difficulty performing ADLs |

### 2.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 2–6 T-score points | Varies by population (knee OA ~3, LBP ~4, general ~5). Context-dependent. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | T-score ≥50 (population average) | Varies by condition; knee OA often ≥55. |
| **Response Criterion (reliable change)** | ≥8.5 T-score points | Represents change beyond measurement error (95% confidence). |

**Clinical Application:**
- **Improvement:** Change ≥MCID (e.g., +5 T-points for general MSK) = clinically meaningful improvement
- **Worsening:** Change ≤-MCID = clinically meaningful decline
- **No meaningful change:** Change within MCID range = measurement noise or minimal clinical relevance

### 2.6 PROMIS Physical Function CAT (Computerized Adaptive Testing)

**When to Use CAT vs. 6b:**
- **Use 6b:** Paper/form-based administration; consistency with prior administrations
- **Use CAT:** When Kimi has access to PROMIS online administration platform; provides more precise, fewer items

**CAT Advantages:**
- Adapts item difficulty to patient's response level
- 4–12 items instead of fixed 6, reducing patient burden
- Comparable T-score output
- Higher precision for individual patient monitoring

**CAT Administration:** If using PROMIS CAT version, refer patient to HealthMeasures online portal or integrated EHR system. Kimi does not manually score CAT; the platform provides T-score automatically.

### 2.7 Kimi Administration Guide for PROMIS PF 6b

**Opening:** "Let's talk about what kinds of things you're able to do. I'm going to ask you about everyday activities over the past week. For each one, tell me honestly — are you able to do it, and how much difficulty would you have?"

**Probing for Clarity (if patient is uncertain):**
- "Think about the last 7 days. Have you done that activity or something similar?"
- "If you were to try that today, how would you manage?"

**Avoid Leading Language:**
- **DO NOT say:** "Most people with your condition have trouble with this — do you?"
- **DO say:** "Let's talk about [item]. How would that go for you?"

## 3. PROMIS Pain Interference 6a & CAT

### 3.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** PROMIS Pain Interference 6a
**Abbreviation:** PROMIS PI 6a (Fixed Form); PROMIS PI CAT (Computerized Adaptive Testing)
**Validated Version:** PROMIS v1.0
**Copyright:** HealthMeasures, Northwestern University
**LOINC Code:** 61763-4 (PROMIS Pain Interference domain)

**What It Measures:** The extent to which pain hinders engagement with social, occupational, recreational, and family roles; restricts activities of daily living; and interferes with sleep and emotional well-being
**Body Regions:** General MSK (applicable to all body sites)
**Item Count:** 6 items (fixed form); 4–12 items (CAT)
**Estimated Completion Time:** 1–2 minutes (fixed form); 2–3 minutes (CAT)
**Response Scale:** 5-point frequency scale (Not at all → Very much)
**Score Range:** T-score metric (mean = 50, SD = 10; range typically 25–80)

### 3.2 PROMIS Pain Interference 6a Items

**Administration Format:** Conversational or form-based
**Instruction to Patient:** "These questions ask about how much your pain has interfered with your daily activities over the past 7 days. For each statement, tell me how much your pain has gotten in the way. There are no right or wrong answers."

| Item # | Item Text | Response Options |
|---|---|---|
| PI 1 | In the past 7 days: My pain has interfered with my day-to-day activities. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| PI 2 | In the past 7 days: My pain has interfered with my work or other daily activities (including housework). | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| PI 3 | In the past 7 days: I have had trouble doing the things I enjoy. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| PI 4 | In the past 7 days: My pain has interfered with my ability to participate in social activities. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| PI 5 | In the past 7 days: Pain has limited my ability to think clearly. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| PI 6 | In the past 7 days: I have had trouble sleeping because of my pain. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = A little bit<br>3 = Somewhat<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |

### 3.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Raw Responses**
Record the numeric response (1–5) for each of the 6 items.

**Step 2: Check for Missing Items**
- If ≤1 item is missing, impute with the mean of completed items (see Section 13).
- If ≥2 items are missing, DO NOT score. Report as "insufficient data."

**Step 3: Convert to T-Score**
Use the PROMIS Conversion Table (HealthMeasures). Raw sum range: 6–30.

**Raw Sum Calculation:**
```
Raw Sum = Sum of all 6 item responses (after imputation if applicable)
```

Refer to official HealthMeasures PROMIS PI 6a conversion table to convert raw sum to T-score.

### 3.4 Score Interpretation

| T-Score Range | Interpretation | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **70+** | Very High Pain Interference | Pain severely disrupts daily activities, work, social participation; major quality-of-life impact |
| **60–69** | High Pain Interference | Pain substantially interferes with many activities and roles; moderate-to-severe quality-of-life impact |
| **50–59** | Average Pain Interference | Pain has noticeable impact on activities but manageable; typical for chronic pain population |
| **40–49** | Below-Average Pain Interference | Mild-to-moderate pain impact; patient maintains most activities |
| **30–39** | Low Pain Interference | Pain minimally interferes with activities; patient functioning near normal |
| **<30** | Very Low Pain Interference | Pain has negligible interference with daily life |

### 3.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 3–5 T-score points | General MSK ~4 points; context-dependent by condition. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | T-score ≤50–55 | Patients typically accept symptom state when PI ≤population average. |
| **Response Criterion** | ≥8.5 T-score points | Reliable change beyond measurement error. |

### 3.6 PROMIS Pain Interference CAT

**CAT Administration:** Same principle as PROMIS PF CAT — uses adaptive item selection, fewer items, higher precision. Use HealthMeasures online portal or integrated EHR.

### 3.7 Kimi Administration Guide for PROMIS PI 6a

**Opening:** "Now let's talk about how your pain has been affecting what you do. I'll ask about activities and roles that pain might get in the way of. Think about the last week."

**Clarification (if needed):**
- **For item PI 1:** "By day-to-day activities, I mean things like getting dressed, eating, bathing, or running errands."
- **For item PI 2:** "Think of work, housework, yard work — anything you do regularly."
- **For item PI 5:** "Do you find your pain makes it hard to concentrate or remember things?"

**Avoid Anchoring:**
- **DO NOT say:** "Most people with your diagnosis have lots of interference — do you?"
- **DO say:** "How much has pain gotten in the way of that for you?"

## 4. PROMIS Pain Intensity 1a (NRS v1.0)

### 4.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** PROMIS Pain Intensity Single Item (Numeric Rating Scale v1.0)
**Abbreviation:** PROMIS NRS v1.0 Pain Intensity 1a; or colloquially "NRS" or "Pain Scale 0–10"
**Validated Version:** PROMIS v1.0
**Copyright:** HealthMeasures, Northwestern University
**LOINC Code:** 84102-5 (Pain intensity - worst; Numeric Rating Scale), 72514-3 (Pain intensity - average)

**What It Measures:** Current or recent pain intensity on a 0–10 numeric scale
**Body Regions:** General MSK (applicable to all body sites)
**Item Count:** 1 item
**Estimated Completion Time:** <1 minute
**Response Scale:** 0–10 numeric scale
**Score Range:** 0 (no pain) to 10 (worst possible pain)

### 4.2 Item Text and Administration

**Standard Item (Most Common):**
"On a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 is no pain at all and 10 is the worst pain you could possibly imagine, how much pain are you having right now?"

**Alternative (Past 24 Hours Average):**
"Over the past 24 hours, on a scale from 0 to 10, what was your average pain level?"

**Alternative (Past 7 Days Worst):**
"Over the past 7 days, what was the worst pain you experienced? Rate it from 0 to 10, where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst pain you could imagine."

**Kimi's Task:** Use the timeframe specified in the care plan. If none specified, use **current pain intensity** (present moment or last 24 hours).

### 4.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Numeric Response**
Patient provides single integer or half-point value (0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, ... 9.5, 10).

**Step 2: Validate Response**
Confirm response is within 0–10 range. If patient gives non-numeric response (e.g., "moderate"), ask for numeric anchor: "On a scale of 0–10, where would you place that?"

**Step 3: Report Score**
The reported value IS the score. No conversion or calculation required.

### 4.4 Score Interpretation

| NRS Score | Category | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | No pain | Patient reports no pain |
| **1–3** | Mild pain | Minimal interference with daily activities |
| **4–6** | Moderate pain | Noticeable interference; activities modified but manageable |
| **7–8** | Severe pain | Substantial interference with most activities; significant distress |
| **9–10** | Worst possible pain | Disabling; patient in acute crisis or severe distress |

### 4.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 2-point change OR 30% reduction from baseline | Industry standard for MSK pain. Example: baseline 8 → end 6 (2-point drop) = MCID achieved. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | ≤3–4 (mild range) OR 50% reduction from baseline | Varies by condition; most patients accept symptom state when pain is mild (≤3). |
| **Reliable Change** | 2–3 point change | Differentiates true change from measurement error/fluctuation. |

**Clinical Example:**
- **Baseline NRS:** 7 (severe)
- **Week 6 NRS:** 5 (moderate)
- **Absolute Change:** -2 points → MCID achieved
- **Percent Change:** (7-5)/7 = 29% reduction → Near 30% MCID threshold
- **Clinical Interpretation:** Clinically meaningful improvement in pain intensity

### 4.6 Kimi Administration Guide for NRS

**Opening:** "I'd like to check in on your pain. On a scale from 0 to 10, where 0 means you have no pain at all and 10 means the worst pain you could possibly imagine, where would you say your pain is today?"

**If Patient Struggles with Numeric Scale:**
- Offer verbal anchors: "Think of it this way — a 0 is no pain. A 3 is mild, like a nuisance. A 5 is moderate, noticeable but you can get by. A 7 is severe, it's getting in the way of things. A 10 is the absolute worst pain imaginable. Where fits your pain?"
- Use visual: If form-based, show a 0–10 scale with faces or color gradient
- Allow half-points: "You can say 6.5 if that's more accurate than 6 or 7."

**Consistency Over Time:**
- Use same timeframe at each assessment (e.g., always "right now" or always "average over past week")
- Record the timeframe in the documentation

## 5. Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) v2.1a

### 5.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** Oswestry Disability Index (ODI), Version 2.1a (Standard Version)
**Abbreviation:** ODI, ODI-2.1
**Validated Version:** 2.1a (most recent; supersedes older versions like 1.0)
**Copyright:** Public domain; freely available for research and clinical use
**LOINC Code:** 54632-0 (Disability from back pain; Oswestry scale)

**What It Measures:** Functional disability and symptom severity due to low back pain; covers pain intensity, personal care, lifting, walking, sitting, standing, sleeping, social life, travel, and employment
**Body Regions:** Low back / Lumbar spine
**Item Count:** 10 sections (each has 6 response options; patient selects 1)
**Estimated Completion Time:** 5–7 minutes
**Response Scale:** 6-point ordinal (0–5 per section)
**Score Range:** 0% (no disability) to 100% (complete disability)

### 5.2 ODI v2.1a Items (Full Text)

**Administration Format:** Conversational or form-based
**Instruction to Patient:** "These questions ask about how your back pain affects your ability to do everyday activities. For each section, read all the options and choose the ONE statement that best describes your situation today. If more than one statement applies, choose the one that most applies to you."

| Section | Topic | Response Options (Select 1 per section) |
|---|---|---|
| **Section 1** | **Pain Intensity** | 0 = I have no pain at the moment<br>1 = The pain is very mild at the moment<br>2 = The pain is moderate at the moment<br>3 = The pain is fairly severe at the moment<br>4 = The pain is very severe at the moment<br>5 = The pain is the worst imaginable at the moment |
| **Section 2** | **Personal Care (washing, dressing, etc.)** | 0 = I can look after myself normally without causing increased pain<br>1 = I can look after myself normally, but it causes increased pain<br>2 = It is painful to look after myself and I am slow and careful<br>3 = I need some help, but manage most of my personal care<br>4 = I need help every day in most aspects of self-care<br>5 = I do not get dressed, I wash with difficulty and stay in bed |
| **Section 3** | **Lifting** | 0 = I can lift heavy weights without increased pain<br>1 = I can lift heavy weights, but it causes increased pain<br>2 = Pain prevents me from lifting heavy weights off the floor, but I can manage if they are conveniently positioned (e.g., on a table)<br>3 = Pain prevents me from lifting heavy weights, but I can manage light to medium weights if they are conveniently positioned<br>4 = I can lift very light weights, but I cannot carry them with my hands far from my body<br>5 = I cannot lift or carry anything at all |
| **Section 4** | **Walking** | 0 = Pain does not prevent me from walking any distance<br>1 = Pain prevents me from walking more than 1 mile<br>2 = Pain prevents me from walking more than 1/4 mile<br>3 = Pain prevents me from walking more than across a room<br>4 = I can only walk while using a cane or crutches<br>5 = I am in bed most of the time and have to crawl to the toilet |
| **Section 5** | **Sitting** | 0 = I can sit in any chair as long as I like<br>1 = I can sit in my favorite chair as long as I like<br>2 = Pain prevents me from sitting for more than 1 hour<br>3 = Pain prevents me from sitting for more than 1/2 hour<br>4 = Pain prevents me from sitting for more than 10 minutes<br>5 = I cannot sit at all |
| **Section 6** | **Standing** | 0 = I can stand as long as I want without increased pain<br>1 = I can stand as long as I want, but it increases pain<br>2 = Pain prevents me from standing for more than 1 hour<br>3 = Pain prevents me from standing for more than 1/2 hour<br>4 = Pain prevents me from standing for more than 10 minutes<br>5 = I cannot stand at all |
| **Section 7** | **Sleeping** | 0 = Pain does not prevent me from sleeping well<br>1 = I can sleep well only by using medication<br>2 = Even when I take medication, I sleep less than 6 hours<br>3 = Even when I take medication, I sleep less than 4 hours<br>4 = Even when I take medication, I sleep less than 2 hours<br>5 = Pain prevents me from sleeping at all |
| **Section 8** | **Social Life** | 0 = My social life is normal and does not increase my pain<br>1 = My social life is normal, but increases my pain<br>2 = Pain has no significant effect on my social life apart from limiting my more energetic interests, e.g., sports<br>3 = Pain restricts my social life, and I do not go out as often<br>4 = Pain has restricted my social life to my home<br>5 = I have hardly any social life because of the pain |
| **Section 9** | **Traveling** | 0 = I can travel anywhere without increased pain<br>1 = I can travel anywhere, but it increases my pain<br>2 = My pain limits the distance I can travel, e.g., I can only travel short distances<br>3 = My pain restricts all traveling except essential travel (to the doctor, etc.)<br>4 = My pain restricts all traveling except essential travel, and it is exhausting<br>5 = I cannot travel at all except to receive medical treatment |
| **Section 10** | **Employment/Work** | 0 = My normal job activities do not cause pain and I can do them all<br>1 = My normal job activities do not cause pain, but I can only do them for a limited time<br>2 = My pain is aggravated by my job or career (or normal activities if not employed), and I cannot do them all<br>3 = I cannot do my job as I would normally do it because of pain, but I can do some modified work<br>4 = I can barely do any work because of pain and need frequent pain relief treatment<br>5 = I cannot do any form of work because of the pain |

### 5.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Section Scores**
For each of the 10 sections, record the chosen response value (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5).

**Step 2: Calculate Raw Sum**
```
Raw Sum = Sum of all 10 section scores
Range: 0–50
```

**Step 3: Convert to Percentage Disability**
```
ODI Percentage = (Raw Sum / 50) × 100

Example:
- If raw sum = 25: (25/50) × 100 = 50% (moderate disability)
- If raw sum = 12: (12/50) × 100 = 24% (mild-to-moderate disability)
- If raw sum = 48: (48/50) × 100 = 96% (severe/bedbound)
```

**Step 4: Check for Missing Items**
- If ≤2 items are missing: Divide by the sum of scored items, then multiply by 50 to normalize.
  Example: If 9 items scored with sum = 22: (22/45) × 50 = 24.4%
- If ≥3 items are missing: DO NOT score. Report as "insufficient data."

### 5.4 Score Interpretation

| ODI % | Disability Category | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **0–20%** | Minimal Disability | Patient coping well; mild limitations in some activities; good prognosis |
| **21–40%** | Moderate Disability | Noticeable impact on daily activities; some functional restrictions; requires intervention |
| **41–60%** | Severe Disability | Substantial functional impairment; significant restriction in most activities; strong indication for intensive treatment |
| **61–80%** | Crippling Disability | Major functional limitation; severe disability; patient may be unable to work or manage ADLs independently |
| **81–100%** | Bedbound / Symptom Magnification | Patient severely limited or confined; suggests severe pathology, psychosocial factors, or non-organic causes |

### 5.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 10–12.8 percentage points | Consensus: 10-point change in ODI % = clinically significant. Literature range 4–15 points. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | ≤20% | Patient typically satisfied when disability is minimal. Some populations accept up to 25%. |
| **Reliable Change Index** | 15 percentage points | Change distinguishing true improvement from measurement error (95% CI). |

**Clinical Application:**
- **Improvement:** Change ≥10 percentage points (e.g., 50% → 40%) = MCID achieved
- **Worsening:** Change ≤-10 percentage points = clinically meaningful decline
- **Stabilization:** Change within ±10 points = no meaningful change

### 5.6 Kimi Administration Guide for ODI v2.1a

**Opening:** "I'm going to ask you 10 questions about how your back pain affects your daily life. For each question, read or listen to all the choices and pick the ONE statement that best describes how you're doing today. There are no right or wrong answers — just be honest about what's true for you."

**Key Administration Points:**
- **One answer per section only:** "You need to choose just one option for each question."
- **Today's status:** "Think about how you are TODAY, not how you usually are."
- **All activities count:** If they don't do an activity due to pain, that should be reflected in their choice.
- **Normalize difficulty:** "It's okay if multiple options seem to fit — just pick the one that's MOST true for you."

**Clarifying (if needed):**
- **For Section 2 (Personal Care):** "This means things like brushing your teeth, combing your hair, bathing, getting dressed."
- **For Section 8 (Social Life):** "Think about visiting friends, going out to eat, hobbies, activities you enjoy."

## 6. Neck Disability Index (NDI)

### 6.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** Neck Disability Index (NDI), Standard Version
**Abbreviation:** NDI
**Validated Version:** Original (Vernacular) version (most widely used)
**Copyright:** Public domain; freely available for clinical and research use
**LOINC Code:** 54633-8 (Disability from neck pain; Neck Disability Index)

**What It Measures:** Functional disability and symptom severity due to neck pain; covers pain intensity, personal care, lifting, reading, headaches, concentration, work, driving, sleeping, and recreation
**Body Regions:** Neck / Cervical spine
**Item Count:** 10 sections (each has 6 response options; patient selects 1)
**Estimated Completion Time:** 5–7 minutes
**Response Scale:** 6-point ordinal (0–5 per section)
**Score Range:** 0% (no disability) to 100% (complete disability)

### 6.2 NDI Items (Full Text)

**Administration Format:** Conversational or form-based
**Instruction to Patient:** "These questions ask about how your neck pain affects your daily life. For each section, read all the choices and pick the ONE statement that best describes your situation today. If more than one statement applies, choose the one that fits best."

| Section | Topic | Response Options (Select 1 per section) |
|---|---|---|
| **Section 1** | **Pain Intensity** | 0 = I have no pain at the moment<br>1 = I have mild pain at the moment<br>2 = I have moderate pain at the moment<br>3 = I have fairly severe pain at the moment<br>4 = I have very severe pain at the moment<br>5 = I have the worst pain imaginable at the moment |
| **Section 2** | **Personal Care (washing, dressing, etc.)** | 0 = I can look after myself without causing increased pain<br>1 = I can look after myself normally, but it causes increased pain<br>2 = It is painful to look after myself and I am slow and careful<br>3 = I need some help, but manage most of my personal care<br>4 = I need help every day in most aspects of my personal care<br>5 = I cannot get dressed, have difficulty washing, and stay in bed |
| **Section 3** | **Lifting** | 0 = I can lift heavy weights without increased pain<br>1 = I can lift heavy weights, but it causes increased pain<br>2 = Pain prevents me from lifting heavy weights off the floor<br>3 = Pain prevents me from lifting heavy weights off a table (e.g., lifting a heavy object)<br>4 = I can lift very light weights<br>5 = I cannot lift or carry anything at all |
| **Section 4** | **Reading** | 0 = I can read as much as I want without neck pain<br>1 = I can read as much as I want, but it causes neck pain<br>2 = I have slight difficulty reading because of neck pain<br>3 = I have moderate difficulty reading because of neck pain<br>4 = I have severe difficulty reading because of neck pain<br>5 = I cannot read at all because of neck pain |
| **Section 5** | **Headaches** | 0 = I have no headaches at all<br>1 = I have slight headaches which are not very often<br>2 = I have mild headaches which are fairly often<br>3 = I have moderate headaches which are fairly often<br>4 = I have severe headaches which are very often<br>5 = I have headaches almost all the time |
| **Section 6** | **Concentration** | 0 = I can concentrate fully when I want to with no difficulty<br>1 = I can concentrate fully when I want to with slight difficulty<br>2 = I have a fair degree of difficulty in concentrating when I want to<br>3 = I have moderate difficulty in concentrating when I want to<br>4 = I have considerable difficulty in concentrating when I want to<br>5 = I cannot concentrate at all |
| **Section 7** | **Work** | 0 = I can do as much work as I want<br>1 = I can only do my usual work, but no more<br>2 = I can do most of my usual work, but no more<br>3 = I cannot do my usual work<br>4 = I can hardly do any work at all<br>5 = I cannot do any work at all |
| **Section 8** | **Driving** | 0 = I can drive a car without any neck pain<br>1 = I can drive a car as long as I want with slight neck pain<br>2 = I can drive a car as long as I want with moderate neck pain<br>3 = I cannot drive a car as long as I want because of neck pain<br>4 = I can hardly drive at all because of neck pain<br>5 = I cannot drive a car at all |
| **Section 9** | **Sleeping** | 0 = No neck pain does not interfere with my sleep<br>1 = My neck pain does not prevent me from sleeping well<br>2 = My neck pain slightly disturbs my sleep<br>3 = My neck pain moderately disturbs my sleep<br>4 = My neck pain makes it difficult to sleep<br>5 = My neck pain prevents me from sleeping at all |
| **Section 10** | **Recreation (hobbies, sports, leisure)** | 0 = I am able to engage in all my usual hobbies and recreation activities without neck pain<br>1 = I am able to engage in all my usual hobbies and recreation activities, with some neck pain<br>2 = I am able to engage in most, but not all of my usual hobbies and recreation activities because of neck pain<br>3 = I am able to engage in some of my usual hobbies and recreation activities because of neck pain<br>4 = I am able to engage in a few of my usual hobbies and recreation activities because of neck pain<br>5 = I am unable to engage in any of my usual hobbies and recreation activities because of neck pain |

### 6.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Section Scores**
For each of the 10 sections, record the chosen response value (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5).

**Step 2: Calculate Raw Sum**
```
Raw Sum = Sum of all 10 section scores
Range: 0–50
```

**Step 3: Convert to Percentage Disability**
```
NDI Percentage = (Raw Sum / 50) × 100

Example:
- If raw sum = 15: (15/50) × 100 = 30% (moderate disability)
- If raw sum = 8: (8/50) × 100 = 16% (mild disability)
- If raw sum = 42: (42/50) × 100 = 84% (severe disability)
```

**Step 4: Check for Missing Items**
- If ≤2 items are missing: Calculate mean of completed items, then multiply by 5 to estimate missing section scores.
  Example: If 9 items scored with sum = 18: (18/9) × 5 = 10 for missing section. Total estimate = 28/50 = 56%.
- If ≥3 items are missing: DO NOT score. Report as "insufficient data."

### 6.4 Score Interpretation

| NDI % | Disability Category | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **0–8%** | No Disability | Patient functioning well; minimal symptoms |
| **10–28%** | Mild Disability | Minor functional limitations; manageable symptoms; good prognosis |
| **30–48%** | Moderate Disability | Noticeable functional impairment; multiple activity restrictions; requires intervention |
| **50–68%** | Severe Disability | Substantial disability; major limitations in work, driving, ADLs; warrants intensive treatment |
| **70–100%** | Complete Disability | Extreme functional limitation; potential for serious pathology or significant psychosocial factors |

### 6.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 5.5 percentage points (non-radicular) to 8.5 percentage points (with radicular symptoms) | Literature consensus varies; mechanical neck pain often uses 10-point threshold as practically meaningful. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | ≤15–20% | Patient typically satisfied when NDI indicates minimal disability. |
| **Reliable Change Index** | 15 percentage points | Differentiates true change from measurement error. |

**Clinical Application:**
- **Non-radicular neck pain:** MCID = 5.5 percentage points
- **Neck pain with radicular symptoms (arm pain, numbness):** MCID = 8.5 percentage points
- **Practical threshold:** Many clinicians use 10-point change as "clinically meaningful" for routine monitoring

### 6.6 Kimi Administration Guide for NDI

**Opening:** "I'm going to ask you 10 questions about how your neck pain is affecting your daily life. For each question, read all the choices and pick the ONE that best describes how you're doing today. Be honest — there are no right or wrong answers."

**Key Points:**
- **Radicular symptoms clarification:** Early in assessment, ask: "Are you having pain that goes down your arm? Numbness or tingling in your arm or hand?" This may influence MCID threshold later.
- **Work/recreation context:** "When we ask about work, if you're retired or don't work outside the home, think about the activities you normally do — like household tasks or hobbies."
- **Driving importance:** In neck pain, driving is often a key functional domain. Probe: "How is your neck affecting your ability to drive or sit as a passenger?"

**Clarifications (if needed):**
- **Section 4 (Reading):** "Can you read books, phone screens, or printed materials?"
- **Section 5 (Headaches):** "Are you getting headaches along with your neck pain? How often and how severe?"
- **Section 8 (Driving):** "Think about being in the car — both the turning of your head and the sitting position."

## 7. QuickDASH

### 7.1 Instrument Overview

**Full Name:** Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder, and Hand (QuickDASH) — Quick Version
**Abbreviation:** QuickDASH
**Validated Version:** Standard (modified from original 30-item DASH)
**Copyright:** American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons; freely available for research and clinical use
**LOINC Code:** 54632-0 (appears to map to general disability from arm/shoulder pain)

**What It Measures:** Physical disability and symptom burden from upper extremity (shoulder, arm, hand, wrist) conditions; covers ADLs, work/leisure, social activities, sleep, pain, and psychological symptoms
**Body Regions:** Shoulder, arm, hand, wrist / Upper extremity
**Item Count:** 11 items
**Estimated Completion Time:** 2–3 minutes
**Response Scale:** 5-point Likert (No difficulty → Unable to do)
**Score Range:** 0 (no disability) to 100 (most disability)

### 7.2 QuickDASH Items (Full Text)

**Administration Format:** Conversational or form-based
**Instruction to Patient:** "These questions ask about your ability to do activities and your symptoms. For each activity, think about the past week. Tell me the level of difficulty you have. If you don't normally do an activity, estimate the difficulty if you had to do it."

| Item # | Item Text | Response Options |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Over the past week, how much difficulty have you had with usual work, schoolwork, or other daily activities because of arm, shoulder, or hand problems? | 1 = No difficulty<br>2 = Mild difficulty<br>3 = Moderate difficulty<br>4 = Severe difficulty<br>5 = Unable to do |
| **2** | Over the past week, have you had pain in your arm, shoulder, or hand? | 1 = No pain<br>2 = Mild pain<br>3 = Moderate pain<br>4 = Severe pain<br>5 = Extreme pain |
| **3** | During the past week, activities such as throwing a ball, hammering, or carrying a heavy bag were limited because of arm, shoulder, or hand problems. | 1 = Not limited at all<br>2 = Slightly limited<br>3 = Moderately limited<br>4 = Very limited<br>5 = Unable to do |
| **4** | Over the past week, have you had symptoms such as tingling (pins and needles) in any of your fingers? | 1 = Never<br>2 = Rarely<br>3 = Sometimes<br>4 = Often<br>5 = Very often |
| **5** | Over the past week, has pain in the arm, shoulder, or hand interfered with your normal social activities with family, friends, groups, or work colleagues? | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = Slightly<br>3 = Moderately<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| **6** | Over the past week, your arm, shoulder, or hand symptoms have limited your ability to do household chores, yard work, or home repairs. | 1 = Not limited at all<br>2 = Slightly limited<br>3 = Moderately limited<br>4 = Very limited<br>5 = Unable to do |
| **7** | Over the past week, have you had difficulty sleeping because of the pain in your arm, shoulder, or hand? | 1 = No difficulty<br>2 = Mild difficulty<br>3 = Moderate difficulty<br>4 = Severe difficulty<br>5 = Unable to sleep |
| **8** | Over the past week, I feel less capable, confident, or effective at work because of arm, shoulder, or hand problems. | 1 = Strongly disagree<br>2 = Disagree<br>3 = Neutral<br>4 = Agree<br>5 = Strongly agree |
| **9** | Over the past week, arm, shoulder, or hand symptoms have interfered with your ability to use your computer, cellphone, or other device for writing or holding objects. | 1 = Not at all<br>2 = Slightly<br>3 = Moderately<br>4 = Quite a bit<br>5 = Very much |
| **10** | Over the past week, have you been worried about injuring your arm, shoulder, or hand further or re-injuring it? | 1 = Not worried at all<br>2 = Mildly worried<br>3 = Moderately worried<br>4 = Very worried<br>5 = Extremely worried |
| **11** | Over the past week, I have had trouble carrying objects because of weakness, pain, or other symptoms in my arm, shoulder, or hand. | 1 = Strongly disagree<br>2 = Disagree<br>3 = Neutral<br>4 = Agree<br>5 = Strongly agree |

### 7.3 Scoring Algorithm

**Step 1: Record Item Responses**
For each of the 11 items, record the numeric response (1–5).

**Step 2: Check for Missing Items**
- If ≤1 item is missing: Calculate the mean of completed items and substitute for the missing item.
- If ≥2 items are missing: DO NOT score. Report as "insufficient data."

**Step 3: Calculate Raw Sum**
```
Raw Sum = Sum of all 11 item responses (after substitution if applicable)
Range: 11–55
```

**Step 4: Transform to 0–100 Scale**
```
QuickDASH Score = [(Raw Sum / 55) – 1] × 25

Or equivalently:
QuickDASH Score = [(Raw Sum – 11) / 44] × 100

Example 1:
- Raw sum = 22: [(22/55) – 1] × 25 = [-0.6] × 25 = -15  [ERROR — check formula]
- Correct: [(22 – 11) / 44] × 100 = (11/44) × 100 = 25

Example 2:
- Raw sum = 33: [(33 – 11) / 44] × 100 = (22/44) × 100 = 50

Example 3:
- Raw sum = 55: [(55 – 11) / 44] × 100 = (44/44) × 100 = 100
```

**Recommended Formula (Clearer):**
```
QuickDASH Score = [Sum of responses – 11] / 44 × 100
```

**Step 5: Round to Nearest Integer**
Report QuickDASH as whole number (0–100).

### 7.4 Score Interpretation

| QuickDASH Score | Disability Level | Clinical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **0–11** | Minimal Disability | Minimal or no symptoms; normal function |
| **12–27** | Mild Disability | Mild functional limitations; minor symptoms; manageable |
| **28–44** | Moderate Disability | Moderate functional restrictions; noticeable impact on activities; requires intervention |
| **45–61** | Severe Disability | Substantial disability; significant limitations in most upper extremity activities; warrants intensive treatment |
| **62–100** | Very Severe Disability | Extreme functional limitation; major restrictions across all domains |

### 7.5 MCID and PASS Thresholds

| Metric | Value | Citation |
|---|---|---|
| **MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)** | 12–15 points | Systematic review consensus (Beaton et al., CMAJ 2001); some sources suggest up to 20 points as upper boundary. |
| **PASS (Patient Acceptable Symptom State)** | ≤27 (mild range or better) | Threshold where patients report satisfaction with symptom state; varies by condition. |
| **Reliable Change Index** | 17–20 points | Represents change beyond measurement error. |

**Clinical Application:**
- **Improvement:** Change ≥12–15 points (e.g., 50 → 35) = MCID achieved
- **Worsening:** Change ≤-12 points = clinically meaningful decline
- **Stabilization:** Change within ±12 points = no clinically meaningful change

### 7.6 Kimi Administration Guide for QuickDASH

**Opening:** "I'm going to ask you 11 questions about your shoulder, arm, or hand symptoms over the past week. Think about activities you normally do, and rate your difficulty with each. If you don't do the activity normally, estimate how difficult it would be if you had to do it. Be honest about how much your arm or hand is limiting you."

**Key Clarifications:**
- **Item 3 (Throwing, hammering, carrying):** "Think about activities that take strength or force in your arm — lifting a heavy grocery bag, throwing something, swinging a hammer. How limited are you?"
- **Item 4 (Tingling/paresthesias):** "Have you noticed pins-and-needles sensations, numbness, or 'asleep' feeling in your fingers or hand?"
- **Item 8 (Work confidence):** "This is about how your symptoms are affecting your confidence in your ability to do your work or daily tasks — not your actual performance, but how capable you feel."
- **Item 10 (Fear of re-injury):** "Have you been worried about hurting your arm or hand further, or making it worse?"

**Avoiding Bias:**
- **DO NOT say:** "With your diagnosis, most people can't throw — can you?"
- **DO say:** "If you were to try to throw a ball (or hammer a nail), how would that go?"

**Validated Version:** Standard (single-item measure)
**Copyright:** Public domain; widely used in clinical trials and practice
**LOINC Code:** 81525-3 (Participant self-assessed feeling better or worse; Likert scale)

**What It Measures:** Patient's self-assessed global perception of change (improvement or deterioration) since the beginning of treatment; captures overall sense of benefit
**Body Regions:** Any MSK region (summary of overall change)
**Item Count:** 1 item
**Estimated Completion Time:** <1 minute
**Response Scale:** 7-point Likert (Very much improved → Very much worse)
**Score Range:** 1–7 (1 = very much improved; 7 = very much worse)

### 10.2 PGIC Item

**Administration Format:** Conversational
**Timing:** Administered ONLY at end-of-care period (typically 12 weeks or end of treatment); NOT at baseline or interim timepoints
**Instruction to Patient:** "Compared to when you started the program, how would you say your [condition/symptoms/overall condition] has changed?"

| Response | Scale Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Very much improved** | 1 | Substantial improvement; patient very satisfied with outcome |
| **Much improved** | 2 | Clinically meaningful improvement; patient satisfied |
| **Slightly improved** | 3 | Noticeable improvement; partial benefit; room for further improvement |
| **No change** | 4 | No meaningful change in symptoms or function |
| **Slightly worse** | 5 | Slight deterioration; minimal worsening |
| **Much worse** | 6 | Substantial worsening; patient dissatisfied |
| **Very much worse** | 7 | Severe deterioration; major negative outcome |

### 10.3 Scoring

**No calculation required.** The PGIC score IS the patient's selected response (1–7).

### 10.4 Score Interpretation

| PGIC Response | Category | Clinical Meaning | Alignment with MCID |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 or 2** | Improved | Patient reports clinically meaningful global improvement | Typically correlates with ≥MCID on objective PROMs |
| **3** | Slightly Improved | Mild benefit; partial improvement | May align with small MCID on some measures |
| **4** | No Change | Stable; no perceived improvement or worsening | No clinically meaningful change |
| **5, 6, or 7** | Worse | Patient perceives deterioration | May indicate failure of intervention or adverse event |

### 10.5 Clinical Significance

**CMS ACCESS Model Requirement:** PGIC is a mandatory outcome measure at end-of-period. Patients responding 1 or 2 ("Much improved" or "Very much improved") are considered to have achieved a clinically meaningful outcome.

**Research Evidence:** A PGIC response of 2 ("Much improved") often corresponds to:
- Approximately 30% reduction in pain intensity (NRS)
- Change ≥MCID on region-specific disability scores (ODI, NDI, QuickDASH, KOOS JR, HOOS JR)
- Patient satisfaction with treatment outcome

**Caveat:** PGIC is a subjective, global measure. It can be influenced by:
- Patient expectations (managed during intake)
- Emotional factors (mood, coping, psychosocial adjustment)
- Anchoring (comparing to the worst point vs. to baseline)
- Regression to the mean (especially if baseline was a crisis point)

### 10.6 Kimi Administration Guide for PGIC

**Timing:** Administer ONLY at end of care (e.g., 12 weeks or at discharge).

**Opening:** "We've been working together for [duration]. Now I'd like to ask you one final question: Comparing how you're doing now to when we started, how would you say your [back pain / neck pain / shoulder pain / knee pain / overall symptoms] has changed?"

**Response Options (Read all before patient answers):**
"Here are the options:
1. Very much improved — much better than before
2. Much improved — better, but not quite as much as 'very much'
3. Slightly improved — a little bit better
4. No change — about the same as when we started
5. Slightly worse — a little bit worse
6. Much worse — noticeably worse
7. Very much worse — much worse than when we started

Which one fits best?"

**Clarifications (if needed):**
- "Think about how you're doing overall — your pain, your ability to do activities, your function. Has that improved, worsened, or stayed about the same?"
- "Some people compare to when they felt worst. I'm asking you to compare to how you were when we started."
- "Be honest. If you don't feel better, that's important information."

**Avoid Leading Language:**
- **DO NOT say:** "You've been working hard — you must feel much better, right?"
- **DO say:** "Looking back, how do you feel your symptoms have changed?"

## 11. CMS ACCESS Model Administration Schedule

### 11.1 Two-Tier PROM Collection Strategy

KIMI operates with a two-tier PROM collection strategy. **Tier 1 (CMS-Mandated)** is the payment-critical requirement — these are the collections that MUST happen or RevelAi does not get paid. **Tier 2 (Clinical Optimization)** is the more frequent collection cadence that supports real-time clinical decision-making, phase progression, and patient engagement. Both tiers matter, but Tier 1 is non-negotiable.

#### Tier 1: CMS-Mandated Collection (Payment-Critical)

Per the CMS ACCESS Model Request for Applications (RFA) and the March 2026 API Implementation Technical Office Hour, the MSK track requires the following OAP Measure reporting:

| Timepoint | Schedule | Required OAP Measures (CMS) | Submission Deadline | Payment Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Baseline** | Start of care period | PROMIS PF 6b/CAT, PROMIS PI 6a/CAT, NRS (or PROMIS NRS v1.0 Pain Intensity 1a), site-specific PROMs (ODI, NDI, QuickDASH, KOOS JR, HOOS JR) | 60 days from enrollment | **No baseline = no improvement denominator = no payment eligibility** |
| **Quarterly** (Q1, Q2, Q3) | Every 3 months | Same as baseline (PROMIS PF, PI, NRS, site-specific PROMs) | Within **15-day clinical validity window** of collection date | **Quarterly data required for OAP payment determination** |
| **End-of-Period** (Month 12) | At program completion | Same as baseline **+ PGIC** (Patient Global Impression of Change) | Within 15-day validity window; may be submitted up to **180 days before** the 1-year mark (early success reporting) | **Final payment determination — improvement vs. baseline** |

**Source:** CMS ACCESS Model RFA, Section on OAP Measures; CMS API Implementation Technical Office Hour (March 18, 2026), Slides 27-28.

**Critical Compliance Rules:**
1. **15-day clinical validity window** — Maximum time between PROM collection date and CMS data submission via the Data Reporting API. Miss this window and the data is not accepted.
2. **Same instrument throughout** — The PROM instrument used at baseline must be used at every subsequent timepoint. You cannot switch from ODI to NDI mid-course.
3. **Early success reporting** — End-of-period MSK measures may be submitted up to 180 days before the 1-year mark. If scores are favorable at the 6-month mark, submit early to lock in the outcome.
4. **PGIC is end-of-period only** — Do not administer PGIC at baseline or quarterly; it is only valid as a retrospective global impression at program completion.

#### Tier 2: Clinical Optimization Collection (Supports Care Quality)

More frequent collection enables real-time clinical decision-making, phase progression, and early detection of deterioration. This cadence is driven by the Limber platform's rehabilitation protocol and KIMI's Phase Manager:

| Collection | Cadence | Instruments | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Daily pain check-in** | Daily | NRS (0-10, brief) | Exercise pain monitoring, flare-up detection, dosing adjustment |
| **Weekly PROMIS assessment** | Weekly | PROMIS PF, PROMIS PI | Phase progression criteria (PROMIS thresholds drive advancement/regression) |
| **Ad hoc assessment** | As needed | NRS + relevant site-specific PROM | Triggered by patient-reported flare-up, notable change, or clinician request |

**Important:** Tier 2 collections serve KIMI's clinical intelligence — they drive phase progression, exercise modification, and escalation decisions. However, **Tier 1 quarterly submissions are what CMS uses to determine payment.** If resource constraints force a trade-off, always prioritize Tier 1 completeness.

#### Combined View: Full Collection Calendar

| Timepoint | Tier 1 (CMS-Mandated) | Tier 2 (Clinical) |
|---|---|---|
| **Day 1-10** | Baseline: Full PROM battery | — |
| **Daily** | — | NRS pain check-in |
| **Weekly** | — | PROMIS PF + PI |
| **Month 3** | **Q1 Quarterly: Full PROM battery** | (Quarterly supersedes weekly) |
| **Month 6** | **Q2 Quarterly: Full PROM battery** | Consider early success reporting |
| **Month 9** | **Q3 Quarterly: Full PROM battery** | — |
| **Month 12** | **End-of-Period: Full battery + PGIC** | Final outcome determination |

### 11.2 CMS Reporting Requirements

- **Raw Item-Level Data:** Store responses to each individual item (not just summary scores) for CMS audit and validation
- **Administration Metadata:** Record date, time, method (conversational vs. form), and any administration notes
- **Missing Items:** Document which items were skipped, refused, or unable to be administered, and reason
- **Score Transformation:** For each PROM, document the raw sum and final score; show calculation for transparency
- **Change Scores:** For all PROMs, calculate and document change from baseline at each timepoint

## 12. Standardized Administration Protocol

### 12.1 Pre-Administration Setup

**Environment:**
- Private, quiet, distraction-free setting
- Patient seated comfortably
- No time pressure; allow patient adequate time to consider each item

**Patient Preparation:**
- Explain purpose: "These questionnaires help me understand how your [condition] is affecting your daily life. Your honest answers are important."
- Reassure confidentiality and non-judgment: "There are no right or wrong answers. I just want to hear about your real experience."
- Clarify timeframe: "When I ask about the past week (or month), think about what's typical for you during that time."

### 12.2 During Administration

**Reading & Wording:**
- Read item text verbatim from this reference document
- Do NOT paraphrase, simplify, or modify wording
- Use the exact response options provided
- Repeat an item if patient asks; do NOT interpret or clarify beyond the item itself

**Pacing:**
- Allow patient sufficient time to think and answer; do NOT rush
- Pause between items
- Allow patient to ask clarifying questions (within boundaries — see below)

**Clarification Boundaries:**
- **ALLOWED:** "What do you mean by that?" "Can you tell me more about that?" "What was a typical week like?"
- **NOT ALLOWED:** "Most people with your problem have trouble with this — do you?" (leading) or "I think you should answer that way" (directing)

### 12.3 Handling Patient Confusion

**If Patient Doesn't Understand an Item:**
1. **First:** Repeat the item verbatim
2. **If Still Unclear:** Offer a contextualized example: "For instance, if this asks about walking, think about how far you can walk before pain stops you."
3. **If Still Unclear:** Mark as unanswered and continue (see Section 13 for missing item protocol)

**If Patient Asks About "Correct" Response:**
- "I'm not looking for a right answer. I want to know what's true for YOU. Whatever you experience is the right answer for you."

### 12.4 Body Language & Tone

- **Neutral tone:** Do NOT use inflection that suggests surprise, judgment, or preference for any response
- **Open body language:** Lean slightly forward; maintain eye contact; nod to acknowledge; do NOT shake head or frown
- **Timing:** If patient is emotional or in distress, allow them to compose themselves; validate their experience

## 13. Handling Missing Items

### 13.1 Missing Item Protocol by Instrument

| Instrument | Missing Item Threshold | Imputation Method | Scoring Decision |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PROMIS PF 6b** | ≤1 missing | Mean of completed items | Acceptable; use conversion table |
| **PROMIS PI 6a** | ≤1 missing | Mean of completed items | Acceptable; use conversion table |
| **NRS (1 item)** | 0 missing | N/A (single item) | If missing, report as "not assessed" |
| **ODI (10 items)** | ≤2 missing | Divide by completed items; normalize to /50 | Acceptable with caution |
| **NDI (10 items)** | ≤2 missing | Calculate mean of completed, estimate missing | Acceptable with caution |
| **QuickDASH (11 items)** | ≤1 missing | Mean of completed items | Acceptable |
| **KOOS JR (7 items)** | ≤2 missing | Mean of completed items | Acceptable |
| **HOOS JR (6 items)** | ≤1 missing | Mean of completed items | Acceptable |
| **PGIC (1 item)** | 0 missing | N/A (single item) | If missing, report as "not assessed" |

### 13.2 Preventing Missing Items During Administration

**Strategies:**
1. **Explicit instruction:** "I need you to answer all of the questions. Even if you don't normally do an activity, estimate how difficult it would be if you had to."
2. **Normalize inability:** "If an activity doesn't apply to you, that's okay — just tell me how difficult you think it would be."
3. **Check completeness:** At the end, review with patient: "Let me make sure we've answered all the questions..." and fill in any gaps
4. **Document reason for missing:** If patient genuinely cannot answer, record reason (e.g., "not applicable," "patient unable to decide," "physical limitation prevented response")

## 14. Presenting Results to Patients

### 14.1 Plain-Language Score Interpretation

When sharing PROM results with patients, Kimi MUST translate clinical scores into understandable language. Use 6th–8th grade reading level and concrete examples.

### 14.2 Baseline Results Presentation

**Sample Script (for any baseline PROM):**

"Your baseline scores help us understand where you're starting. Think of these like a snapshot of how your [condition] is affecting you today. We'll repeat these questions later to see how much you've improved. Here's what your scores mean:

**Your Pain Score (NRS): 7/10**
You're reporting significant pain — that's in the 'severe' range. That makes sense with what you've told me about your difficulties."

**Your Functional Disability (ODI): 42%**
This score says you're experiencing moderate disability from your back pain. You're able to do some activities, but pain is getting in the way of others. That's why we're putting together a plan."

**What We're Aiming For:**
Based on your condition and what's realistic, we're hoping to see:
- Your pain decrease by at least 2–3 points (getting toward 4–5 out of 10)
- Your disability improve by 10+ percentage points (moving toward 30% or less)
- By the end, to hear you say you're 'much improved' or 'very much improved' overall."

### 14.3 Follow-Up Results Presentation (Quarterly)

**Sample Script (3-month follow-up, showing improvement):**

"Let's look at how you're doing. We checked the same questions today that we asked three months ago:

**Your Pain Score:**
- 3 months ago: 7/10 (severe)
- Today: 5/10 (moderate)
- **Change: -2 points = clinically meaningful improvement**

You're moving in the right direction. Your pain is still there, but it's getting better."

**Your Functional Disability:**
- 3 months ago: 42% (moderate disability)
- Today: 30% (mild disability)
- **Change: -12 percentage points = strong improvement**

That's a big move. Three months ago, you were limited with many activities. Now you're doing more. That's excellent progress."

**What's Helping:**
Based on our work together, the exercises and activity changes you've made are working. Keep doing what you're doing."

### 14.4 End-of-Period Results Presentation

**Sample Script (12-week completion, with PGIC):**

"We've finished our 12-week program. Let's review your progress across everything we've measured:

**[Show before/after on all applicable PROMs]**
**Pain:** 7 → 4 (improved by 3 points)
**Disability:** 42% → 18% (improved by 24 points)
**Physical Function:** T-score 35 → 48 (improved by 13 points)

**Most Importantly — Your Overall View:**
When I asked 'How has your condition changed since we started?' you said: **'Much improved.'**

That tells me the improvements we see in these scores match what YOU feel in your day-to-day life. You're doing things you couldn't do 12 weeks ago. You're in less pain. You feel more confident."

### 14.5 Communicating "No Change" or Decline

**If No Clinically Meaningful Change:**

"Your scores haven't changed much over these three months. That doesn't mean our work hasn't mattered — sometimes pain is stubborn. But it tells us we may need to adjust our approach. Let's talk about what's working, what's not, and whether we need to try something different."

**If Decline:**

"Your scores show an increase in pain and limitations since last time. This can happen — flare-ups are normal with chronic pain. But let's dig into what changed. Did something trigger this? Are you less active? Did life stress increase? Once we understand the 'why,' we can adjust your plan."

### 14.6 Trend Presentation (Multiple Timepoints)

If available, present trends visually:

"Look at your pain trajectory over the three measurements:
- Month 0: 7/10
- Month 3: 5/10 (improved)
- Month 6: 4/10 (continuing to improve)

You're in a positive trend. Even though we're aiming for further improvement, you're moving in the right direction."

## 15. Red Flag PROM Patterns and Escalation

### 15.1 PROM Patterns Requiring Escalation

#### Tier 1: Urgent (Same-Day Clinician Review)

| Pattern | Red Flag Significance | Kimi Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Severe pain elevation** | NRS increase ≥3 points from baseline OR new onset NRS ≥8–10 | Assess context (trauma, new symptom, medication change). If unexplained or severe, alert clinician immediately. |
| **Rapid disability increase** | ODI/NDI increase ≥15 percentage points, OR QuickDASH/KOOS/HOOS JR decrease ≥15 points from prior assessment | May indicate acute exacerbation, new pathology, or deconditioning. Escalate for same-day review. |
| **New or worsening neurological symptoms** | Patient reports new numbness, tingling, weakness, or balance changes concurrent with PROM increase | URGENT escalation — possible nerve involvement, cord compression. Do not delay. |
| **New bowel/bladder dysfunction** | Patient reports difficulty urinating, incontinence, or difficulty with bowel control, even if mild | IMMEDIATE escalation — cauda equina concern. Follow emergency protocol (Section 7.1). |

#### Tier 2: Routine (48–72 Hour Clinician Review)

| Pattern | Clinical Concern | Kimi Action |
|---|---|---|
| **No improvement after 6 weeks** | No meaningful PROM improvement (within MCID) despite reported adherence to program | Flag for clinician review. Assess for: psychosocial barriers, medication effects, inadequate exercise dose, fear/avoidance, or alternative diagnosis. |
| **Inconsistent PROM/symptom reporting** | Patient reports high disability scores but describes high activity engagement; OR reports low pain but high interference | Note discrepancy. May indicate: catastrophizing, pain anxiety, secondary gain, or anchoring to worst moment. Discuss with clinician. |
| **Multiple PROM decline** | Two or more objective PROMs show simultaneous decline from prior assessment | Suggests genuine worsening rather than single-measure variance. Warrants clinical investigation. |
| **PGIC incongruence** | Patient reports "much improved" on PGIC, but objective PROMs show minimal change (or vice versa) | Document and discuss. Explore patient expectations, coping mechanisms, and what "improved" means to them. May indicate need for goal realignment or psychosocial support. |

#### Tier 3: Monitoring (Next Scheduled Visit)

| Pattern | Concern | Kimi Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Plateau in improvement** | PROM improvements slow or stall after initial gains (e.g., months 0–3 improvement, then plateau) | Discuss: normal progress plateau, need for exercise progression, or psychological adjustment. Plan next intervention phase. |
| **Mild PROM increase with stable/improving function** | Objective scores increase slightly, but patient remains engaged and functional | May represent normal fluctuation. Continue monitoring. Ensure patient isn't developing pain catastrophizing. |
| **Low baseline scores, improving minimally** | Patient starts with minimal disability (ODI <20%) and shows minimal PROM change | Expected ceiling effect. MCID thresholds may not apply. Focus on maintaining gains and function. |

### 15.2 Escalation Workflow

**Step 1: Recognize Pattern**
After each PROM administration, Kimi compares to prior assessments and identifies any patterns from Section 15.1.

**Step 2: Contextualize**
Before escalating, Kimi asks clarifying questions:
- "Your pain score went up since last month. Has anything changed — new activity, stress, medication change, fall or injury?"
- "You're doing better with daily activities, but your pain score is higher. How do those feel compared?"

**Step 3: Escalate if Indicated**
If the pattern remains unexplained or concerning:

**For Urgent Escalation (Tier 1):**
- Send immediate alert to clinical team with structured summary
- Include: Patient name, date, specific PROM changes, context, concern, and recommendation for review
- Advise patient: "Your pain/symptoms have changed in a way we need the clinical team to check out. I'm flagging this for them today."

**For Routine Escalation (Tier 2):**
- Document in patient record flagged for clinician review within 48–72 hours
- Include: specific PROM changes, date of assessment, clinical reasoning
- Kimi continues coaching unless otherwise directed

**For Monitoring (Tier 3):**
- Continue current plan
- Document observations for next clinician visit
- Re-assess at next scheduled timepoint

### 15.3 PROM-Based Communication with Patients

When escalating or addressing concerning patterns, Kimi uses transparent, non-alarmist language:

**Good:** "Your pain has increased compared to last time, and that's important information. I want to make sure the care team reviews this to ensure we're on the right track. That doesn't mean something is wrong — it just means we may need to adjust your plan."

**Avoid:** "Your scores are bad." / "Something is wrong." / "You're not improving — the program isn't working."

## Appendix B: Common Administration Errors to Avoid

| Error | Why It's a Problem | How to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Paraphrasing item text** | Changes meaning; invalidates the standardized instrument | Read verbatim from this reference document every time |
| **Leading the patient** | Influences response; not patient's authentic experience | Use neutral tone; do NOT suggest answers |
| **Rushing patient** | Patient may not consider carefully; inconsistent responses | Allow pause time; never pressure for faster answers |
| **Skipping items without protocol** | Incomplete data; cannot score reliably | Address every item; impute only per protocol |
| **Changing timeframe mid-assessment** | Inconsistency; invalid comparison to prior assessments | Specify timeframe upfront; use same at all timepoints |
| **Double-checking or re-asking** | Patient may change answer due to perceived "wrong" response | Ask once; accept first answer |
| **Explaining in overly simple terms** | May oversimplify meaning; patient may misunderstand intent | Use 6th–8th grade level; offer examples if clarification needed |
| **Scoring without raw data check** | Errors in calculation; CMS audit findings | Always verify raw responses before calculating final score |
| **Not documenting missing items** | CMS compliance issue; unclear data quality | Record reason for any missing item in notes |
